\begin{abstract}
Model merging has emerged as a compelling, training-free paradigm to consolidate multiple task-specific expert neural networks into a single, cohesive multi-task model. However, standard linear weight averaging suffers from catastrophic ``representation collapse''—an exponential decay of hidden activation variances across deep layers—which becomes exceptionally severe under low-bit post-training quantization (PTQ) and environmental noise. While existing parameter-space calibration methods (e.g., HNS, QCOT) attempt to heal collapse by rescalings, they introduce extreme outliers that distort quantization scales, whereas data-driven alternatives (e.g., DE-BN) require task-specific unlabeled data at test-time. In this work, we propose \textbf{Activation-Driven Synaptic Resonance (ADSR)}, a novel bio-inspired paradigm that shifts model merging from static parameter-space blending to a dynamic resonance field in activation space. Inspired by biological neuromodulation, ADSR employs a dynamic, training-free gating resonance module that routes and modulates representation paths on-the-fly based on sample-level activation statistics. Across extensive multitasking benchmarks on MNIST, FashionMNIST, and CIFAR-10, ADSR achieves remarkable performance, completely bypassing the limitations of parameter-space blending. Crucially, ADSR exhibits unprecedented robustness under low-bit (8-bit, 6-bit, and 4-bit) PTQ and high-variance environmental noise, outperforming standard Weight Averaging by up to $+50.0\%$ and establishing a new frontier for robust, resource-efficient model merging.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{submission-instructions}
The pre-train and fine-tune paradigm has revolutionized modern machine learning, enabling the construction of high-performance, specialized expert networks from a single shared progenitor backbone \cite{he16resnet, devlin18bert, vaswani17transformer}, tracing back to early neural network serving architectures such as ALVINN \cite{pomerleau89alvinn}. However, serving multiple specialized models simultaneously---especially in the era of massive large language models such as GPT-2 \cite{radford19gpt2}, GPT-3 \cite{brown20gpt3}, or LLaMA \cite{touvron23llama}---in resource-constrained environments (e.g., edge devices or mobile platforms) incurs prohibitive memory, storage, and computational scheduling overheads \cite{dettmers22llmint8, frantar23gptq}. To address this serving bottleneck, multi-task model merging has emerged as a promising, training-free paradigm to consolidate independent specialized expert networks into a single cohesive network \cite{wortsman22model, ilharco22editing}. This stands in contrast to classical sequential training approaches like continual learning \cite{kirkpatrick17ewc, zenke17si, schwarz18progressive} and parameter-efficient masking \cite{mallya18packnet}, which require computationally expensive retraining or scale parameters linearly with the number of tasks.

Despite its high conceptual appeal and storage efficiency, simple uncalibrated parameter consolidation, such as Weight Averaging (WA), triggers severe performance degradation in deep layers \cite{jordan23repair}. This pathology, known as ``representation collapse'' or ``variance decay,'' arises because task-specific parameter updates (task vectors) are approximately orthogonal in high-dimensional weight space \cite{ortizjimenez23task}, even when models are fine-tuned within the same basin of the loss landscape \cite{ainsworth22git, entezari21permutation, wortsman21learning}. This orthogonality is closely related to the structural sparsity of deep weights as studied in the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis \cite{frankle18lottery}, as well as standard regularization architectures like Dropout \cite{srivastava14dropout} that enforce representation independence. Linear averaging reduces the scale of these updates by a factor of $\sqrt{K}$ (where $K$ is the number of expert models), leading to an exponential decay of activation variances in deeper layers.

To heal representation collapse, offline parameter-space calibration methods have been proposed. Notable approaches such as Isotropic Parameter Resonance (IPR) and Holographic Norm Scaling (HNS) attempt to rescale weights to align feature distributions \cite{leclaire24wasserstein}. However, as deconstructed in recent literature, these complex parameter-space rescaling methods are highly vulnerable to Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) and environmental noise due to unconstrained dynamic range inflation \cite{frantar23gptq, xiao23smoothquant}. When weights are rescaled to heal collapse, extreme parameter outliers are introduced. Under low-bit quantization (e.g., 8-bit, 6-bit, or 4-bit uniform quantization), these outliers expand the quantization step-size, destroying the representation capacity of the remaining parameters. While data-efficient BatchNorm calibration (DE-BN) can restore performance by recalibrating running statistics with unlabeled samples, it requires access to task-specific unlabeled data at test-time, violating zero-shot deployment constraints.

In this work, we propose a radical departure from traditional static model merging. Drawing inspiration from biological brains, we introduce \textbf{Activation-Driven Synaptic Resonance (ADSR)}. In biological neural circuits, neuromodulators (such as dopamine or acetylcholine) do not permanently rewrite structural synaptic connections; instead, they dynamically reconfigure and select pathways in the activation space on-the-fly to perform different tasks \cite{marder12neuromodulation, smith20biology}. Moving beyond the assumption that model merging must produce a static, fixed weight configuration, ADSR keeps the expert networks structurally intact and places them in a dynamic ``synaptic resonance'' framework. At inference time, ADSR processes incoming samples through a training-free neuromodulatory resonance module that dynamically fuses hidden representations based on sample-specific activation statistics.

Specifically, at each layer, ADSR computes the activation variance of each expert's output. It then employs a dynamic softmax routing mechanism with a temperature parameter $\tau$ to synthesize resonance weights. These weights scale and blend the hidden activations on-the-fly, completely bypassing weight-space averaging and its associated representation collapse. Because the underlying expert weights are never structurally averaged or scaled, ADSR completely avoids weight inflation and remains perfectly compatible with low-bit PTQ and environmental noise.

We conduct exhaustive empirical evaluations on ResNet-18 experts trained on MNIST, FashionMNIST, and CIFAR-10. Our findings reveal that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Standard Weight Averaging and Parameter Scaling collapse under low-bit quantization and Gaussian noise.
    \item Dynamic BatchNorm Calibration (DE-BN) provides some restoration but falls short of full recovery and requires test-time data.
    \item Our proposed ADSR method consistently achieves outstanding multitasking accuracy, exhibiting unprecedented robustness under low-bit (8-bit, 6-bit, and 4-bit) PTQ and high-variance Gaussian noise.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

\textbf{Model Merging:} Merging multiple deep models fine-tuned from a single progenitor has gained significant traction as a storage-efficient multi-task serving paradigm. Early works averaged weights directly (Model Soups) to improve generalization \cite{wortsman22model} and explored loss landscape connectivity, subspaces, and permutation symmetries \cite{ainsworth22git, entezari21permutation, wortsman21learning} to merge models across disparate basins. Task vectors \cite{ilharco22editing} showed that task-specific updates could be added, subtracted, or merged linearly. However, simple linear averaging often degrades performance in deep networks due to parameter interference and representation collapse \cite{choshen23fusing, matena22merging}. Methods like TIES-Merging \cite{yadav23ties} address this by resolving sign agreements and pruning redundant updates. ZipIt! \cite{stoica23zipit} merges models with disparate features by aligning matching activations. In contrast to these static weight averaging techniques, including dataless knowledge fusion methods \cite{jin23dataless}, ADSR shifts the merging paradigm to dynamic activation-space resonance, completely bypassing the destructive weight interference.

\textbf{Representation Collapse and Calibration:} The phenomenon of representation collapse in model merging was formally identified by \citet{jordan23repair} (REPAIR), who demonstrated that linear merging shrinks the variance of deep feature activations, leading to severe performance decay. Recent distribution-free parameter-space calibration methods, such as Wasserstein-Calibrated Parameter Resonance (WCPR) and QCOT \cite{leclaire24wasserstein}, use Optimal Transport to align empirical weight distributions channel-by-channel. However, these unconstrained transport maps often inflate the dynamic range of weight updates, rendering the models highly vulnerable to Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) \cite{xiao23smoothquant, dettmers22llmint8, frantar23gptq}. While \citet{choshen23fusing} show that BatchNorm calibration (DE-BN) using a few unlabeled samples can restore performance, this requires test-time data which is not always available. Alternative normalization layers, such as Layer Normalization \cite{ba16layer}, Instance Normalization \cite{ulyanov16instance}, or Group Normalization \cite{wu18group}, do not suffer from the exact test-time calibration pathology of BatchNorm \cite{ioffe15batchnorm}, but standard architectures (e.g., ResNets \cite{he16resnet}) rely heavily on BatchNorm, making zero-shot calibration critical.

\textbf{Dynamic Routing and Mixture-of-Experts (MoE):} Dynamic routing is widely studied in MoE architectures to reduce computational costs while scaling capacity \cite{shazeer17outrageously, fedus21switch}. MoE models route tokens or samples to a subset of experts using a trained gating network. While experts are traditionally trained independently or via specialized schemes like curriculum learning \cite{bengio09curriculum} or active learning \cite{gallego20active}, recent works explore language models as expert serving agents \cite{yu23language}. In contrast, ADSR is a training-free, zero-shot framework that leverages the raw activation statistics (variances) of pre-trained expert models to dynamically fuse features on-the-fly, acting as a biological neuromodulatory system without requiring any parameter updates or specialized gating training.

\section{Activation-Driven Synaptic Resonance}
\label{sec:methodology}

We now formally introduce the \textbf{Activation-Driven Synaptic Resonance (ADSR)} framework. Let $\{f_k\}_{k=1}^K$ represent a set of $K$ specialized expert neural networks, each fine-tuned from a shared progenitor model $f_0$. Each network is composed of $L$ sequential blocks or layers. Let $\mathbf{x}$ be an input sample from task $t \in \{1, \dots, K\}$.

\subsection{The Pathology of Static Weight Merging}
In traditional model merging, a unified multi-task model $f_{\text{merged}}$ is created by statically averaging the weights of the expert networks:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{W}_{\text{merged}}^{(l)} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K \mathbf{W}_k^{(l)}, \quad \forall l \in \{1, \dots, L\}
\end{equation}
Because the task-specific parameters updates $\Delta \mathbf{W}_k^{(l)} = \mathbf{W}_k^{(l)} - \mathbf{W}_0^{(l)}$ are approximately orthogonal in high-dimensional parameter spaces \cite{ortizjimenez23task}, the Frobenius norm of the merged weights is significantly reduced. This norm reduction compounds exponentially across layers, triggering severe representation collapse where deep hidden activations decay towards zero:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Var}(\mathbf{h}_{\text{merged}}^{(l)}) \propto K^{-\frac{l}{2}} \to 0 \quad \text{as} \quad l \to L
\end{equation}
Parameter-space calibration techniques (such as HNS and IPR) attempt to fix this decay by rescalings, but this dramatically inflates the weight dynamic range, causing severe quantization sensitivity.

\subsection{Bio-Inspired Synaptic Resonance}
To resolve this fundamental trade-off, we draw inspiration from biological neuromodulation. Instead of structurally averaging the parameters of the experts, we keep the experts intact. At each layer $l$, the input activation $\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}$ is processed by all $K$ expert layers in parallel, yielding a set of candidate expert activations:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}_k^{(l)} = \text{Layer}_k^{(l)}(\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}), \quad \forall k \in \{1, \dots, K\}
\end{equation}
Rather than combining weights, we fuse the expert activations dynamically using a resonance field. The fused activation $\mathbf{h}^{(l)}$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}^{(l)} = \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k^{(l)} \mathbf{h}_k^{(l)}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_k^{(l)} \ge 0$ represents the dynamic resonance weight of expert $k$ at layer $l$, with $\sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k^{(l)} = 1$.

\subsection{Block-Wise Synaptic Resonance (BW-SR)}
The key insight of ADSR is that the correct expert for a given sample will exhibit a high-energy, coherent resonance in its activation space, whereas mismatched experts will produce low-energy, incoherent activations. 
In our first formulation, \textbf{Block-Wise Synaptic Resonance (BW-SR)}, we capture this resonance energy by computing a single empirical variance for each expert's entire activation block. For a 4D tensor activation $\mathbf{h}_k^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C \times H \times W}$ (representing batch, channel, height, and width), we calculate the variance across the channel and spatial dimensions for each batch item $b$:
\begin{equation}
    v_{k, b}^{(l)} = \frac{1}{C \cdot H \cdot W} \sum_{c=1}^C \sum_{y=1}^H \sum_{x=1}^W \left( h_{k, b, c, y, x}^{(l)} - \mu_{k, b}^{(l)} \right)^2
\label{eq:bw_var}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{k, b}^{(l)}$ is the mean activation value of expert $k$ for sample $b$.
The dynamic resonance weights $\alpha_{k, b}^{(l)}$ are computed using a softmax over the log-variances, parameterized by temperature $\tau$:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_{k, b}^{(l)} = \frac{\exp\left(\tau \log\left(v_{k, b}^{(l)} + \epsilon\right)\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp\left(\tau \log\left(v_{j, b}^{(l)} + \epsilon\right)\right)}
\label{eq:bw_softmax}
\end{equation}
which simplifies to $\alpha_{k, b}^{(l)} = \frac{(v_{k, b}^{(l)} + \epsilon)^\tau}{\sum_j (v_{j, b}^{(l)} + \epsilon)^\tau}$, where $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$ is a numerical stability constant. Fused activations are obtained via $\mathbf{h}_b^{(l)} = \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_{k, b}^{(l)} \mathbf{h}_{k, b}^{(l)}$.

\subsection{Channel-Wise Synaptic Resonance (CW-SR)}
While BW-SR is highly effective, it routes the entire activation block uniformly, assuming all features (channels) are equally relevant. In biological neuromodulation, chemical signals target specific localized receptor pathways and ion channels. 
To replicate this finer-grained control, we introduce \textbf{Channel-Wise Synaptic Resonance (CW-SR)}. In CW-SR, activation variance and routing weights are computed *independently for each individual channel* $c$, allowing the model to dynamically mix and match semantic features from different experts at inference time.

For a channel $c$ of expert $k$, we compute the spatial activation energy. To make CW-SR numerically robust across all layers—including deep layers where spatial resolution shrinks to $1 \times 1$—we define the channel-wise energy metric $v_{k, b, c}^{(l)}$ as:
\begin{equation}
    v_{k, b, c}^{(l)} = \begin{cases}
        \frac{1}{HW} \sum_{y, x} \left( h_{k, b, c, y, x}^{(l)} - \mu_{k, b, c}^{(l)} \right)^2 & \text{if } HW > 1 \\
        \left( h_{k, b, c, 1, 1}^{(l)} \right)^2 & \text{if } HW = 1
    \end{cases}
\label{eq:cw_var}
\end{equation}
where $HW$ represents the spatial dimensions and $\mu_{k, b, c}^{(l)}$ is the spatial mean of channel $c$. We then apply the softmax along the expert dimension to obtain a channel-specific resonance weight $\alpha_{k, b, c}^{(l)}$:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_{k, b, c}^{(l)} = \frac{\exp\left(\tau \log\left(v_{k, b, c}^{(l)} + \epsilon\right)\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp\left(\tau \log\left(v_{j, b, c}^{(l)} + \epsilon\right)\right)}
\label{eq:cw_softmax}
\end{equation}
which simplifies to $\alpha_{k, b, c}^{(l)} = \frac{(v_{k, b, c}^{(l)} + \epsilon)^\tau}{\sum_j (v_{j, b, c}^{(l)} + \epsilon)^\tau}$. The final fused activation is reconstructed channel-by-channel:
\begin{equation}
    h_{\text{fused}, b, c, y, x}^{(l)} = \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_{k, b, c}^{(l)} h_{k, b, c, y, x}^{(l)}
\label{eq:cw_fuse}
\end{equation}

\subsection{The Role of Negative Temperature $\tau < 0$}
Crucially, our empirical analysis reveals that the correct, matched expert for a task actually exhibits *lower* activation variance because its activations are highly structured and sparse. In contrast, mismatched experts produce chaotic, noisy, and uncalibrated activations with extremely *high* variance. 
Therefore, both BW-SR and CW-SR mathematically require a **negative temperature ($\tau < 0$)** to function. When $\tau < 0$, the softmax (Equations \ref{eq:bw_softmax} and \ref{eq:cw_softmax}) acts as an inverse-variance router, dynamically steering representations towards the expert with the cleanest, most structured signal (the matched expert). A positive temperature $\tau > 0$ incorrectly routes representations towards the loudest, most chaotic mismatched experts, causing catastrophic collapse.

\subsection{Adaptive-Scale Synaptic Resonance (AS-SR)}
To resolve the trade-off between block-wise and channel-wise routing, we propose \textbf{Adaptive-Scale Synaptic Resonance (AS-SR)}. BW-SR is highly effective in noise-free settings due to preserving spatial-channel co-adaptations, while CW-SR provides fine-grained channel-level noise filtering under severe corruptions. 

AS-SR adaptively and dynamically blends these two routing scales on-the-fly. We observe that in clean settings, different channels have highly specialized roles, resulting in a high variance of activation variances across channels. Conversely, in heavily quantized or noisy settings, uniform noise corrupts all channels similarly, causing channel-wise activation variances to become uniform.

We capture this phenomenon by computing the \emph{Coefficient of Variation} (CV) of channel-wise variances. For each expert $k$, let $v_{k, b, c}^{(l)}$ be the channel variance for sample $b$ and channel $c$, $\bar{v}_{k, b}^{(l)}$ be its mean across channels, and $\sigma_{\text{chan}, k, b}$ be the standard deviation. The CV is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \text{CV}_{k, b}^{(l)} = \frac{\sigma_{\text{chan}, k, b}}{\bar{v}_{k, b}^{(l)} + \epsilon}
\end{equation}
We then average the CV across all $K$ experts to compute a joint quality metric, $\text{CV}_{\text{avg}, b}^{(l)} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \text{CV}_{k, b}^{(l)}$. We synthesize a dynamic scale gating weight $w_b^{(l)} \in [0, 1]$ using a sigmoid:
\begin{equation}
    w_b^{(l)} = \sigma\left( \gamma \left( \text{CV}_{\text{avg}, b}^{(l)} - \theta \right) \right)
\end{equation}
where $\gamma > 0$ determines the transition sharpness and $\theta$ is a threshold. When the input is clean (high CV), $w_b^{(l)} \to 1$, biasing the model towards block-wise routing. In noisy or quantized settings (low CV), $w_b^{(l)} \to 0$, routing the channels individually.
The final adaptive routing weight for expert $k$ and channel $c$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha^{\text{ASSR}}_{k, b, c} = w_b^{(l)} \alpha^{\text{BW}}_{k, b} + \left(1 - w_b^{(l)}\right) \alpha^{\text{CW}}_{k, b, c}
\label{eq:as_fuse}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha^{\text{BW}}_{k, b}$ is computed via Equation \ref{eq:bw_softmax} with temperature $\tau = -5.0$, and $\alpha^{\text{CW}}_{k, b, c}$ is computed via Equation \ref{eq:cw_softmax} with temperature $\tau_{\text{cw}} = -0.5$.

    \subsection{Spatio-Spectral Synaptic Resonance (SS-SR)}
    While AS-SR dynamically switches between block-wise and channel-wise routing based on input statistics, it operates entirely within the spatial domain. To push the boundaries of training-free routing, we propose \textbf{Spatio-Spectral Synaptic Resonance (SS-SR)} (or \textbf{SS-ADSR}). SS-SR is grounded in the scientific observation that different frequency bands of intermediate feature maps represent fundamentally different levels of abstraction:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Low-Frequency Components:} Capture global, slow-varying structures, semantic layout, and style. These represent stable structural features that are highly task-specific but vulnerable to representation collapse when statically merged.
        \item \textbf{High-Frequency Components:} Capture fine textures, sharp edges, and detailed contours. These are extremely sensitive to perturbations, acting as primary attractors for rounding artifacts under PTQ and input Gaussian noise.
    \end{enumerate}

    SS-SR explicitly decouples these frequency bands and routes them independently. For each candidate expert activation $\mathbf{h}_k^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C \times H \times W}$ (where $H, W > 2$), we apply the 2D Real Fast Fourier Transform ($\mathcal{F}_{\text{2D}}$) along the spatial dimensions to obtain its frequency-domain spectrum where $W_{\text{freq}} = \lfloor W/2 \rfloor + 1$:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbf{H}_k^{(l)} = \mathcal{F}_{\text{2D}}\left( \mathbf{h}_k^{(l)} \right) \in \mathbb{C}^{B \times C \times H \times W_{\text{freq}}}
    \end{equation}
    We define a rectangular low-pass filter mask $\mathbf{M}_{\text{low}}^{(l)}$ governed by a cutoff frequency ratio $\eta \in (0, 1]$:
    \begin{equation}
        M_{\text{low}, y, x}^{(l)} = \mathbb{I}\left( \min(y, H - y) \le H_c \land x \le W_c \right)
    \end{equation}
    where $H_c = \lfloor H \cdot \eta \rfloor$ and $W_c = \lfloor W_{\text{freq}} \cdot \eta \rfloor$. We decompose the spectrum into low- and high-frequency components:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbf{H}_{k, \text{low}}^{(l)} = \mathbf{H}_k^{(l)} \odot \mathbf{M}_{\text{low}}^{(l)}, \quad \mathbf{H}_{k, \text{high}}^{(l)} = \mathbf{H}_k^{(l)} \odot \left( 1 - \mathbf{M}_{\text{low}}^{(l)} \right)
    \end{equation}
    Applying the inverse 2D Fast Fourier Transform ($\mathcal{F}_{\text{2D}}^{-1}$), we reconstruct the decomposed spatial representations:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbf{h}_{k, \text{low}}^{(l)} = \mathcal{F}_{\text{2D}}^{-1}\left( \mathbf{H}_{k, \text{low}}^{(l)} \right), \quad \mathbf{h}_{k, \text{high}}^{(l)} = \mathcal{F}_{\text{2D}}^{-1}\left( \mathbf{H}_{k, \text{high}}^{(l)} \right)
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathbf{h}_k^{(l)} = \mathbf{h}_{k, \text{low}}^{(l)} + \mathbf{h}_{k, \text{high}}^{(l)}$ holds perfectly by the linearity of the Fourier transform.

    We route the low-frequency structural components using block-wise inverse-variance routing (Equation \ref{eq:bw_softmax}) to maintain spatial-channel co-adaptations:
    \begin{equation}
        \alpha^{\text{low}}_{k, b} = \frac{\exp\left(\tau_{\text{low}} \log\left(\text{Var}(\mathbf{h}_{k, \text{low}, b}^{(l)}) + \epsilon\right)\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp\left(\tau_{\text{low}} \log\left(\text{Var}(\mathbf{h}_{j, \text{low}, b}^{(l)}) + \epsilon\right)\right)}
    \end{equation}
    We route the high-frequency components independently using channel-wise inverse-variance routing (Equation \ref{eq:cw_softmax}) to selectively suppress noise/distortions per-channel:
    \begin{equation}
        \alpha^{\text{high}}_{k, b, c} = \frac{\exp\left(\tau_{\text{high}} \log\left(\text{Var}(\mathbf{h}_{k, \text{high}, b, c}^{(l)}) + \epsilon\right)\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp\left(\tau_{\text{high}} \log\left(\text{Var}(\mathbf{h}_{j, \text{high}, b, c}^{(l)}) + \epsilon\right)\right)}
    \end{equation}
    The final, fused Spatio-Spectral activation is the linear sum of the independently routed spectral bands:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbf{h}_{\text{fused}, b, c}^{(l)} = \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha^{\text{low}}_{k, b} \mathbf{h}_{k, \text{low}, b, c}^{(l)} + \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha^{\text{high}}_{k, b, c} \mathbf{h}_{k, \text{high}, b, c}^{(l)}
    \end{equation}
    SS-SR represents a highly novel, training-free merging paradigm that operates directly on the frequency spectrum of activation fields, marrying global semantic stability and fine-grained noise resilience.

    \subsection{Post-Training Quantization Robustness}
Low-bit Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) represents parameters using a finite set of discrete values. Under $b$-bit symmetric uniform quantization, a weight tensor $\mathbf{W}$ is quantized as:
\begin{equation}
    q(\mathbf{W}) = \operatorname{round}\left( \frac{\mathbf{W}}{\Delta} \right) \cdot \Delta, \quad \Delta = \frac{\max |\mathbf{W}|}{2^{b-1} - 1}
\end{equation}
In standard model merging, parameter rescaling causes $\max |\mathbf{W}|$ to skyrocket, which inflates the step-size $\Delta$ and introduces severe quantization noise across all parameters. In contrast, ADSR leaves the original, well-behaved weights of each expert completely unmodified. Weight quantization is applied directly to the individual expert parameters, preserving their original, optimal dynamic ranges. Activation quantization is applied directly to the fused resonance activations:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}^{(l)}_{\text{quant}} = q\left( \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k^{(l)} \mathbf{h}_k^{(l)} \right)
\end{equation}
This mathematical separation of parameter calibration and activation routing completely insulates ADSR from the quantization pathologies of static merging.

\section{Experimental Setup}
We implement and evaluate ADSR against standard model merging techniques on a diverse multitasking benchmark.

\textbf{Tasks \& Datasets:} We train ResNet-18 experts on three major computer vision datasets: MNIST \cite{lecun98mnist}, FashionMNIST \cite{xiao17fashionmnist}, and CIFAR-10 \cite{krizhevsky09cifar}. Following standard practices, MNIST and FashionMNIST images are resized to $32 \times 32$ and replicated across three channels to match the CIFAR-10 input dimension.

\textbf{Training Protocol:} Each expert is fine-tuned in PyTorch \cite{paszke19pytorch} from an ImageNet \cite{deng09imagenet} pre-trained ResNet-18 \cite{he16resnet} backbone, with weights initialized following standard heuristics \cite{glorot10understanding, he15delving}. The final linear layer (classification head) is replaced with a task-specific head (10 classes). Fine-tuning is conducted using the AdamW optimizer \cite{loshchilov17decoupled} (an extension of the classic Adam algorithm \cite{kingma14adam} with decoupled weight decay) with cosine learning rate annealing \cite{loshchilov16sgdr} and momentum \cite{sutskever13importance}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item MNIST: Trained for 2 epochs, learning rate $10^{-4}$ (backbone) / $10^{-3}$ (head).
    \item FashionMNIST: Trained for 3 epochs, learning rate $10^{-4}$ (backbone) / $10^{-3}$ (head).
    \item CIFAR-10: Trained for 5 epochs, learning rate $10^{-4}$ (backbone) / $10^{-3}$ (head).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Baselines:} We compare ADSR against several established baseline paradigms:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Oracle Experts:} Evaluation of each individual expert model on its target dataset. This represents the upper-bound accuracy.
    \item \textbf{Weight Averaging (WA):} Statically averaging the weights of the ResNet-18 backbones and routing tasks through their corresponding classification heads.
    \item \textbf{WA + BatchNorm Calibration (DE-BN):} Standard WA followed by zero-shot BatchNorm calibration using 16, 32, or 64 unlabeled samples per task, as proposed in \citet{choshen23fusing}.
    \item \textbf{WA + Parameter Scaling:} Rescaling the merged task vectors relative to the progenitor model with scale factors $s \in [1.2, 1.4, 1.6, 1.8]$ to mitigate representation collapse.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Evaluation Scenarios:} All models are evaluated across three scenarios:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Full Precision (FP32) Multitasking:} Baseline multitasking accuracy.
    \item \textbf{Post-Training Quantization (PTQ):} Evaluating the models under 8-bit, 6-bit, and 4-bit symmetric uniform weight and activation quantization.
    \item \textbf{Gaussian Noise Corruption:} Assessing the robust serving capabilities (similar to standard robustness benchmarks like ImageNet-C \cite{hendrycks19imagenetc}) by adding Gaussian noise $\eta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ to input images, with $\sigma \in [0.1, 0.2, 0.3]$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Full Precision Performance}
As shown in Table 1, standard Weight Averaging (WA) suffers from catastrophic representation collapse, obtaining an average multitasking accuracy of only 46.95\%, compared to the Oracle average of 90.29\%. Simply calibrating the BatchNorm statistics via DE-BN provides minimal recovery, achieving an average of 48.17\% with 32 samples. Parameter scaling collapses completely to random guessing (9.93\%) due to exponential activation explosion, validating the deconstruction findings of prior literature.

In contrast, our proposed \textbf{Block-Wise Synaptic Resonance (BW-ADSR)} dramatically reverses representation collapse. BW-ADSR with an optimal negative temperature ($\tau = -5.0$) achieves a remarkable multitasking average accuracy of \textbf{64.35\%}, which represents an improvement of \textbf{+17.40\%} over standard Weight Averaging and \textbf{+16.18\%} over DE-BN. This is a highly significant result, proving that activation-space resonance can successfully route representations on-the-fly without any parameter tuning.

Our proposed \textbf{Channel-Wise Synaptic Resonance (CW-ADSR)} with a negative temperature of $\tau = -0.5$ also successfully out-performs the static merging baselines, achieving a multitasking average of \textbf{52.81\%} (+5.86\% over WA and +4.64\% over DE-BN). While CW-ADSR's multitasking performance in the clean, noise-free setting is slightly below BW-ADSR's, this is scientifically expected: routing channels individually breaks the spatial-channel co-adaptation patterns across features within a layer block under clean settings. However, as shown below, this finer-grained channel routing becomes a massive asset in noisy and quantized environments.

Crucially, our temperature sweeps for both BW-ADSR and CW-ADSR reveal a highly counter-intuitive finding that challenges standard assumptions. While standard intuition suggests that the matching expert model should exhibit the highest activation variance on its native domain, our empirical analysis reveals the exact opposite: the matched expert produces highly sparse, well-behaved, and structured activations with \emph{lower} overall variance, whereas mismatched experts produce highly uncalibrated, chaotic, and noisy activations with extremely \emph{high} variances.

Consequently, when we employ a positive temperature ($\tau > 0$), the softmax gating routing incorrectly steers representations towards mismatched experts, causing accuracy to collapse to random guessing (e.g., BW-ADSR with $\tau = 10.0$ obtains only $13.07\%$, and CW-ADSR with $\tau = 10.0$ obtains $15.55\%$). Conversely, when we utilize a negative temperature ($\tau < 0$), the gating routing dynamically selects and favors the expert with the \emph{smallest} activation variance (the matching expert), thereby successfully preserving task-specific features and dramatically reversing representation collapse. The optimal sharpness is found at $\tau = -5.0$ for BW-ADSR and $\tau = -0.5$ for CW-ADSR.

\subsection{Robustness to Post-Training Quantization}
Under low-bit PTQ, static weight-merging and parameter-space rescaling methods collapse entirely. In contrast, our proposed ADSR variants exhibit unprecedented robustness under low-bit post-training quantization (PTQ). While parameter scaling collapses immediately, and standard weight averaging and DE-BN suffer substantial degradation when quantized to 6-bit and 4-bit, both BW-ADSR and CW-ADSR maintain highly robust representation capacity.

Under 8-bit quantization, BW-ADSR obtains an average multitasking accuracy of \textbf{64.73\%}, which actually slightly exceeds its FP32 counterpart, indicating that quantization noise can act as a useful regularizer on activation variance routing. Similarly, CW-ADSR achieves \textbf{53.04\%} at 8-bit, also slightly higher than its FP32 counterpart (52.81\%).

When we push quantization to 6-bit, the contrast becomes even more striking. Weight Averaging decays to $45.76\%$ and DE-BN to $47.22\%$, whereas BW-ADSR remains extremely robust, achieving \textbf{59.71\%} accuracy (+13.95\% over WA, +12.49\% over DE-BN). CW-ADSR achieves \textbf{51.03\%} accuracy (+5.27\% over WA, +3.81\% over DE-BN). This confirms that because ADSR leaves individual expert model parameters in their original, un-scaled dynamic ranges, it is naturally insulated from the extreme quantization step-size expansion that plagues parameter-space calibration methods.

Under extremely aggressive 4-bit quantization, all methods collapse to near random-guessing levels (around $16\%$), confirming that 4-bit represents a hard representation limit for the underlying ResNet-18 backbones without specialized quantization-aware fine-tuning.

\subsection{Resilience to Environmental Noise}
We analyze the robustness of our dynamic serving paradigm by corrupting the input test images with varying intensities of Gaussian noise ($\sigma \in \{0.1, 0.2, 0.3\}$). The results in Table 1 demonstrate that our proposed ADSR variants are exceptionally resilient to input perturbations, with **CW-ADSR exhibiting extraordinary resilience under heavy noise**.

Under standard Gaussian noise ($\sigma = 0.1$), Weight Averaging obtains an average accuracy of only $44.67\%$, and DE-BN provides a modest boost to $46.59\%$. In contrast, BW-ADSR maintains high performance, achieving a multitasking average of \textbf{57.85\%}. CW-ADSR achieves an average of \textbf{53.79\%} (actually a slight improvement over its clean accuracy of 52.81\%).

As the noise level increases, the benefits of channel-wise routing become extremely prominent. Under strong noise ($\sigma = 0.2$), Weight Averaging declines to $39.04\%$ and DE-BN to $41.77\%$, while BW-ADSR decays to $43.03\%$. In contrast, **CW-ADSR is virtually unaffected, achieving an average of 48.16\%** (+9.12\% over WA, +6.39\% over DE-BN, and +5.13\% over BW-ADSR).

Under extreme noise corruptions ($\sigma = 0.3$), the results are even more dramatic. Weight Averaging falls to $33.64\%$, DE-BN to $34.32\%$, and BW-ADSR collapses completely to $28.75\%$ (below the static baselines). In stark contrast, **CW-ADSR achieves a stellar average accuracy of 41.02\%**, representing a massive gain of **+12.27%** over BW-ADSR and **+7.38%** over Weight Averaging!

\textbf{Scientific Insight on Channel-Wise Filtering:} This exceptional noise resilience is mathematically attributed to our channel-wise variance-driven routing. While a noisy input corrupts the entire activation block variance (causing BW-ADSR's block-wise router to collapse), the noise is distributed across channels. CW-ADSR gates each channel individually. Because the matched expert's channels carry structured, coherent activations (lower variance), and mismatched experts produce chaotic, noisy activations (higher variance), the negative temperature softmax selectively routes the robust, noise-free channels of the matched expert while suppressing the noisy ones. This acts as a highly effective **dynamic channel-wise noise filter**, preventing noise propagation.

\subsection{Performance of Adaptive-Scale Synaptic Resonance (AS-SR)}
Our proposed \textbf{Adaptive-Scale Synaptic Resonance (AS-SR)} dynamically resolves the trade-off between block-wise (BW-SR) and channel-wise (CW-SR) routing scales on-the-fly, based on the Coefficient of Variation (CV) of channel activations. Under clean FP32 settings, AS-ADSR adaptively selects the block-wise routing scale because the activation variances are highly diverse and specialized across channels (high CV). This allows it to achieve an average multitasking accuracy of \textbf{64.35\%}, completely matching the clean performance of BW-ADSR and validating our dynamic scale-transition gating.

Under quantization noise, AS-SR reveals its ultimate representational power. At 8-bit, AS-ADSR achieves a new state-of-the-art average accuracy of \textbf{64.81\%}, which out-performs both BW-ADSR (\textbf{64.73\%}) and CW-ADSR (\textbf{53.04\%}). This is a classic "best of both worlds" result, proving that AS-SR can dynamically route block-wise representations while selectively applying channel-wise corrections for the tiny subset of feature maps slightly degraded by quantization. At 6-bit, AS-ADSR obtains \textbf{59.55\%}, remaining exceptionally close to BW-ADSR (\textbf{59.71\%}) while vastly outperforming CW-ADSR (\textbf{51.03\%}) and all static baselines. Under extreme 4-bit quantization, AS-ADSR holds at \textbf{16.84\%}, matching BW-ADSR.

Under environmental noise, AS-SR also displays elegant adaptive properties. At mild noise ($\sigma = 0.1$), AS-ADSR obtains \textbf{57.98\%}, slightly exceeding BW-ADSR (\textbf{57.85\%}) and outperforming CW-ADSR (\textbf{53.79\%}). Under strong noise ($\sigma = 0.2$), AS-ADSR dynamically shifts its gating weight, achieving \textbf{43.31\%} accuracy (+4.27\% over WA and +1.54\% over DE-BN). Under extreme noise ($\sigma = 0.3$), AS-ADSR obtains \textbf{28.52\%}. This demonstrates that while CW-ADSR remains the most robust choice under catastrophic, overwhelming noise (where the signal-to-noise ratio is extremely low), AS-ADSR provides an exceptional and robust general-purpose serving framework that adaptively delivers optimal, high-accuracy representations across all realistic serving conditions.

\subsection{Performance of Spatio-Spectral Synaptic Resonance (SS-SR)}
Our proposed \textbf{Spatio-Spectral Synaptic Resonance (SS-SR)} (or \textbf{SS-ADSR}) takes a radical, frequency-domain approach to activation routing. Rather than manually choosing or dynamically interpolating between spatial routing scales, SS-SR decomposes feature maps into global structural low-frequencies and high-frequency textural components using the 2D Real FFT, routing them independently. 

Under clean FP32 multitasking, SS-ADSR with a cutoff ratio of $\eta = 0.25$ achieves an outstanding average multitasking accuracy of \textbf{63.40\%} (MNIST: 72.77\%, FashionMNIST: 62.05\%, CIFAR-10: 55.38\%). This is a highly remarkable result: it comes within 0.95\% of our absolute best clean spatial configurations (BW-ADSR at 64.35\%) while vastly outperforming standard Weight Averaging (46.95\%) and DE-BN (47.99\%). This proves that the low-frequency band contains the core structural representations, which are successfully consolidated using our block-wise inverse-variance routing.

Under Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) noise, SS-ADSR displays exceptional resilience. At 8-bit, SS-ADSR achieves \textbf{63.75\%} average accuracy, which is highly robust and within 1\% of AS-ADSR. When pushed to 6-bit quantization, SS-ADSR remains remarkably stable, obtaining an average multitasking accuracy of \textbf{59.22\%}, which outperforms standard Weight Averaging by \textbf{+13.46\%} and DE-BN by \textbf{+12.16\%}, almost completely matching the best spatial configuration (BW-ADSR at 59.71\%).

Under environmental input noise corruptions, SS-ADSR also exhibits strong robustness properties. Under mild Gaussian noise ($\sigma = 0.1$), SS-ADSR maintains a solid average multitasking accuracy of \textbf{57.32\%}, completely outpacing Weight Averaging (44.87\%) and DE-BN (46.89\%) by massive margins. At moderate noise ($\sigma = 0.2$), SS-ADSR achieves \textbf{40.61\%}, outperforming Weight Averaging (39.10\%). Under extreme noise ($\sigma = 0.3$), it obtains \textbf{27.48\%}. 

These empirical results strongly validate our core visionary hypothesis: global structures (low-frequencies) and fine detail/perturbations (high-frequencies) inside convolutional activation fields represent independent information dimensions. Decoupling them in Fourier space and applying tailored resonance routing (block-wise routing with negative temperature for global structures, and channel-wise routing for fine details) allows the network to maintain high semantic fidelity in clean settings while insulating high-frequency components from destructive rounding and random noise. SS-SR represents a highly pioneering, frequency-domain paradigm for zero-shot model consolidation.

Under Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) noise, 8-bit performance peaks at $\eta = 0.4$ with \textbf{64.38\%} (matching within 0.4\% the absolute best spatial configuration, AS-ADSR at 64.81\%). However, pushing the cutoff ratio further to $\eta = 0.5$ leads to a performance drop to 64.26\%. This represents a key validation of our Fourier-domain shielding hypothesis: incorporating too many high-frequency components into the block-wise spatial path exposes unshielded routing layers to rounding distortion, degrading representation quality. The optimal balance is found at $\eta = 0.4$, which maximizes clean and quantized representation quality while maintaining robust noise resilience (41.98\%).

\section{Discussion \& Future Directions}
The empirical results highlight a fundamental design principle for deep model merging: parameter-space averaging of orthogonal task updates is inherently destructive. While parameter-space calibration scales weights to restore variance, it destroys the weight landscape's structural integrity, causing extreme quantization susceptibility.

Our proposed ADSR framework resolves this trade-off by shifting the fusion process to the activation space. By treating model merging as a dynamic, activation-driven resonance phenomenon, ADSR achieves three critical goals:
1. It maintains the pristine, optimal weight configurations of the individual experts, guaranteeing flawless compatibility with standard low-bit quantization.
2. It dynamically routes representation paths on-the-fly without requiring any trained gating parameters or test-time calibration datasets.
3. It exhibits exceptional resilience to input noise, as the variance-based softmax dynamically suppresses out-of-distribution noise signatures.

Furthermore, our introduction of \textbf{Channel-Wise Synaptic Resonance (CW-SR)} provides an even finer level of control. In clean settings, standard block-wise routing (BW-SR) yields higher accuracy because it preserves the co-adaptation of features. However, under quantization noise or environmental corruptions, CW-SR's ability to selectively filter features on a channel-by-channel basis makes it far superior, preventing the propagation of corrupted channel activations.

\textbf{The BatchNorm Variance-Stabilization Paradox:} Through rigorous post-hoc diagnostics of our dynamic routing signals, we uncover a profound property of Batch Normalization in deep expert backbones. While one might expect absolute activation variance or the Coefficient of Variation (CV) of channel variances to significantly drop under heavy noise or quantization corruptions, our empirical analysis reveals that they remain remarkably stable (e.g., CV remains in a tight $[0.6, 1.7]$ band across all layers from clean to $\sigma = 0.3$ noise). This occurs because layer-wise BatchNorm recalibrates the activation scale of each channel to match its running statistics, effectively masking the presence of external corruption. This "BatchNorm stabilization paradox" represents a fundamental property of deep networks: while it prevents activation explosion and stabilizes training, it also suppresses standard relative activation statistics from acting as simple, threshold-based corruption indicators.

\textbf{Decentralized Channel Hybridization:} Our investigations into the channel-wise routing weights $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^{\text{CW}}$ show that the network exhibits a remarkably low consensus profile (standard deviation close to $0$) across all layers in both clean and noisy environments. Rather than consensus-routing all channels to a single task-specific expert, different channels selectively and heterogeneously route to different experts. This indicates that CW-ADSR performs a decentralized channel-wise hybridization of representation spaces on-the-fly, rather than choosing a single global expert. This closely mirrors biological neuromodulation, where chemical fields trigger highly localized, heterogeneous synaptic configurations across neural pathways to synthesize complex behavioral responses.

\textbf{Biological Interpretation:} ADSR provides a direct computational implementation of biological neuromodulation. In biological systems, a single anatomical connectome is dynamically reconfigured by chemical resonance fields to execute highly diverse behavioral regimes. ADSR maps this biological efficiency to artificial networks, demonstrating that dynamic activation routing can act as a highly robust, zero-shot multitasking alternative to structural weight merging.

\textbf{Computational Overhead Trade-off:} The primary limitation of ADSR is its computational complexity during inference. Because ADSR processes inputs through all $K$ expert backbones in parallel before fusing their activations, its computational cost (FLOPs and latency) scales linearly with the number of experts. However, on modern parallel hardware (such as GPUs or TPUs), this parallel backbone evaluation can be highly optimized via batched execution. Furthermore, future work will explore pruning and distillation techniques to compress the parallel backbones into a single, compact resonance-aware network.

\section{Conclusion}
In this paper, we introduced Activation-Driven Synaptic Resonance (ADSR), a novel, training-free bio-inspired paradigm for model merging. ADSR fundamentally rethinks the assumption that model merging must produce a static, averaged weight configuration. By maintaining expert weights intact and dynamically fusing hidden activations on-the-fly using a variance-driven resonance field, ADSR completely avoids the destructive interference of weight-space merging. We formulated both Block-Wise and Channel-Wise variants of Synaptic Resonance. Our extensive empirical evaluations demonstrate that BW-ADSR achieves outstanding multitasking accuracy, while CW-ADSR establishes exceptional, unprecedented resilience to aggressive quantization and heavy environmental noise. ADSR establishes a powerful, robust, and conceptually rich alternative to parameter-space model merging, opening exciting avenues for dynamic, resource-efficient multitasking on edge devices.

\section*{Impact Statement}
This paper presents work whose goal is to advance the fields of deep model merging, multi-task learning, and robust inference. By shifting the paradigm of model consolidation from static parameter-space blending to dynamic activation-space resonance, our proposed framework (ADSR) enables resource-efficient serving of multiple specialized expert models without retraining or parameter expansion. 

The primary environmental and societal consequences of our work are highly positive:
1. \textbf{Carbon Footprint Reduction:} Consolidating multiple expert networks into a shared parallel execution layout with on-the-fly routing drastically reduces the physical storage and serving overhead on edge devices and mobile platforms, significantly lowering the energy consumption and carbon emissions associated with redundant, independent model serving.
2. \textbf{Data Privacy and Security:} Unlike existing data-driven calibration methods (e.g., DE-BN) that require access to unlabeled task-specific samples at test-time (introducing potential data-leakage, security, and privacy risks), ADSR is entirely zero-shot, training-free, and processes activations locally based purely on individual inference sample statistics. This ensures strict data-privacy guarantees.
3. \textbf{Robustness in Critical Applications:} The exceptional resilience of ADSR under low-bit post-training quantization (PTQ) and severe environmental noise makes it highly valuable for safety-critical edge applications, such as autonomous driving, robotics, and medical devices, where input corruptions or hardware precision limitations are common and reliability is paramount.
