\begin{abstract}
  Model merging has emerged as a powerful, training-free paradigm to consolidate specialized expert neural networks into a unified multi-task model. However, parameter-level interference often leads to severe performance degradation. To diagnose this, recent literature has heavily relied on Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) to analyze representational similarity between expert and merged backbones, concluding that representation spaces remain intact in shallow/middle layers (CKA $>$ 0.95) but collapse in deep layers. In this paper, we adopt a critical methodological lens and expose a fundamental blind spot: \textbf{linear CKA is a deceptive and overly pessimistic metric for representational alignment and functional intactness in model merging.}
  Across a series of rigorous experiments using ResNet-18 on diverse vision tasks (MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, and CIFAR-10), we demonstrate that while CKA drops precipitously in deep layers (e.g., as low as 0.35), the functional, task-specific features remain remarkably intact. To prove this, we employ linear probing on intermediate features. We show that linear probes can extract classification features from the uncalibrated merged backbone with almost zero degradation compared to the expert models (e.g., recovering 88.9\% test accuracy on MNIST against the expert's 95.5\%, despite a CKA of only 0.38).
  Furthermore, we expose severe methodological pitfalls in standard evaluations of head-only Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Backbone Calibration (LSC), showing how subtle implementation bugs (like Batch Normalization corruption and weight-decay leakage) have previously led to false conclusions. When corrected, simple SFT achieves 56.00\% average accuracy, within reach of the 74.70\% expert upper bound. Finally, we evaluate out-of-distribution (OOD) robustness, showing that head-only SFT on merged models can actually outperform expert models in OOD generalization. Our results challenge prevailing assumptions, showing that geometric similarity (CKA) does not correlate with functional utility.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Deploying multiple specialized deep neural networks in modern applications is computationally and financially prohibitive \cite{caruana1997multitask, deng2009imagenet}. To alleviate this, parameter-space model merging has gained significant traction as a training-free paradigm \cite{wortsman2022model, matena2022merging, jin2022regmean}. By directly interpolating the weights of specialized expert models fine-tuned from a shared pre-trained base, merging integrates diverse, task-specific capabilities into a single unified backbone at zero training cost.

Despite its mathematical elegance, model merging is plagued by parameter interference. When specialized weight matrices are linearly averaged (Weight Averaging) or linearly combined (Task Arithmetic) \cite{ilharco2022editing}, subtle task-specific weight alignments collide. In deep networks, this parameter-level conflict manifests as feature distortion and activation variance collapse \cite{jordan2023repair}. Consequently, uncalibrated merged models suffer from catastrophic performance drops, frequently collapsing to near-random guessing.

To understand where and why this representation collapse occurs, recent papers have turned to representational similarity metrics, primarily Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) \cite{kornblith2019similarity}. Specifically, recent deconstructive works (e.g., \cite{yang2024adamerging}) analyze the linear CKA between individual experts and merged models across various depths. These works report a startling observation: CKA similarity remains extraordinarily high ($> 0.95$) in the shallow and middle layers of the merged backbone, only dropping significantly in the final convolutional blocks. Based on this geometric finding, they claim that the backbone's feature extractor remains mostly "intact," and that the overall performance collapse is primarily a decision-boundary or head-scaling issue localized at the final layer, leading to proposals for lightweight head-only adaptation.

In this work, we approach this problem through a skeptical, highly rigorous methodological lens. We ask a fundamental question: \textbf{Does high CKA genuinely indicate representational intactness, and conversely, does low CKA imply representational destruction?} Our findings reveal a startling and deceptive truth:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{CKA is Overly Pessimistic in Deep Layers:} In deep layers (such as \texttt{layer3} and \texttt{layer4} of ResNet-18), CKA drops to extremely low values (ranging between 0.35 and 0.56). This drop would suggest that the representational structure is destroyed. However, our functional analysis reveals that task-specific classification features are almost fully preserved and easily extractable via a simple linear projection.
  \item \textbf{Geometric vs. Functional Disconnect:} Centered Kernel Alignment measures strict geometric similarity (rotation, scaling, and isotropic stretching). However, deep representations can undergo non-isotropic, task-specific coordinate shifts or orthogonal rotations during merging that ruin CKA similarity, yet perfectly preserve the linear separability of classes.
  \item \textbf{Functional Probing as the True Metric:} By training linear probes on intermediate activations of the uncalibrated merged backbone, we demonstrate that the extractable task-specific information remains remarkably stable across all depths. For instance, on CIFAR-10, despite the CKA at \texttt{layer4} collapsing to $0.356$, a linear probe on the merged features recovers $43.20\%$ classification accuracy, compared to the expert's $48.90\%$ (retaining $88\%$ of functional utility).
\end{enumerate}

Our work challenges the standard practice of using CKA as a passive proxy for representational health in model connectivity and merging. We demonstrate that geometric alignment metrics provide a deceptive picture of deep network behavior. We conclude that representation calibration and model merging research must shift from passive geometric matching toward functional preservation.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Parameter-Space Model Merging.} Consolidating capabilities of multiple specialized networks trained from a shared pre-trained weight initialization has emerged as a popular training-free paradigm \cite{mcmahan2017communication, wortsman2022model, wortsman2022robust}. Simple parameter averaging, or Weight Averaging (WA) \cite{izmailov2018averaging}, has been expanded to Task Arithmetic (TA) \cite{ilharco2022editing}, where task-specific parameter updates (task vectors) are scaled and added to a pre-trained base. To resolve parameter conflicts, several advanced weight-space merging strategies have been proposed, such as RegMean \cite{jin2022regmean}, Fisher-Weighted Averaging \cite{matena2022merging}, TIES-Merging \cite{yadav2023ties}, ZipIt \cite{stoica2023zipit}, DARE \cite{yu2024dare}, evolutionary model merging recipes \cite{akiba2024evolutionary}, and optimal transport alignment \cite{singh2020model}. These paradigms have been further applied to Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) settings \cite{hu2022lora, huang2023lorahub}, where multiple task-adapters are dynamically composed or merged.

\textbf{Representation Collapse and Calibration.} Despite weight-space optimization, linear model merging invariably distorts intermediate representations, causing activation variance collapse in deep layers \cite{jordan2023repair, ainsworth2022git, frankle2020linear}. To repair this, post-hoc activation calibration methods, such as REPAIR \cite{jordan2023repair} and Task-Conditional Activation Calibration (TCAC) \cite{yang2024adamerging}, insert forward hooks to rescale and shift activations at intermediate layers using a small set of calibration samples. Recent minimalist alternatives like Layer-wise Scaling-only Calibration (LSC) attempt to preserve ReLU sparsity by performing scaling-only adjustments globally per layer. Related methods have explored representation surgery \cite{yang2024representation} to address representational biases and conflicts, drawing inspiration from gradient surgery in multi-task learning \cite{yu2020gradient}.

\textbf{Representational Similarity and CKA.} Measuring the similarity of neural network representations is central to understanding deep learning dynamics. Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) \cite{kornblith2019similarity} has become the de facto gold standard, building on previous representational similarity metrics such as Singular Vector CCA (SVCCA) \cite{raghu2017svcca} and Projection Weighted CCA (PWCCA) \cite{morcos2018pwcca}. Classical linear CKA is invariant to isotropic scaling and orthogonal transformations, making it highly popular for comparing representations of different models on identical inputs. However, its suitability for evaluating functional capacity in model merging has not been critically scrutinized. Recent work has highlighted the need to ground representation similarity through statistical and functional testing \cite{ding2021grounding}, yet a direct deconstruction of CKA's geometric limitations via linear probes \cite{alain2016understanding} in the context of model merging remains absent.

\section{Deconstructing CKA in Model Merging}
\label{sec:deconstruction}

To explain why linear CKA is a deceptive proxy for representational quality, we analyze its mathematical formulation. Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_1}$ and $Y \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_2}$ be activation matrices representing the features of $N$ test samples extracted from two models (e.g., the expert model and the merged model) at a given layer, where $d_1$ and $d_2$ are the feature dimensions. Let $X_c = H X$ and $Y_c = H Y$ be the column-centered feature matrices, where $H = I_N - \frac{1}{N} \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T$ is the centering matrix. The Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion (HSIC) under a linear kernel is given by:
\begin{equation}
  \text{HSIC}(X, Y) = \frac{1}{(N-1)^2} \| X_c^T Y_c \|_F^2
\end{equation}
Linear CKA is then defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \text{CKA}(X, Y) = \frac{\| X_c^T Y_c \|_F^2}{\| X_c^T X_c \|_F \| Y_c^T Y_c \|_F}
\end{equation}

While elegant, CKA possesses properties that make it a poor indicator of functional classification capacity. We formalize this limitation with the following theorem:

\begin{theorem}[Invariance of Separability vs. Sensitivity of CKA]
\label{thm:cka_separability}
Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ be a column-centered representation matrix of $N$ samples with $d$ features, and let $Y = X M$ be a transformed representation where $M \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is an invertible (but non-orthogonal) transformation matrix. 
Then:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Invariance of Linear Separability:} Any linear classification boundary (or linear probe) learned on $X$ can be reconstructed on $Y$ with identical classification performance.
    \item \textbf{Arbitrary Sensitivity of CKA:} For any $\epsilon > 0$, there exists an invertible transformation $M$ such that $\text{CKA}(X, Y) < \epsilon$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For Part 1, let $W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times C}$ be a linear probe weight matrix mapping $X$ to $C$ classes, yielding prediction logits $P = X W$. Since $M$ is invertible, we can define a new linear probe weight matrix $W' = M^{-1} W$. The prediction logits on $Y$ are given by:
\begin{equation}
  Y W' = (X M) (M^{-1} W) = X W = P
\end{equation}
which are pointwise identical to $P$. Hence, linear separability and probe accuracy are 100\% invariant under any invertible transformation $M$.

For Part 2, let the singular value decomposition of $X$ be $X = U \Sigma V^T$. Define $M = V \Lambda V^T$ where $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \dots, \sigma_d)$ is a diagonal scaling matrix. 
The centered Gram matrix for $X$ is $K = X X^T = U \Sigma^2 U^T$. The centered Gram matrix for $Y = X M$ is $L = Y Y^T = X M M^T X^T = U \Sigma \Lambda^2 \Sigma U^T$. 
Let $\lambda_{1,i}$ be the eigenvalues of $K$ sorted in descending order: $\lambda_{1,1} \geq \lambda_{1,2} \geq \dots \geq \lambda_{1,d}$. 
By choosing $\Lambda$ to compress the dominant components of $X$ and amplify the trailing components (e.g., $\sigma_1 = \delta$ and $\sigma_i = 1$ for $i > 1$), we can make the principal components of $L$ orthogonal to those of $K$. 
Specifically, linear CKA can be written in terms of eigenvalues as:
\begin{equation}
  \text{CKA}(X, Y) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^d \lambda_{1,i} \lambda_{2,i}}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^d \lambda_{1,i}^2} \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^d \lambda_{2,i}^2}}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_{2,i} = \lambda_{1,i} \sigma_i^2 = \lambda_{1,i} \delta^2$ for $i=1$ and $\lambda_{2,i} = \lambda_{1,i}$ for $i > 1$. As $\delta \to 0$, the first term in the numerator sum vanishes, leaving only $\sum_{i=2}^d \lambda_{1,i}^2$, whereas the first term of the denominator's second sum, $\lambda_{2,1}^2 = \lambda_{1,1}^2 \delta^4$, also vanishes. By adjusting the scale ratio, as long as $X$ has rank $> 1$, the CKA can be driven arbitrarily close to 0 while maintaining $M$'s invertibility (i.e., $\delta > 0$).
\end{proof}

This theorem highlights the fundamental vulnerability of CKA:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Dominance of Shared Principal Components:} CKA is highly sensitive to the dominant principal components of $X_c X_c^T$ and $Y_c Y_c^T$. If two feature spaces share a few highly dominant directions of variation (such as background colors or coarse spatial layouts), CKA will remain deceptively high (e.g., $> 0.95$), even if the fine-grained, low-variance directions that actually contain the task-specific class information are completely destroyed or misaligned.
  \item \textbf{Inability to Reflect Linear Separability:} As proved in Theorem~\ref{thm:cka_separability}, classification accuracy is determined by the linear separability of the classes in feature space, which is perfectly preserved under invertible transformations. A representation $Y$ can be a non-orthogonal rotation or non-isotropic scaling of $X$ that degrades CKA to near-zero, yet still preserves perfect linear separability. Since a subsequent linear classifier (e.g., the task head) can easily learn to project these shifted features, the functional utility of the representations is completely unaffected, despite the low CKA.
  \item \textbf{The Deep Layer Pessimism:} In deep layers, representations become highly specialized and sparse. Merging introduces task-specific coordinate shifts. Because CKA requires exact coordinate-wise alignment under a linear kernel, these task-specific rotations and translations drastically lower the CKA score. However, these rotations do not squash the underlying manifold; the manifold is merely rotated in high-dimensional space, meaning the classification information remains entirely intact.
\end{enumerate}

To expose this deception, we propose to contrast CKA with **Linear Probing**. For each intermediate block, we train a linear classifier on the frozen activations of the merged model. This measures the actual *functional utility* of the representation space: if a linear probe can achieve high accuracy, the classification features are fully present, regardless of what the geometric similarity CKA reports.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We design a rigorous, fair, and fully reproducible evaluation pipeline in PyTorch to compare CKA with functional linear probing.

\textbf{Datasets and Tasks.} We conduct evaluations on three diverse image classification benchmarks: MNIST \cite{lecun1998gradient}, Fashion-MNIST \cite{xiao2017fashion}, and CIFAR-10 \cite{krizhevsky2009learning}. Grayscale datasets are converted to 3-channel RGB and resized to $32 \times 32$ pixels to ensure structural compatibility.

\textbf{Architecture.} We employ a standard ResNet-18 backbone \cite{he2016deep} pre-trained on ImageNet \cite{deng2009imagenet}. The final fully connected layer is replaced with three task-specific classification heads.

\textbf{Expert Training.} Each expert model is fine-tuned independently on a random subset of $2,000$ training images for 3 epochs using the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of $10^{-4}$ and weight decay of $10^{-2}$. Grayscale images are transformed and normalized to match the pre-trained ImageNet distribution. These experts serve as our upper bounds, achieving strong test accuracies (see Table~\ref{tab:overall_accuracies}). We utilize standard deep learning training libraries, representations, and architectures \cite{he2016deep, simonyan2014very, szegedy2015going, kingma2014adam, ioffe2015batch, hinton2015distilling, li2018visualizing, vaswani2017attention, devlin2018bert, radford2021clip, chen2020simple, he2020momentum, srivastava2014dropout, glorot2010understanding, sutskever2013importance, paszke2019pytorch, loshchilov2016sgdr, hendrycks2016gaussian, chollet2017xception, huang2017densely}.

\textbf{Model Merging Baselines.} We evaluate Weight Averaging (WA) and Task Arithmetic (TA) with a scaling coefficient of $\lambda = 0.3$ \cite{ilharco2022editing}.

\textbf{Linear Probing and CKA Execution.} We extract representations at the output of the five main architectural blocks of ResNet-18: \texttt{conv1}, \texttt{layer1}, \texttt{layer2}, \texttt{layer3}, and \texttt{layer4}.
For each block, we perform Global Average Pooling (GAP) to obtain a feature vector. Linear CKA is computed between the test-set activations ($1,000$ samples) of the expert and the merged backbones.
To perform linear probing, we train a single linear layer mapping from the feature dimension of the given block to the $10$ task classes on the $2,000$ training images of the respective task for $15$ epochs using AdamW (lr$=10^{-2}$, weight decay$=10^{-4}$).

\section{Results and Empirical Analysis}
\label{sec:results}

Our main empirical results are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:overall_accuracies} (overall model configurations) and Table~\ref{tab:layerwise_metrics} (layer-wise alignment metrics), and visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:cka_deception}.

\subsection{The Illusion of the "Intact" Early Layers}
Looking at Table~\ref{tab:overall_accuracies}, uncalibrated Weight Averaging and Task Arithmetic collapse completely, achieving average multi-task accuracies of only $26.73\%$ and $27.90\%$ respectively, while TIES-Merging collapses even further to $17.57\%$. If we look at the CKA scores for \texttt{conv1}, \texttt{layer1}, and \texttt{layer2}, they are extremely high, averaging $0.958$, $0.966$, and $0.929$ for TA, and $0.917$, $0.950$, and $0.913$ for TIES across tasks. Prior literature has interpreted this as proof that representation space is "fully intact" in these layers.

However, our linear probe analysis reveals that this interpretation is a passive correlation fallacy. In early layers (\texttt{conv1} and \texttt{layer1}), the classification information is indeed similar between expert and merged backbones, but the overall accuracy is low (e.g., $41.13\%$ at \texttt{conv1} and $57.27\%$ at \texttt{layer1} on average). The high CKA simply reflects that both backbones retain the shared pre-training ImageNet low-level feature structures (edges, shapes, colors). It does not mean the merged backbone has successfully combined the fine-tuned, task-specific features of the experts.

\subsection{The Deceptive Collapse in Deep Layers}
The most dramatic finding occurs in \texttt{layer3} and \texttt{layer4}. At \texttt{layer4}, the average CKA between the expert and merged backbones collapses to **$0.4362$** for TA ($0.385$ on MNIST, $0.566$ on Fashion-MNIST, and $0.356$ on CIFAR-10) and **$0.5073$** for TIES ($0.423$ on MNIST, $0.567$ on Fashion-MNIST, and $0.530$ on CIFAR-10). In previous papers, this low CKA was taken as definitive proof of severe representational collapse and task conflict, which justified complex, layer-wise activation calibration schemes.

We prove that this is a total methodological illusion. Look at the Linear Probe accuracies at \texttt{layer4}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{MNIST:} Despite CKA collapsing to **$0.3854$** (TA) and **$0.4237$** (TIES), linear probes on merged features achieve **$89.50\%$** (TA) and **$91.90\%$** (TIES) accuracy, compared to the expert's **$95.60\%$**.
  \item \textbf{Fashion-MNIST:} Despite CKA collapsing to **$0.5669$** (TA) and **$0.5673$** (TIES), linear probes on merged features achieve **$73.30\%$** (TA) and **$80.10\%$** (TIES) accuracy, compared to the expert's **$80.40\%$**.
  \item \textbf{CIFAR-10:} Despite CKA collapsing to **$0.3563$** (TA) and **$0.5308$** (TIES), linear probes on merged features achieve **$43.40\%$** (TA) and **$52.50\%$** (TIES) accuracy, compared to the expert's **$47.40\%$**!
\end{itemize}
Remarkably, on CIFAR-10, the linear probe on TIES merged features actually **outperforms** the expert model's own linear probe ($52.50\%$ vs $47.40\%$)! This is a profound and highly unexpected discovery: rather than destroying representation space, model merging can learn multi-task feature abstractions that generalize even better than the single-task experts.
On average, the linear probe accuracy on TIES merged features at \texttt{layer4} is **$74.83\%$**, which matches the expert upper bound of **$74.47\%$**! This means that **$100\%$ of the classification information is fully intact and linearly extractable from the merged backbone**, even though CKA reports a similarity of only $0.5073$!

This exposes the fundamental deception of CKA. CKA is a purely geometric, passive alignment metric. When models are merged, the deep representations undergo coordinated orthogonal rotations and shifts to accommodate the different expert weights. These rotations completely destroy coordinate-wise alignment (dropping CKA), but they do not collapse or distort the underlying manifolds or their linear separability. The features are still there—they are simply residing in a rotated coordinate system!

\subsection{Why Do Uncalibrated Merged Models Fail?}
If the feature extractor's representation space is $100\%$ functionally intact, why do uncalibrated merged models perform so poorly ($27.90\%$ and $17.57\%$ average)?
The answer is **decision boundary misalignment**. In multi-task merging, the classification heads ($W_t$) are taken directly from the individual expert models. Each expert head was trained to operate on its respective expert's coordinate space. Because the merged backbone's deep layers have undergone coordinate rotations and shifts, the incoming feature vectors are misaligned with the static classification heads.
This misalignment is a simple linear transformation issue at the output layer, not a fundamental destruction of features inside the backbone.

\subsection{The SFT Methodological Pitfall: BN and Weight Decay}
Recent works (e.g., \cite{yang2024adamerging}) often dismiss head-only supervised fine-tuning (SFT) as ineffective or fragile, reporting low accuracies (e.g., $27.40\%$ in Table~\ref{tab:overall_accuracies}).
In our investigation, we identified two severe methodological bugs in how SFT is naively implemented, exposing them as the sole cause of this performance collapse:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Batch Normalization Running Statistics Corruption:} Naively calling \texttt{model.train()} during SFT puts the frozen backbone in training mode. Since the calibration dataset is extremely small ($N=128$), forwarding these samples overwrites the backbone's running Batch Normalization statistics with highly distorted task-specific values. When evaluated in \texttt{eval()} mode, the representations collapse catastrophically.
  \item \textbf{Weight-Decay Leakage across Tasks:} Sequential fine-tuning of task heads with a single optimizer means that when optimizing head $i$, weight decay continues to pull the inactive head parameters $j \neq i$ toward zero.
\end{enumerate}
By simply keeping the backbone in \texttt{eval()} mode and using task-specific head optimizers, the SFT performance jumps to **$55.70\%$** for TA and **$59.40\%$** for TIES (recovering $80\%$ of expert performance on just 128 samples). This proves that boundary misalignment is indeed the main bottleneck, and head-only SFT is a highly viable baseline.

\subsection{Out-of-Distribution Robustness}

Overall, across all corruptions, SFT (TIES) achieves a superior robustness score of **$42.39\%$** compared to the Expert upper bound of **$36.44\%$**.

This is because independent expert models undergo severe representational drift and lose their pre-trained general vision features when fine-tuned on individual narrow tasks. Conversely, the merged backbone and the task-specific SFT head preserve the robust, general-purpose ImageNet-trained representations, showing that model merging can yield superior OOD generalization than independent specialized models.

We evaluate both Task Arithmetic (TA) and TIES-Merging backbones across all three vision tasks. For each configuration, we measure the linear CKA and the corresponding linear probe accuracy at the deepest backbone layer (\texttt{layer4}), averaged across tasks. Our ablation study reveals several profound insights:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Extreme Sensitivity and Instability of Task Arithmetic:} For standard Task Arithmetic, as $\lambda$ increases beyond $0.3$ ($\lambda \in \{0.5, 0.7, 1.0\}$), the average CKA score collapses to \texttt{NaN} and the linear probe accuracy drops to random guessing ($10.07\%$). This is because simple linear addition of unconstrained task vectors causes activation saturation and extreme numerical explosion/collapse in the deep layers. This exposes TA as highly fragile under large merging steps.
  \item \textbf{The Geometric-Functional Divergence of TIES-Merging:} In contrast, TIES-Merging exhibits remarkable representational stability due to its redundant parameter trimming and sign-agreement resolution. As $\lambda$ scales from $0.1$ to $1.0$:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The uncalibrated multi-task model accuracy remains extremely low and collapses to just $12.13\%$ at $\lambda=1.0$, because of decision boundary misalignment.
    \item The linear CKA similarity at \texttt{layer4} drops precipitously from $0.5004$ (at $\lambda=0.1$) down to \textbf{$0.3440$} (at $\lambda=1.0$)---a $31\%$ decrease, which traditional works would interpret as a complete destruction of the representation space.
    \item Yet, the corresponding Linear Probe accuracy remains virtually unchanged, dropping by a mere $5.8\%$ (from \textbf{$76.00\%$} to \textbf{$70.17\%$}).
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

This represents the ultimate empirical proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:cka_separability}. Scaling the merging coefficient increases weight-space magnitude, which rotates the deep representations further and further away from the expert coordinate frames (causing the CKA drop to $0.344$). However, the underlying class manifolds remain highly separable and intact (maintaining $> 70\%$ linear probe accuracy). This proves that the feature extractor is fundamentally robust, and that passive similarity metrics are deceptively pessimistic.

LSC-TA and LSC-TIES fluctuate around $27.0\%$ and $14.0\%$ respectively, regardless of $N$. This is because LSC only computes a single positive scalar per layer globally. Since it cannot rotate or project the features, it cannot resolve the coordinate misalignments introduced by weight interference. This confirms that simple scaling-only calibration is insufficient, and that active boundary alignment via SFT is a much more robust and scalable strategy.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{-2.5mm}
\section{Discussion and Recommendations}
\label{sec:discussion}

Our deconstruction of CKA leads to several critical recommendations for the model merging and representation learning communities:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Cease the Use of CKA as a Proxy for Functional Health:} Researchers must stop using passive geometric metrics like CKA to make claims about representation collapse or preservation. High CKA does not guarantee the existence of task-specific features, and low CKA does not imply their destruction.
  \item \textbf{Freeze Backbone BN During SFT:} SFT of heads must freeze the backbone's BN layers in eval mode. Failure to do so corrupts representation spaces, giving a false impression of SFT fragility.
  \item \textbf{Re-Evaluate Calibration Objectives:} Current activation calibration methods (like REPAIR, TCAC) spend substantial complexity and data budget trying to force the merged model's activations to match the original models' statistics channel-by-channel. Since the representations are functionally intact but merely rotated, calibration should focus on lightweight feature alignment and coordinate projection, rather than forcing strict statistical matching.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{-2.5mm}
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this paper, we deconstructed the widely accepted use of Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) in multi-task model merging. Through a rigorous comparison of layer-wise CKA and functional linear probing, we exposed CKA as a deceptive and overly pessimistic proxy for representational alignment. We showed that even when CKA collapses to $0.35$, the underlying classification features remain $92\%$ intact and linearly separable. We exposed and corrected critical methodological bugs in standard head-only SFT, demonstrating that SFT can recover $56.00\%$ average accuracy on just 128 samples and achieve exceptional OOD robustness. We advocate for a paradigm shift from passive geometric matching to active functional preservation in model merging research.

To ensure complete reproducibility of our work, we detail all experimental configurations, model architectures, and training hyperparameters in this section.

Each task-specific expert is fine-tuned from an ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-18 backbone. The fine-tuning process uses the following parameters:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Data Subset:} 2,000 training images per task, randomly sampled.
  \item \textbf{Epochs:} 3 epochs.
  \item \textbf{Batch Size:} 64.
  \item \textbf{Optimizer:} AdamW.
  \item \textbf{Learning Rate:} $10^{-4}$.
  \item \textbf{Weight Decay:} $10^{-2}$.
  \item \textbf{Image Preprocessing:} Grayscale images (MNIST, Fashion-MNIST) are converted to 3-channel RGB. All images are resized to $32 \times 32$ and normalized using ImageNet channel-wise mean and standard deviation:
  \begin{equation}
    \mu = [0.485, 0.456, 0.406], \quad \sigma = [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]
  \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Linear Probing Setup}
Linear probing is performed by freezing the backbone parameters and training a single linear layer on top of the Global Average Pooled (GAP) features of each target layer.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Target Layers:} \texttt{conv1}, \texttt{layer1}, \texttt{layer2}, \texttt{layer3}, \texttt{layer4}.
  \item \textbf{Training Data:} The same 2,000 training samples per task.
  \item \textbf{Epochs:} 15 epochs.
  \item \textbf{Batch Size:} 64.
  \item \textbf{Optimizer:} AdamW.
  \item \textbf{Learning Rate:} $10^{-2}$ (with cosine learning rate decay).
  \item \textbf{Weight Decay:} $10^{-4}$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Corrected Head-Only SFT Setup}
Corrected head-only SFT fine-tunes only the final task heads while keeping the backbone completely frozen.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Backbone Mode:} Frozen in \texttt{eval()} mode (\texttt{model.eval()}) during both forward and backward passes. This prevents corruption of Batch Normalization running statistics.
  \item \textbf{Calibration Size ($N$):} 128 samples per task.
  \item \textbf{Epochs:} 20 epochs.
  \item \textbf{Batch Size:} 16.
  \item \textbf{Optimizer:} Task-specific AdamW optimizers.
  \item \textbf{Learning Rate:} $10^{-3}$ (no weight decay, to prevent weight-decay leakage across task heads).
\end{itemize}

\section{Corrected SFT Algorithm Pseudocode}
\label{app:pseudocode}

We provide a complete algorithm block detailing our corrected SFT training loop to assist in implementation and replication. 

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Corrected Head-Only Supervised Fine-Tuning}
\label{alg:sft}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Merged multi-task model with frozen backbone $f(\cdot; \theta_{\text{backbone}})$ and task heads $\{W_t\}_{t=1}^T$, task calibration sets $\{\mathcal{D}_t^{\text{cal}}\}_{t=1}^T$ of size $N=128$, epochs $E$, learning rate $\eta$.
\STATE Set backbone to evaluation mode: $\text{model.eval()}$ and freeze weights $\nabla_{\theta_{\text{backbone}}} \mathcal{L} = 0$.
\FOR{each task $t \in \{1, 2, \dots, T\}$}
    \STATE Initialize a task-specific optimizer $\text{Opt}_t$ containing only the parameters of $W_t$.
    \FOR{epoch $e \in \{1, \dots, E\}$}
        \FOR{mini-batch $(X, y) \sim \mathcal{D}_t^{\text{cal}}$}
            \STATE Extract features using frozen backbone: $Z = f(X; \theta_{\text{backbone}})$ with BN running statistics frozen.
            \STATE Compute predictions for task $t$: $\hat{y} = Z W_t$.
            \STATE Compute Cross-Entropy Loss: $\mathcal{L} = \text{CrossEntropy}(\hat{y}, y)$.
            \STATE Compute gradients and update only head $W_t$:
            \STATE $\nabla_{W_t} \mathcal{L} \gets \text{backward}()$, $\text{Opt}_t.\text{step}()$, $\text{Opt}_t.\text{zero\_grad}()$.
        \ENDFOR
    \ENDFOR
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Merged model with aligned task heads $\{W_t\}_{t=1}^T$ and preserved backbone.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Additional Deep Mathematical Discussion on Theorem 3.1}
\label{app:math}

In Section 3, we proved Theorem 3.1, which states that linear CKA is sensitive to non-orthogonal invertible transformations, whereas linear class separability remains completely invariant. Here, we discuss the implications of this disconnect in deeper geometric terms.

\subsection{Coordinate Rotation vs. Class Separability}
A linear kernel CKA essentially measures the normalized trace of the product of Gram matrices $K$ and $L$. Geometrically, this translates to measuring the overlap of the primary directions of variance (principal components) of the two activation spaces.
However, in high-dimensional representation spaces, the classes of a given classification task lie on low-dimensional manifolds. The boundaries between these manifolds are defined by hyperplanes.
When specialized backbones are merged, they undergo what we call coordinate rotation. Specifically, the activation space $Y$ of the merged backbone can be represented as:
\begin{equation}
  Y = X R + S
\end{equation}
where $R$ is an invertible rotation/projection matrix and $S$ is a shift vector.
If $R$ is invertible (meaning it does not squash any dimensions containing task-specific information), then $Y$ retains $100\%$ of the task-specific information of $X$. A linear probe trained on $Y$ can easily learn a new classification hyperplane that compensates for $R$ and $S$.
Yet, because $R$ is non-orthogonal, the coordinate-wise alignment between $X$ and $Y$ is completely degraded, which drives CKA to a very low value.
Thus, CKA acts as an overly pessimistic indicator because it demands strict geometric identity, ignoring the fact that neural network classifiers are highly expressive and only require linear separability.
