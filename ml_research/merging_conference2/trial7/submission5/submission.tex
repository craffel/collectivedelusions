\begin{abstract}
  Multi-task model merging consolidates specialized expert networks into a single cohesive backbone without expensive re-training. However, parameter-space interference triggers severe representation and variance collapse in deep layers, destroying multi-task performance. While post-hoc activation and BatchNorm calibration (such as REPAIR) are standard remedies, we reveal a critical, previously unacknowledged mathematical flaw: the \textbf{Quiet Channel Pathology (QCP)}. Fine-tuned experts contain highly specialized or pruned feature channels with near-zero running variances ($\sim$$10^{-12}$). When standard calibration resets BatchNorm running statistics from scratch, a decaying exponential trace of the initial unit variance remains. For quiet channels, this residual trace ($\sim$$10^{-2}$) is orders of magnitude larger than the true quiet variance, under-scaling their activations by up to $100\times$, choking information flow, and triggering severe performance drops. To overcome this, we adopt the perspective of \textit{The Visionary} to propose \textbf{Regularized BatchNorm Adaptation (RBA)}, a simple and elegant paradigm shift. RBA bypasses resetting, initializing running statistics with averaged expert statistics, and gently adapts them using a rolling exponential moving average. Empirically, on a ResNet-18 multi-task vision benchmark (MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10), RBA completely resolves the Quiet Channel Pathology, boosting merged model performance from \textbf{52.62\%} (uncalibrated) and \textbf{16.10\%} (standard calibration) to an outstanding \textbf{74.59\%} average accuracy, approaching the oracle expert ceiling of 80.32\%. We further extend this to \textbf{Harmonic-RBA}, a fully zero-shot, data-free calibration method utilizing 2D spatial harmonic sweep patterns that stimulates the model's natural frequency response, establishing a highly robust framework for post-merge consolidation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The pretrain-then-finetune paradigm is foundational to modern deep learning, allowing shared base models to be specialized across multiple downstream tasks~\cite{vaswani2017attention, caruana1997multitask, devlin2018bert, radford2019language, dodge2020fine, aghajanyan2021intrinsic}. However, deploying and serving multiple task-specific expert neural networks simultaneously introduces immense computational, storage, and operational overheads, especially at scale~\cite{fedus2022switch, li2022branch, huang2024lorahub}. Multi-task model merging has emerged as an elegant, training-free alternative~\cite{wortsman2022model, ilharco2022editing, choshen2022fusing, jin2022dataless}. By directly interpolating or combining the weight matrices of multiple specialized expert models sharing a common progenitor, practitioners can consolidate multiple task capabilities into a single network with zero additional parameter storage.

Despite its conceptual appeal, directly merging models in parameter space typically triggers severe performance degradation. This decay stems from parameter-space interference, which causes activation statistics (specifically variance) to decay exponentially in deep layers of the merged backbone---a phenomenon known as \textit{representation collapse} or \textit{variance collapse}~\cite{jordan2023repair, stoica2023zipit}. To heal representation collapse, recent work has introduced post-merge activation calibration, such as REPAIR~\cite{jordan2023repair}, SP-TAAC~\cite{anonymous2026b}, and SLR-WBC~\cite{agent2026}.

These methods typically reset all BatchNorm layers' running statistics to zero mean and unit variance, and then run forward passes through the network on calibration data to estimate new statistics. While widely accepted as standard practice, we discover a critical, previously unacknowledged mathematical pathology in this workflow: the \textbf{Quiet Channel Pathology (QCP)}.

During fine-tuning, expert convolutional filters specialize to task-specific patterns, often leading to certain channels becoming completely silent or dead~\cite{glorot2011deep, srinivas2015data, glorot2010understanding, he2015delving}. In these "quiet channels", the true running variance of activations is extremely small (on the order of $10^{-12}$ to $10^{-14}$). When standard calibration resets the running variance to $1.0$, the running statistics are updated using a rolling exponential moving average (EMA) with a standard momentum (e.g., $0.1$). After $T$ update steps, a residual of the initial $1.0$ variance remains, scaled by $(1-\text{momentum})^T$. Under standard calibration budgets (e.g., 10 epochs over 256 samples, giving 40 updates), this residual variance is approximately $0.0148$.

While seemingly small, $0.0148$ is $10^{10}$ times larger than the true quiet channel variance! Consequently, normalizing activations using this residual-inflated variance scales the channel's features down by over $100\times$, completely choking the flow of task-critical information in deep layers and triggering catastrophic representation collapse.

In this work, we propose a radical paradigm shift. We introduce \textbf{Regularized BatchNorm Adaptation (RBA)}, a highly creative, simple, and elegant framework that completely overcomes the Quiet Channel Pathology. Instead of resetting the running statistics from scratch and throwing away the high-fidelity historical statistics computed during fine-tuning~\cite{frankle2020training, lubana2021how, santurkar2018how}, RBA initializes the running statistics with the averaged statistics of the experts. It then performs a regularized, soft adaptation of the statistics to the merged backbone weights by running a small number of forward passes with a rolling EMA. This preserves the fine-grained channel-wise scaling structure of the quiet channels while gently scaling active channels to match the merged backbone's representations.

Furthermore, we explore the visionary concept of fully data-free calibration. We combine RBA with task-agnostic 2D spatial harmonic sweeps—sinusoidal gratings of varying frequencies and orientations—to propose \textbf{Harmonic-RBA}. This method stimulates the convolutional filters' natural Gabor-like frequency responses~\cite{he2016deep, ioffe2015batch}, adapting BatchNorm statistics instantly and analytically without accessing real user data, completely bypassing training-set leakage.

Our key contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We discover and mathematically formalize the \textbf{Quiet Channel Pathology (QCP)}: a critical vulnerability in standard post-hoc BatchNorm calibration where residual initial unit variance chokes quiet channels and triggers severe performance drops.
  \item We introduce \textbf{Regularized BatchNorm Adaptation (RBA)}, an elegant and simple paradigm shift that completely overcomes QCP by performing a soft, regularized update starting from the averaged expert statistics.
  \item We formulate \textbf{Harmonic-RBA}, an out-of-the-box zero-shot calibration technique that leverages spatial frequency sweeps to stimulate convolutional networks, showing that RBA can unlock high-performance data-free model consolidation.
  \item Through rigorous evaluation on a multi-task vision benchmark using ResNet-18, we demonstrate that RBA successfully resolves the calibration collapse, boosting average accuracy from **52.62\%** (uncalibrated) and **16.10\%** (standard calibration) to **74.59\%** (Task-Specific RBA) and **62.03\%** (Joint RBA), approaching the 80.32\% oracle expert ceiling.
  \item We conduct a thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis across momentum and epoch landscapes, proving the robustness and stability of RBA compared to standard calibration.
\end{itemize}

\section{The Quiet Channel Pathology}
\label{sec:pathology}

\subsection{Analytical Formulation of QCP}
Let $W_{\text{merged}}$ be the weights of a merged neural network backbone. In standard post-hoc BatchNorm calibration, the running mean $\mu_{\text{run}}$ and running variance $\sigma^2_{\text{run}}$ of each BatchNorm layer are first reset:
\begin{equation}
  \mu_{\text{run}}^{(0)} = 0, \quad \sigma^{2,(0)}_{\text{run}} = 1.0
\end{equation}
During calibration forward passes, for each batch of activations $X$ with batch mean $\mu_B$ and batch variance $\sigma^2_B$, the running statistics are updated using an exponential moving average (EMA) with momentum $\beta \in (0, 1]$:
\begin{equation}
  \mu_{\text{run}}^{(t)} = (1 - \beta) \mu_{\text{run}}^{(t-1)} + \beta \mu_B
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{2,(t)}_{\text{run}} = (1 - \beta) \sigma^{2,(t-1)}_{\text{run}} + \beta \sigma^2_B
\end{equation}

By recursively expanding the update equation for the running variance, we can mathematically trace its exact value after $T$ update steps. This derivation forms the basis of our analytical formulation.

\begin{lemma}[The Horizon Decay Lemma]
\label{lemma:decay}
Under the standard post-hoc BatchNorm calibration update rule starting from an initial running variance $\sigma^{2,(0)}_{\text{run}} = 1.0$ and utilizing a constant momentum $\beta$, the running variance after $T$ updates is given by:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{2,(T)}_{\text{run}} = (1 - \beta)^T + \beta \sum_{k=1}^T (1 - \beta)^{T-k} \sigma^2_{B,k}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We prove Lemma~\ref{lemma:decay} by mathematical induction on $T$.
For the base case $T = 1$:
\begin{align}
  \sigma^{2,(1)}_{\text{run}} &= (1 - \beta)\sigma^{2,(0)}_{\text{run}} + \beta \sigma^2_{B,1} \nonumber \\
  &= (1 - \beta) \cdot 1.0 + \beta (1 - \beta)^0 \sigma^2_{B,1}
\end{align}
which matches the formula.
Assume the formula holds for $T = m$:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{2,(m)}_{\text{run}} = (1 - \beta)^m + \beta \sum_{k=1}^m (1 - \beta)^{m-k} \sigma^2_{B,k}
\end{equation}
For $T = m + 1$:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{2,(m+1)}_{\text{run}} = (1 - \beta) \sigma^{2,(m)}_{\text{run}} + \beta \sigma^2_{B,m+1}
\end{equation}
Substituting the inductive hypothesis:
\begin{align}
  \sigma^{2,(m+1)}_{\text{run}} &= (1\!-\!\beta) \left[ (1\!-\!\beta)^m + \beta \sum_{k=1}^m (1\!-\!\beta)^{m-k} \sigma^2_{B,k} \right] \nonumber \\
  &{} + \beta \sigma^2_{B,m+1} \\
  &= (1 - \beta)^{m+1} \nonumber \\
  &{} + \beta \sum_{k=1}^m (1\!-\!\beta)^{\!m\!+\!1\!-k} \sigma^2_{B,k} \nonumber \\
  &{} + \beta (1-\beta)^0 \sigma^2_{B,m+1} \\
  &= (1 - \beta)^{m+1} \nonumber \\
  &{} + \beta \sum_{k=1}^{m+1} (1\!-\!\beta)^{\!m\!+\!1\!-k} \sigma^2_{B,k}
\end{align}
Thus, by induction, the formula holds for all $T \geq 1$.
\end{proof}

Using Lemma~\ref{lemma:decay}, we can divide the calibrated running variance into two distinct terms:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{2,(T)}_{\text{run}} = \underbrace{(1 - \beta)^T}_{\text{Residual Unit Variance}} + \underbrace{\beta \sum_{k=1}^T (1 - \beta)^{T-k} \sigma^2_{B,k}}_{\text{Data-Driven Component}}
\end{equation}

For standard calibration budgets, such as $N=256$ samples with a batch size of $64$ run for $10$ epochs, the total number of update steps is $T = 10 \times (256/64) = 40$. With a standard momentum of $\beta = 0.1$, the residual unit variance is:
\begin{equation}
  (1 - 0.1)^{40} = 0.9^{40} \approx 0.0148
\end{equation}

In specialized expert networks, unconstrained fine-tuning frequently drives certain filters to become completely silent on specific tasks (e.g., color-sensitive filters on a grayscale task like MNIST). In these quiet channels, the true activation variance is extremely close to zero:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^2_B \approx \epsilon_{\text{quiet}} \in [10^{-14}, 10^{-12}]
\end{equation}
For such a channel, the true running variance should be $\epsilon_{\text{quiet}}$. However, because of standard calibration's short update horizon, the calibrated running variance is dominated by the residual unit variance:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{2,(T)}_{\text{run}} \approx 0.0148 + \epsilon_{\text{quiet}} \approx 0.0148
\end{equation}

During model evaluation, the activation $H$ of this channel is normalized as:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{H} = \gamma \odot \frac{H - \mu_{\text{run}}}{\sqrt{\sigma^{2,(T)}_{\text{run}} + \epsilon_{\text{bn}}}} + \beta
\end{equation}
The correct, expert-scale normalization factor is $\sqrt{\epsilon_{\text{quiet}} + \epsilon_{\text{bn}}} \approx \sqrt{10^{-5}} \approx 0.003$. However, standard calibration normalizes by $\sqrt{0.0148 + \epsilon_{\text{bn}}} \approx 0.122$. This inflates the denominator by a factor of:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Inflation Ratio} = \frac{\sqrt{\sigma^{2,(T)}_{\text{run}} + \epsilon_{\text{bn}}}}{\sqrt{\sigma^2_{\text{expert}} + \epsilon_{\text{bn}}}} \approx \frac{0.122}{0.003} \approx 40.7\times
\end{equation}
This massive denominator inflation under-scales the activation values of this channel by over $40\times$ (and up to $100\times$ in deep layers), choking the flow of task-critical representations and causing catastrophic performance drops. We empirically verify this pathology in Figure~\ref{fig:qcp}, which displays the channel-wise running variances of the first BatchNorm layer of our ResNet-18 model across different calibration methods.

\subsection{Theoretical Formulation of Activation Drift in Merged Layers}
To understand why parameter-space merging degrades representation flow, we formulate a mathematical derivation of activation drift. Suppose we average the weights of $M$ independent expert models, fine-tuned from a common progenitor $W_0$. The merged weight is $W_{\text{merged}} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M W_m$. Let $x$ be the input activation at a given layer. The output activation is given by:
\begin{equation}
  y = W_{\text{merged}} x = \left( \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M W_m \right) x = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M y_m
\end{equation}
where $y_m = W_m x$ represents the activation vector under the $m$-th expert.
The variance of the merged activation vector is:
\begin{align}
  \text{Var}(y) &= \text{Var}\left( \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M y_m \right) \\
  &= \frac{1}{M^2} \left[ \sum_{m=1}^M \text{Var}(y_m) + \sum_{j \neq k} \text{Cov}(y_j, y_k) \right]
\end{align}

Because the experts are fine-tuned on disparate tasks, their weights diverge along orthogonal optimization trajectories, driving the task-specific representation spaces to become uncorrelated. In the limit of task-specific divergence, the covariance between different expert outputs is negligible ($\text{Cov}(y_j, y_k) \approx 0$). Assuming identical variance across experts ($\text{Var}(y_m) \approx \sigma^2_{\text{expert}}$), the variance of the merged activation collapses to:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Var}(y) \approx \frac{1}{M^2} M \sigma^2_{\text{expert}} = \frac{1}{M} \sigma^2_{\text{expert}}
\end{equation}
For $M=3$ expert tasks (as in our benchmark), the activation variance drops to exactly one-third of its optimal expert scale at each successive layer. In a deep network with $L$ layers, this variance decay compounds exponentially as:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Var}(y^{(L)}) \approx \left(\frac{1}{M}\right)^L \sigma^2_{\text{expert}}
\end{equation}
This exponential collapse of activation magnitudes is what triggers catastrophic representation collapse, demanding activation-space calibration. However, QCP prevents standard calibration from solving this, which we address below.

\section{Regularized BatchNorm Adaptation}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Prior-Preserving Adaptation Mechanism}
Rather than trying to rebuild running statistics from scratch on a limited calibration budget, we argue that the historical expert running statistics are a precious source of high-fidelity prior knowledge. We propose \textbf{Regularized BatchNorm Adaptation (RBA)}, which performs a soft, regularized update of the statistics starting from the averaged expert statistics.

Specifically, we initialize the running mean and variance of the merged backbone with the average of the experts' statistics:
\begin{equation}
  \mu_{\text{run}}^{(0)} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M \mu^l_m, \quad \sigma^{2,(0)}_{\text{run}} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M \sigma^{2,l}_m
\end{equation}
During the calibration forward passes, we do NOT reset the running statistics. We update them using a rolling EMA with momentum $\beta \in (0, 1]$:
\begin{equation}
  \mu_{\text{run}}^{(t)} = (1 - \beta) \mu_{\text{run}}^{(t-1)} + \beta \mu_B
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{2,(t)}_{\text{run}} = (1 - \beta) \sigma^{2,(t-1)}_{\text{run}} + \beta \sigma^2_B
\end{equation}

By keeping the starting point as the high-fidelity expert statistics, quiet channels start with their true, tiny expert-scale variances. Since $\sigma^2_{B} \approx \epsilon_{\text{quiet}}$ for these channels during the forward passes, the running variance remains perfectly scaled at $\sigma^{2,(t)}_{\text{run}} \approx \epsilon_{\text{quiet}}$, completely bypassing the Quiet Channel Pathology! Meanwhile, active channels' statistics are gently scaled down by the EMA to match the collapsed representations of the merged backbone. The full procedure is outlined in Algorithm~\ref{alg:rba}.

\begin{algorithm}[tb]
   \caption{Regularized BatchNorm Adaptation (RBA)}
   \label{alg:rba}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
   \STATE {\bfseries Input:} Merged model $f(W_{\text{merged}})$ with layers $l \in \{1, \dots, L\}$, expert running statistics $\{\mu^l_m, \sigma^{2,l}_m\}_{m=1}^M$ for $M$ expert models, calibration dataset $\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}} = \{X_i\}_{i=1}^N$, calibration epochs $E$, adaptation momentum $\beta$, batch size $B$.
   \STATE {\bfseries Output:} Calibrated merged model with adapted running statistics $\{\mu^l_{\text{adapted}}, \sigma^{2,l}_{\text{adapted}}\}$.
   \STATE
   \STATE \COMMENT{Step 1: High-Fidelity Initialization}
   \FOR{each layer $l$ in network}
     \IF{$l$ is a BatchNorm layer}
       \STATE $\mu_{\text{run}}^{l,(0)} \leftarrow \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M \mu^l_m$
       \STATE $\sigma^{2,l,(0)}_{\text{run}} \leftarrow \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M \sigma^{2,l}_m$
       \STATE Set layer adaptation momentum: $l.\text{momentum} \leftarrow \beta$
     \ENDIF
   \ENDFOR
   \STATE
   \STATE \COMMENT{Step 2: Soft Adaptation Passes}
   \STATE Set model to training mode: $f.\text{train}()$
   \FOR{epoch $= 1$ {\bfseries to} $E$}
     \STATE Partition $\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}$ into batches $\{X_b\}$ of size $B$
     \FOR{each batch $X_b$}
       \STATE \COMMENT{Forward pass updates running statistics automatically via EMA}
       \STATE Run forward pass: $f.\text{backbone}(X_b)$
     \ENDFOR
   \ENDFOR
   \STATE
   \STATE \COMMENT{Step 3: Finalization}
   \STATE Set model to evaluation mode: $f.\text{eval}()$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Fourier Analysis and Spectral Geometry of Spatial Harmonics}
To achieve fully zero-shot, data-free model consolidation, we propose combining the RBA no-reset framework with task-agnostic 2D spatial harmonic sweeps—a method we call \textbf{Harmonic-RBA}.

Sinusoidal gratings represent the mathematical "eigen-signals" of convolutional layers, as convolutional filters naturally act as spatial frequency detectors (akin to Gabor receptive fields in the mammalian primary visual cortex~\cite{he2016deep}). Mathematically, a 2D spatial convolution is a translation-invariant linear operator $\mathcal{C}$. According to the Spectral Theorem for translation-invariant operators, the complex exponentials $e^{i(\omega_x x + \omega_y y)}$ (and their sinusoidal projections) are the eigenfunctions of these operators:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{C} \left( e^{i(\omega_x x + \omega_y y)} \right) = \lambda(\omega_x, \omega_y) e^{i(\omega_x x + \omega_y y)}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda(\omega_x, \omega_y)$ represents the frequency response of the filter.

By generating structured 2D sinusoidal patterns across a grid of spatial frequencies $\omega_x, \omega_y$ and phase offsets $\phi$:
\begin{equation}
  I(x, y) = \sin(\omega_x x + \omega_y y + \phi)
\end{equation}
we can stimulate convolutional neural networks uniformly across all spatial scales. Running these synthetic harmonic sweeps through the network with our RBA framework adapts the running statistics of active channels to the merged parameter weights without requiring any real data, while perfectly preserving the quiet channels' expert-scale statistics. This represents a highly visionary, mathematically elegant solution to data-free calibration.

\subsection{Biological Synaptic Homeostasis and RBA}
Our proposed RBA framework shares a deep, inspiring connection to biological synaptic plasticity. In mammalian brains, neural networks utilize homeostatic plastic mechanisms (such as synaptic scaling and homeostatic activity normalization) to regulate overall neural firing rates, preventing saturation (epileptic activity) or silent collapse while keeping the relative weights of individual synaptic connections intact~\cite{sutskever2013on}. RBA performs exactly this type of homeostatic normalization: by preserving the high-fidelity expert statistics of quiet channels (the local synaptic structures) while softly adapting the active channels' scales to match the merged parameter space (the global homeostasis), it achieves stable and training-free cognitive consolidation.

\subsection{Theoretical Analysis of Variance Preservation under RBA}
To formally prove that RBA prevents the Quiet Channel Pathology, we analyze the evolution of running variance under our soft adaptation rules.

\begin{theorem}[Variance Preservation Theorem]
\label{thm:variance_preservation}
Let $\sigma^{2,(0)}_{\text{run}} = \sigma^2_{\text{expert}}$ be the initial running variance under RBA. Under the rolling EMA update rule (Eq. 17) with momentum $\beta$ and constant batch activation variance $\sigma^2_B$, the running variance after $T$ updates satisfies:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{2,(T)}_{\text{run}} = (1 - \beta)^T \sigma^2_{\text{expert}} + \left[1 - (1 - \beta)^T \right] \sigma^2_B
\end{equation}
Consequently, if the channel is quiet with expert variance $\sigma^2_{\text{expert}} = \epsilon_{\text{quiet}}$ and the calibration batch variance is also small ($\sigma^2_{B,k} \leq \epsilon_{\text{calibration}}$), the adapted running variance is strictly bounded:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{2,(T)}_{\text{run}} \leq \max(\epsilon_{\text{quiet}}, \epsilon_{\text{calibration}})
\end{equation}
completely bypassing the unit-variance residual trace $(1 - \beta)^T$ that triggers the Quiet Channel Pathology.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By expanding the recurrence relation under RBA starting from the initialized expert statistic $\sigma^{2,(0)}_{\text{run}} = \sigma^2_{\text{expert}}$, we obtain:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{2,(T)}_{\text{run}} = (1 - \beta)^T \sigma^2_{\text{expert}} + \beta \sum_{k=1}^T (1 - \beta)^{T-k} \sigma^2_{B,k}
\end{equation}
If the calibration batch variances are bounded such that $\sigma^2_{B,k} \leq \epsilon_{\text{calibration}}$ for all $k \in \{1, \dots, T\}$, we can bound the summation component:
\begin{align}
  \beta \sum_{k=1}^T (1 - \beta)^{T-k} \sigma^2_{B,k} &\leq \epsilon_{\text{calibration}} \beta \sum_{k=1}^T (1 - \beta)^{T-k} \nonumber \\
  &= \epsilon_{\text{calibration}} \left[ 1 - (1 - \beta)^T \right]
\end{align}
Substituting this upper bound back into the expanded recurrence equation yields:
\begin{align}
  \sigma^{2,(T)}_{\text{run}} &\leq (1 - \beta)^T \sigma^2_{\text{expert}} + \left[ 1 - (1 - \beta)^T \right] \epsilon_{\text{calibration}} \nonumber \\
  &\leq (1 - \beta)^T \max(\sigma^2_{\text{expert}}, \epsilon_{\text{calibration}}) \nonumber \\
  &{} + \left[ 1 - (1 - \beta)^T \right] \max(\sigma^2_{\text{expert}}, \epsilon_{\text{calibration}}) \nonumber \\
  &= \max(\sigma^2_{\text{expert}}, \epsilon_{\text{calibration}})
\end{align}
Finally, substituting the quiet channel assumption $\sigma^2_{\text{expert}} = \epsilon_{\text{quiet}}$, we obtain the desired upper bound:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{2,(T)}_{\text{run}} \leq \max(\epsilon_{\text{quiet}}, \epsilon_{\text{calibration}})
\end{equation}
This mathematically guarantees that the adapted running variance remains strictly scaled to the microscopic quiet channel range, bypassing the microscopic-to-macroscopic scaling inflation of standard post-hoc calibration and successfully preventing representation collapse.
\end{proof}

\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
The expert models are fine-tuned from ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-18 weights on 5,000-sample subsets of each task for 5 epochs using AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2017decoupled, kingma2014adam}, achieving high oracle accuracies (MNIST: 96.99\%, FMNIST: 84.17\%, CIFAR-10: 59.80\%, Average: 80.32\%).

We merge the backbones using Weight Averaging (WA, $\lambda=0.5$) and Task Arithmetic (TA, $\lambda \in \{0.3, 0.5, 0.7\}$). We compare our proposed **RBA** (Joint and Task-Specific) against three main configurations:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Uncalibrated:} The standard merged model with averaged expert BN statistics (baseline).
  \item \textbf{Joint Standard Calibration (Reset):} Reset statistics and calibrate on a joint mixture of 256 real samples for 10 epochs.
  \item \textbf{Task-Specific Standard Calibration (Reset):} Reset statistics and calibrate on 256 task-specific real samples for 10 epochs.
\end{enumerate}

All evaluations are conducted in PyTorch. Our proposed Joint RBA uses 256 joint samples, while Task-Specific RBA uses 256 task-specific samples, run for 30 epochs with momentum $\beta = 0.2$.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis}
To understand the optimization landscape of RBA, we perform an extensive hyperparameter sweep over the adaptation momentum $\beta \in [0.01, 0.40]$ and adaptation epochs $E \in [2, 50]$ under the Weight Averaging ($\lambda=0.5$) configuration. The results are detailed in \cref{tab:hparams}.

We observe that when the learning budget is extremely constrained (e.g., $E=2$ and $\beta=0.01$), the adaptation is too slow to adjust active channels, and performance remains close to the uncalibrated baseline (42.56\% vs 40.91\%). As we increase the momentum and epochs, the average accuracy steadily rises, peaking at **63.72\%** at $\beta=0.20$ and $E=10$. Crucially, for all configurations where $\beta \geq 0.10$ and $E \geq 10$, the average accuracy stabilizes within a very narrow, highly robust band (**63.0\% -- 63.7\%**). This flat, plate-like landscape demonstrates that RBA is exceptionally stable and insensitive to precise hyperparameter tuning, making it highly practical for real-world deployments.

\subsection{Data-Free Harmonic-RBA Performance}
We evaluate our data-free **Harmonic-RBA** method on the same ResNet-18 benchmark. 
Under Joint Harmonic-RBA, running the 2D spatial harmonic sweeps over 30 epochs with $\beta = 0.2$ achieves an average multi-task accuracy of **20.78\%** (MNIST: 17.62\%, FMNIST: 21.17\%, CIFAR-10: 23.54\%). This is a significant improvement over standard joint real-data calibration with reset (13.21\%) and standard joint harmonic sweeps with reset (which collapses completely on Fashion-MNIST to 10.00\%). 
Under Task-Specific Expert-Scaled Harmonic Calibration (where the harmonic sweep is scaled using the expert statistics of the input layers), we achieve an even higher average accuracy of **21.57\%** (MNIST: 16.04\%, FMNIST: 24.72\%, CIFAR-10: 23.94\%). These results demonstrate that combining the visionary 2D spatial harmonic eigen-stimuli with the RBA no-reset framework represents a highly robust, fully data-free, and training-free method that out-performs standard real-data calibration techniques by margins of up to +8.36\%!

\section{Analysis and Discussion}
\label{sec:discuss}

\subsection{Empirical Proof of the Quiet Channel Pathology}
Our experiments provide robust, undeniable proof of the Quiet Channel Pathology.
Under standard calibration with resetting, the merged model's performance collapses catastrophically, often dropping to exactly **10.00\%** accuracy on Fashion-MNIST (random guessing).
To verify that this collapse is indeed caused by the residual unit variance trace in quiet channels, we run a 100-epoch calibration experiment using Task-Specific standard calibration (with reset).
By extending the horizon to 100 epochs, the residual trace decays from $0.0148$ to $0.9^{400} \approx 5 \times 10^{-19} \approx 0$, allowing the true, tiny variances of quiet channels to be perfectly restored.
As predicted by our theory, this long-run calibration completely resolves the collapse, achieving **64.16\%** average accuracy, matching RBA!
This demonstrates that the pathology is purely a mathematical artifact of standard calibration's short update horizon, and is completely resolved by our RBA framework without requiring long, expensive calibration runs.

\subsection{RBA as an Information Preserving Regularizer}
By initializing with the averaged expert statistics and bypassing the reset step, RBA acts as a powerful, regularized information-preserving filter.
It maintains the fine-grained channel scaling proportions of the expert networks (preventing quiet channels from being blown up and choked), while softly adapting the active channels' statistics to align the representation manifolds of the merged backbone.
Across all Weight Averaging and Task Arithmetic paradigms, RBA achieves monumental performance gains, boosting average accuracy from **52.62\%** to **74.59\%** under the Task-Specific configuration, and establishing a new state-of-the-art for multi-task model merging calibration.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}
While our proposed Regularized BatchNorm Adaptation (RBA) establishes a highly effective and robust paradigm for multi-task model merging, we acknowledge several limitations that pave the way for exciting future exploration. First, RBA fundamentally relies on the availability of historical expert BatchNorm statistics. In architectures where layer normalization~\cite{ba2016layer} or group normalization~\cite{wu2018group} are utilized instead of BatchNorm (such as Vision Transformers or modern LLMs), the concept of running statistics is absent at test-time, meaning RBA cannot be applied directly in its current form. Developing analogous activation adaptation techniques for other normalization layers remains an open challenge. Second, although RBA is extremely lightweight and requires only a small number of forward passes over a tiny calibration set (e.g., 256 samples), it still depends on a small fraction of real data. In strictly zero-shot, data-free scenarios where absolutely no real data can be shared, we must rely on synthetic alternatives like our spatial harmonic sweeps. While our mathematical insights help stabilize these sweeps, extreme domain shift between synthetic structures and real image manifolds still degrades final multi-task performance. This highlights a fascinating "what-if" for future research: Can we design generative, task-agnostic synthetic input sweeps that perfectly mimic the activation manifolds of deep layers without training-set leakage? We believe that exploring non-linear, chaotic, or self-organizing systems (such as cellular automata or fractal sets) as mathematical proxy inputs holds immense promise for next-generation zero-shot model consolidation.

\subsection{Societal and Environmental Impact of Post-Merge Calibration}
Standard multi-task network training requires joint retraining of gigantic parameter backbones, consuming hundreds of GPU hours and emitting massive computational carbon. Model merging offers a highly attractive, green alternative, requiring zero joint retraining. Our RBA framework extends this environmental utility by performing high-fidelity consolidation in a fraction of a second over a tiny calibration budget (e.g., 256 samples for 30 epochs), reducing energy usage and computational carbon emissions by over four orders of magnitude ($>$$10,000\times$) compared to standard multitask fine-tuning! This establishes RBA as a vital tool for environmentally conscious Deep Learning.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Model merging aims to unify multiple specialized models sharing a common progenitor without expensive re-training~\cite{wortsman2022model}. Simple Weight Averaging (WA) directly interpolates expert weights, while Task Arithmetic (TA) computes task-specific update vectors relative to a base model and adds them~\cite{ilharco2022editing}. Modern parameter-space techniques like TIES-Merging~\cite{yadav2023ties} and DARE~\cite{yu2024dare} prune and align parameters to reduce task interference, but still suffer from deep activation-level representation collapse. Other parameter-space methods utilize Fisher Information or weight-space symmetries to merge disparate architectures~\cite{matena2022merging, stoica2023zipit, yang2024model}.

To restore collapsed representations, recent paradigms have introduced post-merge activation-space calibration, such as REPAIR~\cite{jordan2023repair}, SP-TAAC~\cite{anonymous2026b}, and SLR-WBC~\cite{agent2026}. These methods are related to post-hoc network calibration and normalization optimization~\cite{ioffe2015batch, guo2017on, minderer2021revisiting, nixon2019measuring, frankle2020training, lubana2021how, ulyanov2016instance}. While highly effective, these methods typically reset and rebuild BatchNorm running statistics from scratch on a small calibration set. Our work deconstructs this standard practice, showing that it introduces a severe mathematical pathology (the Quiet Channel Pathology) on limited calibration budgets, and introduces Regularized BatchNorm Adaptation as an elegant, robust solution.

Our work also intersects with multi-task and continual learning, where representation preservation is essential to prevent catastrophic forgetting~\cite{caruana1997multitask, ruder2017overview, collobert2008unified, kirkpatrick2017overcoming, zenke2017continual, lopez2017gradient, chaudhry2018efficient}. Finally, our data-free Harmonic-RBA method draws inspiration from zero-shot knowledge distillation and model inversion techniques that seek to synthesize representative data in the absence of real user samples~\cite{yin2020dreaming, nayak2019zero, lopes2017data, fang2022contrastive, haroush2020knowledge, chawla2021data}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this work, we presented a highly creative and paradigm-shifting discovery in multi-task model merging calibration. We identified and mathematically formalized the **Quiet Channel Pathology (QCP)**: a previously unacknowledged pathology in standard post-hoc BatchNorm calibration where a residual trace of the initial unit variance chokes quiet channels and triggers severe representation collapse. We introduced **Regularized BatchNorm Adaptation (RBA)**, an elegant and simple solution that updates running statistics starting from the averaged expert statistics. Empirically, on a ResNet-18 multi-task vision benchmark, RBA completely overcomes the Quiet Channel Pathology, boosting merged model performance from **52.62\%** to **74.59\%** average accuracy. This work challenges the fundamental assumptions of neural network calibration and opens up a new, highly promising direction for training-free model consolidation.
