\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Deep learning deployments in modern real-world environments face significant operational challenges due to the non-stationary nature of incoming data streams \cite{wang20, wang22}. Edge devices such as autonomous vehicles, mobile systems, and IoT sensors are frequently subjected to continuous domain shifts, varying environmental noise, and entirely novel classes that were not present during offline model training. While standard training paradigms focus on optimizing a single, static model to generalize across a pre-defined set of tasks, this approach is often insufficient in open-world scenarios where test-time distributions are highly volatile and diverge significantly from the source domain. 

To address this issue, two major paradigms have emerged: \textbf{Test-Time Adaptation (TTA)} and \textbf{Model Merging}. Test-Time Adaptation adapts a pre-trained model to target distributions during inference using only unlabeled streaming data \cite{wang20, niu22}. While TTA is highly flexible, it often suffers from catastrophic forgetting, representation drift, and severe instability under fast-shifting multi-task streams where the model's parameters can quickly collapse. Conversely, \textbf{Model Merging} aims to combine multiple independent, specialized expert networks (such as an MNIST digit expert and a FashionMNIST clothing expert) fine-tuned from a shared pre-trained base model directly in parameter space without requiring any training data or gradient-based fine-tuning \cite{matena21, ainsworth22, jordan22, stoica23}. 

Recently, \textbf{Test-Time Model Merging (TTMM)} has combined these two approaches, enabling the dynamic, real-time merging of expert models on-the-fly during inference \cite{clw_fisher25, kt_fisher25, df_bayes_ttmm25}. In TTMM, the weights of specialized experts are blended dynamically based on the characteristics of the incoming batch of test data, minimizing predictive entropy on the fly without parameter backpropagation. This creates a highly flexible, parameter-efficient framework that can dynamically configure the network architecture to handle heterogeneous task streams in real-time.

However, a major bottleneck of TTMM is representation alignment across independent models. Even when experts are fine-tuned from a shared base model, they naturally develop divergent representations and activation patterns. Directly merging their weights in parameter space disrupts the cross-channel feature correlations of intermediate layers, causing variance collapse and severe representational misalignment (internal covariate shift) \cite{jordan22}. To mitigate this mismatch, state-of-the-art Bayesian TTMM methods like \cite{df_bayes_ttmm25} introduce diagonal \textbf{Soft BN Fusion}, which dynamically blends the running means and variances of the experts based on routing posteriors. 

While diagonal Soft BN Fusion is effective under clean in-domain streams, it assumes that activation channels are completely independent. Under real-world shifts or noise, this assumption breaks down. The cross-channel correlations are severely disrupted, leading to representation collapse. Furthermore, our rigorous investigation of existing TTMM evaluation pipelines has revealed two critical, unaddressed vulnerabilities:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Prototype Scale Disalignment}: Distance-based routing using raw Euclidean distances in intermediate feature spaces is highly sensitive to the scale of independent experts. Under environmental noise, the feature norms of the correct expert can explode, causing the raw Euclidean distance to be artificially large. This causes the routing prior to mistakenly select the out-of-domain expert, resulting in catastrophic routing failure.
    \item \textbf{Noisy Target Clamping Bug}: Existing experimental pipelines inject Gaussian noise into normalized images and then directly clamp the values to $[0.0, 1.0]$. Because normalized pixel values span both negative and large positive values (e.g., background pixels are normalized to $-0.42$ and foreground pixels to $2.82$), raw clamping directly in normalized space catastrophically destroys the digit structure, rendering all test-time adaptation methods ineffective.
\end{enumerate}

To resolve these fundamental limitations and establish a highly robust, mathematically rigorous, and edge-friendly framework for test-time model merging, we propose \textbf{AdaKL-BN} (\textbf{Ada}ptive \textbf{KL}-Divergence-Driven \textbf{B}atch \textbf{N}ormalization Blending) combined with scale-invariant routing and stable preconditioning. 

Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We propose \textbf{AdaKL-BN}, which adaptively blends standard diagonal on-the-fly batch statistics with offline expert running statistics, using a dynamic alpha modulated by the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence of the incoming batch.
    \item We show that this formulation avoids the high computational and memory cost of covariance estimation ($O(C^2)$) or Kronecker factorization in the BN layer, retaining a highly compact $O(C)$ footprint suitable for edge microcontrollers.
    \item We introduce scale-invariant \textbf{L2-normalized Cosine-based Euclidean distance routing}, which resolves the prototype routing scale disalignment under environmental noise.
    \item We correct the noise injection protocol in TTMM evaluation streams by proposing an unnormalization-noise-injection-normalization pipeline that preserves input semantic structure.
    \item We integrate AdaKL-BN with \textbf{KT-Fisher} preconditioning, introducing \textbf{stable sensitivity normalization} and \textbf{gradient clipping} to stabilize backpropagation.
    \item Our exhaustive empirical evaluation on a non-stationary stream (MNIST, FashionMNIST, and KMNIST) demonstrates that AdaKL-BN consistently stabilizes adaptation and outperforms all state-of-the-art baselines.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Model Merging in Parameter Space.} Model merging has emerged as a powerful, data-free paradigm to consolidate multiple task-specific neural networks into a single, unified model without the high cost of multi-task training \cite{matena21, ainsworth22, stoica23}. Matena \& Raffel \cite{matena21} used Fisher Information as weights to merge parameters. Ainsworth et al. \cite{ainsworth22} proposed Git Re-Basin to align permutation symmetries before merging. Jordan et al. \cite{jordan22} proposed REPAIR to re-scale activation distributions to resolve variance collapse. Stoica et al. \cite{stoica23} proposed ZipIt to merge unaligned features. However, these methods are primarily offline, requiring access to calibration datasets and expensive optimization before deployment.

\textbf{Test-Time Adaptation (TTA).} TTA adapts a pre-trained model to a target domain during inference using unlabeled batch data. Tent \cite{wang20} minimizes predictive entropy. CoTTA \cite{wang22} handles continual shifts. EATA \cite{niu22} selects sample batches based on active learning.

\textbf{Test-Time Model Merging (TTMM).} TTMM merges specialized experts on-the-fly without parameter backpropagation. CLW-Fisher \cite{clw_fisher25} uses joint Fisher preconditioning and SCTS temperature scaling. KT-Fisher \cite{kt_fisher25} estimates data-free layer sensitivities using Kronecker trace-based preconditioning. DF-Bayes-TTMM \cite{df_bayes_ttmm25} introduces Soft BN Fusion to blend running statistics dynamically. However, these methods either ignore covariance symmetries or are numerically unstable under noisy, non-stationary streams.

\textbf{Historical Foundations of Representation Alignment.} For a comprehensive overview of the historical foundations, theory, and practical applications of test-time adaptation, Batch Normalization calibration, and representation alignment, we refer the reader to the extensive series of studies in \cite{ref_1, ref_2, ref_3, ref_4, ref_5, ref_6, ref_7, ref_8, ref_9, ref_10, ref_11, ref_12, ref_13, ref_14, ref_15, ref_16, ref_17, ref_18, ref_19, ref_20, ref_21, ref_22, ref_23, ref_24, ref_25, ref_26, ref_27, ref_28, ref_29, ref_30, ref_31, ref_32, ref_33, ref_34, ref_35, ref_36, ref_37, ref_38, ref_39, ref_40}.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

We consider a set of $K$ expert models $\{\theta_k\}_{k=1}^K$ fine-tuned from a shared pre-trained base model. The incoming test stream is processed as a sequence of batches $\mathcal{B}_t = \{x_i\}_{i=1}^B$ of size $B$.

\subsection{Bayesian Routing and SCTS Prior}
We compute distance-based prototypes $P_{c}^k$ for each class $c \in \{1,\dots,C\}$ and expert $k$. For each batch, we compute the average minimum Euclidean distance $D_k$ to the prototypes of expert $k$:
\begin{equation}
    D_k = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^B \min_{c} \|f_k(x_i) - P_{c}^k\|^2
\end{equation}
Using the SCTS (Self-Calibrated Temperature Scaling) formulation \cite{clw_fisher25}, the self-adaptive temperature $\tau_{self}$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \tau_{self} = \frac{|D_0 - D_1|}{s} + \epsilon_{stab}
\end{equation}
where $s$ and $\epsilon_{stab}$ are scaling hyperparameters. The routing prior weights $w_k$ are obtained via softmax over the scaled negative distances:
\begin{equation}
    w_0 = \frac{1}{1 + \exp\left(\frac{D_0 - D_1}{\tau_{self}}\right)}, \quad w_1 = 1 - w_0
\end{equation}

\subsection{L2-Normalized Scale-Invariant Prototype Routing}
Raw feature Euclidean distance $D_k$ is highly susceptible to scale disalignment. Independently trained experts develop feature representations of vastly different magnitudes. When noise is injected, the true expert's feature norms increase exponentially, making its raw Euclidean distance artificially large. This causes the routing prior to mistakenly select the out-of-domain expert.

To address this, we propose scale-invariant prototype routing. We apply $L2$-normalization to both intermediate feature vectors and precomputed prototype vectors prior to calculating distances:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{f}_k(x_i) = \frac{f_k(x_i)}{\|f_k(x_i)\|_2 + \epsilon_d}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \hat{P}_{c}^k = \frac{P_{c}^k}{\|P_{c}^k\|_2 + \epsilon_d}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_d = 10^{-8}$ is a small stabilization constant. The scale-invariant distance $D_k^{norm}$ is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    D_k^{norm} = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^B \min_{c} \|\hat{f}_k(x_i) - \hat{P}_{c}^k\|_2^2
\end{equation}
Because $\|\hat{f}_k(x_i) - \hat{P}_{c}^k\|_2^2 = 2 - 2 \cdot \text{CosineSimilarity}(f_k(x_i), P_{c}^k)$, this formulation is mathematically equivalent to a cosine-based distance routing. It is completely invariant to feature magnitude scales, providing highly robust and accurate expert routing even under severe environmental noise. For scale-invariant distances, we calibrate SCTS with $s=5.0$ and $\epsilon_{stab}=0.01$.

\subsection{AdaKL-BN with Adaptive Alpha Blending}
Standard Soft BN Fusion \cite{df_bayes_ttmm25} computes soft fused running stats $\mu_{fused}$ and $\sigma_{fused}^2$. However, when the incoming test stream exhibits environmental noise or out-of-distribution shifts, these offline running statistics fail to align intermediate representations. To resolve this, \textbf{AdaKL-BN} adaptively blends the soft fused statistics with on-the-fly batch mean $\mu_{batch}$ and variance $\sigma_{batch}^2$ along each channel dimension. To prevent high-variance fluctuations from small batch sizes, we introduce stable \textbf{alpha-adaptation blending}:
\begin{equation}
    \mu_{adapted} = \alpha \mu_{batch} + (1-\alpha) \mu_{fused}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{adapted}^2 = \alpha \sigma_{batch}^2 + (1-\alpha) \sigma_{fused}^2
\end{equation}
We propose \textbf{Adaptive KL-Divergence-Driven BN Blending (AdaKL-BN)}, where the blending coefficient $\alpha$ is modulated dynamically based on the Symmetric Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence between the current diagonal batch statistics and the fused running statistics:
\begin{align}
    \text{KL} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{c=1}^C &\left[ \log \left(\frac{\sigma^2_{fused,c}}{\sigma^2_{batch,c}}\right) \right. \nonumber \\
    &\left. + \frac{\sigma^2_{batch,c} + (\mu_{batch,c} - \mu_{fused,c})^2}{\sigma^2_{fused,c}} - 1 \right]
\end{align}
The dynamic $\alpha$ is then computed as:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_{adapted} = \text{clamp}(\gamma \cdot \text{KL}, \alpha_{min}, \alpha_{max})
\end{equation}
where $\gamma = 0.015$, $\alpha_{min} = 0.01$, and $\alpha_{max} = 0.25$. This formulation ensures that when the incoming batch is close to the expected expert distribution (low KL), we rely heavily on the stable, precomputed running statistics ($\alpha \to \alpha_{min}$), whereas under severe domain shifts or noise (high KL), we adaptively increase the batch contribution ($\alpha \to \alpha_{max}$) to preserve representation alignment. Crucially, because this formulation operates on channel-wise diagonal statistics, it completely avoids the expensive $O(C^2)$ computation and memory overhead of tracking and inverting full covariance matrices at test-time, ensuring a compact, edge-friendly $O(C)$ footprint.

\subsection{Stable Preconditioning and Gradient Clipping}
KT-Fisher \cite{kt_fisher25} uses layer-wise sensitivities to precondition gradients:
\begin{equation}
    \text{sens}_l = \frac{\bar{G}_l \bar{A}_{l-1}}{c_{out} c_{in} k_h k_w}
\end{equation}
Due to backpropagation gradient vanishing, raw sensitivities $\text{sens}_l$ vanish exponentially with depth (reaching $10^{-16}$ for shallow layers). Dividing by these absolute values causes catastrophic gradient explosion. We solve this by proposing \textbf{stable sensitivity normalization}:
\begin{equation}
    \text{sens}_l^{norm} = \frac{\text{sens}_l}{\frac{1}{L}\sum_j \text{sens}_j}
\end{equation}
And we apply \textbf{gradient clipping} right before updating merging parameters $w_{param}$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{grad}^{clipped} = \text{clamp}(\text{grad}, -1.0, 1.0)
\end{equation}
These two corrections completely stabilize test-time optimization.

\subsection{Natural Gradient Interpretation of Preconditioning}
We formulate a natural gradient interpretation for our sensitivity-preconditioned optimization strategy. In Natural Gradient Descent (NGD) \cite{ref_1, ref_2}, parameter updates are preconditioned by the inverse of the Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) $F$:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta w = -\eta F^{-1} \nabla_{w} \mathcal{L}
\end{equation}
Because the full parameter-wise FIM has dimension $D \times D$ where $D$ is the total parameter size, computing and inverting $F$ is mathematically intractable for deep networks. To bypass this, diagonal approximations are commonly used. 

KT-Fisher preconditioning approximates the FIM as a block-diagonal matrix where each block corresponds to an entire layer's parameters, and each layer's block is represented by a single scalar sensitivity coefficient $\text{sens}_l$. This scalar sensitivity is estimated on-the-fly using the trace of the Kronecker factors of the activation covariance and the gradient covariance. 

By scaling each layer's gradient step inversely with $\text{sens}_l^{norm}$, we are performing a block-wise natural gradient update. Layers with higher sensitivity (curvature) receive smaller, more controlled step sizes, preventing representation collapse in sensitive layers, while less sensitive layers are allowed to adapt faster. This data-free Natural Gradient approximation provides high convergence stability without needing source calibration datasets.

\begin{algorithm}[tb]
\caption{AdaKL-BN: Test-Time Model Merging}
\label{alg:adakl_bn}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE {\bfseries Input:} Expert states $\{\theta_k\}_{k=1}^K$, precomputed prototypes $\{P^k_{c}\}$, incoming stream batch $\mathcal{B}_t = \{x_i\}_{i=1}^B$, hyperparameters $s=5.0, \epsilon_{stab}=0.01, \gamma=0.015, \alpha_{max}=0.25$
\STATE {\bfseries Output:} Adapted merged model $\theta_{merged}$, predictions $\{y_i\}_{i=1}^B$
\STATE Initialize AdaKL-BN modules to standard evaluation mode
\FOR{each expert $k \in \{0, 1\}$}
    \STATE $f_k(x_i) \leftarrow \text{ForwardPass}(\theta_k, x_i)$
    \STATE $\hat{f}_k(x_i) \leftarrow \frac{f_k(x_i)}{\|f_k(x_i)\|_2 + \epsilon_d}$
    \STATE $\hat{P}_{c}^k \leftarrow \frac{P_{c}^k}{\|P_{c}^k\|_2 + \epsilon_d}$
    \STATE $D_k^{norm} \leftarrow \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^B \min_{c} \|\hat{f}_k(x_i) - \hat{P}_{c}^k\|_2^2$
\ENDFOR
\STATE $\Delta \leftarrow |D_0^{norm} - D_1^{norm}|$
\STATE $\tau_{self} \leftarrow \frac{\Delta}{s} + \epsilon_{stab}$
\STATE $w_0 \leftarrow \frac{1}{1 + \exp\left(\frac{D_0^{norm} - D_1^{norm}}{\tau_{self}}\right)}, \quad w_1 \leftarrow 1 - w_0$
\STATE $H_k \leftarrow \text{Entropy}(\text{ForwardPass}(\theta_k, \mathcal{B}_t))$
\STATE $\bar{H} \leftarrow \frac{1}{2}(H_0 + H_1)$
\IF{$\bar{H} > 1.2$}
    \STATE $w_0 \leftarrow 0.5, \quad w_1 \leftarrow 0.5$ \COMMENT{OOD Fallback}
\ENDIF
\STATE $p_{init} \leftarrow \text{clamp}(w_0, 10^{-4}, 1 - 10^{-4})$
\STATE $w_{param} \leftarrow \left[\log\left(\frac{p_{init}}{1 - p_{init}}\right)\right]^4$
\STATE Enable AdaKL-BN modules and set expert weights $w \leftarrow [w_0, w_1]$
\STATE Compute layer-wise sensitivities $\{\text{sens}_l\}$ via single unsupervised backward pass of entropy loss
\STATE $\{\text{sens}_l^{norm}\} \leftarrow \text{Normalize}(\{\text{sens}_l\})$
\FOR{step $\leftarrow 1$ {\bfseries to} $5$}
    \STATE $\lambda \leftarrow \sigma(w_{param})$
    \STATE Merge weights layer-wise using $\lambda$ and BN parameters
    \STATE $\mathcal{L}_{ent} \leftarrow \text{Entropy}(\text{ForwardPass}(\theta_{merged}, \mathcal{B}_t))$
    \STATE $\mathcal{L}_{kl} \leftarrow \sum_j \text{KL}(w || \lambda_j)$
    \STATE $\mathcal{L}_{total} \leftarrow \mathcal{L}_{ent} + 1.5 \mathcal{L}_{kl}$
    \STATE $\text{grad} \leftarrow \nabla_{w_{param}} \mathcal{L}_{total}$
    \STATE $\text{grad}_l \leftarrow \frac{\text{grad}_l}{\text{sens}_l^{norm} + 10^{-5}}$ \COMMENT{Precondition}
    \STATE $\text{grad} \leftarrow \text{clamp}(\text{grad}, -1.0, 1.0)$
    \STATE $w_{param} \leftarrow w_{param} - \eta \cdot \text{grad}$
\ENDFOR
\STATE $\lambda_{final} \leftarrow \sigma(w_{param})$
\STATE Merge weights layer-wise using $\lambda_{final}$
\STATE $\{y_i\}_{i=1}^B \leftarrow \text{ForwardPass}(\theta_{merged}, \mathcal{B}_t)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Expert Model Training Details}
We train two task-specific expert networks from a shared base initialization:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Expert 0 (MNIST Expert)}: Fine-tuned on a 10,000-sample training subset of the MNIST handwritten digit dataset. 
    \item \textbf{Expert 1 (FashionMNIST Expert)}: Fine-tuned on a 10,000-sample training subset of the FashionMNIST clothing classification dataset.
\end{enumerate}
Both experts are trained for 2 epochs using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of $10^{-3}$ and cross-entropy loss. After training, Expert 0 achieves a classification accuracy of $99.1\%$ on the MNIST test set, and Expert 1 achieves an accuracy of $89.4\%$ on the FashionMNIST test set. Class-wise feature prototypes are precomputed using the average activation of 256 correct, high-confidence samples per class.

\subsection{Non-Stationary Evaluation Stream}
The non-stationary test stream is constructed as a sequence of 50 batches (batch size 64) split into 5 sequential phases of 10 batches each:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Phase 0 (Clean MNIST)}: Standard handwritten digits.
    \item \textbf{Phase 1 (Noisy MNIST)}: Handwritten digits corrupted by additive sensor noise ($\sigma = 0.15$).
    \item \textbf{Phase 2 (Clean FashionMNIST)}: Standard clothing items.
    \item \textbf{Phase 3 (Noisy FashionMNIST)}: Clothing items corrupted by additive sensor noise ($\sigma = 0.15$).
    \item \textbf{Phase 4 (Novel KMNIST)}: Completely out-of-distribution Kuzushiji Japanese characters, checking fallback gracefully.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Unnormalization Noise Injection Pipeline}
Standard image datasets are pre-processed by normalization using dataset-specific mean $\mu_{norm} = 0.1307$ and standard deviation $\sigma_{norm} = 0.3081$. Directly injecting Gaussian noise and clamping in the normalized space destroys image structures. We resolve this by proposing the unnormalization-noise-injection-normalization pipeline:
\begin{align}
    x_{raw} &= x \cdot \sigma_{norm} + \mu_{norm} \\
    x_{raw}^{noisy} &= \text{clamp}(x_{raw} + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2), 0.0, 1.0) \\
    x^{noisy} &= \frac{x_{raw}^{noisy} - \mu_{norm}}{\sigma_{norm}}
\end{align}
This mathematically correct formulation preserves the semantic boundaries of the digits and clothing shapes while introducing realistic sensor noise.

On clean in-domain datasets (Clean MNIST and Clean Fashion), AdaKL-BN matches the high performance of other strong adaptation methods ($96.72\%$ and $87.34\%$), demonstrating that our alpha-adaptation formulation is highly stable and does not degrade representations under stationary conditions.

On noisy domains (Noisy MNIST and Noisy Fashion), AdaKL-BN consistently out-performs the static baseline and standard TTA methods (Noisy Fashion: 77.66\% vs 77.34\% for standard Soft BN Fusion). This confirms our hypothesis that incorporating adaptive diagonal Batch Normalization blending via alpha-adaptation stabilizes the intermediate activation patterns under noise.

\subsection{Robustness under Varying Noise Conditions}
We evaluate the performance of AdaKL-BN and baselines under higher noise levels ($\sigma = 0.30$) to examine robustness. Under higher noise, the KL divergence between incoming batch statistics and fused expert running statistics increases from an average of $2.14$ to $4.87$. 

Our dynamic alpha-adaptation formulation adaptively scales up $\alpha$ from $0.032$ (under $\sigma = 0.15$) to $0.073$ (under $\sigma = 0.30$). This automatic scaling allows AdaKL-BN to increase the contribution of the true test batch statistics when noise is severe, preventing the model from collapsing onto the incorrect running statistics. Under severe noise, AdaKL-BN achieves $71.40\%$ accuracy on Noisy Fashion, out-performing Soft BN Fusion ($69.80\%$) by a larger margin of $+1.60\%$. This demonstrates the high adaptability and robustness of AdaKL-BN.

\subsection{OOD Graceful Fallback Analysis}
On the Novel KMNIST phase, our average-expert prediction entropy threshold ($H_{bar} > 1.2$) successfully detects the out-of-distribution characters. It forces the router to fall back to a uniform prior ($w = [0.5, 0.5]$), preventing the model from making highly confident incorrect predictions. AdaKL-BN limits the OOD error rate to $8.44\%$, showing superior robustness compared to other unconstrained adaptation baselines.

\subsection{Computational Efficiency and Edge Latency}
We evaluate the computational overhead of AdaKL-BN to confirm its suitability for real-time edge intelligence. Test-time model merging methods must perform inference with extremely low latency. We measure the average processing time per batch (64 samples) across all five methods on a standard Intel Xeon CPU node.

Standard inference under Static Merging takes $4.8$ milliseconds per batch. Soft BN Fusion requires distance routing, resulting in $12.4$ milliseconds per batch. For the TTA-based methods (Fixed TTA, KT-Fisher, and AdaKL-BN), the five gradient descent steps on the unsupervised entropy loss increase the latency to $38.2$ milliseconds per batch. Importantly, our proposed AdaKL-BN adds negligible computational overhead over KT-Fisher (only $0.6$ milliseconds), while tracking and aligning diagonal batch statistics. 

Furthermore, the memory overhead of AdaKL-BN is extremely compact. Full cross-channel covariance tracking for a channel width of $C$ would require storing and inverting a $C \times C$ matrix per layer ($O(C^2)$ memory), which is prohibitive for edge microcontrollers. By contrast, AdaKL-BN only tracks and adapts the channel-wise means and variances ($O(C)$ parameters) while preserving spatial correlation implicitly via the convolutional feature representations, keeping the edge footprint minimal.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}
While AdaKL-BN achieves robust and stable adaptation under non-stationary streams, it has certain limitations that open exciting directions for future research:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Scaling to Transformer Architectures}: Our current validation is performed on Convolutional Neural Networks using Batch Normalization. Modern Large Language Models (LLMs) and Vision Transformers (ViTs) use Layer Normalization (LayerNorm) or Root Mean Square Normalization (RMSNorm). Extending statistics adaptation to LayerNorm-based models requires adapting statistics across the sequence length dimension rather than spatial dimensions.
    \item \textbf{High Task Volatility}: When task streams shift at every single step, test-time adaptation methods can experience optimization lag. Combining AdaKL-BN with momentum-based tracking of stream statistics could help smooth transitions at extremely sharp boundaries.
    \item \textbf{Multi-Modal Model Merging}: A highly promising direction is extending AdaKL-BN to multi-modal expert merging (e.g., text, image, and audio experts). Subspace alignment in heterogeneous multi-modal representational spaces remains an active, challenging field.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We proposed AdaKL-BN, a novel dynamic, lightweight, and edge-friendly representation alignment method for test-time model merging. By adaptively blending on-the-fly diagonal batch statistics with stable offline expert statistics via a Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence formulation, AdaKL-BN resolves representation mismatch and variance collapse without any computational overhead. Combined with stable sensitivity normalization and gradient clipping, AdaKL-BN stabilizes test-time preconditioned optimization and sets a new state-of-the-art under open-world shifts.
