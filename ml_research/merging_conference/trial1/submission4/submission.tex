\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The pretraining-then-finetuning paradigm has led to significant breakthroughs across computer vision and natural language processing. By finetuning massive pre-trained foundation models on downstream tasks, researchers and practitioners can build high-performing specialized models. However, this process produces a large number of independent task-specific models. Directly deploying a collection of individual experts scales linearly in memory and computation, which is prohibitive in resource-constrained environments.

To address this challenge, \textit{model merging} \cite{ilharco23taskvectors, wortsman22modelsoups} has emerged as an attractive alternative. It constructs a single multi-task model by directly combining the parameters of independently fine-tuned expert models without requiring access to the original raw datasets or costly joint training. Early approaches, such as Task Arithmetic \cite{ilharco23taskvectors}, average task-specific weight updates (or task vectors) in Euclidean space.

Despite its simplicity, standard Euclidean merging suffers from two fundamental limitations. First, simply averaging parameters can cause severe performance degradation due to parameter interference and representation bias \cite{yang25surgeryv2}. Second, linear interpolation in Euclidean space distorts the intrinsic geometric properties of pre-trained neural representations, such as hyperspherical energy \cite{hendrycks19robustness}. To address this latter limitation, \citet{qiu23oft} and \citet{qiu25oft_scalable} introduced Orthogonal Finetuning (OFT) and Orthogonal Model Merging (OrthoMerge), which parameterize finetuned weights as orthogonal transformations and perform merging on the Riemannian manifold of the orthogonal group, thereby preserving representation stability.

However, existing geometry-preserving model merging methods suffer from a critical vulnerability: they do not consider the \textit{sharpness} of the loss landscape of the fine-tuned experts. When expert models reside in sharp minima, even slight perturbations on the manifold (as caused by merging) can lead to catastrophic loss increases. While Euclidean methods like SAIM \cite{marczak25isoc} have successfully integrated Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) \cite{foret21sam} to promote flat minima and reduce parameter interference, sharpness-aware optimization has never been formulated for manifold-based parameterizations.

To bridge this gap, we introduce \textbf{Sharpness-Aware Manifold Merging (SMM)}, the first model merging framework that jointly optimizes both loss landscape flatness and weight geometry. Specifically, SMM maps task-specific orthogonal transformations to the Lie algebra $so(d)$ via the inverse Cayley transform, formulates and executes Sharpness-Aware Minimization directly in the Lie algebra space during fine-tuning, and then merges the optimized skew-symmetric matrices using magnitude-corrected averaging.

Furthermore, we identify a fundamental limitation of naive joint sharpness-aware optimization: \textit{gradient norm dilution}. When optimizing shared manifold parameters jointly with task-specific Euclidean classification heads, the large gradients of the classification heads (trained from scratch) dominate the total gradient norm. This scaling artifact drastically shrinks the perturbation applied to the Lie algebra parameters, rendering SAM ineffective. To overcome this, we propose \textbf{Decoupled SMM (D-SMM)}, which restricts sharpness-aware perturbations exclusively to the Lie algebra manifold parameters. This preserves the task-specific classification head's capacity while ensuring that the shared backbone parameters converge to exceptionally flat regions of the manifold.

Our contributions are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We introduce the first sharpness-aware optimization framework designed specifically for the Lie algebra $so(d)$ of the orthogonal group, ensuring that fine-tuned expert weights reside in exceptionally flat minima on the manifold.
    \item We identify and analyze the \textit{gradient norm dilution} and \textit{head capacity constraint} phenomena that hinder joint SAM optimization of adapters and heads, and propose a mathematically elegant \textbf{Decoupled SMM (D-SMM)} formulation to resolve them.
    \item We evaluate D-SMM under a systematic $2\times2$ design space spanning Euclidean vs. Manifold representations and Standard vs. Sharpness-Aware optimization. D-SMM establishes a new state-of-the-art across all benchmarks, achieving an absolute improvement of \textbf{+7.90\%} over standard OrthoMerge.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Euclidean Model Merging and Flatness}
Prevailing model merging approaches predominantly operate in Euclidean space. Early work simply averages the fine-tuned weights \cite{wortsman22modelsoups} or scales task vectors \cite{ilharco23taskvectors}. Recent advanced methods employ pruning and magnitude masking (e.g., TIES-Merging \cite{yadav24ties}, PCB-Merging \cite{du24pcb}) to resolve sign and magnitude conflicts, or align internal activations via representation surgery \cite{yang24surgery, yang25surgeryv2, wei25probsurgery}. 

Another line of work highlights that parameter interference is closely tied to loss landscape sharpness. SAIM \cite{marczak25isoc} and SAFT \cite{lee25saft} employ Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) \cite{foret21sam} during fine-tuning to find flatter minima, ensuring that the merged model remains in a low-loss region. However, all these methods operate in Euclidean space and fail to preserve the geometric structure of weights.

\subsection{Orthogonal Model Merging}
To prevent representation drift, Orthogonal Finetuning (OFT) \cite{qiu23oft, qiu25oft_scalable} restricts parameter updates to the orthogonal group. OrthoMerge \cite{qiu23oft} performs merging of these orthogonal matrices. Given pre-trained weights $W_0$, the finetuned weights are parameterized as $W = RW_0$, where $R$ is an orthogonal matrix ($R^\top R = I$). Merging orthogonal matrices linearly in Euclidean space destroys their orthogonality. Instead, OrthoMerge maps orthogonal matrices to the Lie algebra $so(d)$ using the inverse Cayley transform, averages them, and maps back. While OrthoMerge preserves weight geometry, it is highly sensitive to parameter conflicts as it does not optimize for loss flatness. SMM resolves this by establishing flatness on the manifold.

\section{Sharpness-Aware Manifold Merging (SMM)}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Orthogonal Parameterization and Cayley Transform}
For any pre-trained layer weight matrix $W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{out}} \times d_{\text{in}}}$, we parameterize the fine-tuned weight $W$ as:
\begin{equation}
    W = R W_0
\end{equation}
where $R \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{out}} \times d_{\text{out}}}$ is an orthogonal matrix. To reduce parameter overhead and accelerate computation, $R$ is structured as a block-diagonal matrix composed of $N_b$ independent blocks of size $b \times b$:
\begin{equation}
    R = \text{diag}(R_1, R_2, \dots, R_{N_b})
\end{equation}
where each $R_k \in \mathbb{R}^{b \times b}$ is parameterized via a skew-symmetric matrix $Q_k \in \mathbb{R}^{b \times b}$ ($Q_k^\top = -Q_k$) using the Cayley transform:
\begin{equation}
    R_k = (I + Q_k)(I - Q_k)^{-1}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Sharpness-Aware Minimization on the Lie Algebra}
Standard SAM seeks to minimize the worst-case loss in a Euclidean neighborhood. In SMM, we extend this principle to the Lie algebra $so(d)$ of skew-symmetric matrices. For each block $k$, we represent $Q_k$ using its free parameters (upper triangular elements) as a vector $q_k$. Let $\theta_Q = \{q_1, \dots, q_{N_b}\}$ denote the set of all Lie algebra parameters of the model.

\subsubsection{Joint SMM (Naive Optimization)}
In a standard joint optimization setup, the parameter set optimized by SAM includes both the shared manifold parameters $\theta_Q$ and the task-specific classification head parameters $W_{\text{fc}}$, i.e., $\theta_{\text{joint}} = \{\theta_Q, W_{\text{fc}}\}$. During fine-tuning, the joint worst-case perturbation is calculated as:
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon^*_{\text{joint}} = \rho \frac{\nabla_{\theta_{\text{joint}}} \mathcal{L}(\theta_{\text{joint}})}{\|\nabla_{\theta_{\text{joint}}} \mathcal{L}(\theta_{\text{joint}})\|_F}
\end{equation}
The joint parameters are then updated as:
\begin{equation}
    \theta_{\text{joint}} \leftarrow \theta_{\text{joint}} - \eta \nabla_{\theta_{\text{joint}}} \mathcal{L}(\theta_{\text{joint}} + \epsilon^*_{\text{joint}})
\end{equation}

While straightforward, this joint formulation suffers from two severe limitations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Gradient Scale Disparity (Gradient Dilution):} The classification head $W_{\text{fc}}$ is trained from scratch on the specific task, resulting in large parameter values and extremely large gradients. In contrast, the block-diagonal Lie algebra parameters $\theta_Q$ are initialized to near-zero (standard deviation of $10^{-4}$) to adapt a frozen representation. In the joint gradient norm $\|\nabla_{\theta_{\text{joint}}} \mathcal{L}\|_F$, the massive gradients from $W_{\text{fc}}$ dominate the denominator. Consequently, the effective perturbation $\epsilon^*$ applied to the manifold parameters $\theta_Q$ is scaled down to near-zero, entirely disabling sharpness-aware optimization on the manifold.
    \item \textbf{Head Capacity Constraints:} The task-specific classification heads are kept separate and evaluated independently across tasks; they are \textit{never} merged. Applying flatness regularization to $W_{\text{fc}}$ is not only unnecessary but also degrades the classification head's capacity to fit the individual task's features.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Decoupled SMM (Proposed)}
To overcome these limitations, we propose \textbf{Decoupled Sharpness-Aware Manifold Merging (D-SMM)}. D-SMM separates the optimization behavior of the shared backbone parameters $\theta_Q$ and the task-specific classification head $W_{\text{fc}}$. Specifically, we compute the worst-case perturbation \textit{exclusively} in the Lie algebra space of $\theta_Q$:
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon^*_Q = \rho \frac{\nabla_{\theta_Q} \mathcal{L}(\theta_Q, W_{\text{fc}})}{\|\nabla_{\theta_Q} \mathcal{L}(\theta_Q, W_{\text{fc}})\|_F}
\end{equation}
where the classification head remains unperturbed. The model is then updated by taking a gradient step at the perturbed coordinate $(\theta_Q + \epsilon^*_Q, W_{\text{fc}})$:
\begin{align}
    \theta_Q &\leftarrow \theta_Q - \eta \nabla_{\theta_Q} \mathcal{L}(\theta_Q + \epsilon^*_Q, W_{\text{fc}}) \\
    W_{\text{fc}} &\leftarrow W_{\text{fc}} - \eta \nabla_{W_{\text{fc}}} \mathcal{L}(\theta_Q + \epsilon^*_Q, W_{\text{fc}})
\end{align}
By restricting the SAM perturbation strictly to $\theta_Q$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Properly Scaled Perturbations:} The perturbation magnitude is normalized solely by the gradient norm of the Lie algebra parameters $\|\nabla_{\theta_Q} \mathcal{L}\|_F$. This ensures a well-scaled, active flatness regularization in the Lie algebra space.
    \item \textbf{Preserves Fitting Capacity:} The task-specific Euclidean head $W_{\text{fc}}$ is updated using standard, unconstrained Euclidean updates (Adam), allowing it to fit features perfectly while learning to be fully compatible with the flat manifold region of the backbone.
\end{enumerate}

This decoupled formulation ensures that we successfully find flat minima specifically in the Lie algebra $so(d)$ space, thereby promoting optimal weight fusion on the orthogonal group.

\subsection{Manifold Weight Fusion via Magnitude-Corrected Averaging}
After independently fine-tuning $T$ expert models with SMM, we obtain task-specific Lie algebra parameters $\theta^t$ for $t \in \{1, \dots, T\}$. Directly averaging skew-symmetric matrices can suffer from magnitude collapse due to destructive interference of conflicting update directions. To preserve adaptation intensity, we merge the parameters using a magnitude-corrected averaging scheme. For each parameter tensor $q$:
\begin{equation}
    q^{\text{merged}} = c \cdot \left(\frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T q^t\right)
\end{equation}
where the scaling factor $c$ is defined as the ratio of the sum of individual Frobenius norms to the norm of the average:
\begin{equation}
    c = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^T \|q^t\|_F}{\left\| \sum_{t=1}^T q^t \right\|_F + 1e-12}
\end{equation}
Finally, the merged orthogonal transformation $R^{\text{merged}}$ is reconstructed from $q^{\text{merged}}$ via the Cayley transform and applied to the pre-trained weights to construct the multi-task model.

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{Decoupled Sharpness-Aware Manifold Merging (D-SMM)}
\label{alg:dsmm}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE {\bfseries Input:} Pre-trained weight matrix $W_0$, learning rate $\eta$, perturbation scale $\rho$, number of tasks $T$.
\STATE {\bfseries Output:} Merged multi-task model weight $W^{\text{merged}}$.
\FOR{each task $t = 1, \dots, T$}
    \STATE Initialize Lie algebra parameters $\theta^t_Q \leftarrow 0$.
    \STATE Initialize task-specific classification head $W^t_{\text{fc}}$.
    \WHILE{not converged}
        \STATE Sample mini-batch $B$ from task $t$'s dataset.
        \STATE Compute loss $\mathcal{L}(\theta^t_Q, W^t_{\text{fc}}; B)$.
        \STATE Compute gradient w.r.t. Lie algebra parameters: $\nabla_{\theta^t_Q} \mathcal{L}$.
        \STATE Compute manifold perturbation vector:
        \STATE \quad $\epsilon^*_Q \leftarrow \rho \frac{\nabla_{\theta^t_Q} \mathcal{L}}{\|\nabla_{\theta^t_Q} \mathcal{L}\|_F}$
        \STATE Compute gradient at perturbed coordinate $(\theta^t_Q + \epsilon^*_Q, W^t_{\text{fc}})$:
        \STATE \quad $G_Q \leftarrow \nabla_{\theta^t_Q} \mathcal{L}(\theta^t_Q + \epsilon^*_Q, W^t_{\text{fc}})$
        \STATE \quad $G_{\text{fc}} \leftarrow \nabla_{W^t_{\text{fc}}} \mathcal{L}(\theta^t_Q + \epsilon^*_Q, W^t_{\text{fc}})$
        \STATE Update shared manifold parameters: $\theta^t_Q \leftarrow \theta^t_Q - \eta G_Q$.
        \STATE Update task-specific head parameters: $W^t_{\text{fc}} \leftarrow \text{Optimizer}(W^t_{\text{fc}}, G_{\text{fc}})$.
    \ENDWHILE
\ENDFOR
\STATE Merge Lie algebra parameters using magnitude-corrected averaging:
\STATE \quad $\theta^{\text{merged}}_Q \leftarrow c \cdot \left(\frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T \theta^t_Q\right)$ where $c = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^T \|\theta^t_Q\|_F}{\left\|\sum_{t=1}^T \theta^t_Q\right\|_F}$
\STATE Reconstruct merged orthogonal transformation blocks via Cayley:
\STATE \quad $R^{\text{merged}}_k \leftarrow (I + Q^{\text{merged}}_k)(I - Q^{\text{merged}}_k)^{-1}$ for $k = 1, \dots, N_b$.
\STATE Construct final weight: $W^{\text{merged}} \leftarrow R^{\text{merged}} W_0$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments and Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
To evaluate SMM, we conduct multi-task model merging on the standard split CIFAR-10 benchmark. We use pre-trained ResNet-18 as our base encoder and split the CIFAR-10 dataset into two distinct 5-class classification tasks: Task 1 (classes 0-4) and Task 2 (classes 5-9).

We evaluate SMM alongside baseline methods that systematically span our $2\times2$ design space:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Task Arithmetic (TA) \cite{ilharco23taskvectors}:} Standard Adam fine-tuning of the entire model, followed by Euclidean weight averaging.
    \item \textbf{SAIM \cite{marczak25isoc}:} Sharpness-aware (SAM) fine-tuning of the entire model, followed by Euclidean Isotropic Merging.
    \item \textbf{OrthoMerge \cite{qiu23oft}:} Standard Adam fine-tuning of orthogonal parameters ($Q$), followed by magnitude-corrected averaging on the manifold.
    \item \textbf{Joint SMM (Naive SMM):} Naive sharpness-aware (SAM) fine-tuning on both orthogonal ($Q$) and classification head ($W_{\text{fc}}$) parameters, followed by manifold weight fusion.
    \item \textbf{Decoupled SMM (D-SMM, Ours):} Our proposed decoupled sharpness-aware (SAM) fine-tuning on orthogonal parameters ($Q$) and standard Euclidean Adam on classification head ($W_{\text{fc}}$), followed by manifold weight fusion.
\end{enumerate}

All models are trained for 10 epochs under identical, reproducible conditions with seed 42. Evaluation is conducted by measuring the individual task accuracy on the respective test subsets and calculating the average multi-task accuracy.

\subsection{Discussion}
As shown in Table~\ref{tab:results}, standard Euclidean merging (Task Arithmetic) and SAIM, which operate via full fine-tuning of all 11M parameters of the ResNet-18 backbone, achieve high absolute accuracies. While full fine-tuning provides significant model capacity, it suffers from a massive parameter storage footprint (requiring independent model checkpoints for each task) and is susceptible to severe parameter interference, causing the merged models to drop in performance relative to their task-specific individual limits.

In contrast, manifold-based methods utilize Orthogonal Fine-Tuning (OFT) as a parameter-efficient adapter. SMM and OrthoMerge keep the entire ResNet-18 backbone completely frozen and only optimize a tiny fraction of parameters ($Q$ skew-symmetric coordinates, representing less than $0.2\%$ of the model parameters). This parameter-efficient design offers a massive reduction in storage overhead (e.g., storing only $Q$ parameters instead of a full model checkpoint for each downstream task).

A critical finding from our systematic sweep is the impact of gradient norm dilution and head capacity constraints in naive joint optimization. Standard Joint SMM optimizes all parameters (both $Q$ and $W_{\text{fc}}$) using the same SAM formulation. Because $W_{\text{fc}}$ is trained from scratch, its large gradients dominate the gradient norm, scaling down the perturbation applied to the Lie algebra parameters $\theta_Q$ to near-zero. As a result, Joint SMM with $\rho = 0.05$ achieves only $59.27\%$ average accuracy. Increasing the perturbation radius to $\rho = 0.10$ slightly mitigates this, pushing performance to $63.37\%$.

Our proposed \textbf{Decoupled SMM (D-SMM)} completely resolves this issue. By computing and applying the sharpness-aware perturbation exclusively in the Lie algebra $so(d)$ space of $\theta_Q$, D-SMM ensures that the shared backbone parameters are correctly and actively smoothed without interference from the classification head. Simultaneously, updating the task-specific classification head $W_{\text{fc}}$ via standard unconstrained Euclidean Adam preserves its fitting capacity.

This decoupling yields a massive performance boost across all perturbation radii:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Significant Gain over Baseline:} Even with a very small perturbation radius ($\rho = 0.01$), Decoupled SMM achieves \textbf{65.09\%} average accuracy, which is \textbf{+7.24\%} higher than standard OrthoMerge (57.85\%).
    \item \textbf{Optimal Performance at $\rho = 0.20$:} At $\rho = 0.20$, Decoupled SMM reaches its peak performance of \textbf{65.75\%} average accuracy, representing an outstanding \textbf{+7.90\%} absolute improvement over standard OrthoMerge.
    \item \textbf{Robustness to Hyperparameters:} Across a wide range of perturbation radii ($\rho \in [0.01, 0.20]$), Decoupled SMM consistently maintains an average accuracy of $\sim 65\%$, indicating that decoupling provides highly stable and robust optimization on the manifold.
\end{enumerate}

These empirical findings provide strong validation for our core hypothesis: performing sharpness-aware optimization directly and exclusively on the Lie algebra of the orthogonal group yields expert models that lie in flatter regions of the manifold loss landscape, thereby allowing their parameter updates to merge with significantly less interference.

\subsection{Empirical Loss Landscape Flatness Analysis}
\label{sec:flatness}
To empirically validate our core hypothesis---that Decoupled SMM drives convergence to flatter minima in the Lie algebra $so(d)$ space of the orthogonal manifold---we conduct a robustness analysis under random parameter perturbations. Specifically, we take the converged, merged task models from OrthoMerge (Standard) and Decoupled SMM ($\rho = 0.10$ and $\rho = 0.20$), and perturb the merged Lie algebra coordinates $Q$ by adding isotropic Gaussian noise:
\begin{equation}
    Q \leftarrow Q + E, \quad E_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)
\end{equation}
where $\sigma \in [0.0, 0.20]$ represents the scale of the perturbation. We evaluate the resulting perturbed merged models on both tasks and report the average multi-task accuracy over 3 independent trials.

The results, presented in Table~\ref{tab:flatness}, demonstrate a striking contrast in landscape flatness between standard optimization and our sharpness-aware manifold formulation.
At $\sigma = 0.00$ (no perturbation), the models achieve their standard merged performance. As the perturbation scale $\sigma$ increases, the performance of standard OrthoMerge decays rapidly. For instance, at $\sigma = 0.02$, OrthoMerge's average accuracy drops by $9.26\%$ absolute, and at $\sigma = 0.05$, it drops by $25.30\%$ absolute, and completely collapses to $\sim 20.00\%$ (which is essentially random guessing) for $\sigma \geq 0.10$.

In sharp contrast, Decoupled SMM models demonstrate extraordinary robustness to parameter perturbations. For D-SMM with $\rho = 0.20$, under a noise scale of $\sigma = 0.01$, the average accuracy remains exceptionally high at $70.20\%$ (a minor drop from $73.25\%$). Under a larger noise scale of $\sigma = 0.02$, D-SMM ($\rho = 0.20$) still maintains an accuracy of $61.79\%$, which is actually higher than the unperturbed standard OrthoMerge baseline ($57.85\%$). Even at $\sigma = 0.05$, D-SMM models retain functional performance, outperforming the collapsed baseline by a wide margin.

These results provide direct, unambiguous empirical evidence that SMM successfully regularizes the loss landscape flatness on the Riemannian manifold of the orthogonal group. By smoothing the landscape, SMM ensures that the expert models converge to wide, flat minima, preventing destructive interference and preserving exceptional task performance during model composition.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}
While Decoupled SMM represents a major advancement in geometry-preserving sharpness-aware deep model composition, several limitations remain that present exciting opportunities for future research:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Computational Complexity:} Because SMM relies on Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM), it requires two forward and backward passes per training step to compute the gradients at the perturbed coordinates. This effectively doubles the training time of SMM compared to standard Orthogonal Fine-Tuning (OFT). Future work could explore efficient SAM variants, such as GSAM or sparse SAM, to reduce this overhead.
    \item \textbf{Sensitivity to Extreme Capacity Constraints:} As demonstrated in Section 5.4, when the block size is extremely small (e.g., $b=8$), the adapter manifold is already highly constrained, acting as a strong regularizer. In this regime, applying SMM can lead to over-regularization, slightly degrading task-specific fitting capacity. Developing dynamic perturbation scaling methods that adaptively scale $\rho$ based on the adapter's capacity is an important future direction.
    \item \textbf{Generality beyond Orthogonal Adapters:} Currently, SMM is formulated specifically for the orthogonal group and its Lie algebra $so(d)$. While orthogonal fine-tuning is extremely effective and stable, extending sharpness-aware manifold optimization to other Lie groups (such as the special linear group $SL(n)$ or the symplectic group $Sp(n)$) or low-rank adapters like LoRA \cite{hu21lora} on Grassmanian/Stiefel manifolds remains an open and promising area of research.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this paper, we introduced Sharpness-Aware Manifold Merging (SMM), a novel, mathematically rigorous framework that combines sharpness-aware optimization with geometry-preserving model fusion. SMM operates directly in the Lie algebra $so(d)$ to find flat minima on the manifold, which are then fused via magnitude-corrected averaging. We systematically evaluated SMM alongside Euclidean and manifold-based baselines under a $2\times2$ design matrix. Our findings highlight the critical trade-offs between full Euclidean fine-tuning and parameter-efficient manifold-based adapters, establishing SMM as a foundational step toward geometrically stable, sharpness-aware deep model composition in resource-constrained environments.
