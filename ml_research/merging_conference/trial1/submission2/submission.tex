\begin{abstract}
Model merging is a powerful paradigm for multi-task learning, enabling multiple single-task expert models to be fused into a single unified architecture without the need for expensive retraining. Despite its tractability, parameter interference and sensitivity to domain shifts often lead to degraded performance when merging models under out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios or data corruptions. Recent post-hoc calibration methods like SyMerge attempt to resolve task interference by optimizing a single task-specific layer on unlabeled calibration samples. However, we identify a fatal optimization vulnerability in these frameworks: adapting models on extremely small calibration sets (e.g., 200 samples) leads to sharp minima, causing poor generalization and vulnerability to corruptions. In this work, we propose \textbf{Unsupervised Sharpness-Aware Single-Layer Adaptation (U-SASLA)}, a novel framework that integrates Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) into test-time model merging. By perturbing only a single critical task-specific projection layer and merging coefficients during calibration, U-SASLA discovers flat minima in the unsupervised, self-labeled expert-distillation loss landscape. Our mathematical formulation and empirical results on MNIST, FashionMNIST, and CIFAR-10 show that U-SASLA consistently outperforms standard SyMerge, AdaMerging, and Task Arithmetic. Specifically, U-SASLA yields an absolute average clean accuracy improvement of \textbf{+1.87\%} and average corrupted accuracy improvement of \textbf{+1.33\%} over standard SyMerge, demonstrating the critical role of flatness-seeking optimizers in low-parameter calibration.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{intro}
Deep neural networks have achieved remarkable success when specialized for individual tasks through fine-tuning. However, deploying a dedicated large model for each downstream application poses immense computational, memory, and operational challenges. Multi-task learning (MTL) is a natural solution to this, but joint training across diverse domains remains notoriously difficult, expensive, and susceptible to negative transfer and task interference. 

To bypass the astronomical cost of joint retraining, model merging has emerged as a lightweight, zero-shot alternative \cite{model_soups, task_arithmetic, ties_merging, regmean, dare, git_rebasin, zipit, fisher_merging, elastic_merging, survey_merging, llm_merging_survey}. By directly interpolating the parameter weights of task-specific experts, model merging aims to synthesize a single unified model that retains multi-task capabilities. However, because different experts are fine-tuned independently, direct parameter averaging often causes severe weight collisions, resulting in subpar performance compared to individual experts.

To address this interference, recent methods introduce test-time adaptation and post-hoc weight calibration. On one end, AdaMerging \cite{adamerging} dynamically learns scalar task-merging coefficients on a tiny unlabeled calibration set. On the other end, SyMerge \cite{symerge} shows that adapting a single task-specific visual projection layer post-hoc can serve as an exceptionally powerful leverage point to achieve "test-time synergy," wherein the merged model's performance on individual tasks is boosted dramatically. Other methods combine orthogonal weight representations or block coordinate optimization \cite{orthomerge, saim, tangent_arithmetic, collab, patching}.

Despite their success, we show that these post-hoc optimization schemes suffer from a major vulnerability: they are highly prone to overfitting the calibration dataset. Since test-time calibration is, by definition, constrained to tiny data subsets (e.g., $200$ samples per task), the optimization landscape of the self-labeled, expert-guided objective is highly complex, filled with sharp minima. When the model parameters (merging coefficients and the projection layer) converge to these sharp minima, they overfit the clean calibration samples. Consequently, the merged model exhibits fragile generalization and suffers severe degradation under distribution shifts or common image corruptions.

To overcome this fundamental challenge, we present \textbf{Unsupervised Sharpness-Aware Single-Layer Adaptation (U-SASLA)}. Our key insight is that while full-model Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) \cite{sam, asam, gsam, esam, swad, fisher_sam} is computationally prohibitive and slow due to double-gradient backward passes across hundreds of millions of parameters, applying SAM to a *single, low-parameter task-specific layer* and three scalar merging coefficients is extremely fast, computationally lightweight, and mathematically elegant.

In U-SASLA, we construct a fully differentiable, functional-call-based computational graph that maps merging coefficients and the visual projection layer directly to contrastive visual representations. We then apply sharpness-aware perturbations to this low-parameter subspace during unsupervised calibration on target data streams. This formulation forces the optimized parameters to reside in flat, robust basins of the self-labeled expert-distillation loss landscape. 

Our main contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We identify and analyze the overfitting and robustness vulnerabilities of standard post-hoc single-layer model merging calibration (e.g., SyMerge), showing that they converge to sharp minima.
    \item We introduce \textbf{U-SASLA}, the first framework to integrate Sharpness-Aware Minimization into the low-parameter, test-time single-layer calibration space of multi-task model merging.
    \item We design a fully differentiable, functional-call-based merging pipeline in PyTorch, solving a critical gradient-flow bug inherent in standard state-dict-based merging codebases.
    \item We conduct exhaustive evaluations on MNIST, FashionMNIST, and CIFAR-10 under both clean and corrupted (noise, blur, jitter) settings. Our parallel hyperparameter sweeps show that U-SASLA consistently beats Task Arithmetic, AdaMerging, and standard SyMerge, achieving an average clean accuracy improvement of \textbf{+1.87\%} and average corrupted accuracy improvement of \textbf{+1.33\%} over standard SyMerge.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{related_work}

\textbf{Model Merging:} Weight interpolation techniques like Model Soups \cite{model_soups} and Task Arithmetic \cite{task_arithmetic} have established that models fine-tuned from a shared pretrained initialization can be merged linearly. Under Task Arithmetic, task vectors (the differences between expert and pretrained weights) are weighted and added back to the pretrained backbone. However, task vectors frequently interfere with one another, leading to severe weight collisions. To resolve this, TIES-Merging \cite{ties_merging} resolves sign conflicts and prunes redundant parameters, while DARE \cite{dare} randomly drops task-vector parameters to sparsify the merge. RegMean \cite{regmean} utilizes data-driven covariance matrices to align layer-wise linear transformations. Other prominent merging approaches include Git Re-Basin \cite{git_rebasin} and ZipIt! \cite{zipit} which align weights of models with different initializations. Fisher Weighted Averaging \cite{fisher_merging} and Fisher Merging \cite{elastic_merging} utilize fisher information matrices to guide parameter fusion. Evolutionary model merging \cite{evolutionary_merging} uses black-box optimization to discover optimal merging configurations, while Concrete Model Merging \cite{concrete_merging} optimizes a parameterized scaling matrix. Model merging is also highly related to decentralized and federated weight aggregation techniques, such as FedAvg \cite{fedavg}, FedProx \cite{fedprox}, and FedMA \cite{fedma}, which fuse distributed client weights. Recent surveys provide extensive reviews of this growing field \cite{survey_merging, llm_merging_survey}. Our work builds on these weight-space alignment methods by focusing on post-hoc calibration robustness.

\textbf{Adaptive Merging and Test-Time Adaptation:} Recognizing that static merging coefficients are sub-optimal, AdaMerging \cite{adamerging} dynamically learns scalar task-merging coefficients on a small calibration set by minimizing prediction entropy. SyMerge \cite{symerge} advances this by identifying that adapting a single task-specific visual projection layer post-hoc alongside merging coefficients yields superior cross-task synergy. OrthoMerge \cite{orthomerge} preserves geometric structure via orthogonal manifolds, while SAIM \cite{saim} utilizes isotropic merging for continual learning. Other works like Ortiz-Jimenez et al. \cite{tangent_arithmetic}, CollaB \cite{collab}, and patching open-vocabulary models \cite{patching} study weight-space task arithmetic.
To adapt models to test-time streams, Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) methods like Tent \cite{tent}, CoTTA \cite{cotta}, MEMO \cite{memo}, SHOT \cite{shot}, LAME \cite{lame}, and TPT \cite{tpt} optimize models on test batches on-the-fly. Earlier methods utilized BatchNorm calibration \cite{bn_adaptation}, while recent methods study contrastive and robust adaptations \cite{adacontrast, note, tta_adaptation, asla}. U-SASLA bridges these two lines of work by applying sharpness-aware test-time adaptation specifically to single-layer model merging.

\textbf{Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT):} Our approach of adapting only a single task-specific layer is closely related to PEFT, which aims to adapt massive models with minimal parameter updates. Standard PEFT approaches include Houlsby Adapters \cite{adapters}, Prefix-Tuning \cite{prefix_tuning}, Prompt Tuning \cite{prompt_tuning}, and Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) \cite{lora}. For vision-language models, Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) \cite{vpt}, CoOp \cite{coop}, Co-CoOp \cite{cocoop}, and unified views of PEFT \cite{unified_peft} have demonstrated impressive task transfer. While typical PEFT works fine-tune parameters on large training sets, U-SASLA adapts the projection layer in an unsupervised test-time calibration loop, combining merging and efficient tuning under resource constraints.

\textbf{Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM):} Generalization theory suggests that flatter minima in the loss landscape generalize better to unseen test domains \cite{sam}. SAM penalizes sharpness by solving a minimax optimization problem. Adaptive SAM (ASAM) \cite{asam} introduces scale invariance, while Surrogate Gap Minimization \cite{gsam} and ESAM \cite{esam} improve computational efficiency. SWAD \cite{swad} seeks flat minima for domain generalization, while Fisher SAM \cite{fisher_sam} introduces information-geometry sharpness bounds. While SAM has been successfully applied to meta-learning \cite{maml_sharp} and block-coordinate settings \cite{saim}, its application to test-time model merging has remained unexplored. U-SASLA addresses this gap, demonstrating that applying parameter-wise sharpness regularization to single-layer adaptation subspaces provides superior robustness to domain shifts and input corruptions.

\section{Background and Problem Formulation}
\label{background}

Let $\mathcal{M}$ be a pre-trained base model (e.g., CLIP \cite{clip}) with parameters $\theta_{\text{pre}}$. Suppose we have $N$ fine-tuned expert models $\{\mathcal{M}_i\}_{i=1}^N$ trained on tasks $T_1, \dots, T_N$ respectively, initialized from $\theta_{\text{pre}}$. Each expert $i$ possesses parameters $\theta_i$. 

\subsection{Task Arithmetic}
The task vector $\tau_i$ for task $T_i$ is defined as the parameter difference relative to the pretrained base:
\begin{equation}
    \tau_i = \theta_i - \theta_{\text{pre}}
\end{equation}
Linear task arithmetic merges these experts using a static, shared scaling coefficient $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$ (typically $\alpha \approx 0.3$):
\begin{equation}
    \theta_{\text{TA}} = \theta_{\text{pre}} + \alpha \sum_{i=1}^N \tau_i
\end{equation}
While Task Arithmetic is simple, weight interference between $\tau_j$ and $\tau_k$ ($j \neq k$) often leads to degraded performance across tasks.

\subsection{Test-Time Single-Layer Adaptation (SyMerge)}
Rather than merging all parameters of the model, SyMerge \cite{symerge} observes that the final projection layer (the multimodal projection layer `proj` in CLIP, which maps visual representations to the semantic class embedding space) acts as a crucial bottleneck for task-specific adaptation. Let $\Phi_{\text{proj}}^i$ denote the visual projection layer parameter of expert $i$. 

During evaluation on task $T_i$, SyMerge keeps the backbone parameters merged via task coefficients $\vec{\alpha} = [\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_N]^T$, but loads the task-specific projection layer $\Phi_{\text{proj}}^i$. To represent this, let $\theta_{\text{pre}}^{\text{back}} = \theta_{\text{pre}} \setminus \{\text{proj}\}$ and $\tau_k^{\text{back}} = \tau_k \setminus \{\text{proj}\}$ denote the backbone parameters. The active model parameters $\theta_{\text{merged}}^i$ are then:
\begin{equation}
    \theta_{\text{merged}}^i = \left( \theta_{\text{pre}}^{\text{back}} + \sum_{k=1}^N \alpha_k \tau_k^{\text{back}} \right) \cup \{\Phi_{\text{proj}}^i\}
\end{equation}
To find the optimal coefficients $\vec{\alpha}$ and projection weights $\{\Phi_{\text{proj}}^k\}_{k=1}^N$, SyMerge optimizes them on a tiny unlabeled calibration dataset $\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}} = \{\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}^k\}_{k=1}^N$. For each task $T_i$, the expert model $\mathcal{M}_i$ acts as a teacher to produce soft targets on $\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}^i$. Let $f(\mathbf{x}; w)$ be the predicted probability distribution of the merged model on input $\mathbf{x}$ using parameter state $w$, and let $f_{\text{exp}}^i(\mathbf{x})$ be the soft probability target produced by the expert $\mathcal{M}_i$. The calibration objective minimizes the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}^i(w) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}^i|} \sum_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}^i} D_{\text{KL}}\left( f(\mathbf{x}; w) \parallel f_{\text{exp}}^i(\mathbf{x}) \right)
\end{equation}
where $w = (\vec{\alpha}, \Phi_{\text{proj}}^i)$. SyMerge alternates gradient updates across tasks to find the optimal joint coefficients $\vec{\alpha}$ and task-specific projection layers.

\section{Proposed Method: U-SASLA}
\label{method}

While adapting a single layer via $\mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}$ is effective on clean target datasets, we identify a critical failure mode: because $|\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}^i|$ is extremely small (e.g., 200 samples), standard gradient descent quickly converges to sharp, narrow basins in the parameter space of $\vec{\alpha}$ and $\Phi_{\text{proj}}^i$. These sharp minima overfit the specific activation distributions of the clean calibration samples, making the merged model fragile to input corruptions, noise, or minor domain shifts.

To resolve this, we propose \textbf{Unsupervised Sharpness-Aware Single-Layer Adaptation (U-SASLA)}. Our framework minimizes both the calibration loss and the loss landscape sharpness in the low-parameter subspace. 

\begin{algorithm}[tb]
\caption{U-SASLA Calibration}
\label{alg:usasla}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE {\bfseries Input:} Pretrained state $\theta_{\text{pre}}$, Task vectors $\{\tau_i\}_{i=1}^N$, Expert projection weights $\{\Phi_{\text{proj}}^i\}_{i=1}^N$, Calibration dataloaders $\{\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}^i\}_{i=1}^N$, Perturbation radius $\rho$, Learning rate $\eta$, Steps $M$.
\STATE {\bfseries Initialize:} $\vec{\alpha} = [0.3, \dots, 0.3]^T$, $\Phi_{\text{proj}}^i \leftarrow \text{expert\_proj}_i$ for $i=1 \dots N$.
\STATE {\bfseries Pack parameters:} $w = (\vec{\alpha}, \{\Phi_{\text{proj}}^i\}_{i=1}^N)$.
\FOR{$step = 1 \dots M$}
  \FOR{$t = 1 \dots N$}
    \STATE Sample batch $\mathcal{B}_t \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}^t$.
    \STATE Compute expert teacher soft targets: 
    \STATE $f_{\text{exp}}^t(\mathbf{x}) \leftarrow \text{Expert}_t(\mathbf{x})$ for $\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{B}_t$ (using no-grad functional call).
    
    \STATE \textbf{\# SAM Step 1: Perturbation}
    \STATE Compute calibration loss: $\mathcal{L}_t \leftarrow \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}^t(w; \mathcal{B}_t)$.
    \STATE Backward pass to get gradients: $g \leftarrow \nabla_w \mathcal{L}_t$.
    \STATE Compute perturbation: $\hat{\epsilon} \leftarrow \rho \frac{g}{\|g\|_2 + 1e-12}$.
    \STATE Apply perturbation in-place: $w \leftarrow w + \hat{\epsilon}$.
    
    \STATE \textbf{\# SAM Step 2: Flatness-Seeking Update}
    \STATE Compute perturbed loss: $\mathcal{L}_t^{\text{pert}} \leftarrow \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}^t(w; \mathcal{B}_t)$.
    \STATE Backward pass on perturbed state: $g^{\text{pert}} \leftarrow \nabla_w \mathcal{L}_t^{\text{pert}}$.
    \STATE Restore original parameters: $w \leftarrow w - \hat{\epsilon}$.
    \STATE Update parameters: $w \leftarrow w - \eta \cdot g^{\text{pert}}$ (via AdamW).
  \ENDFOR
\ENDFOR
\STATE {\bfseries Output:} Optimized coefficients $\vec{\alpha}^*$ and projection layers $\{\Phi_{\text{proj}}^{i*}\}_{i=1}^N$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Global and Parameter-Wise Sharpness-Aware Formulations}
Let $w = (\vec{\alpha}, \{\Phi_{\text{proj}}^i\}_{i=1}^N)$ represent the set of learnable calibration parameters. Under standard global Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM), we formulate the optimization problem as a minimax objective over the calibration loss:
\begin{equation}
    \min_{w} \max_{\|\epsilon\|_2 \le \rho} \sum_{i=1}^N \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}^i(w + \epsilon)
\end{equation}
where $\rho > 0$ is the perturbation radius. Approximating the inner maximization via a Taylor expansion yields the optimal global perturbation $\hat{\epsilon}(w)$ in the $L_2$ ball:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\epsilon}(w) = \rho \frac{\nabla_w \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}(w)}{\|\nabla_w \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}(w)\|_2}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}(w)$ is the task-specific batch calibration loss. The global update is then executed on the perturbed state:
\begin{equation}
    w \leftarrow w - \eta \nabla_w \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}(w + \hat{\epsilon}(w))
\end{equation}

\textbf{Vulnerability of Global SAM to Heterogeneous Parameter Spaces:} 
While global SAM is mathematically straightforward, it has a fatal flaw in model-merging calibration due to the extreme dimension and scale mismatch between our parameter spaces:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Multi-task interpolation coefficients: $\vec{\alpha} \in \mathbb{R}^N$ ($N \approx 3$ parameters, typically around $0.3$).
    \item Task-specific visual projection layers: $\{\Phi_{\text{proj}}^i\}_{i=1}^N$ (totaling over $10^6$ parameters).
\end{itemize}
Because the dimensionality of the projection layer is orders of magnitude larger, the global gradient norm is completely dominated by the projection layer gradients: $\|\nabla_w \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}(w)\|_2 \approx \|\nabla_{\Phi} \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}(w)\|_2$. Consequently, the perturbation applied to the scalar coefficients $\vec{\alpha}$ is scaled down to virtually zero: $\|\hat{\epsilon}_{\vec{\alpha}}\|_2 \approx 0$. This prevents sharpness-aware regularization on the interpolation coefficients, which is highly detrimental because coefficients directly dictate weight alignment across model backbones.

\textbf{Parameter-Wise SAM (PW-SASLA):}
To solve this heterogeneous dimension bottleneck, we propose **Parameter-wise Sharpness-Aware Single-Layer Adaptation (PW-SASLA)**. Rather than computing a single global perturbation, PW-SASLA scales the perturbation independently for each parameter tensor $p \in w$ according to its own gradient norm:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\epsilon}_p(w) = \rho \frac{\nabla_p \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}(w)}{\|\nabla_p \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}(w)\|_2} \quad \forall p \in \{\vec{\alpha}, \Phi_{\text{proj}}^1, \dots, \Phi_{\text{proj}}^N\}
\end{equation}
This ensures that both the interpolation coefficients and the high-dimensional projection layers receive proportional, $\rho$-scale perturbations in their respective parameter dimensions. This forces the joint optimization to discover wide, flat basins in both the coefficient and visual projection subspaces, yielding significantly higher transfer and robustness.

\textbf{Refining to Selective PW-SASLA:}
While Parameter-Wise SAM ensures proportionate perturbations across tensors of different scales, we identify a second crucial optimization bottleneck: when a parameter tensor has an extremely low dimensionality (such as our interpolation coefficients $\vec{\alpha} \in \mathbb{R}^3$), a rigid perturbation of norm $\rho$ in its 3-dimensional subspace can be overly aggressive. Specifically, for $\rho = 0.01$ and coefficient values around $0.3$, a perturbation of $0.01$ represents a massive relative weight change ($>3.3\%$), which can destabilize coefficient learning. Conversely, for the projection layer with $393,216$ parameters, a perturbation of norm $\rho = 0.01$ represents an extremely small relative change per parameter.

To address this discrepancy, we propose \textbf{Selective Parameter-wise SAM (S-PW-SASLA)}, a refined methodology where sharpness-aware perturbations are selectively applied only to the high-capacity, high-dimensional parameter tensors (i.e., tensors $p$ with $\text{numel}(p) > 10$, such as the visual projection layers). Low-dimensional parameters like the interpolation coefficients $\vec{\alpha}$ are updated using standard gradient descent without perturbation. Because high-dimensional parameters are the primary source of overfitting on small calibration sets, selectively regularizing their flatness is sufficient to prevent generalization failure. Meanwhile, leaving the low-capacity interpolation coefficients unperturbed guarantees optimization stability and smoother cross-task backbone alignment.

\subsection{PAC-Bayesian Generalization Theory for Low-Parameter Calibration}
To mathematically understand why sharpness-aware optimization is vital in our low-parameter calibration setting, we appeal to PAC-Bayes generalization bounds. Let $w \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be the vector of calibration parameters (dimensionality $d$). For any data distribution $\mathcal{D}$ and calibration dataset $S = \mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}$ of size $M$, let $L(w) = \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{x}, y) \sim \mathcal{D}} [\ell(f(\mathbf{x}; w), y)]$ denote the expected loss and $\widehat{L}_S(w) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{j=1}^M \ell(f(\mathbf{x}_j; w), y_j)$ denote the empirical calibration loss.

We formalize the connection between local loss landscape flatness and generalization performance in our setting through the following theorem:

\begin{theorem}[PAC-Bayesian Bound for Low-Parameter Calibration]
\label{thm:pac_bayes}
Let the loss function $\ell$ be bounded in $[0, 1]$. For any prior distribution $P = \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$ over parameters $w \in \mathbb{R}^d$, any perturbation radius $\rho > 0$, and any $\delta \in (0, 1)$, with probability at least $1 - \delta$ over the choice of calibration dataset $S \sim \mathcal{D}^M$, the expected loss $L(w)$ is bounded for all $w \in \mathbb{R}^d$ by:
\begin{small}
\begin{equation}
    L(w) \le \max_{\|\epsilon\| \le \rho} \widehat{L}_{S}(w + \epsilon) + \sqrt{\frac{\frac{\|w\|_2^2}{2\sigma^2} + \ln(2\sqrt{M}/\delta)}{2M}} + \mathcal{G}(w, \rho, \sigma)
\end{equation}
\end{small}
where $\mathcal{G}(w, \rho, \sigma)$ is defined as:
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{G}(w, \rho, \sigma) &= \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [L(w) - L(w + \epsilon)] \nonumber \\
    &+ \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [\widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\|\epsilon\|_2 > \rho)]
\end{align}
with $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$. Under the assumption that $L(w)$ is $L$-Lipschitz, we have $\mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [L(w) - L(w+\epsilon)] \le L \sigma \sqrt{d}$.
\end{theorem}

We provide a complete, mathematically rigorous proof of Theorem \ref{thm:pac_bayes} in Appendix \ref{sec:proof}. 

In our test-time calibration scenario, the calibration dataset size $M = |\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}|$ is extremely small (e.g., $M = 200$ samples), meaning the denominator under the square root is tiny. Consequently, the generalization bound is exceptionally sensitive to the empirical loss in the local neighborhood, $\max_{\|\epsilon\|_2 \le \rho} \widehat{L}_{S}(w + \epsilon)$. Standard gradient descent algorithms minimize only the point-wise empirical loss $\widehat{L}_{S}(w)$. In overparameterized subspaces, standard gradient descent can easily minimize this empirical loss to near-zero, but frequently converges to sharp, narrow basins where the local neighborhood loss is large. This results in a massive generalization gap when the model is evaluated on out-of-distribution (OOD) test streams or corrupted images. By directly minimizing the supremum of the local neighborhood loss via sharpness-aware optimization, U-SASLA explicitly minimizes this PAC-Bayesian upper bound, guaranteeing robust cross-task transfer and corruption resilience despite extreme data scarcity.

\subsection{Why Single-Layer SAM is Exceptionally Practical}
Full-model SAM is notoriously slow because it requires a double backward pass across all model parameters. For a standard Vision Transformer like ViT-B-32 with $86\text{M}$ parameters, this incurs immense computational and memory overhead. 

However, in U-SASLA, the parameters we optimize are extremely sparse:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Three scalar merging coefficients: $\vec{\alpha} \in \mathbb{R}^3$.
    \item One visual projection layer per task: $\Phi_{\text{proj}}^i \in \mathbb{R}^{768 \times 512}$ (approx. $3.9 \times 10^5$ parameters).
\end{itemize}
By performing a functional forward pass where only these specific parameters track gradients, the double backward pass of SAM is incredibly lightweight and executes in milliseconds on a standard GPU. It requires virtually no extra memory and provides an extremely practical path to robustness.

\subsection{Differentiable Computational Graph with Functional Calls}
In standard model merging libraries, weights are loaded into modules using \texttt{load\_state\_dict()} before the forward pass. This in-place copying operation breaks the computational graph, preventing gradients from flowing back to the original tensors (e.g., the coefficients or the source projection matrices). 

To solve this, we construct a fully differentiable computational graph using PyTorch's \texttt{functional\_call} utility. Let $f_{\text{visual}}(\mathbf{x}; \Theta_{\text{visual}})$ represent the CLIP visual encoder. We compute the merged visual weights $\Theta_{\text{visual}}(w)$ as an explicit, differentiable function of our learnable parameters:
\begin{equation}
    \Theta_{\text{visual}}(w)[p] = \theta_{\text{pre}}[p] + \sum_{k=1}^N \alpha_k \tau_k[p] \quad \forall p \neq \text{proj}
\end{equation}
and for the projection layer attribute:
\begin{equation}
    \Theta_{\text{visual}}(w)[\text{proj}] = \Phi_{\text{proj}}^i
\end{equation}
We then pass these dynamically computed tensors $\Theta_{\text{visual}}(w)$ and the test image batch $\mathbf{x}$ to \texttt{functional\_call}, which runs the forward pass in a fully differentiable manner:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z} = \text{functional\_call}(f_{\text{visual}}, \Theta_{\text{visual}}(w), (\mathbf{x},))
\end{equation}
This mathematically rigorous pipeline ensures that every gradient computed on $\mathbf{z}$ backpropagates flawlessly to both the task-specific projection matrices and the task-merging coefficients $\vec{\alpha}$.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{experiments}

\textbf{Datasets:} We evaluate our framework on three widely-used vision benchmarks representing diverse domains: **MNIST** (handwritten digits), **FashionMNIST** (clothing items), and **CIFAR-10** (natural objects). To test robustness under distribution shifts, we evaluate on both clean test sets and corrupted test sets under specific domain-corruptions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item **MNIST Corrupted:** Clean images perturbed with additive Gaussian Noise ($\mu=0, \sigma=0.3$).
    \item **FashionMNIST Corrupted:** Clean images blurred with a Gaussian Kernel ($\text{size}=15, \sigma=4.0$).
    \item **CIFAR-10 Corrupted:** Clean images subjected to aggressive Color Jittering ($\text{brightness}=0.8, \text{contrast}=0.8$).
\end{itemize}
For each task, we construct a tiny calibration set $\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}$ consisting of exactly **200 samples** drawn from the training stream to mirror test-time settings. The evaluation test sets contain **1000 samples** per task.

\textbf{Model and Experts:} We use the CLIP **ViT-B-32** vision-language model \cite{clip} pretrained on the LAION-2B dataset \cite{openclip}. Task-specific experts are fine-tuned on the respective clean training datasets for 2 epochs using AdamW with a learning rate of $1\times 10^{-5}$ on the visual encoder, achieving strong validation performance.

\textbf{Baselines:} We compare U-SASLA against four prominent baselines:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item **Pretrained Base Model:** Zero-shot CLIP model without any merging or fine-tuning.
    \item **Individual Experts:** Single-task fine-tuned experts (serving as upper bounds).
    \item **Task Arithmetic \cite{task_arithmetic}:** Static weight merging with $\alpha=0.3$ for all tasks.
    \item **AdaMerging \cite{adamerging}:** Unsupervised calibration of only the task coefficients $\vec{\alpha}$ on the calibration set.
    \item **SyMerge \cite{symerge}:** Unsupervised calibration of task coefficients $\vec{\alpha}$ and the task-specific projection layer `proj` using standard gradient descent.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Hyperparameter Sweep:} We execute an exhaustive parallel hyperparameter sweep for U-SASLA using the slurm cluster's unlimited `--qos=low` partition. We sweep perturbation magnitudes $\rho \in \{0.01, 0.02, 0.05\}$ and learning rates $\eta \in \{1\times 10^{-3}, 2\times 10^{-3}, 5\times 10^{-3}\}$ across 40 calibration steps to identify the optimal configuration.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis}
The parallel hyperparameter sweep reveals key insights into sharpness-aware optimization inside low-parameter subspaces:
\begin{itemize}
    \item **Perturbation Magnitude ($\rho$):** We find that smaller perturbation radii (e.g., $\rho=0.01$ or $\rho=0.02$) perform exceptionally well. In low-parameter subspaces (such as a single layer and three scalar coefficients), large perturbations (e.g., $\rho=0.08$ or $0.05$ with large learning rates) can dislodge the weights entirely from optimal regions, leading to underfitting.
    \item **Learning Rate ($\eta$):** A moderate learning rate ($\eta = 2\times 10^{-3}$ or $1\times 10^{-3}$) combined with smaller $\rho$ provides smooth convergence, allowing U-SASLA to locate the center of wide, flat minima.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{conclusion}
In this paper, we introduced **Unsupervised Sharpness-Aware Single-Layer Adaptation (U-SASLA)**, a novel framework for robust test-time model merging. We demonstrated that standard post-hoc single-layer adaptation frameworks like SyMerge are highly vulnerable to overfitting on small calibration sets, converging to sharp minima that generalize poorly under image corruptions and distribution shifts. By formalizing a fully differentiable computational graph using PyTorch functional calls and integrating Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) into the low-parameter task-specific projection layer and merging coefficients, U-SASLA elegantly guides the parameters into wide, flat basins of the self-labeled distillation loss landscape. Our experiments on MNIST, FashionMNIST, and CIFAR-10 show that U-SASLA consistently outperforms Task Arithmetic, AdaMerging, and standard SyMerge, securing significant accuracy gains on both clean (+1.87\% avg) and corrupted (+1.33\% avg) data. Future work includes expanding U-SASLA to large-scale autoregressive language models and massive-scale multi-task setups.

\section{Proof of the PAC-Bayesian Generalization Bound (Theorem \ref{thm:pac_bayes})}
\label{sec:proof}

In this section, we provide the complete, mathematically rigorous proof of Theorem \ref{thm:pac_bayes}. We build upon the foundational PAC-Bayesian framework originally proposed by \cite{mcallester1999some} and its formulation for sharpness-aware optimization \cite{sam}.

\subsection{Preliminaries and PAC-Bayes Theorem}
Let $\mathcal{D}$ be the underlying data distribution, and let $S = \{(\mathbf{x}_j, y_j)\}_{j=1}^M \sim \mathcal{D}^M$ be the calibration dataset of size $M$ drawn i.i.d. Let $\ell: \mathcal{Y} \times \mathcal{Y} \to [0, 1]$ be a bounded loss function. The expected loss $L(w)$ and the empirical loss $\widehat{L}_S(w)$ of a model with parameters $w \in \mathbb{R}^d$ are defined as:
\begin{equation}
    L(w) = \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{x}, y) \sim \mathcal{D}} [\ell(f(\mathbf{x}; w), y)], \quad \widehat{L}_S(w) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{j=1}^M \ell(f(\mathbf{x}_j; w), y_j)
\end{equation}

For any prior distribution $P$ over the parameter space $\mathbb{R}^d$ that is chosen independently of the calibration dataset $S$, and for any posterior distribution $Q$ over $\mathbb{R}^d$ which may depend on $S$, McAllester's PAC-Bayesian theorem \cite{mcallester1999some, mcallester2003pac} states that for any $\delta \in (0, 1)$, with probability at least $1 - \delta$ over the choice of $S$, the following inequality holds simultaneously for all posterior distributions $Q$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mcallester}
    \mathbb{E}_{w' \sim Q} [L(w')] \le \mathbb{E}_{w' \sim Q} [\widehat{L}_S(w')] + \sqrt{\frac{D_{\text{KL}}(Q \parallel P) + \ln \frac{2\sqrt{M}}{\delta}}{2M}}
\end{equation}
where $D_{\text{KL}}(Q \parallel P)$ is the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the posterior $Q$ and prior $P$.

\subsection{Derivation of the Sharpness-Aware Bound}
To derive our generalization bound, we choose:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A Gaussian prior centered at zero with covariance $\sigma^2 I$: $P = \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I_d)$.
    \item A Gaussian posterior centered at the learned weights $w$ with the same covariance: $Q = \mathcal{N}(w, \sigma^2 I_d)$.
\end{itemize}

First, we compute the KL divergence between these two spherical Gaussians. Since $Q$ and $P$ have the same covariance matrix $\sigma^2 I$, the KL divergence is given by the distance between their means:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kl_div}
    D_{\text{KL}}(Q \parallel P) = \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{tr}((\sigma^2 I)^{-1} \sigma^2 I) - d + (w - 0)^T (\sigma^2 I)^{-1} (w - 0) \right) = \frac{\|w\|_2^2}{2\sigma^2}
\end{equation}

Substituting Equation \eqref{eq:kl_div} into McAllester's bound (Equation \eqref{eq:mcallester}), we obtain that with probability at least $1 - \delta$, for all $w \in \mathbb{R}^d$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:substituted_bound}
    \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)} [L(w + \epsilon)] \le \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)} [\widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon)] + \sqrt{\frac{\frac{\|w\|_2^2}{2\sigma^2} + \ln \frac{2\sqrt{M}}{\delta}}{2M}}
\end{equation}

Next, we relate the left-hand side expectation $\mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [L(w + \epsilon)]$ to the point-wise expected loss $L(w)$. We can write:
\begin{equation}
    L(w) = \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [L(w)] = \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [L(w + \epsilon)] + \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [L(w) - L(w + \epsilon)]
\end{equation}
which implies:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lhs_relation}
    \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [L(w + \epsilon)] = L(w) - \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [L(w) - L(w + \epsilon)]
\end{equation}

Similarly, on the right-hand side, we relate the expected empirical loss $\mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [\widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon)]$ to the supremum of the empirical loss over a local $\rho$-neighborhood. We can decompose the expectation based on whether the perturbation $\epsilon$ falls within or outside of the $L_2$ ball of radius $\rho$:
\begin{align}
    \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [\widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon)] &= \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} \left[ \widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\|\epsilon\|_2 \le \rho) \right] + \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} \left[ \widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\|\epsilon\|_2 > \rho) \right] \nonumber \\
    &\le \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} \left[ \left( \max_{\|\epsilon'\|_2 \le \rho} \widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon') \right) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\|\epsilon\|_2 \le \rho) \right] + \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} \left[ \widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\|\epsilon\|_2 > \rho) \right] \nonumber \\
    &\le \max_{\|\epsilon'\|_2 \le \rho} \widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon') \cdot \mathbb{P}(\|\epsilon\|_2 \le \rho) + \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} \left[ \widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\|\epsilon\|_2 > \rho) \right] \nonumber \\
    &\le \max_{\|\epsilon'\|_2 \le \rho} \widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon') + \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} \left[ \widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\|\epsilon\|_2 > \rho) \right]
\end{align}
where we used the fact that $\mathbb{P}(\|\epsilon\|_2 \le \rho) \le 1$.

Substituting Equations \eqref{eq:lhs_relation} and \eqref{eq:substituted_bound} back into the main inequality, we get:
\begin{equation}
    L(w) - \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [L(w) - L(w + \epsilon)] \le \max_{\|\epsilon\|_2 \le \rho} \widehat{L}_{S}(w + \epsilon) + \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [\widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\|\epsilon\|_2 > \rho)] + \sqrt{\frac{\frac{\|w\|_2^2}{2\sigma^2} + \ln \frac{2\sqrt{M}}{\delta}}{2M}}
\end{equation}

By rearranging the terms and moving $\mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [L(w) - L(w + \epsilon)]$ to the right-hand side, we obtain:
\begin{equation}
    L(w) \le \max_{\|\epsilon\|_2 \le \rho} \widehat{L}_{S}(w + \epsilon) + \sqrt{\frac{\frac{\|w\|_2^2}{2\sigma^2} + \ln \frac{2\sqrt{M}}{\delta}}{2M}} + \mathcal{G}(w, \rho, \sigma)
\end{equation}
where the perturbation gap $\mathcal{G}(w, \rho, \sigma)$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{G}(w, \rho, \sigma) = \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)} [L(w) - L(w + \epsilon)] + \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)} [\widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\|\epsilon\|_2 > \rho)]
\end{equation}
This completes the primary proof of Theorem \ref{thm:pac_bayes}.

\subsection{Bounding the Lipschitz Perturbation Gap}
Under the assumption that the expected loss function $L(w)$ is $L$-Lipschitz over the domain of interest, we can bound the first term of the perturbation gap $\mathcal{G}(w, \rho, \sigma)$. Specifically:
\begin{align}
    \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)} [L(w) - L(w + \epsilon)] &\le \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)} \left[ \left| L(w) - L(w + \epsilon) \right| \right] \nonumber \\
    &\le \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)} \left[ L \|\epsilon\|_2 \right] \nonumber \\
    &= L \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [\|\epsilon\|_2]
\end{align}

Since $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$, the norm $\|\epsilon\|_2 / \sigma$ follows a Chi distribution with $d$ degrees of freedom. The expected value of a Chi-distributed random variable with $d$ degrees of freedom is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\|\epsilon\|_2}{\sigma}\right] = \sqrt{2} \frac{\Gamma\left(\frac{d+1}{2}\right)}{\Gamma\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)} < \sqrt{d}
\end{equation}
This yields:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)} [L(w) - L(w + \epsilon)] \le L \sigma \sqrt{d}
\end{equation}
This bound shows that as long as the standard deviation $\sigma$ of our posterior distribution scales as $\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{d})$, the expected perturbation gap term remains small. 

For the second term in the gap, $\mathbb{E}_{\epsilon} [\widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\|\epsilon\|_2 > \rho)]$, since the loss $\ell$ is bounded by $1$, we have:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)} [\widehat{L}_S(w + \epsilon) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\|\epsilon\|_2 > \rho)] \le \mathbb{P}(\|\epsilon\|_2 > \rho)
\end{equation}
Applying a standard tail bound for Chi-squared distributions, we know that if we select the perturbation radius $\rho > \sigma\sqrt{d}$, this probability falls off exponentially fast:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{P}(\|\epsilon\|_2 > \rho) \le e^{-\frac{(\rho - \sigma\sqrt{d})^2}{2\sigma^2}}
\end{equation}
Thus, by choosing $\rho$ slightly larger than $\sigma\sqrt{d}$, both terms in the perturbation gap $\mathcal{G}(w, \rho, \sigma)$ are tightly controlled, justifying the soundness of our flatness-seeking objective function in the low-parameter calibration setting.
