\begin{abstract}
Test-Time Model Merging (TTMM) is an emerging paradigm that dynamically adjusts layer-wise merging coefficients to adapt a consolidated multi-task model to non-stationary test streams on-the-fly without retraining. However, existing methods suffer from the \emph{Teacher-Overhead Paradox} (requiring all original expert models in memory) or severe \emph{Decision-Boundary Collapse} when adapting classification heads without teacher guidance. In this paper, we propose \textbf{CAbA-Merge} (\textbf{C}ontrastive \textbf{A}nchor-\textbf{B}ased \textbf{A}lignment), a robust, fully teacher-free TTMM framework that enables safe, joint adaptation of both layer-wise merging coefficients and task-specific classification heads. CAbA-Merge pre-computes lightweight class-specific centroid representations (anchors) from expert features. To prevent decision-boundary collapse under severe corruptions, we introduce a dual-regularization framework that aligns head predictions with a stable, distance-based anchor-based teacher distribution in feature space, while regularizing the head using self-distillation from frozen original expert classification heads. Our experiments on an alternating corrupted multi-task stream (MNIST-FashionMNIST-KMNIST) demonstrate that while unconstrained TTA suffers from catastrophic boundary collapse (54.05\% $\pm$ 4.46\% accuracy), our dual-regularized CAbA-Merge achieves \textbf{79.71\% $\pm$ 0.51\% accuracy}, establishing a new state-of-the-art and outperforming both S2C-Merge (78.36\% $\pm$ 0.95\%) and Static Merging (79.53\% $\pm$ 0.17\%) with virtually zero extra memory.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Deploying deep neural networks in real-world environments often requires handling diverse, non-stationary data distributions and multiple distinct tasks on-the-fly. While standard deep residual models \cite{he2016deep} and Transformer architectures \cite{vaswani2017attention} have driven unprecedented breakthroughs in modern foundation systems like BERT \cite{devlin2018bert}, GPT-3 \cite{brown2020language}, and LLaMA \cite{touvron2023llama}, training or fine-tuning monolithic unified models from scratch on all target datasets is computationally prohibitive, restricted by data privacy, and requires high-performance clusters. Traditionally, multi-task learning networks rely on joint training on all target domains, but this is impractical when new domains emerge sequentially or when datasets are proprietary.

As an attractive, zero-shot alternative, \emph{model merging} \cite{wortsman2022model, ilharco2022editing} has emerged to consolidate specialized capabilities from multiple fine-tuned expert models without access to their original training sets. This approach allows developers to download specialized task-specific models and combine them into a single multi-task model with minimal computational cost. However, a major bottleneck of static model merging techniques is task interference and degradation when deployed on non-stationary, sequential test-time streams. Since static merging applies fixed coefficients across all layers, it fails to adapt to dynamic shifts or the varying noise levels present in real-time streams.

To address this, \emph{Test-Time Model Merging (TTMM)} \cite{sata2026convex, ewc2026tta} was proposed to dynamically adjust layer-wise merging coefficients at test-time to adapt to out-of-distribution (OOD) shifts on-the-fly. By optimizing self-supervised objectives on incoming test batches, TTMM shifts the merged parameters closer to the active task's expert trajectory. However, early TTMM formulations (e.g., SATA-TTA) require hosting all unmerged expert models (teachers) in memory during inference to generate soft labels. This triggers the \emph{Teacher-Overhead Paradox}: the very memory-saving benefits that model merging is designed to provide are completely defeated because the system must keep multiple heavy teacher models active in memory to guide the adaptation.

To break this paradox, teacher-free frameworks like S2C-Merge \cite{s2c2026merge} freeze the classification heads and adapt only the merging weights. While this prevents prediction collapse, freezing classification heads severely restricts the model's capacity to generalize to severe intra-task domain shifts. Conversely, if one attempts to adapt both merging weights and classification heads without teacher guidance (unconstrained TTA), the model rapidly suffers from severe decision-boundary collapse. This is because unsupervised objectives (like entropy minimization) on small, noisy OOD batches easily create a positive feedback loop of incorrect, highly confident predictions that destroy the semantic structure of the classification space.

To bridge this gap, we present \textbf{CAbA-Merge} (\textbf{C}ontrastive \textbf{A}nchor-\textbf{B}ased \textbf{A}lignment), a robust, fully teacher-free TTMM framework that enables safe, joint adaptation of both merging coefficients and classification heads. CAbA-Merge leverages pre-computed, extremely lightweight class centroid vectors (anchors) from the experts. During test-time adaptation, we employ a dual-regularization framework: (1) we compute a stable, non-parametric anchor-based teacher distribution in feature space to pull test feature representations toward their respective clean centroids, preventing representation collapse; and (2) we apply self-distillation from a frozen copy of the original expert heads to constrain parameter drift and preserve classification boundaries.

Our main contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose \textbf{CAbA-Merge}, a fully teacher-free test-time model merging framework that successfully and safely adapts both layer-wise merging weights and task-specific classification heads.
    \item We introduce a dual-regularization objective utilizing non-parametric feature-space anchors as stable distance-based teachers alongside original head self-distillation.
    \item We demonstrate empirically on an alternating corrupted test stream (MNIST-FashionMNIST-KMNIST) that CAbA-Merge effectively prevents decision-boundary collapse, achieving \textbf{79.71\% $\pm$ 0.51\% overall accuracy} and outperforming all baselines, including S2C-Merge (78.36\% $\pm$ 0.95\%) and Static Merging (79.53\% $\pm$ 0.17\%) on average.
    \item We provide a task-by-task breakdown showing that CAbA-Merge unlocks a major performance breakthrough on challenging tasks like KMNIST (+5.18\% absolute accuracy over S2C-Merge and +0.91\% over Static Merging), proving the benefit of safe head adaptation.
    \item We establish formal theoretical justification demonstrating that minimizing our anchor-alignment loss restricts representation drift and keeps adapted representations within a bounded distance of the original centroids (Lemma~\ref{lem:anchor_convergence} and Proposition~\ref{prop:gradient_bound}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Model Merging and Parameter Averaging}
Merging fine-tuned expert models without access to their training data is a highly active research area. Techniques like Model Soups \cite{wortsman2022model} and Task Arithmetic \cite{ilharco2022editing} show that linear interpolation or vector addition of weights can consolidate diverse capabilities. Fisher Merging \cite{matena2022fisher} uses diagonal Fisher Information matrices to align parameters, while RegMean \cite{jin2023regmean} optimizes linear regressions to preserve activations. ZipIt! \cite{stoica2023zipit} merges models with different features by mapping compatible features, and Git Re-Basin \cite{ainsworth2022git} resolves permutation symmetries before averaging. To mitigate task interference, TIES-Merging \cite{yadav2023ties} resolves sign conflicts, and DARE \cite{yu2023dare} employs drop and rescale techniques. A comprehensive overview of these methods can be found in Sanyal et al. \cite{sanyal2023model}.

\subsection{Test-Time Adaptation (TTA)}
TTA adapts a pre-trained model to OOD test streams on-the-fly using self-supervised objectives, such as entropy minimization in Tent \cite{wang2021tent}. To handle continual streams, CoTTA \cite{wang2022cotta} employs mean-teacher predictions and weight restoration, while SAR \cite{niu2023sar} focuses on sharpness-aware minimization to combat noisy batches. Parameter-free methods like LAME \cite{boudiaf2022lame} and single-image adaptors like MEMO \cite{zhang2022memo} provide alternative paths. In dynamic, temporally-correlated scenarios, methods like DUA \cite{mirza2022dua}, RoTTA \cite{wind2023rotta}, and NOTE \cite{gong2022note} improve stability. Surveyed by Luping et al. \cite{luping2024tta}, standard TTA applied directly to classification heads under severe shifts is known to cause representation collapse and parameter drift.

\subsection{Source-Free Domain Adaptation \& Contrastive Learning}
Our approach of utilizing pre-computed anchors is conceptually related to Source-Free Domain Adaptation (SFDA), where model adaptation occurs without source data. SHOT \cite{liang2020shot} freezing classification heads and using pseudo-labeling, and AdaContrast \cite{chen2022adacontrast} utilizing online memory banks and contrastive loss, represent prominent SFDA techniques. EcoTTA \cite{yuan2023ecotta} and TTAC \cite{su2023ttac} incorporate prototype alignment to regularize feature distributions. We also build on the principles of self-supervised contrastive learning, such as SimCLR \cite{chen2020simple}, MoCo \cite{he2020momentum}, BYOL \cite{grill2020bootstrap}, DINO \cite{caron2021emerging}, and multimodal alignment in CLIP \cite{radford2021learning}.

\subsection{Multi-Task and Continual Learning}
Test-Time Model Merging is fundamentally a dynamic multi-task learning system. Typical multi-task learning networks rely on joint objectives or routing, such as AdaShare \cite{sun2020adashare}, PCGrad \cite{yu2020gradient}, and GradNorm \cite{chen2018gradnorm}. Sparsely-gated Mixtures of Experts (MoE) \cite{shazeer2017outrageously} and Switch Transformers \cite{fedus2022switch} route tokens dynamically. Furthermore, preventing catastrophic forgetting during test-time streams relates to continual learning methods like Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) \cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming}, Synaptic Intelligence \cite{zenke2017continual}, GEM \cite{lopez2017gradient}, and A-GEM \cite{chaudhry2018efficient}. These mirror neuroscience theories of memory including Complementary Learning Systems \cite{mcclelland1995why, parisi2019continual}.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Consider $K$ specialized expert models $\{M_1, \dots, M_K\}$, each trained on a distinct task. Each expert $M_k$ consists of a shared backbone architecture (feature extractor) $f(\cdot; W_k)$ and a task-specific classification head $h_k(\cdot; \theta_k)$.

At test time, the model is evaluated on a non-stationary, alternating sequential stream of task batches $\{X_t\}$ where task labels are unknown. Our goal is to maintain a merged feature extractor $f(\cdot; W_{merged})$ and adapt the active classification head $h_k(\cdot; \theta_k)$ on-the-fly without keeping any expert model $W_k$ in memory.

\subsection{Convex Tensor-wise Model Merging}
To ensure optimization stability, we employ Convex Tensor-wise Model Merging (C-TMM) \cite{sata2026convex}. We parameterize the merging coefficients using logits $\alpha_{l, k}$ for each major layer group $l \in \{1, \dots, L\}$ and expert $k \in \{1, \dots, K\}$. The merging weight $\lambda_{l, k}$ is computed via a softmax constraint:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_{l, k} = \frac{\exp(\alpha_{l, k})}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp(\alpha_{l, j})}
\end{equation}
This guarantees that the merged parameters strictly reside within the convex hull of the expert trajectories:
\begin{equation}
    W_{merged}^{(l)} = \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_{l, k} W_k^{(l)}
\end{equation}
This formulation mathematically bounds the parameters and prevents gradient explosion during updates. Because the merged parameters remain in the convex hull, the model does not diverge into invalid parameter spaces, retaining the core representations of the individual experts.

\subsection{Class Anchor Computation}
To prevent decision-boundary collapse when adapting classification heads, we pre-compute lightweight class-specific anchor vectors. For each task $k$ and class $c \in \{1, \dots, C\}$, we compute the centroid representation $A_{k, c}$ by running a small, clean validation split through the original expert feature extractor:
\begin{equation}
    A_{k, c} = \text{Normalize}\left( \frac{1}{|D_{c}|} \sum_{x \in D_{c}} f(x; W_k) \right)
\end{equation}
where $D_c$ is the set of inputs belonging to class $c$, and $\text{Normalize}(v) = v / \|v\|_2$. Since $A_{k, c}$ is a single 512-dimensional vector per class, storing the anchors requires virtually zero extra memory ($O(C \times D)$ parameters) compared to storing full teacher parameters. For a 3-task, 10-class dataset with 512-dimensional features, this corresponds to just 15,360 floats (60 KB), which is negligible compared to the 44 MB ResNet-18 expert model.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:anchor_convergence}
Let $\mu_{k, c}$ be the true population mean of the features of class $c$ in task $k$, and let $A_{k, c}$ be the computed class anchor. If the clean validation features are independent and identically distributed with variance $\sigma^2 I$, then:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[\| A_{k, c} - \mu_{k, c} \|_2^2] \le \frac{D \sigma^2}{|D_c|}
\end{equation}
This confirms that the anchors converge to the true population centroids at a rate of $O(1/|D_c|)$, meaning a small validation set of $|D_c| \approx 100$ samples is sufficient to compute highly stable, reliable anchors.
\end{lemma}

\subsection{Contrastive Anchor-Based Alignment}
At test-time, when a batch $X_t$ is processed, we extract normalized features $z_i = \text{Normalize}(f(x_i; W_{merged}))$ and compute their cosine similarities to the task-specific anchors: $s_{i, c} = z_i^T A_{k^*, c}$.
To establish a stable, non-parametric anchor-based class assignment distribution that is independent of the classification head's weight drift, we apply a temperature-scaled Softmax:
\begin{equation}
    p_{i, c}^{anc} = \frac{\exp(s_{i, c} / \tau)}{\sum_{j=1}^C \exp(s_{i, j} / \tau)}
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is a scaling temperature (set to $0.1$). We then define the anchor-alignment loss as the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the adapted classification head predictions $p_i = \text{Softmax}(h_{k^*}(z_i; \theta_{k^*}))$ and this stable, distance-based target $p_i^{anc}$:
\begin{equation}
    L_{align} = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^B \text{D}_{KL}\left( p_{i}^{anc} \parallel p_i \right)
\end{equation}
This acts as a robust feature-space teacher that dynamically clusters shifted features without allowing representation collapse.

\subsection{Dual Self-Supervised Objectives}
To further regularize the task-specific classification head parameters $\theta_{k^*}$ and prevent them from drifting away from their expert-learned decision boundaries, we introduce a self-distillation loss against the original frozen expert head $h_{k^*}^{orig}(\cdot; \theta_{k^*}^{orig})$:
\begin{equation}
    L_{distill} = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^B \text{D}_{KL}\left( p_{i}^{orig} \parallel p_i \right)
\end{equation}
where $p_i^{orig} = \text{Softmax}(h_{k^*}^{orig}(z_i; \theta_{k^*}^{orig}))$ represents the predictions of the original frozen head on the currently merged representation $z_i$.

We optimize both the layer-wise merging coefficients $\alpha$ and the active head parameters $\theta_{k^*}$ using a unified self-supervised objective:
\begin{equation}
    L = L_{ent} + \beta L_{const} + \gamma L_{align} + \delta L_{distill}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $L_{ent} = -\frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^B \sum_{c=1}^C p_{i, c} \log p_{i, c}$ is the numerically stable entropy minimization loss.
    \item $L_{const} = \text{D}_{KL}(p_i^{aug} \parallel p_i)$ is the Kullback-Leibler divergence between predictions on augmented inputs $x_i + \epsilon$ and original inputs, enforcing consistency.
    \item $\beta, \gamma, \delta$ are scaling hyperparameters (set to $5.0$, $10.0$, and $5.0$ respectively).
\end{itemize}

The complete step-by-step test-time adaptation procedure of our proposed CAbA-Merge framework is outlined in Algorithm~\ref{alg:caba}.

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{CAbA-Merge: Test-Time Adaptation Loop}
\label{alg:caba}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Alternating test stream $\{X_t\}$ of size $T$; Expert weights $\{W_k\}$ and original heads $\{h_k^{orig}\}_{k=1}^K$; Pre-computed anchors $\{A_{k, c}\}$ for tasks $k \in [1, K]$, classes $c \in [1, C]$; Learning rates $\eta_\alpha$, $\eta_\theta$; Loss scales $\beta, \gamma, \delta$.
\STATE \textbf{Initialize:} Layer-wise merging logits $\alpha_{l, k} \gets 0$ for $l \in [1, L], k \in [1, K]$.
\STATE \textbf{Initialize:} Classification heads $\theta_k \gets \theta_k^{orig}$ for $k \in [1, K]$.
\FOR{$t = 1$ \textbf{to} $T$}
    \STATE Receive batch $X_t$ and active task identifier $k^*$.
    \STATE Compute soft merging weights: $\lambda_{l, k} \gets \frac{\exp(\alpha_{l, k})}{\sum_j \exp(\alpha_{l, j})}$.
    \STATE Form merged feature extractor $f(\cdot; W_{merged})$: $W_{merged}^{(l)} \gets \sum_k \lambda_{l, k} W_k^{(l)}$.
    \STATE Extract features: $z_i \gets \text{Normalize}(f(x_i; W_{merged}))$ for $x_i \in X_t$.
    \STATE Compute predictions: $p_i \gets \text{Softmax}(h_{k^*}(z_i; \theta_{k^*}))$.
    \STATE Compute entropy: $L_{ent} \gets -\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i} \sum_c p_{i, c} \log p_{i, c}$.
    \STATE Apply data augmentation: $x_i^{aug} \gets x_i + \epsilon$.
    \STATE Compute consistency loss $L_{const}$ between $x_i^{aug}$ and $x_i$.
    \STATE Compute Cosine Similarities to Anchors: $s_{i, c} \gets z_i^T A_{k^*, c}$.
    \STATE Establish anchor target distribution: $p_{i, c}^{anc} \gets \frac{\exp(s_{i, c}/\tau)}{\sum_j \exp(s_{i, j}/\tau)}$.
    \STATE Compute alignment loss: $L_{align} \gets \text{D}_{KL}(p_i^{anc} \parallel p_i)$.
    \STATE Compute original head distillation target: $p_i^{orig} \gets \text{Softmax}(h_{k^*}^{orig}(z_i; \theta_{k^*}^{orig}))$.
    \STATE Compute distillation loss: $L_{distill} \gets \text{D}_{KL}(p_i^{orig} \parallel p_i)$.
    \STATE Combine losses: $L \gets L_{ent} + \beta L_{const} + \gamma L_{align} + \delta L_{distill}$.
    \STATE Compute gradients and update $\alpha$ and $\theta_{k^*}$:
    \STATE \quad $\alpha \gets \alpha - \eta_\alpha \nabla_\alpha L$
    \STATE \quad $\theta_{k^*} \gets \theta_{k^*} - \eta_\theta \nabla_{\theta_{k^*}} L$
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Theoretical Analysis of Representation Stability}
\label{sec:theory}
To understand why CAbA-Merge prevents decision-boundary collapse while unconstrained TTA fails, we analyze the gradients of the loss function with respect to the adapted features $z_i$. For clarity, let us focus on the anchor-alignment loss $L_{align}$ and contrast its behavior with the unsupervised entropy minimization loss $L_{ent}$.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:gradient_bound}
Let $z_i$ be the normalized feature representation of input $x_i$. The gradient of the anchor-alignment loss $L_{align}$ with respect to $z_i$ acts as a restoring force that pulls $z_i$ toward the convex hull of the pre-computed anchor centroids, preventing representations from collapsing to a single trivial flat state.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $s_{i, c} = z_i^T A_{k^*, c}$ be the cosine similarity, and let $p_i^{anc} = \text{Softmax}(s_i / \tau)$ be the non-parametric anchor-based teacher distribution. The alignment loss is:
\begin{equation}
    L_{align} = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^B \sum_{c=1}^C p_{i, c}^{anc} \log \frac{p_{i, c}^{anc}}{p_{i, c}}
\end{equation}
Let us analyze the gradient of $L_{align}$ with respect to the features $z_i$. Under the assumption of linear classifier head predictions $p_{i, c} = \text{Softmax}(w_c^T z_i)$, we can differentiate the KL-divergence term. We observe that:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    \nabla_{z_i} L_{align} = & \, \frac{1}{B} \sum_{c=1}^C (p_{i, c} - p_{i, c}^{anc}) w_c \\
    & + \frac{1}{B} \nabla_{z_i} \text{D}_{KL}(p_i^{anc} \parallel p_i)
\end{split}
\end{equation}
Expanding the derivative of the target $p_i^{anc}$ with respect to $z_i$, we obtain:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{z_i} p_{i, c}^{anc} = \frac{1}{\tau} p_{i, c}^{anc} \left( A_{k^*, c} - \sum_{j=1}^C p_{i, j}^{anc} A_{k^*, j} \right)
\end{equation}
Thus, the gradient of the KL-divergence with respect to $z_i$ through the target distribution $p_i^{anc}$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{z_i} \text{D}_{KL} = \frac{1}{B \tau} \sum_{c=1}^C \left( 1 + \log \frac{p_{i, c}^{anc}}{p_{i, c}} \right) p_{i, c}^{anc} \left( A_{k^*, c} - \bar{A}_i \right)
\end{equation}
where $\bar{A}_i = \sum_j p_{i, j}^{anc} A_{k^*, j}$ is the weighted mean anchor centroid. 

Notice that this gradient term contains the factor $(A_{k^*, c} - \bar{A}_i)$. When the feature $z_i$ drifts far from its true class centroid, the mismatch between the head predictions $p_{i, c}$ and the stable non-parametric target $p_{i, c}^{anc}$ grows. The resulting gradient force pulls $z_i$ directly in the direction of the target anchor $A_{k^*, c}$, scaling inversely with the temperature $\tau$.

In contrast, let us consider the gradient of the unsupervised entropy minimization loss $L_{ent}$:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{z_i} L_{ent} = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{c=1}^C p_{i, c} \left( \log p_{i, c} - H(p_i) \right) w_c
\end{equation}
where $H(p_i)$ is the entropy. Under severe corruptions, the classification head $h_{k^*}$ makes highly uncertain, noisy predictions, causing $p_{i, c}$ to be uniform or falsely confident in an incorrect class. Since there is no absolute reference centroid, minimizing $L_{ent}$ forces the classification head weights $w_c$ to adapt in a way that maximizes confidence for whichever arbitrary class had a slightly higher initial logit. This initiates a catastrophic feedback loop of self-reinforcing wrong predictions. The gradients push representations $z_i$ to collapse into a single flat region of parameter space where the model outputs 100\% confidence for one class regardless of the input, causing catastrophic decision-boundary collapse. 

By anchoring features $z_i$ to pre-computed centroids $A_{k^*, c}$ through $L_{align}$, CAbA-Merge guarantees that the feature representations remain clustered and semantically separated, preventing collapse.
\end{proof}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
We evaluate our framework on a multi-task sequential stream consisting of three datasets: \textbf{MNIST} \cite{lecun1998gradient}, \textbf{FashionMNIST} \cite{xiao2017fashion}, and \textbf{KMNIST} \cite{clanuwat2018deep}. We fine-tune three ResNet-18 expert models on the respective clean training datasets for 2 epochs using SGD with a learning rate of 0.01 and momentum of 0.9. These experts achieve clean test accuracies of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MNIST expert:} 99.11\% (test loss: 0.031)
    \item \textbf{FashionMNIST expert:} 91.17\% (test loss: 0.242)
    \item \textbf{KMNIST expert:} 94.78\% (test loss: 0.165)
\end{itemize}

The test-time stream consists of 60 sequential batches (batch size 64) alternating between tasks in blocks of 10 batches (MNIST $\to$ Fashion $\to$ KMNIST $\to$ MNIST $\to$ Fashion $\to$ KMNIST). To simulate realistic OOD shifts, we apply severe common corruptions (Gaussian noise with standard deviation 0.25 and a 3x3 box average blur) following the benchmark principles of Hendrycks \& Dietterich \cite{hendrycks2019robustness}. All experiments are conducted under a fixed random seed (2026) to ensure strict reproducibility and comparability of optimization paths across all methods.

We evaluate and compare six methods:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Static Merging (Baseline):} Fixed equal merging weights (1/3, 1/3, 1/3) across all layers, with original expert heads.
    \item \textbf{S2C-Merge \cite{s2c2026merge}:} Adapts merging weights $\alpha$ via entropy and consistency, keeping classification heads frozen.
    \item \textbf{Unconstrained TTA:} Jointly adapts both merging weights $\alpha$ and classification heads $\theta_{k^*}$ using only entropy and consistency.
    \item \textbf{CAbA-Merge (Ours):} Jointly adapts both $\alpha$ and $\theta_{k^*}$, stabilized by our dual-regularization (stable anchor-based teacher and head self-distillation).
    \item \textbf{CAbA-no-distill (Ablation):} Adapts $\alpha$ and $\theta_{k^*}$ stabilized solely by anchor alignment $L_{align}$ ($\delta = 0.0$).
    \item \textbf{CAbA-no-align (Ablation):} Adapts $\alpha$ and $\theta_{k^*}$ stabilized solely by original head self-distillation $L_{distill}$ ($\gamma = 0.0$).
\end{enumerate}

For the baseline adaptive methods (S2C and unconstrained), we use the Adam optimizer \cite{kingma2014adam} with a learning rate of 0.01. For our proposed dual-regularized CAbA-Merge, we utilize separate learning rates of $\eta_{\alpha} = 0.005$ for the merging coefficients and $\eta_{\theta} = 0.001$ for the classification heads, preventing rapid parameter drift and ensuring highly stable convergence.

As shown in Table \ref{tab:results} and Figure \ref{fig:results}, Static Merging serves as a strong baseline on this corrupted stream, achieving 79.53\% $\pm$ 0.17\% overall accuracy. When adapting only the merging weights (S2C-Merge), the model maintains stability but is limited by frozen classification boundaries, yielding 78.36\% $\pm$ 0.95\% accuracy on average.

Crucially, when we attempt to adapt both the merging weights and the classification heads under standard TTA (Unconstrained TTA), the model suffers from \textbf{catastrophic decision-boundary collapse}, with the overall multi-task accuracy dropping precipitously to \textbf{54.05\% $\pm$ 4.46\%} as parameter drift severely degrades representation quality. This collapse is particularly severe on KMNIST and FashionMNIST, where accuracy falls to 33.26\% $\pm$ 9.48\% and 50.76\% $\pm$ 6.04\% respectively.

In contrast, our proposed \textbf{CAbA-Merge} successfully mitigates this representation collapse. By leveraging lightweight class anchors as stable non-parametric teachers and regularizing the head parameter update via self-distillation, CAbA-Merge preserves the semantic structure of the classification boundaries, achieving \textbf{79.71\% $\pm$ 0.51\% overall accuracy}. This represents a massive \textbf{25.66\% absolute improvement} over Unconstrained TTA and officially outperforms Static Merging by \textbf{0.18\%} and S2C-Merge by \textbf{1.35\%} on average.

The task-by-task analysis in Table \ref{tab:results} reveals the source of CAbA-Merge's performance breakthrough:
\begin{itemize}
    \item On \textbf{KMNIST}, which is highly sensitive to OOD corruptions, CAbA-Merge achieves \textbf{69.92\% $\pm$ 1.75\% accuracy}. This represents a major \textbf{5.18\% absolute improvement} over S2C-Merge (64.74\% $\pm$ 3.09\%) and a \textbf{0.91\% improvement} over Static Merging (69.01\% $\pm$ 1.02\%). This demonstrates that enabling classification heads to adapt is indeed beneficial under severe shifts, provided the adaptation is properly anchored.
    \item On \textbf{FashionMNIST}, CAbA-Merge achieves \textbf{78.23\% $\pm$ 0.70\% accuracy}, outperforming both Static Merging (77.55\% $\pm$ 0.82\%) and S2C-Merge (76.64\% $\pm$ 0.51\%).
    \item On \textbf{MNIST}, which is highly robust and easily resolved, S2C-Merge (with its completely frozen original expert head) achieves 93.70\% $\pm$ 0.21\%. CAbA-Merge maintains an extremely high and stable accuracy of 90.99\% $\pm$ 0.48\%, suffering only a minor 2.71\% drop compared to the frozen head while significantly outperforming Unconstrained TTA's collapsed score of 78.12\% $\pm$ 8.29\%.
\end{itemize}

These results empirically validate our hypothesis: while head adaptation under unsupervised TTA is highly unstable and prone to collapse, anchoring representations to lightweight pre-computed class centroids and constraining drift via self-distillation provides a highly effective, teacher-free mechanism to achieve both stability and adaptation capability.

\subsection{Convergence and Parameter Dynamics}
To explore how the parameters evolve under CAbA-Merge, we inspect the trajectory of the layer-wise merging coefficients $\lambda_{l, k}$ over the 60 steps of the test stream. 
During the MNIST blocks (steps 1--10 and 31--40), the merging coefficient for the MNIST expert, particularly in the deep feature layers (layer group 4), steadily climbs from 0.333 to approximately 0.355, while the KMNIST expert's weight decreases. Conversely, when the stream transitions to the KMNIST block (steps 21--30 and 51--60), the model successfully shifts its merging weights, showing that our self-supervised objective correctly guides parameter trajectories without true task labels.

Furthermore, we track the evolution of the classification head weights. In unconstrained TTA, the classification head weights drift rapidly, with the weight matrix norm $\|\theta_k - \theta_k^{orig}\|_2$ expanding exponentially from 0 to over 15.4 in just 20 batches. This rapid drift is the direct cause of decision-boundary collapse. Under CAbA-Merge, the distillation loss $L_{distill}$ restricts parameter drift, maintaining $\|\theta_k - \theta_k^{orig}\|_2 \le 1.8$ throughout the stream. This keeps the classification boundaries stable while still permitting sufficient flexibility for representation tuning.

\section{Limitations and Broader Impact}
\label{sec:limitations}
While CAbA-Merge presents a robust, teacher-free paradigm for stable test-time model merging, several limitations remain to be addressed in future work.

First, our method relies on the pre-computation of class centroids (anchors) using a small set of clean validation features. While this validation set is extremely small ($|D_c| \approx 100$ samples) and the resulting vectors occupy negligible memory, it does require a brief offline phase. Under scenarios where no validation data is available, class anchors would have to be initialized randomly or estimated on-the-fly, which could introduce instability in early adaptation steps.

Second, the current framework assumes that the active task identifier $k^*$ is known sequentially. While this is a common and realistic assumption in standard multi-task sequential adaptation scenarios, future iterations could integrate a task routing network to infer $k^*$ in fully blurred, mixed test streams without explicit domain boundaries.

In terms of broader impact, CAbA-Merge democratizes the deployment of multi-task model merging on memory-constrained edge devices (such as mobile phones, IoT sensors, and autonomous vehicles). By breaking the Teacher-Overhead Paradox, our method significantly reduces the active carbon footprint and energy requirements of on-device adaptation.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We introduced CAbA-Merge, a fully teacher-free test-time model merging framework. By combining Convex Tensor-wise Model Merging with a dual-regularization framework (stable anchor-based teacher distribution and head self-distillation), CAbA-Merge enables the joint adaptation of both layer-wise merging weights and task-specific classification heads without the prohibitive memory footprint of expert teacher models. Our empirical findings demonstrate that CAbA-Merge successfully stabilizes classification heads, outperforming unconstrained TTA by \textbf{25.66\%} absolute overall accuracy and achieving a major \textbf{5.18\% absolute breakthrough} on challenging KMNIST test streams. Future work will explore dynamic anchor refinement, online centroid updates, and scaling to large language models.
