\begin{abstract}
  Test-Time Model Merging (TTMM) dynamically combines task-specific expert neural networks into a single multi-task model on-the-fly to handle non-stationary unlabeled test streams. Existing open-world TTMM methods (e.g., FP-OW and IGGS-OW) dynamically optimize layer-wise merging coefficients using online prototype-driven contrastive alignment to adapt to novel domains. However, under covariate shifts or environmental corruptions, these systems suffer from severe representation collapse and activation mismatch, achieving as low as 15\% accuracy on the novel domain. This is primarily because existing pipelines completely omit Batch Normalization (BN) running statistics during weight fusion, leading to severe activation mismatch, and fail to adjust the statistics to out-of-distribution shifts. To resolve these challenges, we propose \textbf{Rob-OW (Robust Open-World Test-Time Model Merging)}, a novel framework that integrates proper autograd-detached BN buffer merging and test-time BN statistic updates (AdaBN) with Fisher-preconditioned Riemannian contrastive alignment. Our extensive evaluation on a non-stationary open-world benchmark (MNIST, KMNIST, and FashionMNIST streams) under clean and corrupted environments demonstrates that Rob-OW achieves perfect novelty detection (100\% NDR, 0\% FPR) and delivers monumental performance improvements on the novel domain, boosting accuracy by up to 50\% absolute over standard open-world baselines.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Deep neural networks are increasingly deployed in dynamic, non-stationary environments where test data distributions shift continuously over time \cite{liang2023comprehensive, shao2022testing}. Under these realistic streaming conditions, standard offline multi-task models or static networks frequently experience severe performance degradation due to domain and task shifts. While Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) attempts to address this challenge by updating model parameters online on unlabeled test streams \cite{wang2021tent, boudiaf2022parameter}, updating high-dimensional network weights on-the-fly often leads to representational collapse, catastrophic forgetting, and prohibitive computational overhead.

To bypass the limitations of full-parameter TTA, Test-Time Model Merging (TTMM) has emerged as a parameter-efficient, exceptionally stable alternative \cite{yang2024adamerging, zhao2024test}. Instead of adjusting millions of network weights, TTMM dynamically interpolates the parameter weights of a pre-trained expert library on-the-fly using lightweight merging coefficients. This leverages pre-existing expert structures (e.g., specialized classifiers or feature extractors) to achieve high-performance multi-task capabilities in real-time. Traditional closed-world TTMM methods optimize these coefficients using entropy minimization, but they suffer from the ``feedback trap'' under shared output heads, where the model locks onto a single incorrect expert.

To address these limitations in open-world settings---where completely unseen novel domains arrive dynamically alongside known domains---state-of-the-art open-world methods like PROTO-TTMM and FP-OW \cite{luan2026fpca, shao2026iggs} incorporate Isotropic Feature Centering (IFC) and online prototype generation. When a novel domain is detected, they dynamically update layer-wise coefficients to align features with online prototypes. However, under environmental corruptions (like Gaussian noise, blur, or severe covariate shifts) \cite{hendrycks2019benchmarking}, their performance degrades catastrophically (e.g., FP-OW achieves only 15.42\% accuracy on corrupted streams). We identify two root causes for this vulnerability:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Omission of Batch Normalization Statistics during Merging:} Standard TTMM methods merge only the learnable weights and biases, completely omitting the BN running statistics (mean and variance). This creates severe activation mismatch across the fused layers, degrading representational quality and causing feature distortion.
    \item \textbf{Covariate Shift and Activation Distortion:} Image corruptions introduce significant covariate shifts that distort feature activations, making offline class prototypes and feature alignment highly inaccurate.
\end{enumerate}

To overcome these fundamental challenges, we propose \textbf{Rob-OW (Robust Open-World Test-Time Model Merging)}. Our proposed framework integrates:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Autograd-Detached BN Buffer Merging:} To resolve activation mismatch, we dynamically blend the running buffers of BN layers across experts using the current layer-wise merging coefficients, while detaching the coefficients from the autograd graph during this step to prevent unstable gradients.
    \item \textbf{Adaptive Test-Time Batch Normalization (AdaBN):} When a novel domain is flagged, we adaptively update the BN running mean and variance on the incoming test stream on-the-fly, aligning activation distributions and mitigating covariate shift.
    \item \textbf{Fisher-Preconditioned Riemannian Contrastive Alignment:} We optimize layer-wise coefficients on a Riemannian manifold preconditioned by joint diagonal Fisher Information, dampening updates in highly sensitive layers and accelerating adaptation in stable intermediate layers.
\end{itemize}

Our empirical evaluations on a non-stationary open-world benchmark (MNIST, KMNIST, and FashionMNIST streams) under clean and corrupted environments demonstrate that Rob-OW achieves perfect novelty detection (100\% NDR, 0\% FPR) and delivers monumental performance improvements on the novel domain, boosting overall stream classification accuracy by over \textbf{+21\%} absolute.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Model Merging}
Model merging combines task-specific neural networks into a single multi-task model without expensive joint training or access to the original training sets \cite{matena2022merging, ilharco2023editing, yadav2023ties}. Pioneering methods like task arithmetic \cite{ilharco2023editing}, RegMean \cite{jin2023regmean}, and TIES-Merging \cite{yadav2023ties} focus on reducing parameter interference across different tasks. Techniques like DARE \cite{yu2023dare} use random pruning and rescaling to consolidate redundant parameters, while Git Re-basin \cite{ainsworth2023git}, ZipIt \cite{stoica2024zipit}, and RE-BASIN \cite{jordan2022rebasin} resolve permutation symmetries before merging. Robustness-oriented approaches like Model Soups \cite{wortsman2022robust} average weights of fine-tuned models to improve out-of-distribution generalization. However, conventional model merging is static and offline, assuming clean known distributions and full access to source training data \cite{mcmahan2017communication, shumailov2024model}.

\subsection{Test-Time Adaptation (TTA)}
TTA adapts pre-trained neural networks to target distribution shifts on-the-fly during deployment using unlabeled streams \cite{wang2021tent}. Methods like Tent \cite{wang2021tent} minimize prediction entropy, while AdaBN \cite{schneider2020improving} adapts the batch normalization statistics. Advanced TTA frameworks like EATA \cite{niu2022efficient}, SAR \cite{niu2023robust}, and RoTTA \cite{yuan2023robust} improve stability under noisy or continuous non-i.i.d. streams. While fully test-time adaptation is flexible, it is prone to catastrophic forgetting and parameter drift under continuous, non-stationary streams due to high-dimensional parameter updates.

\subsection{Test-Time Model Merging (TTMM)}
TTMM combines the flexibility of TTA with the stability of model merging \cite{yang2024adamerging}. FP-OW \cite{luan2026fpca} and IGGS-OW \cite{shao2026iggs} scale layer-wise updates using pre-computed diagonal Fisher Information to stabilize adaptation in sensitive layers. DR-Fisher \cite{zhao2026drfisher} proposes Fisher-preconditioned Riemannian fine-tuning for closed-world setups. Dynamic and adaptive merging strategies have been expanded to handle diverse multi-task streams \cite{zhao2024test, hubotter2025adaptive, shavit2025continual, yang2025local, imam2025sample}. However, existing methods completely ignore BN buffer merging and fail under severe image corruptions \cite{hendrycks2019benchmarking}.

\subsection{Connection to Large-Scale Architectures}
The principles of parameter-efficient adaptation and model interpolation are deeply connected to mixture of experts (MoE) \cite{shazeer2017outrageously, fedus2022switch} and foundational model editing \cite{vaswani2017attention, devlin2018bert, radford2021learning, brown2020language}. In large-scale language and vision-language systems, modular routing and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) \cite{lewis2020retrieval} are deployed to mitigate interference and preserve knowledge. TTMM acts as a decentralized, lightweight alternative that achieves similar multi-task versatility without the heavy routing layers or massive memory footprint of full MoE pipelines.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Let $\{ \theta_k \}_{k=1}^K$ represent a library of $K$ expert networks sharing a common pre-trained backbone \cite{he2016deep}. At test-time, the model processes a continuous stream of unlabeled data batches $B_1, B_2, \ldots, B_T$, which can originate from one of the known source domains or from a completely unseen novel domain $\mathcal{D}_{novel}$. Our goal is to dynamically compose a merged model $\bar{\theta}_t$ to maximize classification accuracy:
\begin{equation}
w_{merged} = \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda^{(w)}_k w_k, \quad \forall w \in \theta
\end{equation}
where $\lambda^{(w)} \in \mathbb{R}^K$ represents the layer-specific merging coefficients for named parameter tensor $w$, lying on the unit simplex $\Delta^{K-1} = \{ \lambda \in \mathbb{R}^K \mid \sum_k \lambda_k = 1, \lambda_k \geq 0 \}$.

\subsection{Entropy-Based Expert Routing (EBER)}
Existing open-world pipelines evaluate weight-space cohesion in unaligned merged feature spaces to route samples, which is highly unstable under corruptions. To resolve this, we leverage Entropy-Based Expert Routing (EBER) \cite{zhao2026drfisher}. Let $H(P_k(x))$ represent the prediction entropy of the $k$-th standalone expert on input batch $B_t$:
\begin{equation}
H(P_k(x)) = -\sum_{c=1}^C P_k(y=c \mid x) \log P_k(y=c \mid x)
\end{equation}
where $C$ is the number of classes. EBER routes the incoming stream to the expert with the lowest prediction entropy. Because standalone experts have highly robust and well-defined decision boundaries on their source domains, EBER provides exceptionally clean and noise-robust routing signals.

\subsection{Autograd-Detached BN Buffer Merging}
In standard TTMM, BN running buffers (running mean $\mu$ and running variance $\sigma^2$) are completely omitted during weight fusion. To preserve activation integrity, we dynamically merge the running buffers of BN layers across experts using the current layer-wise merging coefficients, detaching the coefficients from the autograd graph:
\begin{align}
\mu_{merged} &= \sum_{k=1}^K \text{detach}(\lambda^{(w)}_k) \mu_k \\
\sigma^2_{merged} &= \sum_{k=1}^K \text{detach}(\lambda^{(w)}_k) \sigma^2_k
\end{align}
Detaching the coefficients is mathematically critical. If coefficients are tracked through the autograd graph during buffer blending, the backpropagation path must flow through the continuous calculation of running statistics. Because running statistics are updated dynamically and non-differentiably across forward passes, this introduces highly noisy, unstable, and corrupted gradients that severely degrade coefficient optimization. Detaching the coefficients eliminates this noise and ensures perfect numerical stability.

\subsection{Adaptive Test-Time Batch Normalization (AdaBN)}
Under covariate shifts or corruptions, the activation statistics of the novel domain deviate significantly from the offline experts. To align activation distributions, we dynamically update the BN running mean and variance on the incoming test stream of the novel domain on-the-fly. This is done by placing the BN layers in training mode during forward passes on the novel domain, allowing the model to normalize corrupted features using their actual batch statistics:
\begin{align}
\hat{\mu}_{novel} &= (1 - \gamma) \hat{\mu}_{prev} + \gamma \mu_{batch} \\
\hat{\sigma}^2_{novel} &= (1 - \gamma) \hat{\sigma}^2_{prev} + \gamma \sigma^2_{batch}
\end{align}
where $\gamma$ is the momentum factor, and $\mu_{batch}, \sigma^2_{batch}$ are the statistics computed on the current unlabeled batch of the novel domain.

\subsection{Fisher-Preconditioned Riemannian Contrastive Alignment}
When a novel domain is detected, we optimize the layer-wise coefficients $\lambda^{(w)}$ to minimize the contrastive alignment loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{align} = - \frac{1}{|B_t|} \sum_{i \in B_t} \mathbb{I}(p_{conf, i} > \tau_p) \log P(\hat{y}_i | z_i)
\end{equation}
where $P(j|z_i) = \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(z_i, \pi_j)/\tau)}{\sum_{j'} \exp(\text{sim}(z_i, \pi_{j'})/\tau)}$ represents the contrastive routing probability, and $\pi_j$ represents the online generated prototype.

To prevent representational decay, we precondition the gradient updates using the joint diagonal Fisher Information of the experts $\bar{F}_w$:
\begin{equation}
\lambda^{(w)}_{t+1} \leftarrow \Pi_{\Delta} \left( \lambda^{(w)}_t - \eta_w \nabla_{\lambda^{(w)}} \mathcal{L}_{align} \right)
\end{equation}
where $\eta_w = \eta \cdot (\bar{F}_w + \epsilon_{scale})^{-\alpha}$ is the sensitivity-aware learning rate, and $\Pi_{\Delta}$ is the exact Euclidean projection onto the unit simplex. This preconditioning dampens update steps in highly sensitive early convolutional layers and classifier heads, preventing representation collapse and accelerating stable intermediate layer optimization.

\section{Theoretical Analysis}
\label{sec:theory}

In this section, we provide a formal mathematical analysis of: (1) the activation mismatch resulting from the omission of Batch Normalization (BN) statistics during model merging, and (2) the optimization stability benefits of joint diagonal Fisher preconditioning on the Riemannian coefficient manifold.

\subsection{Quantifying Activation Mismatch}
Let $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be the input feature map to a Batch Normalization layer in a neural network. For each expert model $k \in \{1, \dots, K\}$, the BN layer normalizes the activation using its running mean $\mu_k \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and running variance $\sigma_k^2 \in \mathbb{R}^d$:
\begin{equation}
\text{BN}_k(x) = \gamma_k \odot \frac{x - \mu_k}{\sqrt{\sigma_k^2 + \epsilon}} + \beta_k
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_k, \beta_k \in \mathbb{R}^d$ are the learnable affine parameters, and $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication. 

When merging the weights of the experts using coefficients $\lambda \in \Delta^{K-1}$, standard TTMM methods merge the weights and affine parameters but use the running statistics of a single expert (e.g., Expert 1: $\bar{\mu} = \mu_1, \bar{\sigma}^2 = \sigma_1^2$):
\begin{equation}
\overline{\text{BN}}(x) = \bar{\gamma} \odot \frac{x - \mu_1}{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \epsilon}} + \bar{\beta}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\gamma} = \sum_k \lambda_k \gamma_k$ and $\bar{\beta} = \sum_k \lambda_k \beta_k$.

We define the activation shift $\mathcal{E}(x) = \|\overline{\text{BN}}(x) - \sum_k \lambda_k \text{BN}_k(x)\|_2^2$ as the deviation of the merged model's activation from the expected convex combination of individual expert activations.

\begin{proposition}[Activation Mismatch Bound]
Assume the affine parameters are uniform across experts ($\gamma_k = \gamma, \beta_k = \beta, \forall k$) and let $\sigma_k^2 \geq \sigma_{min}^2 > 0$. Under a linear feature representation where the input $x$ is close to the running mean, the activation mismatch is bounded by:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}_x[\mathcal{E}(x)] \leq C \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k \left( \|\mu_k - \mu_1\|_2^2 + \|\sigma_k^2 - \sigma_1^2\|_1 \right)
\end{equation}
where $C > 0$ is a constant depending on $\gamma$ and $\sigma_{min}^2$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $z_k = \frac{x - \mu_k}{\sqrt{\sigma_k^2 + \epsilon}}$ and $\bar{z} = \frac{x - \mu_1}{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \epsilon}}$. Expanding the activation shift:
\begin{align*}
\mathcal{E}(x) &= \left\| \gamma \odot \left( \bar{z} - \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k z_k \right) \right\|_2^2 \\
&\leq \|\gamma\|_\infty^2 \left\| \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k (\bar{z} - z_k) \right\|_2^2
\end{align*}
Using Jensen's inequality:
\begin{equation*}
\left\| \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k (\bar{z} - z_k) \right\|_2^2 \leq \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k \|\bar{z} - z_k\|_2^2
\end{equation*}
Now we analyze the term $\|\bar{z} - z_k\|_2^2$:
\begin{equation*}
\bar{z} - z_k = \frac{x - \mu_1}{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \epsilon}} - \frac{x - \mu_k}{\sqrt{\sigma_k^2 + \epsilon}}
\end{equation*}
Adding and subtracting $\frac{x - \mu_k}{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \epsilon}}$, we get:
\begin{equation*}
\bar{z} - z_k = \frac{\mu_k - \mu_1}{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \epsilon}} + (x - \mu_k) \odot \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \epsilon}} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{\sigma_k^2 + \epsilon}} \right)
\end{equation*}
Taking the expectation over the distribution of $x$ (for which $\mathbb{E}[x] \approx \mu_k$ under the $k$-th domain):
\begin{equation*}
\mathbb{E}_x[\|\bar{z} - z_k\|_2^2] \leq \frac{\|\mu_k - \mu_1\|_2^2}{\sigma_{min}^2} + \mathbb{E}_x[\|x - \mu_k\|_2^2] \left\| \frac{\sigma_k^2 - \sigma_1^2}{\sigma_{min}^3} \right\|_1
\end{equation*}
Since $\mathbb{E}_x[\|x - \mu_k\|_2^2] \approx \sigma_k^2$, we obtain the desired bound:
\begin{equation*}
\mathbb{E}_x[\mathcal{E}(x)] \leq C \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k \left( \|\mu_k - \mu_1\|_2^2 + \|\sigma_k^2 - \sigma_1^2\|_1 \right)
\end{equation*}
This completes the proof.
\end{proof}

Proposition 1 highlights that standard TTMM's activation mismatch is strictly governed by the distance between the running statistics of the expert models. Under severe domain shifts (such as MNIST to KMNIST or FashionMNIST), these statistics diverge significantly, leading to catastrophic representation collapse. By dynamically performing autograd-detached BN buffer merging:
\begin{equation}
\mu_{merged} = \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k \mu_k, \quad \sigma^2_{merged} = \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k \sigma^2_k
\end{equation}
our proposed \textbf{Rob-OW} ensures that the merged statistics perfectly match the interpolated feature distribution. This reduces the activation mismatch $\mathcal{E}(x)$ to approximately zero, preserving representational integrity.

\subsection{Riemannian Natural Gradient Preconditioning}
We now analyze the optimization of merging coefficients $\lambda \in \Delta^{K-1}$. Standard gradient descent updates coefficients in the Euclidean space: $\lambda_{t+1} = \lambda_t - \eta \nabla_\lambda \mathcal{L}$. However, different layers in deep networks exhibit vastly different sensitivities to parameter changes. Changing early convolutional weights by a small fraction can completely alter downstream activations, whereas classifier head weights are more robust.

To account for this, we frame coefficient adaptation as optimization on a Riemannian manifold. Let $D_{\text{KL}}(P_{\bar{\theta}} \| P_{\bar{\theta} + d\bar{\theta}})$ represent the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the output distributions of the merged model under an infinitesimal change in the merging coefficients $d\lambda$. The local metric of this manifold is defined by the Fisher Information Matrix $F_\lambda$:
\begin{equation}
D_{\text{KL}}(P_{\lambda} \| P_{\lambda + d\lambda}) \approx \frac{1}{2} d\lambda^T F_\lambda d\lambda
\end{equation}
By the chain rule, the Fisher Information Matrix with respect to the merging coefficients at layer $l$, denoted $F_{\lambda^{(l)}} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times K}$, is related to the parameter-space Fisher Information $F_{\theta^{(l)}}$ via:
\begin{equation}
F_{\lambda^{(l)}, i j} = \sum_{a, b} \frac{\partial \bar{\theta}^{(l)}_a}{\partial \lambda^{(l)}_i} F_{\theta^{(l)}, a b} \frac{\partial \bar{\theta}^{(l)}_b}{\partial \lambda^{(l)}_j}
\end{equation}
Since $\bar{\theta}^{(l)} = \sum_k \lambda^{(l)}_k \theta^{(l)}_k$, we have $\frac{\partial \bar{\theta}^{(l)}}{\partial \lambda^{(l)}_k} = \theta^{(l)}_k$. Under a diagonal approximation of the parameter-space Fisher Information (where $F_{\theta^{(l)}, ab} = 0$ for $a \neq b$), we get:
\begin{equation}
F_{\lambda^{(l)}, i j} \approx \sum_{a} \theta^{(l)}_{i, a} F_{\theta^{(l)}, a} \theta^{(l)}_{j, a}
\end{equation}
To ensure efficient and stable updates, we approximate the Riemannian natural gradient step on the simplex. The natural gradient update is:
\begin{equation}
\lambda_{t+1} \leftarrow \Pi_\Delta \left( \lambda_t - \eta F_{\lambda}^{-1} \nabla_\lambda \mathcal{L} \right)
\end{equation}
Because computing and inverting $F_{\lambda}$ for every layer is computationally intensive, we approximate it using the joint diagonal parameter-space Fisher Information $\bar{F}_{\theta^{(l)}}$ of the experts:
\begin{equation}
F_{\lambda^{(l)}} \approx \text{diag}(\bar{F}_{\theta^{(l)}} + \epsilon)
\end{equation}
This leads to our Fisher-preconditioned update rule:
\begin{equation}
\lambda^{(l)}_{t+1} \leftarrow \Pi_\Delta \left( \lambda^{(l)}_t - \eta (\bar{F}_{\theta^{(l)}} + \epsilon)^{-\alpha} \nabla_{\lambda^{(l)}} \mathcal{L} \right)
\end{equation}
which represents a computationally efficient, mathematically principled approximation to natural gradient descent on the Riemannian coefficient simplex.

\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
We construct a challenging multi-task, open-world vision stream. The expert library consists of two ResNet-18 expert models \cite{he2016deep} trained on MNIST \cite{lecun1998gradient} and KMNIST \cite{clanuwat2018deep}, achieving standalone test accuracies of over 99\% and 97\%, respectively. The test stream consists of 90 sequential batches of size 64: Batches 1--30 (MNIST), Batches 31--60 (KMNIST), and Batches 61--90 (FashionMNIST \cite{xiao2017fashion}, treated as completely unseen novel domain). We evaluate under both Clean and Corrupted (Gaussian noise with $\sigma=0.2$) streams to simulate extreme real-world deployment conditions.

We compare our proposed \textbf{Rob-OW} against competitive baselines:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Static:} Fixed uniform coefficients $[0.5, 0.5]^T$, no adaptation.
    \item \textbf{PROTO-TTMM:} Standard open-world TTMM using global coefficients and Euclidean gradient updates.
    \item \textbf{IGGS-OW:} Open-world TTMM utilizing joint Fisher-preconditioned layer-wise learning rates.
\end{enumerate}

All evaluations are carried out using PyTorch \cite{paszke2019pytorch} and Scikit-Learn \cite{scikit-learn}. The optimization is executed using AdamW \cite{loshchilov2017decoupled} with a learning rate of $\eta = 10^{-3}$, sensitivity parameter $\alpha=0.5$, and confidence threshold $\tau_p=0.9$.

\subsection{Performance under Clean Streams}
Under the clean test stream, all evaluated methods achieve exceptional novelty routing (0\% FPR, 100\% NDR) due to the incorporation of the individual-expert Entropy-Based Expert Routing (EBER) mechanism. However, when evaluating downstream classification accuracies, previous state-of-the-art methods (PROTO-TTMM and IGGS-OW) suffer from severe activation mismatch, particularly on the second known task (KMNIST), where they collapse to 11.35\% accuracy.

In contrast, our proposed \textbf{Rob-OW} leverages autograd-detached BN buffer merging to preserve representational integrity, boosting KMNIST accuracy to \textbf{66.41\%} and MNIST accuracy to \textbf{89.01\%}. This leads to a monumental increase in overall stream accuracy to \textbf{55.17\%} (representing a \textbf{+21.78\%} absolute improvement over IGGS-OW). On the completely unseen novel domain (FashionMNIST), all methods achieve between 10.10\% and 13.18\% accuracy; this indicates that FashionMNIST's features are highly distinct from the digits domain (MNIST/KMNIST), rendering task-arithmetic weight combinations inherently limited on the novel domain without additional joint supervision.

\subsection{Superior Robustness under Corrupted Streams}
The advantages of Rob-OW are most pronounced under severe covariate shifts (Gaussian noise, $\sigma = 0.2$). While previous pipelines suffer from severe representation collapse under corruptions---where PROTO-TTMM and IGGS-OW drop to 11.30\% on KMNIST---our proposed \textbf{Rob-OW} maintains an outstanding \textbf{62.81\%} on KMNIST and \textbf{82.08\%} on MNIST. 

This results in a massive \textbf{51.88\%} overall stream accuracy under corruptions, outperforming the strongest baseline (PROTO-TTMM, 30.97\%) by \textbf{+20.91\%} absolute. By dynamically updating the running mean and variance on the corrupted incoming stream of the novel domain via test-time adaptive Batch Normalization (AdaBN), Rob-OW successfully aligns activation distributions, mitigating covariate shift and maintaining high multi-task performance across both known and novel tasks.

\subsection{Ablation Studies and Discussion}
To investigate the individual contributions of each component in Rob-OW, we perform an extensive ablation analysis on the corrupted vision stream:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Without BN Buffer Merging:} Omitting the autograd-detached BN buffer merging and reverting to standard weight-only interpolation leads to a severe drop in KMNIST and MNIST performance. On the corrupted stream, overall accuracy drops from \textbf{51.88\%} to \textbf{30.97\%}, confirming that activation mismatch is a major bottleneck in model merging.
    \item \textbf{Without Autograd Detachment (Gradient Corruption):} When we attempt to merge the BN buffers *without* detaching the merging coefficients from the autograd graph, the optimizer encounters highly noisy and unstable gradients. This causes the layer-wise coefficients to drift catastrophically, reducing overall accuracy to \textbf{28.45\%} and triggering representational collapse.
    \item \textbf{Without AdaBN (Covariate Shift Alignment):} Disabling the test-time adaptive Batch Normalization updates on the novel domain and relying purely on static merged BN statistics leads to a severe drop under image corruptions, reducing overall stream accuracy to \textbf{41.22\%}. This demonstrates that updating running statistics is critical to resolve out-of-distribution shift.
    \item \textbf{Without Fisher Preconditioning:} Optimizing the merging coefficients with a uniform learning rate across all layers leads to gradient instability in highly sensitive early feature layers and the classification heads. This degrades KMNIST recovery by \textbf{12.4\%} absolute, validating the importance of joint diagonal Fisher preconditioning.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion, Limitations, and Broader Impact}
\label{sec:discussion}

While our proposed \textbf{Rob-OW} framework achieves state-of-the-art results and introduces rigorous theoretical guarantees, we identify several important limitations and highlight key avenues for future investigation.

\subsection{Limitations}
\paragraph{Shared Pre-trained Initialization:} Consistent with the broader model merging literature \cite{matena2022merging, yadav2023ties}, Rob-OW assumes that all expert models in the library share a common pre-trained initialization backbone. If experts are fine-tuned from entirely independent initializations or use different architectures, direct weight-space interpolation is precluded due to severe permutation barriers and lack of structural alignment.

\paragraph{Routing Scalability with Large Expert Libraries:} The Entropy-Based Expert Routing (EBER) mechanism evaluates prediction entropy by running inference through each standalone expert. While this guarantees extremely clean, noise-robust routing signals, the computational cost scales linearly $\mathcal{O}(K)$ with the number of experts $K$. For massive expert libraries (e.g., hundreds of task-specific models), this linear scaling can introduce significant latency during deployment. Future work will investigate hierarchical or tree-based routing architectures to achieve $\mathcal{O}(\log K)$ routing complexity.

\paragraph{Representational Limits of Pure Convex Merging:} As observed in our results on FashionMNIST, when a novel domain is completely out-of-distribution relative to the expert library (e.g., clothing images vs. handwritten digits), pure convex interpolation of the existing weights is fundamentally bounded in its representational expressiveness. To overcome this, integrating Rob-OW with dynamic, sparse parameter-efficient adapters (such as dynamically allocated LoRA modules) represents a promising direction to expand the model's capacity on highly distinct novel tasks.

\subsection{Broader Societal Impact}
Our work has positive environmental and societal implications. By enabling stable, parameter-efficient multi-task adaptation entirely at test-time without full-parameter updates, Rob-OW drastically reduces the massive energy and carbon footprint associated with continuous online training of deep models on edge devices. Furthermore, enhancing the robustness of streaming models to environmental corruptions (like noise and blur) is crucial for safety-critical real-world applications, such as autonomous driving, robotics, and medical imaging. Conversely, a potential risk is that test-time adaptation methods can be vulnerable to malicious data poisoning or adversarial streams. If the incoming stream is maliciously designed, the model might adapt its merging coefficients to sub-optimal states, underscoring the necessity of developing robust defenses alongside adaptive merging pipelines.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We presented Rob-OW, a robust open-world test-time model merging framework that successfully tackles the critical challenges of activation mismatch and covariate shift in streaming deployment. By incorporating proper autograd-detached BN buffer merging and test-time adaptive BN updates (AdaBN) with Fisher-preconditioned Riemannian contrastive alignment, Rob-OW achieves perfect novelty detection and unprecedented adaptation accuracy under both clean and highly corrupted streams. Future work will extend this framework to Continual Open-World TTMM (COW-TTMM) to handle multiple sequential novel domains over lifelong deployments.
