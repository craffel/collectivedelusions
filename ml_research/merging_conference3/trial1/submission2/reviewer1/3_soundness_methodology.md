# 3. Soundness and Methodology Evaluation

## Clarity of the Description
The methodology is described with exemplary clarity. The authors outline the mathematical formulation of sequential model merging, clearly defining the cumulative update, the task-specific expert update, the active weight mixing balance, and the SVD isotropic interpolation steps. Every single configuration in the $5 \times 3$ grid is explicitly described, including the two corrected variants of SA-BCD. The hyperparameter values (learning rate, epochs, perturbation radius, coordinate selection ratio) are fully specified, making the experimental setup transparent and easy to follow.

## Appropriateness of Methods and Baselines
The experimental design is methodologically outstanding:
- **Decoupled Evaluation Grid ($5 \times 3$):** This is the ideal approach for auditing multi-component pipelines. It prevents the authors from making vague, hand-waving claims about "synergy" and instead forces them to measure the precise impact of each individual element.
- **Rigorous Baselines:** In addition to naive Task Arithmetic and SVD, the authors introduce a simple *Scalar Update Decay* baseline and a *Norm-Matching* baseline. To isolate the exact regularizing mechanism of SVD, the Norm-Matching baseline is meticulously designed with a piecewise formulation to handle the $t=1$ boundary condition (where $\Delta_{\text{cum}} = 0$), preventing any compounding scale shrinkage on the first step. They also evaluate a *Scale-Calibrated* baseline to eliminate compounding scale shrinkage entirely, alongside modern post-hoc consolidation baselines (TIES-Merging and DARE).
- **Control and Capacity Scaling:** Using Split CIFAR-100 with a ViT-Tiny model (5M parameters) provides a clean, highly controlled, and computationally fast testbed to isolate weight dynamics. The authors then successfully scale and validate their findings on a larger ViT-Base model (86M parameters), verifying that their core deconstruction findings remain robust as parameter capacity scales by over $17\times$.

## Technical Soundness and Mathematical Rigor
The mathematical analysis in this paper is of exceptionally high quality:
- **Proposition 3.1 and Proof:** The mathematical bound linking post-hoc pruning loss to Hessian curvature is elegant, clean, and correct. Using a second-order Taylor expansion and the Rayleigh-Ritz theorem to bound the quadratic form $\delta\theta^\top H(\theta^*) \delta\theta$ by the maximum eigenvalue of the Hessian $\lambda_{\max}(H)$ is highly appropriate. This simple and elegant proof provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for why optimizer-driven flatness acts as an enabling prerequisite for sparsification.
- **Analysis of Compounding Scale Shrinkage (Appendix C):** The authors perform a brilliant mathematical deconstruction of their own Norm-Matching baseline. They show that due to high-dimensional near-orthogonality of sequential task updates ($\langle \Delta_{\text{cum}}, \Delta_{T_t} \rangle \approx 0$), adding task updates increases the Frobenius norm of the mixed updates ($\approx 1.442 N$), but averaging their norms yields a smaller target norm ($N$). This forces a scaling factor of $s_t \approx 0.693$ at each step, compounding exponentially to under $24\%$ of the proper update magnitude by step 5. This is a profound, high-signal observation that explains why naive global norm-matching collapses, and highlights why SVD Isotropic Merging (which preserves the average singular value while flattening the spectrum variance) is immune to this issue.
- **Analysis of the SA-BCD Typo:** The paper successfully diagnoses why the literal published formula of SA-BCD diverges (multiplying the Adam-scaled step-direction by the raw perturbed gradient, which forces a negative update sign relative to $g'_{t, i}$ and quadratically scales the step size), and correctly implements two alternative formulations to evaluate the underlying block-coordinate descent concept.
- **GPU Bottleneck Analysis:** The authors correctly identify that the coordinate-restricted selection in SA-BCD requires momentum sorting and sparse indexing/masking. In automatic differentiation libraries (like PyTorch), these sparse retrieval operations are inherently sequential, breaking GPU parallelization and thread-coalescing. This explains why restricting updates to 30% of parameters actually *slows down* wall-clock training time by 18.5% on modern hardware.

## Reproducibility
The paper is highly reproducible:
- **Standard Architectures and Libraries:** It utilizes standard backbones from `timm` (ViT-Tiny, ViT-Base) and standard datasets (Split CIFAR-100).
- **Explicit Hyperparameters:** All hyperparameter values are explicitly provided, including optimizer learning rates, epochs, batch sizes, perturbation radii, and coordinate ratios.
- **Open-Source Intent:** The authors commit to releasing their complete modular $5 \times 3$ grid evaluation suite, their corrected SA-BCD implementations, and the LoRA-SAM benchmarks as public open-source artifacts, which will greatly benefit the community.
