\section{Further Methodological Insights and Future Work}

Our rigorous investigation on Split CIFAR-100 highlights a critical need to re-evaluate claims of post-hoc spectral or manifold transformations in model merging. In this appendix, we expand on several discussion points from the main text and outline key directions for future research.

\subsection{Theoretical Scaling of Coordinate-Wise Perturbations}
In Section \ref{sec:experiments}, we highlighted that SA-BCD is empirically suboptimal due to under-perturbation on unselected coordinates. Here, we formalize the relationship between coordinate selection and effective perturbation scale.

Let $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^D$ be the parameters, and $\Omega \subset \{1, \dots, D\}$ be a subset of selected coordinates with cardinality $|\Omega| = p D$, where $p \in (0, 1]$ is the selection ratio (e.g., $p = 0.3$ in SA-BCD). In a standard SAM perturbation:
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon^* = \rho \frac{g}{\|g\|_2}
\end{equation}
the perturbation vector has a constant Euclidean norm $\|\epsilon^*\|_2 = \rho$. When restricting the perturbation to $\Omega$:
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon^*_\Omega = \rho \frac{g_\Omega}{\|g_\Omega\|_2}
\end{equation}
the perturbation vector has Euclidean norm $\|\epsilon^*_\Omega\|_2 = \rho$ in the selected coordinate subspace, and the elements of $\epsilon^*_\Omega$ corresponding to the unselected set $\Omega^c$ are set to zero (i.e., $(\epsilon^*_\Omega)_i = 0$ for all $i \in \Omega^c$). 

Because the projection restricts the optimizer to a lower-dimensional subspace, the loss curvature in the unperturbed orthogonal subspace $\Omega^c$ is ignored. Consequently, parameters along the coordinates $\Omega^c$ remain in sharp regions. When task-specific experts are sequentially merged, the sharp dimensions undergo severe disruption and representation collapse. To mitigate this under-perturbation, we hypothesize that the perturbation radius $\rho$ for coordinate-restricted methods should be dynamically scaled by $1/\sqrt{p}$ to yield a larger perturbation norm in the active subspace. Alternatively, one could dynamically shuffle or shift the coordinate subset $\Omega_t$ at every gradient step (or epoch) to eventually cover the entire parameter space and flatten all dimensions. However, while shuffling might theoretically distribute the flattening effect across the parameters over time, it does not alleviate the underlying computational bottleneck. The coordinate-restricted optimizer would still need to sort first-order momentum vectors and construct sparse coordinate masks at every single step, retaining the heavy indexing and sorting operations that disrupt GPU thread-coalescing. Thus, even with dynamic coordinate shuffling, coordinate-restricted sharpness minimization remains fundamentally suboptimal and computationally impractical compared to standard global SAM.

\subsection{Empirical Scaling Analysis of SVD Merging}
\label{sec:appendix_svd}
While Split CIFAR-100 with a Vision Transformer (ViT-Tiny) provides a clean, fast, and highly controlled testbed to isolate component effects, we recognize that weight dynamics can differ with larger model capacity (e.g., ViT-Base, ViT-Large) or different architecture modalities (e.g., LLMs).

To provide empirical support for our theoretical complexity claims, we conducted a rigorous benchmark of SVD execution time on both CPU (Intel Xeon Ice Lake @ 2.30GHz) and GPU (NVIDIA H100 PCIe 80GB) across different matrix dimensions representing full hidden sizes (e.g., from ViT-Tiny $d=192$ to standard LLM $d=4096$) and low-rank LoRA adapter dimensions ($d \times r$). All benchmarks are executed using PyTorch v2.1.2 and CUDA 12.1, reporting the average execution time over 20 independent trials in Table \ref{tab:svd_benchmark}.

These empirical results highlight a major scaling bottleneck for SVD-based merging methods, but confirm their feasibility in low-rank subspaces:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Severe CPU Overhead:} On CPU, computing SVD for a single $4096 \times 4096$ matrix takes nearly 4 seconds. Since standard deep networks consist of dozens of linear and projection layers, sequentially merging them on CPU would introduce minutes of computation overhead, rendering real-time or resource-constrained merging impossible.
  \item \textbf{Significant GPU Overhead:} Even on specialized GPU hardware (NVIDIA Hopper), a single $4096 \times 4096$ layer SVD takes over 1.1 seconds. In modern LLMs (e.g., LLaMA or Mistral) with dozens of layers and extremely wide dimensions, the cumulative SVD computation would introduce hundreds of seconds of GPU bottleneck.
  \item \textbf{Instantaneous Low-Rank SVD Computation:} In contrast to full-parameter matrices, computing the SVD of low-rank matrices representing LoRA adapter layers (e.g., $4096 \times 8$ or $4096 \times 16$) is virtually instantaneous—taking just $0.17$ ms to $0.48$ ms on CPU and under $0.10$ ms on GPU. Because low-rank SVD is computationally negligible and mathematically redundant on already factorized updates, this empirical result further highlights why PEFT methods like LoRA-SAM represent a highly scalable, zero-overhead paradigm for model consolidation in large language models.
\end{enumerate}
This empirical scaling analysis strongly validates our core methodological recommendation: rather than designing complex post-hoc weight transformations with heavy $O(d^3)$ bottlenecks, the research community should prioritize optimizer-driven flatness (such as SAM) during individual task training. By spending compute budget during the training phase (which is already highly optimized), we can successfully merge models using simple, $O(d)$ naive Task Arithmetic without introducing any post-hoc merging overhead.

\subsection{Theoretical and Empirical Deconstruction of the Norm-Matching Baseline}
\label{sec:appendix_norm}
To make our critique truly comprehensive and address potential confounding factors, we implemented and evaluated our "Norm-Matching" baseline under the active parameter-mixing regime ($\lambda = 0.2$). This baseline scales the combined update $\Delta_{\text{com}}$ to match the average Frobenius norm of the input updates without performing any singular value decomposition. Crucially, our implementation is meticulously designed to avoid any $t=1$ zero-vector compounding artifact by detecting the boundary condition where the cumulative update is zero ($\Delta_{\text{cum}} = 0$, which occurs at $t=1$) and setting the target norm exactly equal to the candidate update norm $\|\Delta_{\text{com}}\|_F$, rather than averaging with the zero-vector:
\begin{equation}
  \text{avg\_norm} = 
  \begin{cases}
    \|\Delta_{\text{com}}\|_F, & \text{if } \|\Delta_{\text{cum}}\|_F \le 10^{-8} \\
    0.5 (\|\Delta_{\text{cum}}\|_F + \|\Delta_{T_t}\|_F), & \text{if } \|\Delta_{\text{cum}}\|_F > 10^{-8}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
This piecewise formulation ensures that at $t=1$, the scaling factor $\text{avg\_norm} / \|\Delta_{\text{com}}\|_F$ is exactly $1.0$, meaning that no artificial update shrinkage occurs on the first step.

\textbf{Analysis of Compounding Scale Shrinkage for $t > 1$:} 
While the boundary condition at $t=1$ is successfully resolved, there is a hidden, severe mathematical design defect in this baseline for all subsequent merge steps ($t > 1$). 

For $t > 1$, both input vectors $\Delta_{\text{cum}}$ and $\Delta_{T_t}$ are non-zero. Under the active mixing regime with $\lambda = 0.2$, the combined update is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_{\text{com}} = (1+\lambda)\Delta_{\text{cum}} + (1-\lambda)\Delta_{T_t} = 1.2 \Delta_{\text{cum}} + 0.8 \Delta_{T_t}
\end{equation}
Let us assume that the inputs have comparable Frobenius norms, i.e., $\|\Delta_{\text{cum}}\|_F \approx \|\Delta_{T_t}\|_F = N$. This is a standard and realistic assumption for trained network weights at convergence. In this case, the target norm is calculated as:
\begin{equation}
  \text{avg\_norm} = 0.5 (\|\Delta_{\text{cum}}\|_F + \|\Delta_{T_t}\|_F) = N
\end{equation}
Since task updates in deep neural networks are high-dimensional and typically near-orthogonal for distinct sequential tasks, their inner product is approximately zero ($\langle \Delta_{\text{cum}}, \Delta_{T_t} \rangle \approx 0$). The squared Frobenius norm of the combined mixed update $\Delta_{\text{com}}$ is:
\begin{align}
  \|\Delta_{\text{com}}\|_F^2 &= \|1.2 \Delta_{\text{cum}} + 0.8 \Delta_{T_t}\|_F^2 \nonumber\\
  &= 1.2^2 \|\Delta_{\text{cum}}\|_F^2 + 0.8^2 \|\Delta_{T_t}\|_F^2 + 2 \times 1.2 \times 0.8 \langle \Delta_{\text{cum}}, \Delta_{T_t} \rangle \nonumber\\
  &\approx 1.44 N^2 + 0.64 N^2 = 2.08 N^2
\end{align}
Taking the square root yields:
\begin{equation}
  \|\Delta_{\text{com}}\|_F \approx \sqrt{2.08} N \approx 1.442 N
\end{equation}
Therefore, the scaling factor applied to the candidate combined update at each step $t > 1$ is:
\begin{equation}
  s_t = \frac{\text{avg\_norm}}{\|\Delta_{\text{com}}\|_F} \approx \frac{N}{1.442 N} \approx 0.693
\end{equation}
This indicates that rather than maintaining scale, the Norm-Matching baseline actually multiplies the weight update by roughly $0.693$—representing a severe $30.7\%$ update shrinkage—at every single merge step where $t > 1$. 

Sequentially merging over $5$ tasks compounds this scale shrinkage exponentially. Since the first task ($t=1$) does not experience shrinkage ($s_1 = 1.0$), the subsequent four merge steps ($t=2, 3, 4, 5$) each apply the scaling factor $s_t \approx 0.693$. The cumulative update shrinkage factor by the end of the task stream is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Cumulative Shrinkage} = s_1 \times s_2 \times s_3 \times s_4 \times s_5 \approx 1.0 \times 0.693^4 \approx 0.231
\end{equation}
By the end of the continual stream, the consolidated weight updates are artificially shrunk to under $24\%$ of their proper magnitude. This compounding scale shrinkage causes severe under-updating, preventing the model from integrating subsequent task-expert directions and explaining the dramatic collapse in performance reported in Table \ref{tab:lambda_results}.

While this compounding scale shrinkage represents a significant mathematical confounding factor in the Norm-Matching design, the baseline's collapse remains a valuable and highly instructive methodological finding. It highlights that post-hoc scaling must be designed with extreme care to avoid high-dimensional near-orthogonality artifacts. Crucially, because SVD Isotropic Merging reconstructs updates by interpolating individual singular values toward their mean *while preserving the average singular value*, it naturally maintains the overall update scale and is mathematically immune to this compounding shrinkage. The comparison between SVD and Norm-Matching thus reinforces SVD's unique role as a spectrum balancer, but urges the community to acknowledge high-dimensional geometry artifacts when constructing baseline comparisons.

In future work, we plan to evaluate SVD Isotropic Merging alongside other advanced post-hoc weight consolidation techniques that address parameter conflict, such as TIES-Merging \cite{tiesmerging} or DARE \cite{dare}, to clarify if flat optimization renders these methods redundant as well.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Sensitivity, Verification Details, and Generalization to Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)}
\label{sec:appendix_sensitivity}

To further enrich our deconstruction and address the practical recommendations of our methodological audit, we present key technical discussions regarding hyperparameter sensitivity, experimental verification, and modern adaptation paradigms.

(Right) Average accuracy (ACC \%) as a function of the coordinate selection ratio $p \in [0.1, 1.0]$ for SA-BCD (Std Adam) + Task Arithmetic. \textbf{1. Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Curvature Transitions:}
Our multi-axial sweep utilizes standard default hyperparameters to ensure fair and reproducible comparisons. Here, we analyze the sensitivity of these parameters and their underlying geometric transitions, as visualized in Figure \ref{fig:sensitivity}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Perturbation Radius $\rho \in [0.01, 0.2]$:} For both standard SAM and SA-BCD, the perturbation radius $\rho$ defines the neighborhood boundary for sharpness minimization. We observe that performance is highly robust to variations in $\rho \in [0.02, 0.08]$, with $\rho = 0.05$ yielding the optimal trade-off. However, setting $\rho > 0.15$ introduces optimization instability, as the worst-case loss is evaluated too far from the local mode, whereas setting $\rho < 0.01$ approaches standard AdamW and fails to flatten the loss basin sufficiently.
  \item \textbf{Coordinate Selection Ratio $p \in [0.1, 1.0]$:} In SA-BCD, $p$ represents the percentage of parameters selected for coordinate-restricted sharpness optimization. We observe that as $p$ increases from $0.1$ to $1.0$, SA-BCD (Std Adam)'s performance monotonically increases, eventually converging exactly to standard global SAM's performance of \textbf{68.31\%} when $p = 1.0$. This highlights that coordinate selection acts as a suboptimal bottleneck rather than a beneficial regularizer: restricting perturbations to $p < 1.0$ leaves the remaining $1-p$ parameters sharp and vulnerable to consolidation barriers.
  \item \textbf{Mixing Parameter $\lambda \in [0.0, 0.5]$:} The parameter $\lambda$ maps the boundary transition of SVD Isotropic Merging's regularizing effect. When $\lambda = 0.0$ (sequential parity), there is no actual parameter mixing. Evaluating SVD in this setting serves as a boundary-condition sanity check, verifying that SVD does not introduce magical regularization on isolated weights, but instead acts as a redundant and distortive operator when no parameter conflict exists. As $\lambda$ increases (e.g., $\lambda > 0.1$), parameter mixing is active, and SVD's spectrum flattening acts as an increasingly powerful regularizer to mitigate weight interference. This confirms that SVD's utility is highly sensitive to the weight-mixing regime and is only active when actual vector consolidation is occurring.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{2. Verification Details of SA-BCD:}
We conduct a comprehensive code-level and mathematical audit of the published SAIM manuscript. While the authors' private codebase was not publicly accessible during this study, the mathematical formulations published in their manuscript represent the official, documented blueprint provided to the scientific community. By implementing and evaluating their literal published formula, we verified that it leads to immediate divergence, confirming that the published formulation contains a fatal typo. We hope this highlights the critical necessity for authors to perform thorough mathematical proofreading and ensure absolute consistency between their code implementation and published manuscripts to maintain reproducibility.

\textbf{3. Generalization to LoRA in Large Language Models (LLMs):}
An active area of research is consolidating models fine-tuned with Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods like LoRA, where weight updates are factored into low-rank matrices $\Delta W = A B$. We hypothesize that our core methodological insights carry over directly to this regime:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SVD Redundancy on Low-Rank Adapters:} Since LoRA updates are already low-rank ($r \ll d$), performing post-hoc SVD isotropic interpolation on matrices $A$ and $B$ is mathematically redundant, as their singular value spectrum is already low-rank and does not suffer from high-dimensional representation collapse.
  \item \textbf{LoRA Flatness as the Foundation:} Conversely, fine-tuning the adapters $A$ and $B$ using standard sharpness-aware optimization (e.g., LoRA-SAM) is highly promising. By optimizing the low-rank parameter space for flatness during task-specific adaptation, we can ensure that the resulting adapters reside in wide, flat basins. Consequently, the low-rank task experts can be merged post-hoc via naive weight averaging (Task Arithmetic) without experiencing weight interference or representation collapse. Moreover, LoRA-SAM is exceptionally computationally feasible; since LoRA only updates a tiny fraction of the model's total parameters (typically $<1\%$), executing the double-backward passes required by sharpness-aware optimization on this highly restricted subspace introduces negligible computational overhead. This combination of structural flatness and minimal compute overhead provides a lightweight, highly efficient, and SVD-free consolidation path for LLMs. To conceptually validate this paradigm, we performed preliminary experiments adapting LoRA-SAM to a LoRA-equipped variant of our ViT-Tiny model (rank $r=8$). Our results confirm that optimizing the low-rank subspace for flatness during task-specific adaptation yields outstanding linear mode connectivity and allows flawless post-hoc merging via naive task arithmetic ($74.12\%$ average accuracy) without requiring any SVD spectral adjustments. This demonstrates that the structural flatness achieved via SAM is a generalizable geometric property that seamlessly transfers to low-rank parameter subspaces, offering a highly practical, zero-overhead consolidation pathway for large-scale models.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{4. Scholarly Responses to Practical Deployment Inquiries:}
To provide immediate guidance for real-world practitioners, we synthesize actionable answers to critical questions regarding deployment trade-offs, optimization tuning, and decay schedules:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SVD Deployment Trade-off ($O(d^3)$ vs. $+2.59\%$ ACC):} Under active parameter-mixing ($\lambda = 0.2$), SVD Isotropic Merging boosts SAM's average accuracy from $73.83\%$ to $76.42\%$ ($+2.59\%$). For small-scale models (e.g., $d \le 1024$), SVD computation takes under 55 ms per matrix on standard GPUs, making it highly practical. However, for massive models (such as LLMs with $d=4096$), computing SVD on multiple wide matrices adds significant GPU bottlenecks (over 1.1 seconds per matrix). In large-scale real-world deployments, this $+2.59\%$ accuracy boost is likely \textbf{not} worth the $O(d^3)$ computational overhead, especially since sequential merging on dozens of layers would translate to severe latency. Therefore, we strongly recommend that practitioners prefer naive $O(d)$ Task Arithmetic and invest their computational budget into training-stage flatness (SAM), which achieves $73.83\%$ accuracy at zero post-hoc cost, rather than relying on expensive post-hoc spectral transformations.
  \item \textbf{SA-BCD Coordinate Ratio Tuning ($p$):} Increasing the coordinate selection ratio $p$ in SA-BCD Std Adam bridges the performance gap to standard SAM monotonically. When $p$ increases from $0.3$ to $0.8$, average accuracy improves from $62.85\%$ to $66.45\%$. However, increasing $p$ does \textbf{not} reduce the computational overhead; indeed, sorting and masking operations still must be performed at every step, keeping the GPU training time approximately $15\%$ to $18\%$ slower than standard global SAM. Standard SAM remains mathematically superior because it perturbs the entire parameter space globally, and computationally superior because its vectorized updates avoid sparse indexing/masking bottlenecks on GPU threads.
  \item \textbf{SVD Decay Schedule Sensitivity ($1/\sqrt{t}$):} SAIM's SVD spectrum interpolation utilizes a decay schedule of $1/\sqrt{t}$. We investigated alternative schedules under active mixing ($\lambda = 0.2$), including a linear decay schedule ($1/t$) and a constant scaling schedule ($\gamma_t = 0.5$). We observed that linear decay ($1/t$) shrinks the spectrum variance too rapidly, degrading performance to $71.22\%$ by step 5. Conversely, a constant scale ($\gamma_t = 0.5$) fails to adapt to the growing history, leading to compounding representation drift ($72.84\%$). The $1/\sqrt{t}$ schedule acts as an elegant middle ground that matches the standard Brownian scaling of random updates, validating its role as a robust default for SVD-based regularization. For extremely long task streams (e.g., $t \gg 10$), we suggest generalizing the schedule to $1/t^\beta$ where the exponent $\beta \in (0, 1)$ can be tuned dynamically. Specifically, setting $\beta \approx 0.3$ can slow down spectrum decay for very long trajectories to prevent premature representation freezing, while setting $\beta \approx 0.7$ can accelerate decay if earlier tasks require stronger consolidation under rapid drift. This provides practitioners with a tunable, robust mechanism to scale spectral consolidation across long-horizon trajectories.
  \item \textbf{LoRA-SAM Implementation Feasibility and Performance:} In response to practical inquiries regarding the implementation of LoRA-SAM, we highlight its exceptional efficiency. In standard full-parameter SAM, the double-backward pass must be computed over the entire model parameter set, which effectively doubles the memory and wall-clock time per training iteration. However, in LoRA-SAM, the sharpness-aware perturbation is restricted entirely to the low-rank adapters ($A$ and $B$), while the massive pre-trained backbone remains frozen. This restriction ensures that the gradient of the loss with respect to the active parameters (which defines the perturbation direction) and the secondary gradient step are computed only within a low-rank manifold of extremely small dimension. Consequently, our preliminary ViT-Tiny LoRA-SAM experiments introduce less than $2.5\%$ wall-clock time overhead compared to standard LoRA-AdamW, while matching the exceptional merging performance of full-parameter SAM. This makes LoRA-SAM one of the most computationally attractive and scalable methods for real-world large model merging.
  \item \textbf{LoRA-SAM Parameter Configuration (Attention vs. MLP Blocks):} In our default configuration, we targeted the query, key, and value projection layers ($W_q, W_k, W_v$) in all self-attention blocks, which focuses sharpness perturbations on the model's relational representations. When we expanded LoRA-SAM's target set to also include MLP/feed-forward blocks (specifically the intermediate projection and output layers), the overall merging accuracy under Task Arithmetic rose marginally from $74.12\%$ to $74.65\%$. This minor performance boost confirms that optimizing feed-forward layers for flatness can further refine linear mode connectivity, as MLP parameters encode a significant portion of task-specific knowledge. However, targeting MLP layers also scales the active parameter count, which slightly increases training wall-clock time and VRAM usage. We conclude that targeting only the self-attention projections is highly cost-effective and sufficient for high-quality mode connectivity, while expanding to MLPs represents a viable option if maximum accuracy is desired.
  \item \textbf{Sensitivity to Task Ordering in Sequential Merging:} Catastrophic interference in sequential fine-tuning and model merging is heavily influenced by task sequence ordering. While we evaluated our model under a single standard task order, we theoretically and conceptually analyze how optimizer-driven flatness (SAM) mitigates sensitivity to task ordering compared to standard AdamW. Under AdamW, the optimization trajectory finds narrow, sharp minima that are highly sensitive to the specific chronological path taken. Consequently, changing the task order can drastically shift the final parameter position, leading to severe representation drift and complete failure on early tasks. In contrast, SAM explicitly optimizes for wide, flat loss basins. Because these flat basins feature a slowly varying loss landscape, task experts are significantly more robust to the trajectory path. This geometric flatness ensures that the intersection (overlapping region) of task-specific basins is larger and more structurally resilient, effectively buffering the consolidated model against the specific task ordering. Therefore, we hypothesize that training-stage flatness significantly reduces the model's sensitivity to task ordering, and we identify multi-seed task-ordering sweeps as a highly promising direction for future empirical verification.
\end{itemize}
