# Evaluation Component 2: Novelty Check and Literature Contextualization

## Novelty and the "Delta" from Prior Work
The core novelty of this submission is **analytical, methodological, and interpretive**, rather than constructive. While most literature in model merging focuses on designing increasingly complex, parameter-rich, and fine-grained schemes (e.g., AdaMerging [Yang et al., 2024], SyMerge [Jung et al., 2025], DANGLES [Jin et al., 2023], and ZipIt! [Stoica et al., 2023]), this paper takes a critical step back. It is the first to systematically stress-test the fundamental assumption of layer-specificity in model merging.

The "delta" from prior work lies in:
1. **Uncovering the Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox:** No prior work has analyzed how different optimization regimes (zero-order vs. first-order) shape the learned coefficient configurations and their susceptibility to transductive overfitting.
2. **Diagnostic Treatments as Control Mechanisms:** The introduction of Shuffling, Spatial Averaging (Spatial Mean), and Norm-Bounded Noise injection as formal control mechanisms for model merging parameters is highly original and methodologically sound. Prior papers only reported raw test accuracies without validating whether their learned layer-specific curves capture physically meaningful, localized task representational importances.
3. **Representational Decoupling in Merging:** Applying linear Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) [Kornblith et al., 2019] to analyze the representation dynamics of merged models. The finding that CKA similarity can decouple from downstream classification performance (where a spatially averaged model has higher CKA but catastrophically lower accuracy on CIFAR-10) is a critical insight for the neural network interpretability and representation learning communities.
4. **Linking Merging to Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) Overfitting:** The paper situates layer-wise model merging within the broader historical context of TTA literature (such as Tent [Wang et al., 2021], EATA [Niu et al., 2022], and TTA surveys [Liang et al., 2023]). It correctly identifies that optimizing $L \times K$ parameters on a small calibration set of 256 images is a form of transductive learning [Ash et al., 2020] highly prone to transductive overfitting, a concept completely overlooked by previous model merging papers.

## Characterization of Novelty
The novelty is **significant and timely**. The model merging community has been rushing toward hyperparameter-heavy, fine-grained, and adaptive merging schemes without establishing rigorous baselines or validating their foundational assumptions. This paper provides a necessary and well-argued course-correction, showing that what appeared to be "layer-specific representational contributions" was either high-frequency optimization noise (under black-box zero-order search) or a delicate transductive overfitting configuration (under first-order gradient descent).

## Proper Attribution of Ideas and Historical Context
The paper does an excellent job of situating itself within the historical context of deep learning:
- **Model Merging:** Appropriately cites foundational works like Model Soups [Wortsman et al., 2022], Robust Fine-Tuning [Wortsman et al., 2022], and Task Arithmetic [Ilharco et al., 2022].
- **Interference Resolution:** Cites TIES-Merging [Yadav et al., 2023] and Fisher Merging [Matena & Raffel, 2021] to acknowledge existing training-free interference-resolution paradigms.
- **Layer-Wise Merging:** Properly attributes the layer-wise adaptation formulation to AdaMerging [Yang et al., 2024] and SyMerge [Jung et al., 2025].
- **Representational Similarity:** Cites classic interpretability works like CCA [Morcos et al., 2018], SVCCA [Raghu et al., 2017], and linear CKA [Kornblith et al., 2019].
- **Test-Time Adaptation:** Cites Tent [Wang et al., 2021], TTA surveys [Liang et al., 2023], and transductive overfitting [Ash et al., 2020].

## Potential Additions or Missing Literature from a Scholarly Perspective
From a scholarly perspective, while the paper is exceptionally thorough, its discussion of the historical context of optimization landscape flatness could be further enriched:
1. **Flatness and Generalization:** The discovery that the model merging optimization landscape is extremely flat (tolerating up to 50% relative Gaussian noise) is closely connected to the literature on flat minima and generalization. The authors could cite classic papers such as Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997) ("Flat Minima") or Keskar et al. (2017) ("On Large-Batch Training for Deep Learning: Generalization Gap and Sharp Minima") to ground their flatness findings in established optimization theory.
2. **Weight-Space vs. Activation-Space Similarity:** The decoupling of CKA from classification accuracy is a profound finding. The authors could strengthen this discussion by citing work that has explored the mismatch between representation-level metrics and functional model behaviors (e.g., Ding et al., 2021, "What Do Position Embeddings Learn? An Empirical Study of Pre-trained Language Models" or similar studies analyzing the failure modes of CCA/CKA in predicting downstream performance).
3. **Test-Time Model Merging & Neighborhood Experts:** Concurrent or recent 2025/2026 work on Test-Time Model Merging (TTMM) (e.g., retrieving local neighborhood experts at test-time to bypass online gradient descent) is highly relevant. Discussing how their findings of transductive overfitting affect dynamic retrieval-based model merging would make the paper even more comprehensive.
