**Overall Recommendation:** 2: Reject

**Summary:**
This paper introduces Winner-Take-All Sign Election (WTA-Sign), a novel, minimalist, training-free, and hyperparameter-free method for merging deep learning models. It aims to resolve destructive interference (sign conflicts) by letting the expert model with the largest absolute parameter update dictate the sign for each merged parameter. Other updates conforming to this sign are averaged, while conflicting ones are masked out. The authors argue this approach is simpler and outperforms existing methods like TIES-Merging. Experiments on MNIST, SVHN, and CIFAR10 using a CLIP ViT-B-32 backbone claim WTA-Sign maintains zero-shot performance and outperforms TIES-Merging, all with a four-line PyTorch implementation.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty and Simplicity:** The core idea of "Winner-Take-All Sign Election" based on parameter update magnitude is a novel and elegant approach to conflict resolution. It provides a genuinely minimalist alternative to multi-stage heuristic methods like TIES-Merging.
*   **Hyperparameter-Free:** A significant practical strength is that WTA-Sign operates without any task-specific hyperparameters (e.g., pruning thresholds, voting thresholds, scaling factors), making it highly appealing for deployment and reducing tuning effort.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The method is computationally efficient ($O(K \cdot D)$) and memory-light, as demonstrated by its compact, vectorized PyTorch implementation. This is crucial for scaling to large models.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The conceptual figure and mathematical formulation are lucid, and the PyTorch code snippet effectively highlights its simplicity.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Fundamentally Flawed Experimental Setup:** This is the most critical weakness. The "individual expert" models perform *worse* than the pre-trained zero-shot base model on their respective tasks (e.g., MNIST expert: 8.69% vs. zero-shot: 12.70%; CIFAR10 expert: 10.16% vs. zero-shot: 11.23%). The premise of model merging is to combine *specialized models* that excel at their tasks. If fine-tuning degrades performance, the "task vectors" carry negative knowledge, invalidating the entire problem setting and the interpretation of the results. The claim that WTA-Sign "maintains zero-shot base capability" is not a strength if the experts themselves are detrimental.
*   **Weak Theoretical Justification for "Magnitude as Confidence":** The central assumption that parameter update magnitude serves as an unequivocal proxy for "task confidence" or "criticality" is presented intuitively but lacks rigorous theoretical backing. A large update could indicate overfitting, noise, or instability rather than robust "confidence." This fundamental assumption needs deeper exploration and justification.
*   **Limited and Unrepresentative Empirical Evaluation:**
    *   **Small Datasets:** Experiments are confined to small, well-understood vision datasets (MNIST, SVHN, CIFAR10). This does not adequately address the challenges or scale of modern model merging problems, especially for foundation models.
    *   **Insufficient Validation Samples:** Evaluating on "1,000 validation samples per dataset" is statistically weak for a high-stakes conference. This small sample size significantly limits the reliability and generalizability of the reported performance metrics.
    *   **CPU-only Evaluation:** While the method is fast, performing all evaluations on CPU nodes, especially for a ViT-B-32, suggests limited computational resources, which might have contributed to the small sample size and potentially affected other aspects of the experimental rigor.
*   **Missing Baseline:** The prominent model merging method DARE \cite{yu2024dare}, which directly addresses sign conflicts through sparsification, is discussed in related work but conspicuously absent from the empirical comparison. This is a significant omission.
*   **Information Loss:** The strict masking of non-conforming updates, while preventing destructive interference, also inherently discards potentially valuable, complementary information from other experts. This could limit the emergent multi-task capabilities of the merged model in scenarios where nuanced interactions are beneficial.

**Questions for Authors:**
1.  **Expert Model Performance:** The most critical issue is that your "individual experts" perform worse than the zero-shot base model on their respective tasks. Can you explain why your fine-tuning process resulted in performance degradation for MNIST and CIFAR10, and why the experts are consistently worse than the zero-shot base? How do you justify merging such "negative knowledge" task vectors? You must rectify this experimental setup with properly fine-tuned, high-performing experts to validate your claims.
2.  **Theoretical Basis for Magnitude-as-Confidence:** Could you provide a more rigorous theoretical or empirical analysis to support the assumption that larger update magnitudes reliably correlate with "task confidence" or "criticality," rather than potentially noise or overfitting? What are the potential pitfalls of this assumption?
3.  **Comprehensive Benchmarking:**
    *   Please include DARE \cite{yu2024dare} in your experimental comparisons, as it is a highly relevant baseline for conflict resolution.
    *   Can you conduct experiments on larger, more diverse, and challenging datasets (e.g., subsets of ImageNet, specialized domain datasets, or even simple NLP tasks) to demonstrate the generalizability and scalability of WTA-Sign?
    *   Please evaluate on full, standard validation sets rather than small 1,000-sample subsets to ensure statistical robustness.
4.  **Handling of Masked Information:** Given that WTA-Sign explicitly discards non-conforming updates, have you considered scenarios where seemingly conflicting updates might actually be complementary for achieving robust multi-task performance? Is there a risk of information loss that could hinder performance on more complex task combinations?

In summary, while the core idea of WTA-Sign is innovative and appealing for its simplicity, the current empirical validation is fundamentally flawed due to the poor quality of the "expert" models used. This makes it impossible to assess the true efficacy of the method. Without addressing these critical experimental and theoretical weaknesses, the paper cannot meet the high standards of a top-tier machine learning conference.
