The experimental section is the weakest part of this submission and contains critical flaws that undermine the validity and generalizability of the reported results and the paper's central claims.

1.  **Fundamentally Flawed "Expert" Models:**
    *   The most egregious issue is the performance of the "Individual Experts" compared to the "Pretrained (Zero-shot Base)" model, as shown in Table~\ref{tab:main_results}.
    *   For **MNIST**, the zero-shot base model achieves 12.70\%, while the fine-tuned MNIST expert achieves a mere 8.69\%. This indicates that fine-tuning *degraded* the model's performance on MNIST.
    *   Similarly, for **CIFAR10**, the zero-shot base model scores 11.23%, but the fine-tuned CIFAR10 expert only gets 10.16%. Again, fine-tuning resulted in a performance drop.
    *   For **SVHN**, the zero-shot base is 18.65% and the expert is 16.02%, showing a similar pattern of degradation.
    *   The premise of model merging is to combine *specialized expert models* that have *improved* performance on their respective tasks. If the fine-tuned models perform *worse* than the pre-trained base model on their own tasks, they are not "experts" in any meaningful sense. This completely invalidates the experimental setup, as the "task vectors" ($\tau_k = \Theta_k - \Theta_{\text{pre}}$) are effectively capturing negative knowledge or destructive changes. Merging models that are already detrimental to performance is not the standard problem addressed by model merging literature. The conclusion that WTA-Sign "maintains zero-shot base capability" simply means it prevents further degradation from already poorly fine-tuned "experts," which is a much weaker claim than implied.

2.  **Limited and Unrepresentative Datasets:**
    *   The experiments are conducted on MNIST, SVHN, and CIFAR10. While these are common benchmarks, they are relatively small, low-resolution, and well-understood vision datasets. The paper introduces model merging in the context of "deep learning models scale and specialize" and "modern foundation architectures," implying challenges with larger, more complex models and tasks (e.g., ImageNet, medical imaging, natural language processing tasks). Evaluating solely on these three simplistic datasets using a powerful ViT-B-32 backbone does not adequately represent the complexities and scale where model merging is truly critical. The conclusions drawn may not generalize to larger, more challenging scenarios.

3.  **Insufficient Validation Samples and Compute Environment:**
    *   "All models are evaluated on a statistically representative subset of 1,000 validation samples per dataset." This is a severely limited sample size for evaluating neural network performance, especially for a top-tier conference. It significantly reduces the statistical power of the results and makes any observed differences highly susceptible to noise and variability. It's unclear how 1,000 samples can be "statistically representative" when full validation sets for CIFAR10 (10,000) or MNIST (10,000) are much larger.
    *   "Evaluations are executed entirely on standard CPU nodes." While the method itself is lightweight, running a ViT-B-32 for inference on CPU for all experiments suggests limited computational resources. This, combined with the small validation subset, raises concerns about the thoroughness and scalability of the evaluation, potentially driving some of the unusual results.

4.  **Missing Baselines:**
    *   The paper mentions \textbf{DARE} \cite{yu2024dare} in the related work as another method for conflict resolution via randomized dropout and scaling. However, DARE is notably absent from the experimental comparisons. Given its relevance to the problem of sign conflict resolution, its exclusion is a significant oversight and weakens the claim of comprehensive comparison, especially when WTA-Sign is positioned as superior to TIES-Merging (a method with similar goals).
    *   While adaptive methods like AdaMerging and SyMerge are correctly excluded due to their training-time optimization, a brief discussion (even without empirical comparison) regarding their reported performance relative to WTA-Sign would be beneficial.

5.  **Weak Performance Metrics and Interpretations:**
    *   The emphasis on "retaining zero-shot base capability" (14.19% average accuracy) becomes meaningless when the individual fine-tuned experts *themselves* perform worse than this baseline. The goal of merging is typically to create a model that is *better* than the base by combining specialized knowledge, not merely to avoid further degradation from poorly trained components.
    *   The "Unmerged Upper Bound" is an average of individually poor-performing models, which further highlights the issue. A proper upper bound would be the maximum performance achievable across tasks, or perhaps a multi-task trained model.

In conclusion, the experimental section suffers from critical methodological flaws concerning the quality of the fine-tuned expert models, the scale and scope of the datasets, and the evaluation protocol. These issues render the empirical results highly suspect and make it impossible to draw robust conclusions about the true efficacy or superiority of WTA-Sign for the standard model merging problem. The "empirical superiority" claimed in the abstract and conclusion is not convincingly demonstrated under a rigorous experimental setup.
