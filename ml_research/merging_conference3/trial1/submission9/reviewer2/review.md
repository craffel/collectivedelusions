# Peer Review of "Root-Mean-Square Scaling: Unifying Model Merging via Minimalist Scale Calibration"

## 1. Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the critical issue of **representation scale mismatches** in training-free, parameter-space **model merging** (e.g., combining independently fine-tuned task-specific expert neural networks into a single multi-task model). The authors argue that simple linear parameter averaging (e.g., Task Arithmetic) suffers from "task dominance," where tasks with larger parameter updates or layers with disproportionate magnitudes overshadow others during the merge. To resolve this without resorting to computationally expensive singular value decomposition (SVD) or active test-time optimization loops, the authors introduce **Standard-Deviation Scaling (SD-Scale)** and its mathematically stable, non-translation-invariant counterpart, **Root-Mean-Square Scaling (RMS-Scale)**. 

Both methods normalize task vectors layer-wise to establish a balanced, isotropic update direction, and then project them back into the network's natural adaptation space by rescaling with the average of the original task-wise scales. Furthermore, to eliminate the need for disjoint validation datasets, the authors derive a completely **Parameter-Free RMS-Scale (PF-RMS)** variant. PF-RMS analytically estimates and inverts the high-dimensional shrinkage that naturally occurs when orthogonal task vectors are averaged. The authors also present:
1. **A mathematical proof of Frobenius parity:** Demonstrating that layer-wise RMS normalization is equivalent to parameter-count-scaled Frobenius-norm normalization.
2. **LoRA sequential streaming logic:** A layer-by-layer streaming implementation to bound the peak memory footprint to <150MB, making merging of massive foundation models practical on commodity hardware.
3. **A hybrid pipeline (Ties-RMS-Scale):** Resolving coordinate-wise sign conflicts first before applying scale calibration.

Evaluations are conducted on a multi-seed, multi-task CNN benchmark (MNIST, FashionMNIST, KMNIST) fine-tuned under uncoordinated downstream schedules, and physically verified on the actual weight matrices of the official OpenAI CLIP ViT-B/32 visual encoder. On the CNN benchmark, tuned RMS-Scale achieves an average test accuracy of **73.22%** (matching or exceeding SVD Isotropic at 73.13%), and out-of-the-box un-tuned PF-RMS achieves **72.23%** (outperforming un-tuned Ties-Merging and Task Arithmetic). On the CLIP benchmark, RMS-Scale achieves identical activation-space cosine alignment (57.74%) to SVD Isotropic while executing over **100x faster** (5.67ms vs. 571.92ms per layer).

## 2. Main Strengths
*   **Conceptual and Practical Simplicity (Occam's Razor):** The paper makes a highly persuasive case against "complexity creep" and "heuristic bloat" in model merging. It demonstrates that expensive, SVD-based Riemannian alignments or active test-time optimization loops can be matched or exceeded by simple, two-line element-wise scale calibrations.
*   **Theoretical Grounding and Parity Proof:** The formal mathematical proof demonstrating that layer-wise RMS normalization is equivalent to parameter-count-scaled Frobenius-norm normalization on matrix layers is a valuable contribution. It provides a solid geometric bridge to classical manifold alignments and explains why simple RMS scaling achieves exact activation-space alignment parity with SVD-based isotropic merging.
*   **Elegant Shrinkage Compensation (PF-RMS):** The analytical derivation of the layer-wise alignment ratio $\alpha^l = \text{RMS}(\bar{\tau}_{\text{norm}}^l) \le 1.0$ and its dynamic inversion ($\lambda^l = 1 / \alpha^l$) provides a highly elegant and well-reasoned solution to parameter shrinkage in high-dimensional spaces. PF-RMS achieves outstanding performance completely out-of-the-box, offering massive practical value when downstream validation datasets are unavailable.
*   **High-Dimensional Empirical Validation:** The physical evaluation directly on the 36 projection layers of the official OpenAI CLIP ViT-B/32 visual encoder is exemplary. It provides concrete empirical proof of the 100x wall-clock speedup (5.67ms vs. 571.92ms per layer) and exact activation alignment parity with SVD Isotropic on real-world weight distributions, heavily bolstering the paper's scalability arguments.
*   **Extensive Architectural Completeness:** The paper goes far beyond a simple proposal, thoroughly detailing memory-efficient sequential LoRA streaming to bound memory spikes to <150MB, comparing alternative scale estimators (showing the Harmonic Mean excels at dampening outlier tasks), exploring channel-wise partitioning (CW-RMS) for localized scaling, and proposing a hybrid pipeline (Ties-RMS-Scale) to resolve coordinate sign conflicts prior to scale calibration.

## 3. Main Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)
*   **Positioning and Gaps in Prior Art Contextualization:** While the paper situates itself well within standard baselines (Task Arithmetic, Ties-Merging, DARE, AdaMerging, SAIM), it ignores other closely related norm-based normalization and scaling techniques in model-merging literature. Specifically:
    - **DisTaC** (Distribution-aware Task Arithmetic) identifies that systematic differences in task vector norms (caused by uncoordinated schedules) degrade merging, and suggests adjusting the norms via scaling.
    - **FroM** (Frobenius Norm-Based Adaptive Model Merging) uses the Frobenius norm of task vectors to assign merging weights to different experts.
    - To provide a comprehensive historical context, the authors must cite and discuss these closely related works. Explaining how the proposed element-wise, layer-wise RMS normalization represents a much finer and more structured realignment than coarse, model-level norm scaling would heavily strengthen their scholarly positioning and prevent false claims of absolute novelty regarding norm-based scaling.
*   **Evaluation Scale Gap:** Although the physical simulation on OpenAI CLIP ViT-B/32 weight matrices is highly valuable, the main end-to-end multi-task classification accuracy is still evaluated on a custom SimpleCNN on small, toy datasets (MNIST, FashionMNIST, KMNIST). While the activation alignment results strongly imply that the accuracy gains will translate to larger models, fully closing the evaluation scale gap (e.g., evaluating end-to-end downstream classification accuracies of merged CLIP models on complex datasets like Stanford Cars, DTD, EuroSAT, etc.) remains a necessary step to confirm real-world performance.
*   **Philosophical Nuance of the "Parameter-Free" Claim:** The paper presents PF-RMS as "parameter-free." However, it still incorporates a clipping safeguard threshold $\gamma(K) = C \cdot \sqrt{K}$ parameterized by a safety multiplier $C$ (typically $C = 1.2$ or $1.5$). While sensitivity analysis shows that the model is highly robust to the choice of $C \in [1.0, 2.0]$ and performs identically, introducing a clipping safeguard technically introduces a hyperparameter, which slightly undermines the "parameter-free" framing.

---

## 4. Specific Suggestions and Questions for Authors
1.  **Refine Literature Positioning:** In the related work section, please cite and discuss **DisTaC** and **FroM**. Elaborate on the differences between these coarser norm-scaling methods and your granular, layer-wise, and element-wise scale calibration. This will enrich the scholarly landscape and properly attribute the progression of norm-based insights in the field.
2.  **Define Default Scale Estimator:** In Section 3, the formulas are written using the Arithmetic Mean. In Section 4, you demonstrate that the Harmonic Mean and Geometric Mean slightly outperform the Arithmetic Mean by dampening outlier tasks. Please clarify in the final draft which scale estimator is chosen as the default for the main experimental results in Table 1.
3.  **Clarify the Clipping Safeguard:** Address the philosophical nuance of the safety multiplier $C$ in the clipping threshold $\gamma(K)$. Confirm if a default value (such as $C = 1.2$) is recommended as a standard for all future library releases, which would solidify the parameter-free claim for practitioners.
4.  **Discussion on Coordinate-wise Sign Conflicts:** In Section 3.5, you discuss how Ties-RMS-Scale resolves sign conflicts prior to calculating the alignment ratio $\alpha^l$. Have you evaluated Ties-RMS-Scale or PF-Ties-RMS empirically on the CNN benchmark? If so, did it provide an additional boost over standard RMS-Scale? Adding a brief quantitative discussion would be highly valuable.

## 5. Evaluation Scores

*   **Soundness: Good**
    The mathematical formulations, the Frobenius parity proof, and the dynamic scale derivation are highly sound. The clipping safeguard $\gamma(K)$ is mathematically elegant and robustly designed to scale with the task pool size $K$. While the main end-to-end task is on a toy CNN, the physical activation-space simulation on real CLIP visual encoder weight layers heavily supports the scalability claims.
*   **Presentation: Excellent**
    The paper is exceptionally well-structured, clear, and engaging. The transitions from standard deviation to RMS, and the subsequent analytical derivation of PF-RMS, are presented as a cohesive narrative. The figures and tables are highly polished, and the inclusion of a two-line PyTorch implementation snippet makes the method highly accessible.
*   **Significance: Excellent**
    By demonstrating that element-wise RMS scaling achieves identical activation-space alignment and isotropic balance to SVD-based isotropic merging at a **100x wall-clock speedup**, this paper has high significance. It provides a practical, linear-time, training-free, and parameter-free model-merging tool that can steer the community away from "complexity creep."
*   **Originality: Good**
    While normalization and scaling have been explored in other deep learning contexts (e.g., RMSNorm, Weight Standardization) and coarser norm-scaling has been attempted in merging (e.g., DisTaC, FroM), the combination and the analytical derivation of the parameter-free scale factor ($1/\alpha^l$) representing high-dimensional update shrinkage is highly creative and original.

### **Overall Recommendation: 5 (Accept)**
This is a mathematically elegant, highly practical, and scientifically rigorous paper. It successfully applies "Occam's Razor" to model merging, demonstrating that complex SVD-based Riemannian alignments and active test-time optimization loops can be matched or exceeded by minimalist scale calibration in linear time. The physical evaluation on actual CLIP weight matrices, the derivation of the parameter-free PF-RMS variant, and the thorough engineering details (such as sequential LoRA streaming) make this submission highly complete and immediately valuable for the machine learning community. Incorporating the suggested contextual citations (DisTaC, FroM) will fully bridge the scholarly landscape.
