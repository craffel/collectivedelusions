# 2. Novelty Check

## Assessment of Key Novel Aspects
The paper introduces several notable contributions and insights:
1. **Deterministic Norm-Preserving Rescaling ($1/p$ heuristic) for Magnitude Pruning:** While scaling active updates by the reciprocal of the retention rate has been used in stochastic pruning (e.g., DARE's Bernoulli dropout), applying this scale factor deterministically to coordinate-aligned, magnitude-based pruned task vectors is novel. The authors provide mathematical derivations showing that deterministically selecting the largest absolute parameters and scaling them by $1/p$ actually boosts the expected $L_1$ update norm by $2.58\times$ to $3.30\times$ (depending on Laplace/Gaussian distribution assumptions). They characterize this over-scaling as a beneficial "Signal-Strength Boost" that prevents specialized task updates from being drowned out by dense base model weights.
2. **The Saliency Double-Bind Formulation:** The authors formally analyze and expose a fundamental double-bind in layer-wise adaptive budget allocation schemes (like NP-BTVP-S) under rescaling:
   * *Global Scaling Imbalance:* Scaling active parameters by $1/p$ causes inter-layer scale distortion, silencing low-saliency layers or over-amplifying high-saliency layers.
   * *Layer-wise Variance Blowup:* Scaling layers by $1/p_l$ causes local variance and parameter noise amplification in low-saliency layers where $p_l$ is very small.
   This explains why the simpler, global Uniform Pruning (NP-BTVP-U) is superior—representing a refreshing, highly pragmatic contribution that favors simplicity and scale harmony over complex layer-wise optimization.
3. **Geometric Separation of Flatness and Coordinate Sparsification:** The paper challenges the common assumption that training-stage loss-landscape flatness (e.g., via SAM) automatically provides a better buffer against post-hoc unstructured weight pruning. The authors honestly report that when paired with norm-preserving rescaling, both standard AdamW and SAM experts exhibit nearly identical levels of extreme sparsification resilience. This separates the geometry of isotropic weight merging (where flatness is crucial) from coordinate-aligned sparsification.

## The 'Delta' from Prior Work
* **Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2022):** Task Arithmetic requires storing fully dense task vectors (e.g., 115 MB per CLIP ViT-B/32 expert). NP-BTVP reduces this by 90% to 95% post-hoc, allowing storage of sparse vectors in formats like CSR (~11.5 MB to 23 MB) and merging on-the-fly with zero computational overhead.
* **TIES-Merging (Yadav et al., 2023):** TIES uses magnitude pruning to "denoise" task vectors before merging but does not optimize for extreme storage compression or apply norm-preserving scaling. NP-BTVP outscores TIES-Merging by over 3.6% to 3.8% under both SAM and AdamW at half the parameter budget ($p=0.10$ vs $p=0.20$), showing that deterministic scale correction is highly effective.
* **DARE-Merging (Yu et al., 2023):** DARE relies on stochastic Bernoulli dropout to randomly drop parameters and rescales the remaining ones to keep expectations constant. NP-BTVP-U uses deterministic magnitude pruning to retain the most significant coordinates, avoiding the stochastic noise and parameter-discarding risks inherent in random dropout. At $p=0.10$ (90% sparsity), NP-BTVP-U is highly competitive with DARE-Merging at $p_{\text{drop}}=0.80$ (80% sparsity).

## Characterization of Novelty
The novelty of this paper is **significant and highly practical**. It is not a complex, fragile theoretical novelty; instead, it is a **pragmatic, elegant, and training-free** weight-space compression framework. The "delta" from prior merging works (TIES and DARE) is very clear: it re-evaluates weight pruning not merely as a denoising step, but as a practical edge-deployment storage compression pipeline, uncovering valuable trade-offs (the Saliency Double-Bind) and geometric insights along the way.
