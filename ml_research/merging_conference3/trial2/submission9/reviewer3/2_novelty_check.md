# 2. Novelty and Related Work Check

## Characterization of Novelty
The paper's novelty is primarily **conceptual, analytical, and deconstructive**, rather than purely methodological. The proposed algorithm, **Barycentric Proximity-Anchored Merging (BPAM)**, is mathematically simple and consists of well-known components:
1. **Convex Barycentric Simplex Projection:** Using non-negative constraints and a sum-to-one constraint is a standard convex optimization formulation. It has been used widely in classical machine learning (e.g., portfolio optimization, mixture models) and specifically in deep learning weight averaging (e.g., Model Soups, which evaluates convex combinations of weights). The projection method used—ray-scaling ($L_1$-normalization)—is a computationally simple approximation to the exact Euclidean projection on the simplex, which is well-established in optimization literature (e.g., Duchi et al., 2008).
2. **Mean-Field Proximity Regularizer:** This is a standard quadratic penalty (or $\ell_2$ regularization) pulling the weights toward the uniform prior $\frac{1}{K+1}$. Conceptually, this is equivalent to a Gaussian prior centered at the uniform simplex centroid, a highly standard Bayesian or regularized estimation technique.
3. **KL-Divergence Teacher-Guided Objective:** Using KL-divergence to align predictions with a teacher network is a foundational technique in Knowledge Distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and is directly adopted from prior test-time adaptive merging methods like AdaMerging.

The authors do not claim that these mathematical tools are individually novel. Instead, their novelty claim resides in the **surgical application** of this minimalist model to deconstruct and audit more complex, overparameterized test-time adaptive merging methods (such as FoldMerge's 2.6M parameter normalizing flows or AdaMerging's layer-wise scaling). The "delta" is the scientific insight generated by mapping the performance boundaries.

## Detailed Evaluation of the "Delta" from Prior Work

### 1. Delta from Static Merging (Task Arithmetic, TIES, DARE)
- **Static Merging** utilizes training-free heuristics (linear interpolation, pruning, sign consensus) to merge weights once, with zero parameters optimized at test-time.
- **BPAM-Static** introduces test-time adaptation with $K$ parameters. The delta is that BPAM-Static achieves similar or slightly better performance ($69.21\%$) compared to Task Arithmetic ($69.10\%$) with zero training data, but requires local test-time gradient descent epochs. However, it underperforms TIES-Merging ($72.90\%$) under frozen heads, proving that global scaling alone cannot resolve weight conflicts as effectively as sign-consensus and magnitude-pruning.

### 2. Delta from SOTA Test-Time Adaptive Merging (AdaMerging, SyMerge, FoldMerge)
- **AdaMerging** optimizes layer-wise task-coefficients (typically 1,264 parameters) using entropy minimization or teacher KL-divergence.
- **SyMerge** optimizes low-rank coordinate-scaling adapters on a single task-specific layer while optimizing global coefficients.
- **FoldMerge** optimizes a 2.6M parameter normalizing flow to warp weight space.
- **BPAM-Static** reduces the parameter footprint by **99.99%** compared to FoldMerge and **99.3%** compared to AdaMerging, optimizing exactly $K=8$ global task-scalars. The delta here is that BPAM serves as a **boundary probe baseline** showing that:
  - Global task-wise scaling alone under frozen heads ($69.21\%$) is far inferior to layer-wise scaling (AdaMerging: $83.17\%$) or continuous coordinate warps (FoldMerge/SyMerge: $83.56\%$).
  - Layer-wise degrees of freedom are therefore *physically essential* for genuine weight-space adaptation gains, resolving cross-task weight conflicts.
  - Joint optimization under low-parameter weight constraints (BPAM-Full: $75.22\%$) is heavily dominated by downstream classification head adaptation (decision-boundary tuning) rather than representation alignment in the weight space.

## Historical Context and Attribution of Ideas
From a scholarly perspective, the paper does an excellent job of situating itself within the model merging and test-time adaptation literature.
- **Proper Attribution:** The paper properly credits **Task Arithmetic** (Ilharco et al., 2022), **TIES-Merging** (Yadav et al., 2023), **AdaMerging** (Yang et al., 2024), **SyMerge** (Jung et al., 2025/2026), and **FoldMerge** (Anonymous, 2025), alongside classical TTA methods (Tent, SHOT, MEMO).
- **Nuanced Understanding:** The paper references two recent auditing preprints: **SAIM Audit** (Anonymous, 2025) and **Layer-wise Model Merging Sanity Check** (Anonymous, 2025), leveraging their critical findings to motivate its own minimalist philosophy. This builds a coherent, timely narrative around the need for simplicity and the risks of transductive overfitting.
- **Missing Connections (Areas for Scholarly Improvement):**
  - While the authors cite standard convex optimization texts (Boyd & Vandenberghe, 2004), they could more explicitly link their barycentric combination to the literature on **convex model combinations and ensembling** (e.g., classical papers like Perrone & Cooper, 1993, "When networks disagree: Ensemble methods for hybrid neural networks").
  - The "0-weight SVHN/MNIST performance" is an intriguing scholarly observation. The paper explains it via CKA representation similarity and a "compact shared weight-space basin." To strengthen this historical and theoretical grounding, the authors should cite literature on **neural network loss landscapes and weight-space connectivity** (e.g., Frankle et al., 2020, "Linear Mode Connectivity and the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis"; Garipov et al., 2018, "Loss Surfaces, Mode Connectivity, and Fast Ensembling of DNNs"). This would better situate their finding that fine-tuned experts reside in a tight, shared representation basin.
  - The authors discuss how unconstrained scaling can lead to "activation collapse" or "scale distortion," which FoldMerge resolves via normalizing flows. The authors' claim that a convex combination naturally preserves activation scales is geometrically sound. To make this contextualization complete, they should reference works on **network activation scaling and normalization** (e.g., LayerNorm by Ba et al., 2016, which is in the bibliography but not explicitly linked to this mathematical property in the text).

Overall, the paper is highly scholarly, transparent about its incremental methodological novelty, and provides a valuable, historically-informed deconstruction of the current trajectory of the test-time model merging field.
