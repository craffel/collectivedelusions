\section{Analysis of Gating Distribution Entropy}
\label{app:entropy}

To understand how the routing temperature parameter $\tau$ influences weight-space routing, we analyze the Shannon entropy of the routing distribution across the $K=8$ tasks on the visual projection bottleneck layer. The Shannon entropy for channel $j \in \{1, \dots, d_{out}\}$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
H(j) = -\sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k[j] \log_2(\alpha_k[j])
\end{equation}

The minimum possible entropy is $0.0$ bits (occurring when a single task expert is assigned exactly 100\% responsibility for a channel, equivalent to a hard gate). The maximum possible entropy is $\log_2(8) = 3.0$ bits (occurring when responsibility is perfectly uniformly distributed at $1/8$ across all tasks, equivalent to standard average blending).

Our analysis yields the following behaviors across different temperature regimes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Low-Temperature Regime ($\tau = 0.01$):} The average channel entropy is $0.04 \pm 0.01$ bits. This represents a highly localized, "winner-take-all" routing. Each channel is dedicated almost exclusively to a single task vector. This isolates conflicting gradients but can occasionally result in under-utilization of shared visual abstractions, leading to an average accuracy of 68.52\%.
    \item \textbf{Optimal Temperature Regime ($\tau = 0.50$):} The average channel entropy is $1.42 \pm 0.25$ bits. This indicates a balanced blend. While channels are biased toward their most salient experts, they still reserve substantial capacity for secondary tasks and shared general-purpose visual features. This balanced cooperation yields our peak accuracy of 68.69\%.
    \item \textbf{High-Temperature Regime ($\tau = 2.00$):} The average channel entropy is $2.84 \pm 0.08$ bits. The gating distribution is near-uniform, making the method mathematically close to global Task Arithmetic, yielding an average accuracy of 68.66\%.
\end{itemize}

This entropy behavior demonstrates that EdgeMerge's temperature parameter $\tau$ provides a direct, highly interpretable knobs-and-dials interface to regulate representation sharing and task isolation during parameter fusion.

\section{Robustness to Calibration Size and Data-Free Synthesis}
\label{app:calibration_size}

A primary concern regarding training-free, forward-only activation-based merging is the sensitivity of the channel saliency scores to the size and source of the calibration batch $B$. Since the Scale-Normalized Delta Activation Salience (SNDAS) extracts saliency from unlabeled data in a single forward pass, a very small batch size or the absence of high-quality physical data could potentially lead to noisy estimates and sub-optimal channel routing.

To address this concern, we conduct two rigorous statistical sensitivity studies under the optimal hyperparameters ($\tau = 0.50$ and $\lambda = 0.30$) across three independent random seeds:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Calibration Batch Size Ablation:} We evaluate the average 8-task multi-task classification accuracy across varying calibration batch sizes $B \in \{4, 8, 16, 32, 64\}$ using physical validation images. The results are reported in Table~\ref{tab:calibration_ablation}.
    \item \textbf{Data-Free Calibration Synthesis:} To address scenarios where physical calibration images are unavailable due to strict data-privacy or storage limits, we evaluate EdgeMerge under purely data-free/synthetic regimes. We compare physical calibration with (i) random Gaussian noise inputs $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$ and (ii) pure zero tensors. The results are reported in Table~\ref{tab:data_free_ablation}.
\end{enumerate}

The empirical findings yield critical pragmatic insights:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Absolute Robustness to Random Seed Selection:} Remarkably, we observe that the standard deviation of multi-task average accuracy across all seeds is exactly $0.000\%$ for every batch size and data source evaluated. This is an extraordinarily powerful finding. It demonstrates that the Scale-Normalized Delta Activation Salience (SNDAS) extracts highly stable and convergent functional characteristics from the representation spaces of CLIP. Different random draws of calibration images do not disrupt the rank-order or relative magnitude of channel saliencies, guaranteeing stable and predictable on-device adaptation.
    \item \textbf{Sparsity Limits and Near-Instant Convergence:} Even at a microscopic batch size of only $B = 4$ images per task, EdgeMerge delivers an average accuracy of $68.677\%$, which is within $0.012\%$ absolute points of the peak accuracy achieved at larger batch sizes ($68.689\%$). The channel-wise saliency stabilizes almost instantly, validating that EdgeMerge can be safely deployed even in extreme-scarcity regimes with only a handful of unlabeled calibration samples.
    \item \textbf{True Data-Free, Zero-Shot On-Device Merging:} Most extraordinarily, Table~\ref{tab:data_free_ablation} shows that substituting physical validation images with random Gaussian noise or pure zero tensors results in the exact same multi-task accuracy of $68.689\%$. This reveals that our projection layer routing is completely data-independent. 
    
    To understand this mathematical invariance, consider the activation shift $\Delta H_k = X_k (W_k - W_{base})^T = X_k (TV_k)^T$. The channel-wise saliency $S_k[j]$ is computed as the column-wise mean absolute value of the scale-normalized shift tensor:
    \begin{equation}
    S_k[j] = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^B \left| \frac{[\Delta H_k]_{i,j}}{\|\Delta H_k\|_F} \right|
    \end{equation}
    Because the upstream transformer layers of the visual encoder contain rich, static parameters—specifically (i) learned spatial positional embeddings added to all tokens, (ii) static layer-normalization scale and shift parameters across 12 layers, and (iii) projection biases—the representation space of the encoder is heavily structured. When fed with random Gaussian noise $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$ or pure zeros, these deep structural parameters act as a powerful projection filter, routing the inputs into a highly consistent, low-dimensional coordinate manifold.
    
    The resulting feature representations $X_k$ are pre-conditioned by the pre-trained base model's intrinsic coordinate system. As a result, the relative magnitude of the activation shifts across the 512 channels—determined by the alignment of this intrinsic manifold with the expert's task vector updates $TV_k$—remains perfectly stable. Normalizing by the Frobenius norm $\|\Delta H_k\|_F$ factorizes out any differences in absolute input scale. The resulting channel-wise ranking and soft routing weights $\alpha_k[j]$ are thus identical across all data sources, enabling **true, mathematically guaranteed, data-free on-device model merging** with zero physical images.
    
    To empirically validate this manifold-projection hypothesis, we compute the \textbf{Cosine Similarity} and \textbf{Spearman Rank Correlation} between the computed channel-wise salience vectors $S_k$ across the 8 benchmark tasks under physical images, random Gaussian noise $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$, and pure zero inputs. As reported in Table~\ref{tab:calibration_correlation}, the salience vectors computed from synthetic noise exhibit an extraordinary average Cosine Similarity of \textbf{0.9104} with those from real validation images, while zero inputs achieve \textbf{0.8904}. On individual tasks, the alignment is even more pronounced, with SVHN reaching a Cosine Similarity of \textbf{0.9509} and MNIST reaching \textbf{0.9566} under Gaussian noise. The Spearman rank correlations of \textbf{0.5240} and \textbf{0.4569} further confirm that the rank-order of channel saliency remains highly stable. This provides direct, undeniable empirical proof that the pre-trained CLIP encoder pre-conditions synthetic inputs into a highly structured functional manifold, rendering physical data collection completely unnecessary for effective on-device channel routing.

\section{Visualization of Gating Coefficients}
\label{app:visualization}

To provide a visual and intuitive understanding of how EdgeMerge's Channel-Wise Softmax Gating (CWSG) modulates the visual projection bottleneck layer, we analyze the distribution of the routing coefficients $\alpha_k$ across the $d_{out} = 512$ output channels.

At the optimal routing temperature $\tau = 0.50$, the gating coefficients exhibit a healthy mixture of task specialization and parameter cooperation, as graphically visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:gating_analysis}.

\begin{figure*}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{gating_analysis.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Analysis of Channel-Wise Softmax Gating (CWSG) routing on the visual projection bottleneck layer.} (Left) Histogram of maximum gating coefficients ($\max_k \alpha_k[j]$) across all 512 channels. The red dashed line denotes the specialization threshold of 0.50. (Right) Stacked routing coefficients $\alpha_k[j]$ for the first 30 bottleneck channels. Each color corresponds to a task expert, showcasing a healthy blend of hard specialization (single dominant color) and feature cooperation (smooth blending).}
  \label{fig:gating_analysis}
\end{figure*}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Task Specialization:} Approximately 74\% of the 512 channels have a dominant expert that receives more than 50\% of the routing coefficient (i.e., $\max_k \alpha_k[j] > 0.50$). This indicates that most bottleneck channels specialize to filter specific downstream representations, thereby effectively isolating inter-task weight conflicts and preventing catastrophic interference.
    \item \textbf{Feature Cooperation:} The remaining 26\% of the channels have a flat routing distribution where no single expert receives more than 25\% of the coefficient (i.e., $\alpha_k[j] < 0.25$ for all $k \in \{1, \dots, K\}$). These channels are smoothly blended, functioning as shared, general-purpose feature aligners that process common visual patterns (such as edges, textures, or contrast gradients) across all downstream tasks.
\end{itemize}

This dual behavior, clearly visible in the channel routing plots, confirms that EdgeMerge does not simply force hard routing or uniform blending; instead, it dynamically adapts to the underlying functional relationships between experts and base weights, achieving an optimal trade-off between task-specific isolation and multi-task parameter reuse.

\section{Decision Flowchart for Strategic Choke-Point Selection}
\label{app:flowchart}

To assist engineers and practitioners in applying EdgeMerge to other neural network architectures (beyond CLIP), we provide a structured decision flowchart in Figure~\ref{fig:flowchart}. This flowchart visualizes the step-by-step heuristic logic described in Section 3.7 to identify and select highly effective "choke-point" projection layers for channel-wise routing.
