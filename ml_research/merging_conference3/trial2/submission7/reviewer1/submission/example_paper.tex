\section{Detailed Mathematical Derivations}
\label{sec:math_derivation}

In this section, we present the step-by-step algebraic expansion of the connection between the temperature-scaled Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence and the Helmholtz Free Energy Discrepancy objective, establishing the proof for Equation~24 in the main text.

By definition, the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the expert probability distribution $p^{(k)}(x; T)$ and the merged model distribution $p^{(MTL, k)}(x; T)$ on input $x$ is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}_{KL} \left( p^{(k)}(x; T) \parallel p^{(MTL, k)}(x; T) \right) = \sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) \ln \left( \frac{p_c^{(k)}(x; T)}{p_c^{(MTL, k)}(x; T)} \right)
\end{equation}

Using the properties of logarithms, we split the fraction into individual terms:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_split}
\mathcal{D}_{KL} \left( p^{(k)}(x; T) \parallel p^{(MTL, k)}(x; T) \right) = \sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) \ln p_c^{(k)}(x; T) - \sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) \ln p_c^{(MTL, k)}(x; T)
\end{equation}

Recall that our physical Boltzmann distribution mappings map class logits $f(x; \theta)$ to states in a canonical ensemble, where classification logits function as negative microstate energies: $E_c \equiv -f_c(x; \theta)$. Under this mapping:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_boltz_expert}
p_c^{(k)}(x; T) = \frac{\exp\left( \frac{f_c(x; \theta_k)}{T} \right)}{Z_k(x; T)} \quad \text{and} \quad p_c^{(MTL, k)}(x; T) = \frac{\exp\left( \frac{f_c(x; \theta^{MTL})}{T} \right)}{Z_{MTL, k}(x; T)}
\end{equation}
where the associated canonical partition functions are defined as:
\begin{equation}
Z_k(x; T) = \sum_{j=1}^{C_k} \exp\left( \frac{f_j(x; \theta_k)}{T} \right) \quad \text{and} \quad Z_{MTL, k}(x; T) = \sum_{j=1}^{C_k} \exp\left( \frac{f_j(x; \theta^{MTL})}{T} \right)
\end{equation}

Taking the natural logarithm of both Boltzmann distributions in Equation~\eqref{eq:app_boltz_expert}:
\begin{align}
\ln p_c^{(k)}(x; T) &= \frac{f_c(x; \theta_k)}{T} - \ln Z_k(x; T) \\
\ln p_c^{(MTL, k)}(x; T) &= \frac{f_c(x; \theta^{MTL})}{T} - \ln Z_{MTL, k}(x; T)
\end{align}

Now, substitute these logarithmic representations into the split KL divergence formulation in Equation~\eqref{eq:app_split}:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:app_substitute}
\mathcal{D}_{KL} \left( p^{(k)}(x; T) \parallel p^{(MTL, k)}(x; T) \right) = &\sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) \left( \frac{f_c(x; \theta_k)}{T} - \ln Z_k(x; T) \right) \notag \\
&- \sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) \left( \frac{f_c(x; \theta^{MTL})}{T} - \ln Z_{MTL, k}(x; T) \right)
\end{align}

We distribute the summation terms. Since the partition functions $Z_k(x; T)$ and $Z_{MTL, k}(x; T)$ are independent of the class index $c$, we factor their logarithms out of the class-wise summation:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:app_factor}
\mathcal{D}_{KL} \left( p^{(k)} \parallel p^{(MTL, k)} \right) = &\frac{1}{T} \sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) f_c(x; \theta_k) - \ln Z_k(x; T) \sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) \notag \\
&- \frac{1}{T} \sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) f_c(x; \theta^{MTL}) + \ln Z_{MTL, k}(x; T) \sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T)
\end{align}

Because $p^{(k)}(x; T)$ is a valid probability distribution, its elements sum to unity over all possible class states: $\sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) = 1$. Utilizing this property, Equation~\eqref{eq:app_factor} simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}_{KL} \left( p^{(k)} \parallel p^{(MTL, k)} \right) = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) \left( f_c(x; \theta_k) - f_c(x; \theta^{MTL}) \right) - \ln Z_k(x; T) + \ln Z_{MTL, k}(x; T)
\end{equation}

Multiplying both sides of the equation by the global temperature $T$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_temp_kl}
T \cdot \mathcal{D}_{KL} \left( p^{(k)} \parallel p^{(MTL, k)} \right) = \sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) \left( f_c(x; \theta_k) - f_c(x; \theta^{MTL}) \right) - T \ln Z_k(x; T) + T \ln Z_{MTL, k}(x; T)
\end{equation}

By definition, the Helmholtz Free Energy $F(x; T)$ of a canonical Boltzmann ensemble is related to its partition function $Z(x; T)$ via $F(x; T) = -T \ln Z(x; T)$. Therefore:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:app_fe_def}
F_k(x; T) &= -T \ln Z_k(x; T) \\
F_{MTL, k}(x; T) &= -T \ln Z_{MTL, k}(x; T) \implies - F_{MTL, k}(x; T) = T \ln Z_{MTL, k}(x; T)
\end{align}

Finally, substituting the definitions of the Helmholtz Free Energies from Equations~\eqref{eq:app_fe_def} back into our temperature-scaled KL expression in Equation~\eqref{eq:app_temp_kl} yields:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:app_final_deriv}
T \cdot \mathcal{D}_{KL} \left( p^{(k)}(x; T) \parallel p^{(MTL, k)}(x; T) \right) = &\sum_{c=1}^{C_k} p_c^{(k)}(x; T) \left( f_c(x; \theta_k) - f_c(x; \theta^{MTL}) \right) \notag \\
&+ \left( F_k(x; T) - F_{MTL, k}(x; T) \right)
\end{align}

This completes the mathematical proof, demonstrating that the temperature-scaled KL divergence is exactly equal to the sum of the expected negative energy difference (average microstate energy discrepancy) and the global Helmholtz Free Energy state difference between the expert and merged multi-task models.

\section{Conceptual Grounding in Spin Glass and Loss Landscapes}
\label{sec:spin_glass_details}

In this section, we provide a deeper conceptual and theoretical contextualization of ThermoMerge in the physics of disordered systems and high-dimensional optimization surfaces.

\subsection{Model Merging as Navigating Disordered Systems}
A fine-tuned neural network can be understood as a physical system that has converged to a low-energy state (a localized basin of a loss landscape). When merging multiple independent expert networks $\{\theta_k\}_{k=1}^K$ that have been trained on distinct data distributions, the parameter-space fusion process acts as a physical mixing of disparate thermodynamic systems.

In statistical mechanics, spin glasses represent magnetic systems characterized by random, conflicting interactions (frustration) among spins, leading to a highly rugged energy landscape with an exponential number of local minima~\cite{mezard1987spin}. The parameter merging of independent task experts is a highly frustrated process: optimizing for Task A introduces parameter forces that directly contradict those needed for Task B. This parameter frustration creates a complex multi-task loss landscape containing high-loss Euclidean ridges between the localized basins of the experts.

\subsection{The Role of Simulated Thermalization}
Standard model merging approaches (such as Task Arithmetic) attempt to find a single, compromise parameter state via static linear interpolation. This is equivalent to a zero-temperature process that forces the parameters to travel on a straight line. If the experts have diverged into highly non-convex basins, this straight path is highly likely to cross high-energy (high-loss) mountain ridges, causing representational collapse.

ThermoMerge introduces a simulated global temperature $T(t)$ that acts as a thermalizing force. In physical systems, high temperature increases the microstate entropy, allowing the system to cross high potential energy barriers that would block transition at zero temperature. During the early stages of test-time adaptation, our Thermodynamic Annealing Schedule sets $T_{start}=5.0$. This high temperature mathematically flattens the Free Energy discrepancy landscape (Equation~24), blurring the sharp classification boundaries. Under this thermalized state, the optimization path of the coupling coefficients $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ is smoothed out, enabling the parameters to traverse potential energy barriers (high-loss ridges) and navigate smoothly toward a unified multi-task basin.

As the schedule exponentially cools down to $T_{end}=1.0$, the thermal fluctuations subside. This cooling process represents a physical crystallization, where the blurred boundaries progressively sharpen and stabilize around optimal multi-task representation coordinates, culminating in a robust thermodynamic equilibrium.

\section{Concrete Engineering Roadmap for Scaling to Pre-trained Foundation Models}
\label{sec:scaling_roadmap}

While our controlled evaluation using the pre-trained \texttt{ResNet-18} backbone serves as an exceptionally rigorous, fast, and controlled testbed that replicates the critical \emph{Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox} and task-representation interference, deploying ThermoMerge on large-scale pre-trained foundation models (such as CLIP ViT-B/32 or ResNet-50) is highly direct. We outline a concrete, step-by-step engineering roadmap to scale these thermodynamic principles to overparameterized vision and multimodal networks.

\subsection{Parameterization of Merging Coefficients in High-Capacity Architectures}
In standard model merging, foundation experts are fine-tuned from a high-quality pre-trained ancestor. Directly updating billions of raw weights during test-time adaptation is computationally prohibitive and risks destroying the pre-trained linear mode connectivity. To scale ThermoMerge, we restrict the trainable optimization parameters to parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) scales or coarse layers:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Layer-wise Linear Coefficients:} Let $\theta_l^{base}$ and $\theta_{l,k}$ represent the pre-trained base and expert parameters of the $l$-th transformer block (e.g., $L=12$ layers in ViT-B/32). We optimize a compact set of layer-wise, task-wise linear coefficients:
    \begin{equation}
    \theta_l^{MTL} = \theta_l^{base} + \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_{l,k} (\theta_{l,k} - \theta_l^{base})
    \end{equation}
    This scales the active parameter count during TTA to just $L \times K \ll 100$ parameters, ensuring extremely low-overhead optimization.
    \item \textbf{Thermodynamic Adapter Merging (ThermoLoRA):} If experts are fine-tuned using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), we can directly interpolate their adapters under our Free Energy objective:
    \begin{equation}
    \Delta W_l^{MTL} = \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_{l,k} B_{l,k} A_{l,k}
    \end{equation}
    where $A_{l,k}$ and $B_{l,k}$ are the low-rank matrices of expert $k$. This maintains standard weight structures while yielding localized multi-task alignment.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Multimodal Logit-to-Energy Boltzmann Formulation}
For vision-language architectures like CLIP, output logits are computed via the cosine similarity between the image embedding $I(x)$ and the text embeddings of target class descriptions $\{T(c)\}_{c=1}^{C_k}$. Let $s_c(x; \theta_k) = \cos\left( I(x; \theta_k), T(c; \theta_k) \right)$ represent the similarity score for class $c$ under expert $k$. We map these similarities to microstate energies as $E_c \equiv - s_c(x; \theta) \cdot \sigma$, where $\sigma$ is CLIP's native logit scale parameter. Under this formulation, the canonical Boltzmann probability is:
\begin{equation}
p_c^{(k)}(x; T) = \frac{\exp\left( \frac{s_c(x; \theta_k) \cdot \sigma_k}{T} \right)}{\sum_{j=1}^{C_k} \exp\left( \frac{s_j(x; \theta_k) \cdot \sigma_k}{T} \right)}
\end{equation}
The Helmholtz Free Energy of the multimodal system is calculated exactly as:
\begin{equation}
F_k(x; T) = -T \ln \left[ \sum_{j=1}^{C_k} \exp\left( \frac{s_j(x; \theta_k) \cdot \sigma_k}{T} \right) \right]
\end{equation}
This allows us to minimize the exact same Free Energy Discrepancy (F-Min) objective over unsupervised streaming images, aligning the multimodal representation spaces without needing any ground truth annotations.

\subsection{Expert Prediction Caching to Bypass the $\mathcal{O}(K)$ Forward Pass Bottleneck}
For large-scale vision Transformers, running $K$ separate expert models at each of the 100 TTA steps is computationally prohibitive. Because the calibration stream is static and known before adaptation, we can implement \textbf{expert prediction caching} as a primary, zero-overhead solution:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pre-computation Phase:} Run a single parallel forward pass through all $K$ expert networks once on the calibration stream $\mathcal{X}^{cal}$ to obtain and cache all expert similarity scores $s_c(x; \theta_k)$.
    \item \textbf{Optimization Phase:} During the 100 gradient steps, load the pre-computed expert outputs from the cache, completely eliminating the expert forward pass latency. The active optimization step only requires a single forward pass through the merged model $\theta^{MTL}$ to compute $s_c(x; \theta^{MTL})$, reducing the active TTA complexity from $\mathcal{O}(K)$ to $\mathcal{O}(1)$ (identical to single-model TTA).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Layer-wise Heat Capacities to Protect Shared Foundations}
In deep Transformer networks, earlier blocks encode general feature representation (visual patches and word parsing), while deeper blocks specialize in task-specific behaviors. To actively protect the generalist representation and prevent catastrophic interference, we can parameterize layer-specific learning rates using localized physical heat capacities $C_l$:
\begin{equation}
\eta_l = \frac{\eta_0}{C_l}
\end{equation}
By assigning high heat capacity ($C_l \gg 1$) to earlier self-attention layers, these blocks are made highly resistant to thermal updates, keeping the general representation space intact. Deeper layers are given lower heat capacities, allowing them to adapt dynamically to the multi-task distribution.

\subsection{Sensitivity to Initial Temperature $T_{start}$}
The starting temperature $T_{start}$ controls the level of thermal excitation in the Boltzmann distributions during the early stages of test-time adaptation. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} (Left), when $T_{start}$ is low (e.g., $T_{start} = 1.0$, which corresponds to a static zero-temperature setting where no simulated thermalization is present), the average multi-task accuracy is bounded at $28.90\%$. 

As $T_{start}$ is increased, the average multi-task accuracy rises and peaks at $29.30\%$ at a moderate temperature of $T_{start} = 2.0$. It then experiences a mild decay, yielding $28.80\%$ at $T_{start} = 5.0$, $28.05\%$ at $T_{start} = 8.0$, and $28.00\%$ at $T_{start} = 10.0$. Physically, this confirms that under a pre-trained ResNet-18 landscape where linear mode connectivity is preserved, the multi-task loss landscape is significantly smoother and less frustrated than in custom trained-from-scratch architectures. Consequently, the system does not require extreme thermal excitation (such as $T_{start} \ge 8.0$) to cross rugged non-convex barriers. Instead, a moderate starting temperature of $T_{start} = 2.0$ provides the optimal balance, offering sufficient thermal exploration to escape local minima while avoiding unnecessary decision boundary blurring that slows down convergence.

\subsection{Sensitivity to Thermal Cooling Rate $\beta$}
The cooling rate $\beta$ determines how quickly the system transitions from a diffuse, high-entropy state to a crystallized, low-entropy state. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} (Right), the relationship between average multi-task accuracy and $\beta$ exhibits a highly interesting physical trend under pre-trained representations.

Slower cooling rates (e.g., $\beta = 0.01$ and $\beta = 0.02$) yield average multi-task accuracies of $28.00\%$ and $28.00\%$, respectively. In contrast, faster cooling rates (such as $\beta = 0.10$ or $\beta = 0.40$, corresponding to a faster global temperature decay) achieve the highest accuracies, reaching $29.10\%$ and $29.25\%$, respectively.

In statistical physics, this behavior represents a fascinating inversion of the classical \textbf{annealing versus quenching} paradigm:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Slow Annealing ($\beta \to 0$):} While slow annealing is typically required to settle complex, highly frustrated spin glasses into their global minimum-energy state, keeping the temperature elevated for too many adaptation steps in a pre-trained landscape introduces excessive thermal fluctuations. This prolonged thermal noise slightly destabilizes parameter coordinates, dragging them away from the high-quality ancestral representation space.
    \item \textbf{Rapid Quenching ($\beta \to \infty$):} Rapid temperature decay acts as a quenching operator that quickly freezes and locks the parameters onto the nearby optimal multi-task basin. In a pre-trained landscape where experts already reside in a well-connected, cooperative representational manifold, rapid quenching acts as an exceptionally clean and efficient convergence force, locking in specialized decision boundaries before thermal diffusion can degrade the shared ancestor's features.
\end{itemize}
This empirical sensitivity analysis strongly validates the physical grounding of ThermoMerge, demonstrating that simulated thermalization and annealing schedules are highly expressive, robust, and physically sound mechanisms for navigating high-dimensional neural parameter frontiers.
