# 2. Novelty and Originality Check

## 2.1. Assessment of Novelty
The paper makes several highly original and valuable contributions to the weight-space model-merging literature:

1. **Characterization of "Heterogeneity Collapse" and "Batch-Dependency":** While weight-merging literature typically evaluates models sequentially, task-by-task, this paper is among the first to explicitly define, characterize, and empirically expose the vulnerability of traditional dynamic merging methods (such as Linear Routers and QWS-Merge) under realistic mixed-task, heterogeneous streams. The realization that batch-level averaging of routing coefficients violates the standard I.I.D. assumption and collapses performance under domain shifts is a high-signal conceptual contribution.
2. **Offline SVD + Runtime Low-Rank Adapters in Merging:** While Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) is a standard tool for compression, and Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) is widely used in PEFT, applying post-hoc offline SVD directly to the *task vectors* of fine-tuned experts to convert them into runtime, sample-wise parallel adapters is highly original. In model merging, this bridges the gap between training-free weight averaging and high-performance Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) without duplicating full-rank parameters.
3. **Activation-Space Mean Initialization:** This represents an extremely elegant and practical solution to the routing initialization problem. Standard MoE methods require massive pre-training or complex, unstable training regimes (Gumbel-Softmax, reinforcement learning, or straight-through estimators) to optimize routers. Simply using the empirical mean activations (centroids) of each task from a tiny unlabeled validation set as routing basis vectors is extremely simple, elegant, and highly effective, achieving 99.5% of the performance of a fully optimized gradient-based router completely zero-shot.
4. **Autonomous Classification Head Selection:** Traditional merged networks with task-specific heads assume a privileged "oracle" at inference time that selects the active task's head. The paper removes this restrictive assumption by proposing a layer-averaged cosine similarity classifier that determines the head selection completely autonomously, achieving near-perfect (93.26% domain classification and 98.6% of oracle performance) zero-shot accuracy.

## 2.2. Distinctions from Prior Work
- **Static Merging (Uniform, Task Arithmetic, TIES, DARE):** Unlike static methods that average weights once and suffer from severe task interference, SLD-Merge is input-adaptive.
- **Dynamic Merging (AdaMerging, QWS-Merge, Linear Router):** Unlike AdaMerging, which optimizes static coefficients once, SLD-Merge is dynamic at test time. Unlike QWS-Merge and Linear Routers, which compute dynamic weights per batch (leading to batch-dependency and collapse), SLD-Merge is completely batch-independent, executing sample-wise parallel low-rank forward passes.
- **Standard Mixture-of-Experts (MoE):** MoEs require training specialized expert feed-forward blocks from scratch under massive compute budgets. SLD-Merge is a training-free framework that consolidates already fine-tuned, fully dense specialized experts post-hoc.
- **LoRA/PEFT Merging (LoRA-Hub, ZipIt!):** While those methods merge models that were trained with LoRA from the start, SLD-Merge operates on fully dense pre-trained experts that have been fully fine-tuned, compressing their dense task vectors via post-hoc SVD.

## 2.3. Literature Positioning and Convergence
The paper successfully positions itself at the intersection of weight-space model merging, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT), and Mixture of Experts (MoE). It discusses relevant works (such as *Merge-SMoE* for converting experts into sparse MoE, and *LoRA-Hub* or *ZipIt!* for PEFT combining) and frames SLD-Merge as an innovative hybrid framework that marries weight-space decomposition with activation-space routing. This adds substantial theoretical depth and demonstrates high scholarly maturity.
