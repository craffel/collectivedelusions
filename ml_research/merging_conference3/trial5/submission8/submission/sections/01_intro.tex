\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The pre-train-and-fine-tune paradigm has established itself as the cornerstone of modern deep learning. By initializing models from massive self-supervised checkpoints and training specialized experts for specific downstream domains, the community has achieved outstanding performance across vision, language, and reinforcement learning. However, this decentralized success has introduced a new, critical challenge: how do we unify these fragmented, task-specific expert networks into a single, cohesive multi-task model without incurring the astronomical computational costs of multi-task joint retraining?

To address this, \emph{model merging} has emerged as an incredibly elegant and active subfield. By directly operating in the parameter space, model merging seeks to combine the weights of multiple task-specific expert models that share a common pre-trained base initialization. Early approaches, such as Task Arithmetic \cite{task_arithmetic} and RegMean \cite{regmean}, perform simple weight ensembling. More advanced frameworks, such as TIES-Merging \cite{ties}, resolve parameter conflicts by filtering low-magnitude values and resolving sign disagreements. While these methods are computationally lightweight and require no additional parameters, they are bound by a fundamental limitation: they are strictly \textbf{static}. They force a single, fixed weight compromise across the entire parameter manifold, which inevitably triggers catastrophic representation conflicts when the task-specific parameters contain orthogonal or opposing gradients.

To escape the limitations of static merging, researchers have turned to \textbf{dynamic model merging}, where parameters or ensembling coefficients are adapted on-the-fly based on the test-time inputs. For instance, AdaMerging \cite{adamerging} optimizes layer-wise merging coefficients at test-time by minimizing prediction entropy. Other approaches, such as QWS-Merge \cite{qws_merge} or traditional mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures \cite{shazeer2017outrageously, fedus2022switch}, utilize gating networks to route representations to different parameters. This dynamic weight modulation paradigm shares close mathematical roots with foundational literature on Hypernetworks \cite{ha2016hypernetworks} and Dynamic Filter Networks \cite{jia2016dynamic}, which dynamically generate or scale weight matrices on-the-fly in response to input features.

However, existing dynamic merging methods suffer from two fatal weaknesses:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Overfitting to Local Test Streams:} Test-time adaptation (TTA) approaches like AdaMerging rely on unsupervised local batches. Under sudden distribution shifts, non-I.I.D. temporal clustering (e.g., a sudden burst of MNIST images followed by CIFAR-10), or extreme small-batch noise, the unsupervised objectives collapse, leading to severe transductive overfitting and catastrophic failures.
    \item \textbf{Batch-Averaged Transductive Coupling:} To process a batch of heterogeneous inputs efficiently without sequential serialization, current dynamic routers (such as QWS-Merge) compute routing coefficients per sample but then \emph{average} them across the batch dimension. This batch-averaged compromise couples the inference of completely unrelated samples inside a batch, introducing transductive dependencies and violating the standard I.I.D. assumption required for independent sample deployment.
\end{enumerate}

In this work, we propose a radical, out-of-the-box paradigm shift that completely rejects the compromise-driven mindset of static ensembling and the batch-averaged shortcuts of previous dynamic routers. We draw inspiration from one of the most beautiful and sophisticated regulatory systems in nature: \textbf{cellular epigenetics}. 

In molecular biology, the cell does not rewrite its primary DNA sequence (analogous to the static pre-trained base model weights) to adapt to different environmental signals or to differentiate into bone, muscle, or nerve cells. Instead, it utilizes an extremely efficient layer of chemical markers—such as DNA methylation and histone modifications—to reversibly scale, silence, or amplify the expression pathways of specific genes on-the-fly. This allows the same genetic code to express completely different functional phenotypes in response to different localized environmental stimuli.

Translating this molecular mechanism to deep weight spaces, we introduce \textbf{EpiMerge} (\textbf{Epigenetic Weight Masking}). In our framework, the input sample acts as an environmental stimulus. For each task-specific expert, we introduce a tiny, highly parameter-efficient \textbf{Epigenetic Reader Head (ERH)} at each layer. Given an input sample $x$, the ERH projects its global latent representation onto a compact, low-dimensional space and dynamically generates a pair of row-wise and column-wise gating masks via low-rank outer products. These masks scale the coordinate-wise task vectors of each expert, reconstructing a customized, sample-specific weight matrix.

Crucially, to execute this sample-specific customization in parallel without losing GPU concurrency, we formulate our forward pass as a vectorized tensor contraction using PyTorch's \texttt{torch.einsum}. Every single image in a batch, whether from the same task or completely mixed tasks, is processed by its own dynamically modulated parameter state in parallel. This completely eliminates both batch dependencies and the threat of transductive batch coupling.

We evaluate EpiMerge against five competitive baselines using a Vision Transformer (ViT-Tiny) backbone across four diverse tasks (MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, and SVHN). We systematically subject all models to three target stream configurations: Shuffled I.I.D., Bursty temporal clusters, and small batch size ($B=2$). Our results demonstrate that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Online TTA is highly fragile:} Online AdaMerging collapses under temporal burstiness and small-batch regimes, losing over 40\% of its performance compared to its static ceiling.
    \item \textbf{Batch ensembling couples independent inferences:} Prior batch-averaged dynamic routers mathematically couple the inference of unrelated samples inside a batch, violating the standard I.I.D. assumption. EpiMerge guarantees true, decoupled, sample-wise model merging in parallel.
    \item \textbf{EpiMerge guarantees stream consistency:} By maintaining true sample-wise independent inference, EpiMerge's performance is mathematically guaranteed to remain perfectly consistent across Shuffled, Bursty, and Small Batch streams, representing a resilient step toward biologically-mimicking adaptive neural networks.
\end{itemize}

Our major contributions can be summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We propose the concept of \textbf{Epigenetic Weight Masking} (EpiMerge) for model merging, a biologically-inspired framework that enables true sample-specific dynamic model merging.
    \item We design a \textbf{Low-Rank Row-Column Dual Gating} mechanism that provides fine-grained, coordinate-wise scaling of expert task vectors. We generalize this to arbitrary gating ranks ($R \ge 1$) to scale expressive capacity and navigate non-convex saddle points.
    \item We present a fully parallel, vectorized forward pass using tensor contractions (\texttt{torch.einsum}), guaranteeing perfect I.I.D. inference and GPU concurrency. To resolve memory/latency overhead, we introduce an \textbf{Active-Early Sensory Extraction} variant that extracts representations directly from early static layers of the active model, reducing parameters to exactly 1.0x.
    \item We perform exhaustive empirical evaluations across multiple distribution streams and independent random seeds, demonstrating that because EpiMerge maintains sample-wise independent inference, its accuracy remains perfectly consistent across Shuffled, Bursty, and Small-Batch streams, avoiding the transductive local dependencies that plague online test-time adaptation (such as AdaMerging).
\end{enumerate}
