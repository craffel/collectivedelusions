# Soundness and Methodology Evaluation

## Clarity of Description
The methodology is exceptionally well-written, clear, and mathematically rigorous. The problem setup, construction of the joint multi-task update matrix, and left-singular vector projection are described with high clarity. The proof of the Eckart-Young-Mirsky Theorem application is correct and follows standard linear algebra derivations.

## Appropriateness of Methods
- **SVD on Concatenated Updates:** Constructing the joint update matrix $\mathbf{M}^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{out} \times (K \cdot d_{in})}$ by horizontally concatenating task vectors is appropriate. Performing SVD along the output dimension $d_{out}$ is sensible, as the authors provide a solid justification: for a linear layer $y = Wx$, output activations across tasks are directly aligned by the columns of $W$, and projecting onto left-singular vectors directly minimizes representation drift. Furthermore, since $d_{out}$ is typically much smaller than $K \cdot d_{in}$ for modern networks, this output-space projection is computationally efficient.
- **OFS-Tune Optimization:** Optimizing layer-wise coefficients $\alpha$ on a tiny 64-sample validation set is a standard few-shot tuning approach. Using the Adam optimizer with backpropagation through the projected weights is mathematically sound.
- **Task-Conditional Parameter Swapping:** The decision to keep normalization, biases, and embeddings task-specific and swap them at test-time is technically necessary when dealing with highly disparate domains (e.g., MNIST vs. CIFAR-10) because these parameters govern domain-specific running statistics. However, this hybrid design compromises the "single merged model" paradigm, as the system must know which task is being executed at test-time to swap these parameters. 

## Potential Technical Flaws & Conceptual Weaknesses
- **Unverified Scalability Claim:** In Section 3.7, the authors discuss the computational scalability of SVD ($\mathcal{O}(d_{out}^2 \cdot K \cdot d_{in})$) and propose randomized SVD and block-wise decompositions as numerical mitigations for larger architectures (like LLMs). However, they do not provide any empirical validation of these mitigations. All experiments are conducted on a tiny ViT-Tiny model where SVD is trivial to compute, leaving the scalability claims entirely theoretical.
- **Weak Evidence for the Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox:** The core motivation for GSC-Merge over unconstrained OFS-Tune is that unconstrained optimization "rapidly memorizes validation noise, causing the representation to drift... and degrading test performance." Under this premise, the unconstrained baseline should suffer from poor out-of-distribution generalization. However, the empirical results show that the unconstrained baseline consistently achieves the highest or near-highest mean test accuracy across both task-conditional swapping (44.08%) and truly task-agnostic settings (20.86%). If catastrophic validation overfitting were actively occurring, a regularized approach like GSC-Merge should statistically outperform the unconstrained baseline on the unseen test set. This weakens the paper's central motivation.

## Reproducibility
The reproducibility of the proposed methodology is **high**. The authors use a standard, publicly available architecture (ViT-Tiny from the `timm` library) and standard datasets (MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, SVHN) with well-defined optimization hyperparameters (learning rate, weight decay, optimization steps). The math is fully detailed and can be translated to code without ambiguity.
