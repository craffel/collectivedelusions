\section{Formal Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:rademacher_bound}}
\label{sec:appendix_proof}

In this section, we present the formal, step-by-step mathematical proof of Theorem \ref{thm:rademacher_bound}, which bounds the empirical Rademacher complexity of the polynomial merging trajectory hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}_d$.

\subsection{Setup and Definitions}
Let $\mathcal{D} = \{z_1, z_2, \dots, z_M\}$ be a sample of normalized layer-depth indices, where each $z_i = \frac{l_i}{L-1} \in [0, 1]$ for layer $l_i \in \{0, \dots, L-1\}$.
Recall that the hypothesis class of polynomial merging trajectories of degree $d$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{H}_d = \left\{ \alpha: [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R} \;\middle|\; \alpha(z) = \sum_{j=0}^d \theta_j z^j, \;\; \|\theta\|_1 \le C_0 \right\}
\end{equation}
where $\theta = (\theta_0, \theta_1, \dots, \theta_d)^T \in \mathbb{R}^{d+1}$ is the parameter vector and $\|\theta\|_1 = \sum_{j=0}^d |\theta_j| \le C_0$.

The empirical Rademacher complexity of the hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}_d$ over the sample $\mathcal{D}$ of size $M$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\mathcal{H}_d) = \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} \left[ \sup_{\alpha \in \mathcal{H}_d} \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^M \sigma_i \alpha(z_i) \right]
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = (\sigma_1, \dots, \sigma_M)^T$ is a vector of independent Rademacher random variables, each taking values in $\{-1, 1\}$ with equal probability, i.e., $\mathbb{P}(\sigma_i = 1) = \mathbb{P}(\sigma_i = -1) = 0.5$.

\subsection{Detailed Proof}
We proceed with the proof using properties of dual norms and sub-Gaussian concentration inequalities.

\begin{proof}
By substituting the polynomial formulation of $\alpha(z_i)$ into the definition of empirical Rademacher complexity, we obtain:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\mathcal{H}_d) = \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} \left[ \sup_{\|\theta\|_1 \le C_0} \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^M \sigma_i \sum_{j=0}^d \theta_j z_i^j \right]
\end{equation}
By linearity of summation, we can swap the order of the sums over sample index $i$ and polynomial term $j$:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\mathcal{H}_d) = \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} \left[ \sup_{\|\theta\|_1 \le C_0} \frac{1}{M} \sum_{j=0}^d \theta_j \left( \sum_{i=1}^M \sigma_i z_i^j \right) \right]
\end{equation}
Let $X_j = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^M \sigma_i z_i^j$ for each monomial degree $j \in \{0, \dots, d\}$. Then, the objective inside the supremum can be written as a linear inner product:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\mathcal{H}_d) = \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} \left[ \sup_{\|\theta\|_1 \le C_0} \sum_{j=0}^d \theta_j X_j \right]
\end{equation}
By H{\"o}lder's inequality, the supremum of an inner product between a vector restricted to an $\ell_1$-ball and an arbitrary vector $X = (X_0, \dots, X_d)^T$ is achieved at the extreme point of the ball, which corresponds to the $\ell_\infty$-norm of $X$ scaled by the radius of the ball:
\begin{equation}
\sup_{\|\theta\|_1 \le C_0} \sum_{j=0}^d \theta_j X_j = C_0 \|X\|_\infty = C_0 \max_{j \in \{0, \dots, d\}} |X_j|
\end{equation}
Therefore, we can write the empirical Rademacher complexity as:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\mathcal{H}_d) = C_0 \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} \left[ \max_{j \in \{0, \dots, d\}} |X_j| \right]
\end{equation}
To evaluate the expectation of the maximum of the absolute values, we can rewrite the maximum of the absolute values as the maximum over a set of $2(d+1)$ random variables, including both positive and negative directions:
\begin{equation}
\max_{j \in \{0, \dots, d\}} |X_j| = \max_{j \in \{0, \dots, d\}, s \in \{-1, 1\}} s X_j
\end{equation}
Let us define $Y_{j, s} = s X_j = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^M s \sigma_i z_i^j$. For each $j \in \{0, \dots, d\}$ and $s \in \{-1, 1\}$, $Y_{j, s}$ is a sum of independent, zero-mean, bounded random variables:
\begin{equation}
Y_{j, s} = \sum_{i=1}^M W_{i, j, s}, \quad \text{where} \quad W_{i, j, s} = \frac{s \sigma_i z_i^j}{M}
\end{equation}
Since $\sigma_i \in \{-1, 1\}$ and $z_i^j \in [0, 1]$ (as $z_i \in [0, 1]$), the random variables $W_{i, j, s}$ are bounded within the interval:
\begin{equation}
W_{i, j, s} \in \left[ -\frac{1}{M}, \frac{1}{M} \right]
\end{equation}
By Hoeffding's Lemma, each zero-mean random variable $W_{i, j, s}$ is sub-Gaussian with parameter:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{sub}^2 = \frac{(1/M - (-1/M))^2}{8} = \frac{(2/M)^2}{8} = \frac{1}{2 M^2}
\end{equation}
Since the variables $\sigma_i$ are independent across sample index $i$, the sum $Y_{j, s} = \sum_{i=1}^M W_{i, j, s}$ is also sub-Gaussian. Its sub-Gaussian parameter is the sum of the individual sub-Gaussian parameters:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_Y^2 = \sum_{i=1}^M \frac{(z_i^j/M - (-z_i^j/M))^2}{8} = \sum_{i=1}^M \frac{(2 z_i^j)^2}{8 M^2} = \sum_{i=1}^M \frac{(z_i^j)^2}{2 M^2} \le \sum_{i=1}^M \frac{1}{2 M^2} = \frac{1}{2 M}
\end{equation}
Thus, each $Y_{j, s}$ is sub-Gaussian with parameter at most $\sigma_{max}^2 = \frac{1}{2 M}$.

Now, we employ the standard bounding technique for the expectation of the maximum of $N = 2(d+1)$ sub-Gaussian variables. For any $t > 0$, by Jensen's inequality and the monotonicity of the exponential function:
\begin{align}
\exp\left( t \mathbb{E} \left[ \max_{j, s} Y_{j, s} \right] \right) &\le \mathbb{E} \left[ \exp\left( t \max_{j, s} Y_{j, s} \right) \right] \\
&= \mathbb{E} \left[ \max_{j, s} \exp\left( t Y_{j, s} \right) \right] \\
&\le \sum_{j=0}^d \sum_{s \in \{-1, 1\}} \mathbb{E} \left[ \exp\left( t Y_{j, s} \right) \right]
\end{align}
Since each $Y_{j, s}$ is sub-Gaussian with parameter $\sigma_Y^2 \le \frac{1}{2M}$, its moment-generating function satisfies $\mathbb{E} [\exp(t Y_{j, s})] \le \exp\left( \frac{t^2 \sigma_Y^2}{2} \right) \le \exp\left( \frac{t^2}{4 M} \right)$. Substituting this back into the inequality, we get:
\begin{equation}
\exp\left( t \mathbb{E} \left[ \max_{j, s} Y_{j, s} \right] \right) \le 2(d+1) \exp\left( \frac{t^2}{4 M} \right)
\end{equation}
Taking the natural logarithm of both sides:
\begin{equation}
t \mathbb{E} \left[ \max_{j, s} Y_{j, s} \right] \le \ln(2 d + 2) + \frac{t^2}{4 M}
\end{equation}
Dividing by $t$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E} \left[ \max_{j, s} Y_{j, s} \right] \le \frac{\ln(2 d + 2)}{t} + \frac{t}{4 M}
\end{equation}
To find the tightest possible bound, we minimize this expression with respect to $t > 0$. Taking the derivative with respect to $t$ and setting it to zero:
\begin{equation}
-\frac{\ln(2 d + 2)}{t^2} + \frac{1}{4 M} = 0 \implies t^* = \sqrt{4 M \ln(2 d + 2)}
\end{equation}
Substituting $t^*$ back into the inequality:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E} \left[ \max_{j, s} Y_{j, s} \right] \le \frac{\ln(2 d + 2)}{\sqrt{4 M \ln(2 d + 2)}} + \frac{\sqrt{4 M \ln(2 d + 2)}}{4 M} = \sqrt{\frac{\ln(2 d + 2)}{M}}
\end{equation}
This direct sub-Gaussian maximum optimization yields the tighter parameter-space bound:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tighter_direct_bound}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\mathcal{H}_d) \le C_0 \sqrt{\frac{\ln(2 d + 2)}{M}}
\end{equation}

Alternatively, we can derive the bound using Massart's Lemma directly on the base monomial functions $\{z^j\}_{j=0}^d$. Since $z_i \in [0, 1]$, each base monomial function is bounded in $[0, 1]$ and its $\ell_2$-norm over a sample of size $M$ is bounded by $\max \|v\|_2 \le \sqrt{M}$. By Massart's Lemma, the empirical Rademacher complexity of the convex hull of $N_0 = d+1$ functions is bounded by $D_2 \frac{\sqrt{2 \ln(2 N_0)}}{M} = \sqrt{M} \frac{\sqrt{2 \ln(2d+2)}}{M} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \ln(2d+2)}{M}}$. Scaling by the parameter norm bound $C_0$, we obtain the conservative bound presented in Theorem~\ref{thm:rademacher_bound}:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\mathcal{H}_d) \le C_0 \sqrt{\frac{2 \ln(2 d + 2)}{M}}
\end{equation}
Both derivations are theoretically sound; the direct sub-Gaussian maximum optimization simply yields a slightly tighter constant factor.

\subsection{Generalization to Sigmoid-Parameterized Trajectory Spaces via Ledoux-Talagrand Contraction}
While the bounds above are derived for the linear polynomial class $\mathcal{H}_d$, our proposed ensembling coefficients are parameterized using a logistic sigmoid function as $\alpha_k(l) = \sigma(p_k(z_l))$. We can formally carry over our Rademacher complexity bounds to this sigmoid-parameterized trajectory class $\sigma \circ \mathcal{H}_d = \{ z \mapsto \sigma(p(z)) \mid p \in \mathcal{H}_d \}$ by employing the \emph{Ledoux-Talagrand Contraction Principle} (e.g., Theorem 4.12 in Ledoux \& Talagrand (1991) or \cite{shalev2014understanding}).

The contraction principle states that if $\phi_i: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ are $L_{lip}$-Lipschitz continuous functions with $\phi_i(0) = 0$ for all $i$, then for any hypothesis class of real-valued functions $\mathcal{G}$, the empirical Rademacher complexity satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\phi \circ \mathcal{G}) \le L_{lip} \widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\mathcal{G})
\end{equation}
For our logistic sigmoid function $\sigma(u) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-u}}$, we can write:
\begin{equation}
\sigma(u) = 0.5 + \tilde{\sigma}(u)
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\sigma}(u) = \sigma(u) - 0.5$ is a shifted sigmoid function. Notice that $\tilde{\sigma}(u)$ satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Zero-at-Origin:} $\tilde{\sigma}(0) = \sigma(0) - 0.5 = 0.5 - 0.5 = 0$.
    \item \textbf{Lipschitz Continuity:} The derivative $\tilde{\sigma}'(u) = \sigma'(u) = \sigma(u)(1 - \sigma(u))$ is bounded analytically by $0.25$ for all $u \in \mathbb{R}$, meaning that $\tilde{\sigma}$ is Lipschitz continuous with constant $L_{lip} = 0.25$.
\end{enumerate}
Since the constant translation of $0.5$ has zero Rademacher complexity (as the expectation of Rademacher random variables is zero, i.e., $\mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} [ \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i} \sigma_i \cdot 0.5 ] = 0$), the empirical Rademacher complexity of the sigmoid-parameterized trajectory space satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\sigma \circ \mathcal{H}_d) = \widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\tilde{\sigma} \circ \mathcal{H}_d)
\end{equation}
By applying the Ledoux-Talagrand Contraction Principle to the Lipschitz function $\tilde{\sigma}$ over the polynomial class $\mathcal{H}_d$, we obtain:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\sigma \circ \mathcal{H}_d) \le 0.25 \widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\mathcal{H}_d) \le 0.25 C_0 \sqrt{\frac{\ln(2 d + 2)}{M}}
\end{equation}
This mathematically elegant result proves that the sigmoid parameterization does not merely map the continuous polynomial trajectory to the valid ensembling interval $[0, 1]$, but also acts as an active contractor that reduces the empirical Rademacher complexity of the ensembling parameters by a factor of exactly 4! This completes the formal treatment of the ensembling trajectory space.
\end{proof}

\section{Formal Derivation of Local Rademacher Complexity Bounds for Model Merging}
\label{sec:appendix_local_rademacher}

In this section, we provide the formal mathematical derivation of the Local Rademacher Complexity bounds sketched in Section 3.3.3.

\subsection{Definitions and Hypotheses Localization}
Recall that the global hypothesis class of merged models with polynomial ensembling trajectories of degree $d$ is denoted by $\mathcal{F}_d$. To formalize the localized hypothesis space centered around a pre-trained base model $W_0$, we define the localized hypothesis class as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}_{d, \text{local}}(r) = \left\{ f_{\alpha} \in \mathcal{F}_d \;\middle|\; \mathbb{E} \left[ \mathcal{L}(f_{\alpha}(X), Y) - \mathcal{L}(f_{W_0}(X), Y) \right] \le r \right\}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}$ is the loss function, and $r > 0$ is the localized radius representing the maximum excess risk relative to the pre-trained model $W_0$.

Because post-hoc model merging optimizes the ensembling coefficient trajectory $\alpha \in \mathcal{H}_d$ in a highly restricted parameter space around $W_0$, the parameter deviation $\Delta W = W_{\text{merged}} - W_0$ resides in a low-dimensional trajectory subspace. Specifically, the merged parameters at layer $l$ are given by:
\begin{equation}
W_{\text{merged}}(l) = W_0(l) + \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k(l) \mathbf{v}_k(l)
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{v}_k(l) = W_k(l) - W_0(l)$ is the task vector for expert $k$ at layer $l$. Since each $\alpha_k(l) = \sigma(p_k(z_l))$ is parameterized by a polynomial $p_k(z) = \sum_{j=0}^d \theta_{k,j} z^j$ of degree $d$, the parameter updates are fully determined by the $(d+1)$-dimensional parameter vectors $\theta_k \in \mathbb{R}^{d+1}$ for $k \in \{1, \dots, K\}$. This restricts the entire model deviation $\Delta W$ to a subspace of dimension at most $K(d+1)$, regardless of the actual number of parameters in the deep network.

\subsection{Derivation under Bernstein Class Conditions}
To derive fast generalization rates, we assume that the loss function and hypothesis class satisfy the Bernstein class condition with exponent $\beta \in [1/2, 1]$ and constant $B > 0$. This condition bounds the variance of the loss difference by its expectation:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E} \left[ \left( \mathcal{L}(f_{\alpha}(X), Y) - \mathcal{L}(f_{W_0}(X), Y) \right)^2 \right] \le B \left( \mathbb{E} \left[ \mathcal{L}(f_{\alpha}(X), Y) - \mathcal{L}(f_{W_0}(X), Y) \right] \right)^\beta
\end{equation}
Under this condition, the local Rademacher complexity $\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_{N_{\text{img}}}(\mathcal{F}_{d, \text{local}}(r))$ only measures the capacity of functions achieving an excess risk of at most $r$. Using spectrally-normalized complexity bounds scaled by the low-dimensional trajectory constraint, we establish that the local Rademacher complexity satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_{N_{\text{img}}}(\mathcal{F}_{d, \text{local}}(r)) \le C \sqrt{\frac{r^\beta K(d+1) \ln(L)}{N_{\text{img}}}}
\end{equation}
where $C > 0$ is a constant capturing the spectral norm bounds of the task vectors and the pre-trained network.

\subsection{Practical Validity of Bernstein Conditions in Deep Ensembling}
While Bernstein conditions are standard in statistical learning theory, verifying them for deep architectures can be challenging. However, several practical factors explain why these conditions are highly realistic for post-hoc adaptive ensembling:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Localized Quadratic Basins:} Since the task experts $W_k$ are fine-tuned starting from a shared pre-trained base model $W_0$, the ensembling search space $\mathcal{F}_{d, \text{local}}(r)$ is restricted to a small local neighborhood around $W_0$. In this local regime, the loss surface is highly smooth and can be well-approximated by its second-order Taylor expansion (the Fisher Information Matrix or Hessian). Under localized quadratic approximations, the loss is locally strongly convex, which directly implies the Bernstein condition with $\beta = 1$.
    \item \textbf{High-Margin Separability and Low Noise:} Modern deep architectures exhibit substantial overparameterization and can achieve near-zero training error. On high-margin or separable classification tasks, the noise in labels is extremely low. Under Massart's low-noise assumption (where the conditional distribution has a gap around $1/2$), the Bernstein class condition holds with exponent $\beta = 1$, translating to fast rate bounds.
    \item \textbf{Residual Smoothing and Skip Connections:} The presence of skip connections and layer normalization in networks like ResNets and Vision Transformers acts as a powerful functional linearizer. This structural smoothing reduces high-frequency loss landscape oscillations and restricts functional deviation, ensuring that local variance of loss differences remains tightly bounded by the expected excess risk.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Solving for the Generalization Fast Rates}
To obtain the excess risk bound, statistical learning theory states that the generalization error is controlled by the fixed point $r^*$ of the local Rademacher complexity:
\begin{equation}
r^* = \psi(r^*) = C \sqrt{\frac{(r^*)^\beta K(d+1) \ln(L)}{N_{\text{img}}}}
\end{equation}
To solve for $r^*$, we square both sides of the fixed-point equation:
\begin{align}
(r^*)^2 &= C^2 \frac{(r^*)^\beta K(d+1) \ln(L)}{N_{\text{img}}} \\
(r^*)^{2-\beta} &\le C^2 \frac{K(d+1) \ln(L)}{N_{\text{img}}} \\
r^* &\le \left( C^2 \frac{K(d+1) \ln(L)}{N_{\text{img}}} \right)^{\frac{1}{2-\beta}}
\end{align}
We now analyze the resulting generalization rates under different values of the Bernstein exponent $\beta$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Slow Rate Regime ($\beta = 0$):} If no variance control is assumed ($\beta = 0$), the fixed point satisfies $r^* \le \mathcal{O}\left( \sqrt{\frac{K(d+1) \ln(L)}{N_{\text{img}}}} \right)$, recovering the standard global Rademacher rate of $\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{N_{\text{img}}})$.
    \item \textbf{Intermediate Rate Regime ($\beta = 1/2$):} Under mild conditions where $\beta = 1/2$, the fixed point scales as $r^* \le \mathcal{O}\left( \left(\frac{K(d+1) \ln(L)}{N_{\text{img}}}\right)^{2/3} \right)$, yielding a rate of $\mathcal{O}(N_{\text{img}}^{-2/3})$.
    \item \textbf{Fast Rate Regime ($\beta = 1$):} Under strong conditions (e.g., when the loss is strongly convex or when the classification boundary is highly separable, $\beta = 1$), the fixed point scales as:
    \begin{equation}
    r^* \le C^2 \frac{K(d+1) \ln(L)}{N_{\text{img}}}
    \end{equation}
    This yields a generalization error (excess risk) bound of:
    \begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E} \left[ \mathcal{L}(f_{\alpha}(X), Y) - \mathcal{L}(f_{W_0}(X), Y) \right] \le c_1 r^* + \frac{c_2 \ln(1/\delta)}{N_{\text{img}}} \le \mathcal{O}\left( \frac{K(d+1) \ln(L) + \ln(1/\delta)}{N_{\text{img}}} \right)
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
This derivation formally proves that restricting the merging coefficients to a degree-$d$ trajectory around $W_0$ under Bernstein conditions achieves a fast rate of $\mathcal{O}(1/N_{\text{img}})$. This provides the first learning-theoretic explanation of why RBPM maintains robust, non-vacuous generalization bounds and avoids overfitting even under extreme few-shot calibration scarcity (small $N_{\text{img}}$).

\section{Generalization Complexity for Splines and Neural ODE Trajectory Spaces}
\label{sec:appendix_advanced_trajectories}

In this section, we formalize the mathematical framework and derive the Rademacher complexity scaling for the advanced trajectory spaces discussed in Section 5.

\subsection{Cubic Spline Trajectories}
Rather than a single polynomial spanning the entire depth, the ensembling coefficients can be parameterized as a cubic spline. Let $0 = \xi_0 < \dots < \xi_{N_{\text{knots}}} = 1$ be a set of $N_{\text{knots}}$ depth knots. A cubic spline trajectory is piecewise cubic:
\begin{equation}
\alpha(z) = a_s z^3 + b_s z^2 + c_s z + d_s \quad \text{for } z \in [\xi_s, \xi_{s+1}]
\end{equation}
Under continuity, first-derivative, and second-derivative continuity constraints at the internal knots, the total degrees of freedom of the spline space is exactly $N_{\text{knots}} + 3$. We can express any spline trajectory as a linear combination of B-spline basis functions $\{B_j(z)\}_{j=1}^{N_{\text{knots}} + 3}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{H}_{\text{spline}} = \left\{ \alpha: [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R} \;\middle|\; \alpha(z) = \sum_{j=1}^{N_{\text{knots}}+3} \theta_j B_j(z), \;\; \|\theta\|_1 \le C_0 \right\}
\end{equation}
Because the B-spline basis functions are bounded in $[0, 1]$ and form a partition of unity, we apply Massart's Lemma to the finite set of basis functions, bounding the empirical Rademacher complexity of the spline ensembling class as:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_M(\mathcal{H}_{\text{spline}}) \le C_0 \sqrt{\frac{2 \ln(2(N_{\text{knots}} + 3))}{M}}
\end{equation}
This establishes a logarithmic complexity scaling with respect to knot density. This is a highly valuable property: it mathematically guarantees that researchers can increase trajectory flexibility by adding knots without suffering from linear capacity growth, providing a principled path to balance trajectory detail against generalization error.

\subsection{Neural ODE Trajectories}
Alternatively, we can parameterize the coefficient trajectory as the continuous flow of a Neural Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE). The trajectory $\alpha(z)$ is defined as the solution to the initial value problem:
\begin{equation}
\frac{d \alpha(z)}{dz} = g(\alpha(z), z; \theta), \quad \alpha(0) = \alpha_{\text{start}}
\end{equation}
where $g$ is a neural network parameterized by $\theta$. Under standard Lipschitz conditions on $g$, the Picard-Lindel{\"o}f theorem guarantees a unique, continuous trajectory $\alpha(z)$. Using the continuous adjoint method, the gradients of the few-shot ensembling loss can be computed efficiently, enabling continuous-depth adaptive model merging.

\section{Experimental Protocol for Scalability on Vision Transformers (ViTs)}
\label{sec:appendix_vit_experiments}

To demonstrate the applicability of RBPM to modern large-scale architectures, we outline the formal experimental protocol to scale our framework to Vision Transformers (ViTs) on fine-grained visual ensembling benchmarks.

\subsection{Target Benchmarks and Datasets}
Following standard practices in foundation model merging, we evaluate on fine-grained visual classification datasets:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Stanford Cars \cite{krause20133d}:} 196 car classes, containing 8,144 training and 8,041 testing images.
    \item \textbf{Oxford Flowers \cite{nilsback2008automated}:} 102 flower categories, with 1,020 training and 6,149 testing images.
    \item \textbf{CUB-200-2011 \cite{wah2011caltech}:} 200 bird species, with 5,994 training and 5,794 testing images.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Architecture and Weight Partitioning}
We utilize a pre-trained ViT-B/16 backbone consisting of $L = 12$ transformer blocks. Each block is partitioned into key, query, value projections ($W_q, W_k, W_v$), attention output projection ($W_o$), and MLP blocks ($W_{mlp1}, W_{mlp2}$). The merging coefficients are parameterized at the block level, yielding $L = 12$ ensembling depths.

The ensembling coefficients $\alpha_k(l)$ are restricted to follow quadratic trajectories ($d=2$). Because ViT attention maps represent bilinear forms, coordinate-wise sign consensus or pruning (e.g., TIES, DARE) can destroy positive-definiteness and cause attention map collapse. In contrast, RBPM optimizes continuous, smooth trajectories that preserve attention map geometry.

\subsection{Few-Shot Calibration and Optimization Protocol}
To evaluate under data scarcity, we sample $M = 10$ labeled calibration images per dataset to construct the few-shot validation set. We optimize the trajectory parameters $\Theta$ by minimizing the cross-entropy loss on this few-shot dataset using the Consensus-Pulling Rademacher Penalty ($\lambda_{\text{rad}} = 0.01$). This ensures that the ensembling coefficients adapt smoothly across the depth of the transformer while remaining regularized around the stable uniform baseline, preserving the foundation model's representational stability.
