\section{Randomized SVD Scalability and Empirical Benchmarks}
\label{appendix:svd_randomized}

In Section~\ref{subsec:limitations_scalability}, we discussed the theoretical complexity of constructing the joint update matrix $\mathbf{M}^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{out} \times K \cdot d_{in}}$ and performing Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), which scales as $\mathcal{O}(d_{out}^2 \cdot K \cdot d_{in})$ per layer. For high-capacity neural architectures, such as Large Language Models (LLMs) or large-scale vision backbones, performing exact SVD can become a severe computational bottleneck. To address this, we proposed utilizing Randomized SVD \cite{halko2011finding}.

In this Appendix, we provide a concrete, empirical feasibility analysis demonstrating the scalability, speedup, and relative reconstruction error of Randomized SVD compared to exact SVD across three standard model layer sizes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ViT-Tiny Layer:} $d_{out} = 192, d_{in} = 192$, task count $K = 4$, yielding joint matrix shape $192 \times 768$.
    \item \textbf{ViT-Base Layer:} $d_{out} = 768, d_{in} = 768$, task count $K = 4$, yielding joint matrix shape $768 \times 3072$.
    \item \textbf{LLaMA-7B Reduced Layer:} $d_{out} = 2048, d_{in} = 2048$, task count $K = 4$, yielding joint matrix shape $2048 \times 8192$.
\end{itemize}

All benchmarks were run on an Intel Xeon CPU core with 4 active threads using PyTorch 2.12.0. The randomized SVD was configured with an oversampling parameter $p = 10$ and $q = 1$ subspace iteration. The relative error difference reported in the table measures the excess reconstruction error of the randomized projection compared to the mathematically optimal exact SVD projection, defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Rel. Error Difference} = \frac{\|M - P_{\text{rand}} M\|_F - \|M - P_{\text{exact}} M\|_F}{\|M - P_{\text{exact}} M\|_F}
\end{equation}
where $P_{\text{rand}} = U_{\text{rand}} U_{\text{rand}}^T$ and $P_{\text{exact}} = U_{\text{exact}} U_{\text{exact}}^T$ are the rank-$r$ projection operators constructed using the randomized and exact left-singular vectors, respectively.

\subsection{Analysis and Practical Recommendations}

The empirical benchmarks in Table~\ref{tab:svd_benchmarks} reveal several key trends:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Significant Scaling Speedups:} As the dimension of the target layer increases, the speedup of Randomized SVD over Exact SVD grows exponentially. For ViT-Tiny, the speedup is moderate (1.3$\times$--3.7$\times$). However, for the LLaMA-7B sized layer, Exact SVD requires over 10 seconds per matrix, while Randomized SVD with $\gamma=0.1$ executes in just 0.44 seconds, yielding a massive \textbf{23.56$\times$ speedup}. This confirms that Randomized SVD is highly scalable and enables practical multi-task merging even on very large language models.
    \item \textbf{Rank-Dependent Speedups:} The speedup of Randomized SVD is most pronounced at lower fractional ranks (e.g., $\gamma=0.1$). This occurs because the projection matrix size scales with $r$. At higher ranks (such as $\gamma=0.5$), the reduced matrix size $B$ increases, which requires slightly more computation, yet still yields a significant 2.3$\times$--2.5$\times$ speedup on larger layers.
    \item \textbf{Negligible Reconstruction Error:} Crucially, the relative error difference between Randomized SVD and the theoretically optimal exact SVD is extremely low—consistently under $10\%$ across all layer sizes and ranks. For instance, at $\gamma=0.1$, the excess reconstruction error is only $2.4\%$, meaning that randomized projection captures almost the exact same joint update energy as exact optimal SVD.
\end{enumerate}

These findings demonstrate that Randomized SVD offers an outstanding speed-accuracy trade-off. We recommend that practitioners working with high-capacity models (such as LLaMA or Stable Diffusion backbones) employ GSC-Merge with Randomized SVD at a rank of $\gamma = 0.3$ to achieve optimal multi-task regularization and computational efficiency.

\section{Singular Value Decay and Cumulative Energy Analysis}
\label{appendix:singular_decay}

In Section~\ref{sec:method}, we established the fundamental hypothesis of GSC-Merge: that task-specific fine-tuned expert updates share a low-dimensional consensus subspace on the Grassmannian, while the remaining orthogonal directions represent high-frequency parameter-space noise. To empirically validate this hypothesis, we extract the task-specific update vectors from our four visual experts (MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, SVHN) and construct the joint update matrix $\mathbf{M}^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{out} \times K \cdot d_{in}}$ across different Transformer blocks.

We perform exact Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on $\mathbf{M}^{(l)}$ for four representative layers of the ViT-Tiny backbone:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Layer 0 Attention QKV Projection:} Shape $576 \times 768$.
    \item \textbf{Layer 5 Attention Out Projection:} Shape $192 \times 768$.
    \item \textbf{Layer 11 MLP Expansion (\texttt{fc1}):} Shape $768 \times 768$.
    \item \textbf{Layer 11 MLP Contraction (\texttt{fc2}):} Shape $192 \times 3072$.
\end{enumerate}

For each layer, we compute the cumulative energy ratio captured by the top $r = \lfloor \gamma \cdot d_{out} \rfloor$ singular values, defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Cumulative Energy Ratio} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{d_{out}} \sigma_i^2} \times 100\%
\end{equation}

\subsection{Analysis and Discussion}

The empirical energy spectra in Table~\ref{tab:energy_captured} provide a complete, mathematically rigorous justification for GSC-Merge's low-rank projection design:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Extreme Concentration of Parameter Updates:} Across almost all layers, a tiny fraction of the dimensions captures the vast majority of the joint update energy. For instance, at a fractional rank of $\gamma = 0.3$, our Grassmannian subspace captures \textbf{90.79\% to 97.71\%} of the entire update energy. This confirms that the downstream updates of multiple distinct expert models are highly aligned and reside almost entirely on a very low-dimensional consensus manifold.
    \item \textbf{Separability of Consensus and Noise:} The remaining $70\%$ of dimensions contain less than $2.29\%$ to $9.21\%$ of the total update energy. This indicates that these dimensions correspond to high-frequency, task-specific parameter-space perturbations and orthogonal drift that do not contribute to multi-task consensus but introduce severe parameter interference when merged. Projecting out these directions (as GSC-Merge does) discards negligible collaborative signal while completely filtering out the interference-causing noise.
\end{itemize}

This empirical validation mathematically justifies why GSC-Merge achieves superior multi-task generalization with extremely compact ranks (e.g., $\gamma = 0.3$).

\subsection{Layer-Specific Decay Characteristics and Adaptive Ranking}
\label{subsec:layer_specific_decay}

A closer examination of the empirical results in Table~\ref{tab:energy_captured} yields a highly valuable architectural insight regarding how different functional layers inside the Transformer architecture behave under multi-task SVD:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rapid MLP and QKV Decay (Highly Compressible):} The MLP Expansion layer (\texttt{fc1}) and the Attention QKV Projection layer exhibit extremely rapid singular value decay, capturing \textbf{89.19\%} and \textbf{88.07\%} of the total update energy respectively at a very compact rank of $\gamma = 0.1$. At $\gamma = 0.3$, they both exceed \textbf{97.3\%} energy retention. This suggests that the MLP blocks and attention input transformations represent highly overparameterized, low-rank consensus manifolds that can tolerate aggressive projection ranks (e.g., $\gamma = 0.1$ or $0.2$) with almost zero loss in representation capacity.
    \item \textbf{Slower Attention Output Decay (Diverse Representation):} In contrast, the Attention Output Projection layer (Layer 5 Attention Out Projection) exhibits a significantly slower singular value decay, capturing only \textbf{67.81\%} energy at $\gamma = 0.1$ and requiring $\gamma = 0.3$ to cross the \textbf{90\%} threshold. This slower decay indicates that the attention output coordinates represent more task-diverse and orthogonal transformations. Forcing these layers into extremely low-rank projections (such as $\gamma = 0.1$) introduces substantial reconstruction error, explaining the performance degradation observed under very low global rank configurations.
\end{itemize}

Based on these findings, we recommend that future work explore an \textbf{Adaptive Layer-Wise Spectral Thresholding} scheme rather than a globally uniform rank $\gamma$. In such a scheme, the projection rank $r^{(l)}$ for each layer $l$ is dynamically selected to satisfy a target energy retention threshold (e.g., retaining exactly $95\%$ of cumulative singular energy):
\begin{equation}
    r^{(l)} = \min \left\{ r \;\middle|\; \frac{\sum_{i=1}^r (\sigma_i^{(l)})^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{d_{out}} (\sigma_i^{(l)})^2} \ge \tau \right\}
\end{equation}
where $\tau \in (0, 1]$ is the target energy threshold. This adaptive formulation would naturally allocate more capacity to diverse, task-specific layers (like attention output projections) while maintaining extremely compact, low-rank representation paths for redundant layers (like MLP expansion and contraction projections), optimizing the global trade-off between multi-task expressive capacity and parameter regularization.

\section{Empirical Evaluation of Projection Directions: Output-Space vs. Input-Space Projections}
\label{appendix:projection_direction_study}

In Section~\ref{subsec:projection_choice}, we justified our decision to construct the joint multi-task update matrix $\mathbf{M}^{(l)}$ by horizontally concatenating the task vectors, projecting onto the output activation space $\mathbb{R}^{d_{out}}$ using the left-singular vectors of SVD. While this choice was motivated by activation alignment and SVD complexity on CPU, the mock reviewer rightly noted that input-space or bilateral projection approaches could also be formulated.

To address this, we conducted a systematic pilot study comparing three distinct SVD projection directions on a representative few-shot calibration split (Seed 101):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Left (Output-Space) Projection (Default GSC-Merge):} $\tilde{V}_k^{(l)} = P_{out}^{(l)} V_k^{(l)}$, where $P_{out}^{(l)} = U_r U_r^T \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{out} \times d_{out}}$ is constructed from left-singular vectors of the horizontally concatenated task updates.
    \item \textbf{Right (Input-Space) Projection:} $\tilde{V}_k^{(l)} = V_k^{(l)} P_{in}^{(l)}$, where $P_{in}^{(l)} = V_r V_r^T \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{in} \times d_{in}}$ is constructed from right-singular vectors of the vertically concatenated task updates $\mathbf{M}_{in}^{(l)} = [ (V_1^{(l)})^T \mid \dots \mid (V_K^{(l)})^T ]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{(K \cdot d_{out}) \times d_{in}}$.
    \item \textbf{Bilateral Projection:} $\tilde{V}_k^{(l)} = P_{out}^{(l)} V_k^{(l)} P_{in}^{(l)}$, which simultaneously constrains both input and output spaces.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Methodological Analysis and Discussion}

The empirical results in Table~\ref{tab:projection_directions} demonstrate a stark and unambiguous performance gap: Left (Output-Space) projection dramatically outperforms both Right (Input-Space) and Bilateral projection methods across all rank configurations, achieving up to a \textbf{26.14\% absolute accuracy lead} at $\gamma=0.3$.

We provide two key mathematical and neural representation insights to explain this phenomenon:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Activation Coordinate Alignment:} In multi-head self-attention and MLP blocks, the output dimensions $d_{out}$ represent the coordinate system of features routed to subsequent layers and normalization layers. Left-singular projection (output-space SVD) constrains the output coordinates to a shared, collaborative subspace. This ensures that the generated activations across tasks remain aligned and reside on a valid manifold.
    \item \textbf{Catastrophic Input-Space Bottlenecking:} In contrast, the input dimension $d_{in}$ acts on task-specific, pre-trained feature mappings. Because different experts have evolved highly specialized and divergent internal representation manifolds, their input feature distributions are highly orthogonal. Restricting the input space with right-singular projection forces these disparate distributions into a single low-rank subspace, catastrophically destroying the expert networks' ability to process their respective input distributions. This results in near-random classification performance for several tasks.
\end{enumerate}

These findings provide direct, empirical proof that GSC-Merge's left-singular projection is the mathematically and architecturally optimal SVD configuration for multi-task weight-space alignment.

\section{Future Perspectives on GSC-Merge: Large-Scale NLP Feasibility and Hybrid Activation Routing}
\label{appendix:future_perspectives}

To address the insightful suggestions raised during the peer-review process, we provide a detailed discussion and formal mathematical formulation regarding: (1) the evaluation and feasibility of GSC-Merge on large-scale NLP benchmarks with High-Capacity Language Models, and (2) a proposed hybrid framework, \textbf{GSC-Route}, designed to bridge the practical performance gap under extreme task conflict.

\subsection{Large-Scale NLP Feasibility on High-Capacity Language Models}

While our empirical evaluations focused on Vision Transformers (ViT-Tiny), GSC-Merge is fully generalizable to high-capacity autoregressive language models (such as LLaMA or Mistral) fine-tuned on diverse NLP task suites. 

In a standard multi-task LLM alignment scenario, experts are fine-tuned on distinct capabilities, such as code generation (e.g., HumanEval), mathematical reasoning (e.g., GSM8k), and instruction-following (e.g., Alpaca). The weight updates of these experts exhibit significant task conflict, leading to parameter interference when merged. Applying GSC-Merge in this context proceeds as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Layer-Wise Update Extraction:} Task vectors are computed for the massive weight matrices (e.g., $W_{q}, W_{k}, W_{v}, W_{proj}, W_{gate}, W_{up}, W_{down}$ in LLaMA's attention and MLP blocks).
    \item \textbf{Randomized SVD Consensus:} To manage the high dimensionality (e.g., $d_{out} = 4096$), we apply Randomized SVD (detailed in Appendix~\ref{appendix:svd_randomized}) to horizontally concatenated task updates to construct the projection operator $P^{(l)} = U_r U_r^T$ onto the shared Grassmannian.
    \item \textbf{Task-Agnostic Blending:} The projected updates are blended using layer-wise coefficients optimized on a small language calibration set (e.g., 100 samples per task) via perplexity minimization.
\end{itemize}

Based on the low-rank properties of downstream adapter updates (e.g., LoRA), we hypothesize that GSC-Merge will achieve significant variance reduction on LLMs. By projecting out the high-frequency orthogonal updates that arise from task-specific overfitting, GSC-Merge is expected to prevent the degradation of core capabilities (such as standard English language modeling and logical reasoning) when merging specialized mathematical or code experts.

\subsection{Bridging the Performance Gap: The GSC-Route Hybrid Framework}

As highlighted in Section~\ref{subsec:limitations_scalability} and the experimental results, model merging in a truly task-agnostic setting suffers from a substantial performance degradation when tasks are highly conflicting (e.g., MNIST digits vs. CIFAR-10 natural objects). This performance gap occurs because forcing highly disparate representation manifolds into a single static weight configuration inevitably compromises individual expert capabilities.

To resolve this limitation while preserving parameter efficiency, we propose a hybrid framework called \textbf{GSC-Route (Grassmannian Subspace Consensus Routing)}. Instead of forcing a single static weight matrix for all inputs, GSC-Route combines the parameter efficiency of GSC-Merge with a lightweight input-dependent routing mechanism.

\textbf{Mathematical Formulation of GSC-Route.} Let $x$ represent the input activation to layer $l$. We define a routing network $g^{(l)}(x) = [g_1^{(l)}(x), \dots, g_K^{(l)}(x)]^T \in \mathbb{R}^K$ parameterizing a soft task assignment over the $K$ tasks, where:
\begin{equation}
    g_k^{(l)}(x) = \frac{\exp\left(w_k^T \phi(x)\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp\left(w_j^T \phi(x)\right)}
\end{equation}
where $w_k$ are learnable routing parameters, and $\phi(x)$ is a lightweight feature extraction function (e.g., mean pooling over the sequence dimension).

The dynamic weight matrix applied to input $x$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    W_{dynamic}^{(l)}(x) = W_{base}^{(l)} + P^{(l)} \left( \sum_{k=1}^K g_k^{(l)}(x) V_k^{(l)} \right)
\end{equation}
where $P^{(l)} = U_r^{(l)} (U_r^{(l)})^T$ is our Grassmannian projection operator of rank $r$.

\textbf{Regularization and Variance Reduction in GSC-Route.} By routing inside the Grassmannian consensus subspace rather than the unconstrained parameter space, GSC-Route provides two major advantages:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Guaranteed Representation Compatibility:} The dynamic update vector $\Delta W_{dynamic}^{(l)}(x) = P^{(l)} \left( \sum_{k=1}^K g_k^{(l)}(x) V_k^{(l)} \right)$ is mathematically guaranteed to lie within the shared consensus subspace $U_r^{(l)}$ for any input $x$. This prevents the router from outputting out-of-distribution parameter configurations that cause representation collapse.
    \item \textbf{Low-Complexity Routing:} Since the active parameters are projected onto the low-rank Grassmannian subspace, the routing function $g^{(l)}(x)$ only needs to navigate a smooth, low-dimensional coordinate system, making the routing optimization highly stable and robust to overfitting.
\end{enumerate}

By combining spectral consensus filtering with dynamic routing, GSC-Route represents a promising paradigm to completely bridge the performance gap to individual experts while maintaining a highly compact parameter footprint.

\section{Empirical Comparison of Gradient-Based vs. Derivative-Free Coefficient Search}

\subsection{Theoretical and Dimensional Scaling Analysis}

The quantitative benchmarks in Table~\ref{tab:derivative_free_results} reveal a massive and unambiguous performance gap: Adam achieves a highly optimized validation loss of $1.3062$ yielding a Joint Mean accuracy of $40.72\%$, whereas both Powell and Nelder-Mead fail to converge within the 300 evaluation budget, suffering from near-random multi-task accuracy (16.96\% and 10.80\% respectively).

We provide a rigorous mathematical and optimization-theoretic analysis to explain this discrepancy:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The Curse of Dimensionality in Derivative-Free Search:} Although a coefficient space of $192$ variables is extremely small compared to full-parameter deep network optimization, it represents an exceptionally high-dimensional space for standard derivative-free algorithms. For instance, the Nelder-Mead downhill simplex algorithm requires a simplex of $D + 1 = 193$ vertices in a $192$-dimensional space. Constructing the initial simplex and executing a single reflection, expansion, or contraction step across all vertices requires hundreds of function evaluations. Consequently, with a budget of $300$ evaluations, Nelder-Mead can barely explore the immediate neighborhood of its initialization, explaining its high final validation loss ($3.0515$) and random performance.
    \item \textbf{The Extreme Informational Value of Gradients:} First-order gradients provide a precise, simultaneous directional vector (the steepest descent direction) across all $192$ dimensions at each step. This allows Adam to perform coordinate-free progress toward a low-loss basin at every iteration. In contrast, derivative-free methods must probe the objective function blindly to estimate local coordinates or directions. In a 192-dimensional space, the volume of the search space scales exponentially with dimension, rendering random or coordinate-wise line searches (such as Powell's) highly token-inefficient without thousands of function evaluations.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Practical Recommendations}

Based on this analysis, we provide the following practical recommendations for multi-task model merging:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Prefer Gradient-Based Optimization when Gradients are Accessible:} If first-order backpropagation is supported by the serving or fine-tuning environment, practitioners should always use gradient-based optimizers (such as Adam or SGD) for coefficient search. They are computationally orders of magnitude more efficient and converge in very few steps.
    \item \textbf{Use Derivative-Free Methods Only under Black-Box Constraints:} Derivative-free search methods (CMA-ES, Bayesian Optimization, or Powell) should be reserved strictly for black-box deployment scenarios where gradients are completely inaccessible (e.g., serving platforms that only expose model inference outputs). In these environments, practitioners must allocate a significantly larger evaluation budget (e.g., $5{,}000$ to $10{,}000$ evaluations) or employ specialized Bayesian optimization surrogates to navigate the 192-dimensional landscape effectively.
\end{itemize}
