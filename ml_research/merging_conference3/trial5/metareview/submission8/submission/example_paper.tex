\section{Mathematical Analysis of Heterogeneity Collapse}
\label{app:heterogeneity_collapse}

In this appendix, we provide a formal mathematical proof demonstrating why batch-averaged dynamic routing coefficients (as used in prior frameworks such as QWS-Merge \cite{qws_merge}) trigger ``heterogeneity collapse'' when evaluated on multi-task mixed batches, and how our proposed EpiMerge completely avoids this representation compromise.

Let $x = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_B\}$ be a heterogeneous batch of $B$ input samples, where each sample $x_b$ is drawn from a different task distribution $\mathcal{D}_{\tau(b)}$, with $\tau(b) \in \{1, \dots, K\}$ denoting the task label of the $b$-th sample.
Let $\{T_1, T_2, \dots, T_K\}$ be the pre-trained expert task vectors.

\subsection{Prior Batch-Averaged Dynamic Merging}
Prior dynamic ensembling methods compute sample-specific ensembling coefficients $\alpha_k(x_b) \in \mathbb{R}$ for each expert $k$. However, to avoid sequentially reconstructing $B$ separate weight matrices (which would serialize GPU execution), they compute a single, batch-averaged routing coefficient $\bar{\alpha}_k$ across the batch:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\alpha}_k = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{b=1}^B \alpha_k(x_b)
\end{equation}
The merged weight matrix $W_{merged}$ applied to the entire batch is reconstructed as:
\begin{equation}
    W_{merged} = W_{base} + \sum_{k=1}^K \bar{\alpha}_k T_k
\end{equation}
The output activation $Y_b$ for the $b$-th sample is then computed as:
\begin{align}
    Y_b &= X_b \cdot W_{merged}^T \nonumber\\
        &= X_b \cdot \left( W_{base} + \sum_{k=1}^K \bar{\alpha}_k T_k \right)^T \nonumber\\
        &= X_b \cdot W_{base}^T + \sum_{k=1}^K \left( \frac{1}{B} \sum_{b'=1}^B \alpha_k(x_{b'}) \right) X_b \cdot T_k^T
\end{align}
Observe that the activation $Y_b$ of sample $x_b$ is directly scaled by the ensembling coefficients $\alpha_k(x_{b'})$ of all other samples $x_{b'}$ in the batch. 
If the batch is highly heterogeneous (e.g., $B/K$ samples from each of the $K$ tasks), and the individual routing coefficients are close to one-hot selectors ($\alpha_k(x_b) \approx 1$ if $\tau(b) = k$ and $0$ otherwise), the batch-averaged coefficient simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\alpha}_k \approx \frac{1}{K}
\end{equation}
Consequently, the dynamic weight matrix collapses to:
\begin{equation}
    W_{merged} \approx W_{base} + \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K T_k
\end{equation}
This is mathematically equivalent to a fixed, uniform static compromise. The model is forced to process task-specific inputs with a static average weight, which triggers catastrophic interference (heterogeneity collapse) and completely destroys the task specificity.

\subsection{True Sample-Wise Merging via EpiMerge}
In contrast, EpiMerge avoids any batch-averaging ensembling shortcuts. By utilizing vectorized tensor contractions via \texttt{torch.einsum}, we construct a unique weight matrix $W_{merged, b}$ for each sample in parallel:
\begin{equation}
    W_{merged, b} = W_{base} + \sum_{k=1}^K G_{k, b} \odot T_k
\end{equation}
The parallelized forward pass computes the output $Y_b$ as:
\begin{align}
    Y_b &= X_b \cdot W_{merged, b}^T \nonumber\\
        &= X_b \cdot W_{base}^T + \sum_{k=1}^K X_b \cdot \left( G_{k, b} \odot T_k \right)^T
\end{align}
Because the weight matrix $W_{merged, b}$ is sample-specific and independent of any other sample $x_{b'}$ in the batch, the activation $Y_b$ is completely decoupled from other batch elements:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial Y_b}{\partial \mathbf{h}(x)_{b'}} = 0 \quad \forall b' \neq b
\end{equation}
This mathematically guarantees that EpiMerge is completely immune to the batch size, task skew, or non-I.I.D. clustering of the test-time stream, preserving perfect task-specific parameter regulation.
