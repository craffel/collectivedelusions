# Novelty and Delta Analysis

## Key Novel Aspects
1. **Rigorizing Model Merging with Statistical Learning Theory:** Adaptive model merging (both online TTA like AdaMerging and offline few-shot tuning) has previously been treated purely through empirical heuristics. This paper is the first to establish a rigorous learning-theoretic framework. It treats layer-wise ensembling coefficients as a continuous global trajectory and applies Rademacher complexity theory to bound its capacity.
2. **Polynomial Trajectory Projection:** The concept of modeling ensembling coefficients as a smooth, low-degree polynomial trajectory along network depth ($z = \frac{l}{L-1}$) is a highly novel geometric constraint. By projecting the high-dimensional coefficient space onto a low-degree polynomial subspace, it acts as an analytical low-pass filter to remove high-frequency validation noise and prevent overfitting.
3. **Consensus-Pulling Rademacher Penalty:** Standard $L_1$ regularization pulls parameters towards $0$. In ensembling, this is destructive because a coefficient of $0$ removes an expert entirely, while a naive $L_1$ on sigmoid inputs drives them to $0.5$ (which scales the overall weights by $K/2$, causing massive representation explosion). Centering the penalty around the uniform consensus baseline ($\theta_{\text{uniform}} = \sigma^{-1}(1/K)$) is a highly original and mathematically sound contribution that ensures stable weight scaling.
4. **Theoretical Bridge and Localization:** Deriving spectrally-normalized Rademacher bounds on the merged network via functional linearization, proving a derivative bound via Markov's theorem for polynomials, and establishing local Rademacher complexity fast rates are outstanding theoretical contributions that explain *why* trajectory constraints generalize exceptionally well in few-shot regimes.

## Delta from Prior Work
- **Delta from AdaMerging (Yang et al., 2023):** AdaMerging performs unconstrained test-time adaptation on local streams, optimizing $K \times L$ parameters. RBPM constrains this space to a low-degree polynomial trajectory ($d \le 2$), which reduces the search space size from $K \times L$ to $K \times (d+1)$. Empirically, RBPM outperforms AdaMerging and prevents the transductive overfitting and representation collapse that unconstrained AdaMerging suffers from.
- **Delta from PolyMerge (2024):** While PolyMerge restricted coefficients to a polynomial trajectory online under entropy minimization, it lacked any learning-theoretic foundations, bounds, or regularization. RBPM introduces the first formal Rademacher complexity bounds, local Rademacher theory, and the Consensus-Pulling Rademacher Penalty, as well as offline few-shot optimization with label supervision to resolve task conflicts.
- **Delta from Coordinate-wise Merging (TIES-Merging, DARE-Merging, Sparse Task Arithmetic):** These methods prune and merge weights coordinate-wise at a static layer level, assuming coordinate independence. They do not exploit layer depth structure or allow continuous adaptive ensembling. RBPM works at the global trajectory level, preserving dense backbone weights while controlling capacity purely through trajectory-level coefficients, which prevents the representational destruction caused by coordinate pruning in heterogeneous or transformer models.
- **Delta from Offline Few-Shot Tuning (OFS-Tune):** OFS-Tune optimizes unconstrained layer parameters and suffers from extreme overfitting to the tiny calibration set. RBPM geometrically constrains and regularizes the optimization space to resolve this overfitting, and introduces PCGrad to handle multi-task gradient conflict.

## Characterization of Novelty
The novelty of this work is **highly significant**. It is not merely an incremental combination of existing heuristics; rather, it introduces a major paradigm shift by bridging weight-space ensembling with statistical learning theory. The mathematical derivations are complete and deep (including Rademacher complexity of trajectory space, Ledoux-Talagrand contraction, spectrally-normalized complexity, local Rademacher complexity, and Markov's theorem), and they directly guide the design of the consensus-pulling regularizer and the trajectory parameterization.
This theoretical framework successfully solves a major open problem in adaptive merging (overparameterization and overfitting), and its correctness is fully validated by empirical results, establishing a beautiful symmetry between theory and practice.
