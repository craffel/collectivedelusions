# Evaluation Step 4: Experimental Check & Results Validation

## 1. Experimental Design & Benchmarks
The experimental setup is exceptionally thorough, with several high-value aspects:
- **Baseline Selection:** The paper includes a rich set of baselines covering static merging (Uniform Merge / Task Arithmetic, OFS-Tune), online Test-Time Adaptation (AdaMerging), SOTA dynamic merging (QWS-Merge), and classical routing (unregularized Linear Router), which are then compared directly to the proposed BC-Router variants (BL-Router, GLS-Router, and BSigmoid-Router in both unregularized and regularized formats). This exhaustive coverage ensures no key comparisons are omitted.
- **Evaluation Settings:** The experiments are divided into **Homogeneous Evaluation** (independent test sets of 1000 images per task) and **Heterogeneous Evaluation** (temporal streams of 2000 shuffled samples across batch sizes $B \in \{1, 16, 256\}$). This represents a highly realistic evaluation of dynamic model merging behavior under both ideal and noisy, real-world deployment conditions.
- **Ablation & Sensitivity Studies:** The inclusion of an L2 regularization strength sensitivity analysis ($\gamma \in [0, 10^{-5}, 10^{-4}, 10^{-3}]$) and a calibration set size scaling ablation (scaling from 64 to 128 and 256 samples) adds immense empirical depth and supports the authors' key claims with extensive, high-signal data.

## 2. Evidence Supporting the Authors' Key Claims
The empirical results provide compelling and robust evidence for the paper's core scientific arguments:
1. **Classical Routing Competitiveness via Regularization:** The unregularized classical Linear Router suffers from severe SVHN collapse ($74.00 \pm 16.14\%$). Applying standard L2 weight decay ($\gamma = 1 \times 10^{-4}$) during calibration completely rescues this baseline, boosting SVHN accuracy to **$91.73 \pm 3.71\%$** (outperforming QWS-Merge by a massive $+12.00\%$). This strongly supports the claim that prior reports of classical linear routing failures were heavily confounded by under-tuned baselines.
2. **Deconstructing the Softmax Under-Scaling Flaw:** The Softmax-based BL-Router collapses catastrophically on SVHN ($31.73 \pm 16.03\%$ unregularized, $43.20 \pm 8.02\%$ regularized). This supports the authors' mathematical proof of the structural under-scaling design flaw: capping the Softmax sum at $0.3$ forces each task to a meager $0.075$ scale under uncertainty, which is mathematically insufficient to transfer specialized expert weights.
3. **Resolving the Zero-Sum Bottleneck via BSigmoid-Router:** Replacing Softmax with independent, uncoupled Sigmoids in the **BSigmoid-Router (Ours - Reg)** completely resolves the zero-sum competitive bottleneck during calibration, achieving an outstanding and highly robust joint homogeneous mean of **$83.63 \pm 2.07\%$** (outperforming QWS-Merge on MNIST: $93.87\%$ vs. $92.53\%$ and CIFAR-10: $91.87\%$ vs. $88.67\%$).
4. **QWS-Merge as a Structural Regularizer:** The unregularized layer-wise GLS-Router exhibits extreme sensitivity across seeds (SVHN std.\ dev.\ of $24.30\%$) and collapses on FashionMNIST ($64.80\%$). This empirically validates the non-trivial insight that unregularized layer-specific scaling parameters overfit severely on few-shot calibration budgets, and that QWS-Merge's complex wave projection equations serve as a highly effective structural regularizer.
5. **The Batch-Averaging Bottleneck on Heterogeneous Streams:** On temporal interleaved streams, as batch size $B$ increases from 1 to 256, all dynamic routing methods converge to similar performance profiles (~83-84%), matching static Uniform Merge. This strongly supports the authors' claim that batch-averaging acts as a destructive bottleneck that flattens specialized sample-level coefficients at large batch sizes.

## 3. The "Generalist-Specialist Paradox" & Practical Performance Critique
From a **Practitioner's perspective**, there is a highly critical empirical reality that must be highlighted:
- **Underperformance vs. Simple Static Average:** Despite the elegant mathematics and calibration of the proposed dynamic routers, **none of the trainable dynamic routing methods outperform a simple, non-trainable, parameter-free static Uniform Merge** in overall homogeneous joint mean accuracy ($85.10\%$ for Uniform Merge vs. $82.80\%$ for Linear Router (Reg), $78.67\%$ for GLS-Router (Reg), and $83.63\%$ for BSigmoid-Router (Reg)).
- **The Zero-Sum Game of Weight Space Capacity:** Dynamically steering the model's weights toward a specific task's parameters on-the-fly to maximize local accuracy (such as **Linear Router (Reg)** achieving a peak $91.73\%$ on SVHN) inevitably pulls the weight configuration away from other tasks, causing severe degradation (MNIST drops from $93.60\%$ to $89.60\%$, FashionMNIST drops from $75.60\%$ to $65.87\%$, and CIFAR-10 drops from $93.60\%$ to $84.00\%$).
- **Practical Utility Constraints:** For a generalist multi-task application, a simple static Uniform Merge is structurally superior, more stable across random seeds, computationally free, and achieves higher overall multi-task accuracy. Dynamic weight routing is only practically useful in niche deployment scenarios where: (1) local, single-domain specialization is temporarily critical (such as local temporal streaming of SVHN digits on edge devices), (2) real-time safety steering/capability masking is required, or (3) Softmax-free independent sigmoidal projections (BSigmoid-Router) are employed, which minimize the task-degradation penalty and preserve near-Uniform joint performance ($83.63\%$).

The authors must be highly commended for their exceptional transparency in Section 4.4, where they openly discuss and critically deconstruct these operational trade-offs and the "Generalist-Specialist Paradox." This represents an exemplary standard of scientific honesty that is rarely seen in model-merging literature.
