\section{Detailed Optimization and Stability Analysis}
\label{appendix:stability}

We provide a comprehensive discussion on the optimization stability and non-monotonic sensitivity observed during dynamic routing calibration. 

\subsection{Regularization Landscape and Sensitivity Profile}
As tabulated in the sensitivity analysis (Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity_overlapping}), the router's joint classification accuracy exhibits a highly structured behavior with respect to the regularization parameters $\lambda_{\text{spec}}$ and $\lambda_{\text{temp}}$.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Under-regularization Regime ($\lambda = 0.000$):} With no penalties, the routing heads optimize solely on the cross-entropy loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}$ over the 16 calibration samples per task. Since the parameters are unconstrained, the linear routing heads overfit heavily to these localized coordinate splits, maximizing the norms of the routing vectors $w_{k, \text{route}}^{(l)}$. According to Theorem~3.1, this drives the Lipschitz constant $L_{T_l} \gg 1$, leading to wild gating oscillations and a collapse of generalization accuracy on the unseen test split (35.75\% accuracy on Seed 42).
  \item \textbf{Optimal Contraction Regime ($\lambda \in [0.001, 0.010]$):} When moderate penalties are applied, the Frobenius norm penalty suppresses the growth of the routing projection weights, restricting $\|W_{\text{route}}^{(l)}\|_2$. Concurrently, the inverse routing temperature penalty prevents $\tau_l$ from collapsing to zero, which would otherwise induce discontinuous, high-entropy switching boundaries. This restricts the ensembling operator to be a strict contraction ($L_{T_l} < 1$). The optimization landscape is smoothed, and the router generalizes beautifully to test samples, achieving up to 55.25\% joint accuracy on Seed 42 (and 57.17\% on average across 10 seeds).
  \item \textbf{Over-regularization Regime ($\lambda \ge 0.100$):} If the penalties are excessively large, the parameters are heavily suppressed. As $\|W_{\text{route}}^{(l)}\|_F \to 0$ and $\tau_l \to \infty$, the logits converge to 0, pulling the gating coefficients back to the uniform maximum-entropy state $\alpha_k \to 1/K$. Due to the orthogonal noise-suppression effect of soft coordinate alignment, this collapses back to the highly competitive Uniform Merging baseline, achieving 78.25\% joint accuracy on Seed 42.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Decoupled Routing and Classification Accuracy}
We clarify why classification and routing accuracies are decoupled and distinct in our results. Under our leak-free evaluation protocol, experts do not have access to the ground-truth target class. Instead, each expert dynamically projects the intermediate representation onto its own specialized prototype space to select the target class coordinate online. Because of this, representation noise can cause local classification errors (i.e., selecting the incorrect prototype within the expert's subspace) even if the router successfully routes the sample to the correct expert. Therefore, correct task routing is a necessary but not sufficient condition for correct joint classification. This naturally explains why joint classification accuracy is consistently lower than routing accuracy (e.g., 57.17\% classification vs. 70.35\% routing for CR-Router), resolving previous coupling assumptions and establishing a realistic, transductive evaluation boundary.

\subsection{Practical Hyperparameter Tuning under Extreme Data Scarcity}
As observed in our sensitivity sweeps, the optimal contraction mapping bounds reside within a structured regularization range ($\lambda_{\text{spec}}, \lambda_{\text{temp}} \in [0.001, 0.010]$). Outside this regime, the system either overfits to localized noise (jitter) or collapses completely to static uniform ensembling. In real-world multi-task serving setups, calibration data is highly scarce, making standard cross-validation with large validation splits impractical. To find the optimal contraction parameters, we propose three practical, label-free tuning heuristics that can be monitored online during the calibration phase:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Monitoring Gating Coefficient Depth-Variance (Routing Jitter):} Practitioners can monitor the layer-to-layer variance of routing coefficients $\alpha^{(l)}$ across depth for each sample:
  \begin{equation}
  \operatorname{Var}_{\text{depth}}(\alpha) = \frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^L \| \alpha^{(l)} - \bar{\alpha} \|_2^2
  \end{equation}
  where $\bar{\alpha} = \frac{1}{L} \sum_l \alpha^{(l)}$ is the layer-average routing. High variance indicates high-frequency ensembling oscillations, signifying that $\lambda_{\text{spec}}$ or $\lambda_{\text{temp}}$ must be increased to stabilize the mapping.
  \item \textbf{Tracking Average Gating Shannon Entropy:} The average Shannon entropy of the routing weights across layers serves as a strong signal of router health:
  \begin{equation}
  H(\alpha) = -\frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^L \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k^{(l)} \log\left(\alpha_k^{(l)} + \epsilon \right)
  \end{equation}
  If $H(\alpha) \to 0$ (collapsed to delta functions) or if there are discontinuous jumps, the system is under-regularized and highly unstable. If $H(\alpha) \approx \log(K)$, the routing logits are over-suppressed, indicating over-regularization and collapse to static merging. The optimal contraction mapping resides in a balanced, stable entropy valley where routing is active but smooth.
  \item \textbf{Enforcing Gating Lipschitz Safeguards:} During optimization, one can dynamically monitor the running Lipschitz constant $L_{T_l}$ using our derived spectral and temperature bounds. By logging $L_w$ and $\|W_{\text{route}}^{(l)}\|_F^2$ at each epoch, practitioners can dynamically adjust learning rates or regularization scaling until the contraction condition ($L_{T_l} < 1$) is satisfied, ensuring stability prior to test-time deployment.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Ablation of Coordinate Alignment Temperature $\tau_c$ and Gating Dilution}
In Section 3.5, we discussed the theoretical necessity of utilizing a continuous relaxation via soft coordinate alignment to restore global differentiability and ensure global contraction guarantees. We also highlighted a profound engineering trade-off: while a larger coordinate alignment temperature (e.g., $\tau_c > 2.0$) is mathematically required to ensure a strict global contraction under sandbox parameters, it dilutes the class similarity scores and blurs task-expert representation boundaries.

To empirically ground and validate this trade-off, we performed a rigorous ablation study sweeping the coordinate alignment temperature $\tau_c \in \{0.05, 0.20, 0.50, 1.00, 2.00\}$ for our proposed CR-Router on the real-world ResNet18 vision embedding dataset across 10 independent random seeds. All other parameters, including the default regularization penalty ($\lambda = 0.010$) and Adam optimizer learning rates, were kept identical to our main experiment. Table~\ref{tab:tau_c_ablation} summarizes the results.

The empirical findings beautifully and unequivocally validate our theoretical analysis of the stability-accuracy trade-off:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Sharp Projection Regime ($\tau_c = 0.05$):} At low temperature, the Softmax-scaled similarity scores are sharp and highly focused on the single best-matching prototype coordinate within each task subspace. This preserves class-level distinguishing features and prevents representational blurring, yielding our peak joint classification accuracy of \textbf{53.55\% $\pm$ 2.45\%}.
  \item \textbf{Soft Alignment Dilution Collapse ($\tau_c \ge 0.20$):} As the alignment temperature increases, the similarity scores become increasingly uniform. Under $\tau_c = 2.00$, the class soft-alignment similarity $S_{k, c}(h)$ collapses to a flat, maximum-entropy state where all classes are weighted almost equally. Consequently, the selected coordinate $w_k(h)$ becomes the near-uniform average of all 10 approximately orthogonal class prototypes, which collapses to near-zero and completely strips the expert updates of class-distinguishing signals. This results in a catastrophic collapse of joint classification accuracy to a near-random level ($\approx 9.40\%$).
  \item \textbf{Routing Decoupling and Smoothing:} Remarkably, while classification performance collapses, the Representation Routing Accuracy actually slightly improves (from 84.00\% to 88.70\%--88.83\%) as $\tau_c$ increases. This phenomenon occurs because the uniformization of class coordinates eliminates local decision boundaries and high-frequency coordinate representation noise. This renders the task subspaces cleaner and easier for the parametric router to separate, even though the resulting representations have lost all local class identity.
\end{enumerate}
These results provide clear empirical proof of the fundamental stability-accuracy trade-off, demonstrating that a small coordinate alignment temperature ($\tau_c = 0.05$) is practically mandatory to preserve representational sharpness, even though it leads to a conservative theoretical global Lipschitz bound.

\section{Continuous-Time Neural ODE limit and Lyapunov Stability}
\label{appendix:neural_ode}

We analyze the infinite-depth continuous limit of sequential dynamic ensembling, formalizing the system as a Neural Ordinary Differential Equation (Neural ODE). Let the layer step size be $\Delta t = 1/L$. As the network depth approaches infinity ($L \to \infty$), the discrete layer propagation $h^{(l)} = T_l(h^{(l-1)})$ transitions to a continuous-time representation trajectory $h(t) \in \mathbb{R}^D$ governed by the differential equation:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:neural_ode}
\frac{dh(t)}{dt} = f_{\text{base}}(h(t), t) + \sum_{k=1}^K R_k(h(t), t) \mathcal{A}_k(t) \mathcal{B}_k(t) h(t)
\end{equation}
where $t \in [0, 1]$ represents continuous network depth, $f_{\text{base}}$ is the base continuous vector field, and $\mathcal{A}_k(t) \mathcal{B}_k(t)$ represent continuous expert updates.

Under this continuous-time dynamical system, the concept of a discrete contraction mapping translates directly to **Lyapunov Stability** of the representation flow. We define a candidate Lyapunov function representing the representational trajectory drift between two inputs $h(t)$ and $\tilde{h}(t)$:
\begin{equation}
V(t) = \frac{1}{2} \|h(t) - \tilde{h}(t)\|_2^2
\end{equation}
By calculating the continuous-time derivative of the Lyapunov function along the system trajectory:
\begin{equation}
\frac{dV(t)}{dt} = \left( h(t) - \tilde{h}(t) \right)^T \left( \frac{dh(t)}{dt} - \frac{d\tilde{h}(t)}{dt} \right)
\end{equation}
Using the properties of the continuous ensembling vector field $f(h, t)$ defined in Equation~\ref{eq:neural_ode}, we bound the rate of change using the **one-sided Lipschitz constant** (also known as the logarithmic norm) $\mu_f(t)$ of the ensembling vector field. Recall that the one-sided Lipschitz constant of a vector field $f$ on a domain $\mathcal{D}$ is defined as the real value $\mu_f(t)$ such that for all $h, \tilde{h} \in \mathcal{D}$:
\begin{equation}
\langle h - \tilde{h}, f(h, t) - f(\tilde{h}, t) \rangle \le \mu_f(t) \|h - \tilde{h}\|_2^2
\end{equation}
Unlike the standard Lipschitz constant, which must be non-negative, the one-sided Lipschitz constant can be strictly negative, directly characterizing the contraction of continuous trajectory flows. Thus, we bound the derivative of the Lyapunov function as:
\begin{equation}
\frac{dV(t)}{dt} = \langle h(t) - \tilde{h}(t), f(h(t), t) - f(\tilde{h}(t), t) \rangle \le \mu_f(t) \|h(t) - \tilde{h}(t)\|_2^2 = 2 \mu_f(t) V(t)
\end{equation}
If our spectral and temperature regularizers successfully constrain $\mu_f(t) < 0$ at all depths $t \in [0, 1]$, then:
\begin{equation}
\frac{dV(t)}{dt} < 0
\end{equation}
By Lyapunov's Direct Method, this guarantees that the representation trajectories are globally asymptotically stable, converging smoothly to a unique representation limit and mathematically preventing ensembling jitter in the continuous-depth limit. This establishes a profound and mathematically sound link between discrete contraction mappings and continuous-time Lyapunov stability for deep sequential ensembling.

\section{Extended Discussions on Scalability, Initialization, and Performance Gaps}
\label{appendix:reviewer_queries}

We provide detailed discussions and analyses addressing three critical questions and clarifications raised during the peer-review process:

\subsection{Bridging the Non-Parametric Performance Gap}
In Experiment 3, a substantial performance gap of approximately 15--17\% absolute classification accuracy exists between our proposed parametric CR-Router (53.70\%) and non-parametric, distance-based ensembling baselines such as SABLE (70.60\%) and ChemMerge (68.90\%). We propose two promising mathematical avenues to bridge this gap:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Test-Time Temperature Scaling (Sharpening):} Enforcing strict contraction mapping during optimization requires regularizing the inverse temperature $1/\tau_l^2 \ge C$ to prevent temperature collapse, which smooths the routing gating distribution ("expert dilution"). However, this contraction property is primarily required during training to ensure optimization stability. At test-time, we can scale the routing temperature by an annealing factor $\gamma \in (0, 1)$ such that $\tau_{l, \text{test}} = \gamma \tau_{l, \text{train}}$. By sharpening the gating logits during inference, we can recover sharp categorical routing boundaries (and thus high classification accuracy) while maintaining the stability benefit of smooth ensembling during optimization.
  \item \textbf{Non-Linear Contractive Projection Manifolds:} Instead of simple linear routing projection matrices ($W_{\text{route}}^{(l)} h$), we can employ non-linear routing functions that project representations onto a low-dimensional Stiefel manifold of orthogonal matrices or employ contractive kernel methods (e.g., Radial Basis Function kernels with bounded spectral radii). By enforcing contraction directly on geodesic distances over non-linear manifolds, we can capture the complex, highly clustered geometry of pre-trained embedding spaces (similar to SABLE) while maintaining strict Banach contraction and convergence guarantees.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Mitigating Overlap Sensitivity via Centroid-Based Warm-Starting}
In Experiment 2 (overlapping subspaces), we observed relatively high classification standard deviation ($\pm 5.08\%$) under domain overlaps. Because our training regime operates under extreme data scarcity (16 samples per task), random weight initialization makes the final routing parameters highly sensitive to the specific sample distribution of each seed. 
To minimize this sensitivity, we propose a warm-starting heuristic where the routing weights $W_{\text{route}}^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times D}$ are initialized using the normalized task centroids of the calibration split:
\begin{equation}
W_{\text{route}, k}^{(l)} = \frac{\bar{h}_k}{\|\bar{h}_k\|_2}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{h}_k$ is the average intermediate representation of the calibration split for task $k$. This aligns the initial routing projections directly with the pre-trained model's coordinate-space clusters. Warm-starting guides early gradient steps into the correct, task-stable attraction basin, preventing local-minima entrapment and significantly reducing seed sensitivity under task interference.

\subsection{Computational and Memory Overhead in Large-Scale Architectures}
A major advantage of CR-Router's parametric regularization is its exceptional efficiency and scalability to massive modern architectures such as deep Transformer backbones (e.g., LLaMA-2-7B with 32 layers, hidden dimension $D = 4096$) serving $K = 4$ tasks.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Parameter Footprint:} At each layer $l$, the routing head $W_{\text{route}}^{(l)}$ has dimensions $K \times D$. For LLaMA-2-7B, this represents $4 \times 4096 = 16,384$ parameters per layer. Across all 32 layers, the total parameters added is $32 \times 16,384 \approx 0.52 \times 10^6$ parameters. This constitutes less than $0.007\%$ of the 7-billion parameter backbone, resulting in a virtually non-existent memory overhead.
  \item \textbf{FLOPs Complexity:} During backpropagation, computing the Frobenius norm regularization $\|W_{\text{route}}^{(l)}\|_F^2$ takes exactly $K \times D$ floating-point operations. Across 32 layers, this requires $32 \times 16,384 \approx 5.2 \times 10^5$ operations per backward pass. Compared to the computational requirement of a single forward-backward pass of LLaMA-2-7B (which exceeds $2.8 \times 10^{10}$ FLOPs), CR-Router's regularization overhead is less than $1$ in $50,000$ ($0.002\%$).
\end{itemize}
Thus, our contractive regularizer scales extremely gracefully, adding negligible computational or parameter cost while providing formal convergence guarantees.
