# 3. Soundness and Methodology

## Clarity of the Description
The methodology is described with extreme mathematical precision. The authors walk through the Grassmannian manifold $\mathcal{G}(d, D)$, the eigenvalue shrinkage proof, the cut-locus-aware logarithm map, the fixed reference point formulation, differentiability proofs, and computational complexity reductions. Every step of the framework is thoroughly documented, which is commendable.

## Appropriateness of Methods
From a design and engineering perspective, the methods are **overly complex and disproportionate** to the problem. The authors introduce a vast array of high-level mathematical tools (differential geometry, Karcher means, Chebyshev polynomials, custom SVD wrappers with sign alignment and Tikhonov regularization) to solve a representation-space alignment issue that could be handled much more simply. 
The entire premise of the C-Lie-MM framework is to preserve projection manifold properties (e.g., idempotency, $P^2 = P$). However, the practical utility of this preservation is questionable when simpler models with slightly lower geometric fidelity achieve virtually identical performance.

## Potential Technical Flaws & Conceptual Concerns
1. **The "Coordinate Collapse" Strawman**: The main motivation of the paper is that flat linear ensembling causes a catastrophic "coordinate collapse" where representation norms decay exponentially to zero, causing accuracy to drop to random guessing ($25.00\%$). However, as the authors' own self-critical ablation study in Section 4.3 reveals, this catastrophic collapse **only** occurs in an artificial, feedforward stack that lacks residual connections or normalization. When standard architectural components like residual connections and LayerNorm are introduced, the catastrophic collapse disappears, and uniform merging achieves a reasonable performance of $51.90\%$ (while optimized flat baselines like SABLE reach $64.60\%$). Thus, the "catastrophic coordinate collapse" is a worst-case theoretical result that does not represent the behavior of actual, modern neural networks.
2. **Minimal Gain from Soft, Cooperative Ensembling**: The authors argue that flat baselines can only survive coordinate collapse by driving their routing temperatures to zero ($\tau \to 0$), forcing them into a hard gating regime and sacrificing the "ensembling benefits of soft, cooperative blending." However, the quantitative results show that this "soft cooperative blending" provides almost no practical benefit. In the overlapping manifold setting, the flat, hard-gating Temp-Only ERM baseline achieves **$70.00\%$** accuracy, while the highly complex, soft-cooperative C-Lie-MM achieves **$70.30\%$** accuracy. A margin of $+0.30\%$ is statistically insignificant (well within the $\pm 4.01\%$ standard deviation) and raises a critical question: why introduce multiple layers of non-Euclidean geometry and SVD approximations if a simple, flat hard-gating model performs just as well?
3. **Reference Point Instability under Orthogonality**: Under the boundary condition of perfectly orthogonal tasks, the spectral gap of $P_{\text{avg}}$ collapses to zero, and the global minimizer $Y_0$ is no longer unique. The authors argue that SVD solvers choose an arbitrary basis and that this doesn't affect gradient-tracking because $Y_0$ is detached from the gradient graph. However, this means that different training runs or minor floating-point perturbations can lead to wildly different coordinate frames, which compromises the stability and consistency of the local tangent space approximation across different machines or backends.
4. **Complexity of Edge-Serving Approximations**: The authors propose Chebyshev polynomial approximations to bypass the GPU SVD bottleneck. While this is mathematically clever, it adds yet another layer of complexity (pre-computing Chebyshev coefficients, running 6th-order polynomial evaluations on matrices, scaling issues with rank $d$). A simpler, direct approach like SABLE or basic gating completely avoids the need for SVD, SVD-tracking, and polynomial approximations in the first place.

## Reproducibility
The mathematical equations are complete, and the inclusion of a 333-line custom Triton GPU kernel (`triton_kernel.py`) in the codebase demonstrates that the system has been implemented and is technically reproducible. However, the sheer density of custom SVD tracking wrappers, Chebyshev polynomial coefficients, and Triton memory-handling code makes this framework extremely difficult to integrate, debug, or maintain compared to standard Euclidean merging methods.
