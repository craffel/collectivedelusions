\section{Grassmannian Manifold Operators and Numerical Stability}
\label{sec:appendix_stability}

In this appendix, we present detailed algorithmic procedures for computing the Grassmannian logarithm and exponential maps, alongside numerical stability considerations that are crucial when implementing these operators in limited-precision floating-point arithmetic (e.g., standard PyTorch \texttt{float32} or \texttt{bfloat16}).

\subsection{Exact Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) Guarding and Cut-Locus Resolution}
The Riemannian logarithm map on $\mathcal{G}(d, D)$ requires the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the inner product matrix:
\begin{equation}
    Y_0^T Y_1 = V_0 \operatorname{diag}(\Gamma) U_0^T \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}
\end{equation}
In practical deep neural network training and evaluation pipelines, particularly when task experts are highly distinct or orthogonal, the matrix $Y_0^T Y_1$ becomes singular (or zero). Our robust, cut-locus-aware closed-form logarithm map bypasses any direct matrix inversion by extracting the orthogonal component $M_{\perp} = Y_1 - Y_0 (Y_0^T Y_1)$ and evaluating its left singular vectors $U_{\perp}$ using the cosecant of the principal angles:
\begin{equation}
    U_{\perp} = M_{\perp} U_0 \operatorname{diag}\left(\csc(\Theta)\right)
\end{equation}
where $\theta_i = \arccos(\Gamma_i)$. To prevent division-by-zero gradient issues and handle exact matching components ($\theta_i = 0$), we set $\csc(\theta_i) = 0$ when $\theta_i \le 10^{-6}$.

Furthermore, backpropagation through SVD is famously unstable and prone to producing \texttt{NaN} gradients when singular values are extremely close or identical (which corresponds to subspace degeneracy). To address this backpropagation instability, we clamp the principal angles and enforce a minimum angle separation of $\delta = 10^{-5}$ between adjacent singular values during gradient evaluation. This ensures that the denominator of the SVD gradient:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial U}{\partial A} \propto \frac{1}{\sigma_i - \sigma_j}
\end{equation}
remains strictly bounded away from zero, providing outstanding end-to-end numerical stability during joint representation and temperature optimization.

\subsection{Projection Matrix Idempotency Verification}
To numerically verify Theorem~\ref{thm:preservation} in double precision, we can monitor the deviation from idempotency:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta_{\text{idem}} = \left\|P_{\text{merged}}^2 - P_{\text{merged}}\right\|_F
\end{equation}
where $\|\cdot\|_F$ denotes the Frobenius norm. In our PyTorch Coordinate Sandbox, we find that for the proposed \textbf{Lie-MM (GGB Ours)}, $\Delta_{\text{idem}} \approx 1.24 \times 10^{-7}$ across all $14$ layers under overlapping manifolds, confirming perfect adherence to the Grassmannian manifold structure. In contrast, flat ensembling baselines such as SABLE and PAC-ZCA (UN-PCA) yield $\Delta_{\text{idem}} \approx 0.187$, corresponding to a high deviation that directly causes eigenvalue decay and coordinate collapse.

\subsection{Algorithm for Momentum-Based Reference Point Update (EMA-C-Lie-MM)}
\label{sec:appendix_ema}
As discussed in Section~3.4, when task expert adapters are fine-tuned or trained end-to-end concurrently with the C-Lie-MM routing parameters, the central coordinate reference point $Y_0$ must evolve to stay aligned with the moving expert subspaces. While updating $Y_0$ discretely at epoch boundaries is highly efficient, it can introduce step-like coordinate shifts in the tangent space at boundary transitions. We address this using a continuous, momentum-smoothed exponential moving average (EMA) update on the projection-metric centroid. 

Algorithm~\ref{alg:ema_update} presents the formal step-by-step procedure for executing joint end-to-end training of model parameters (backbone and expert low-rank adapter bases) and routing temperatures, while continuously and stably updating the coordinate reference point $Y_0$.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Momentum-Based Reference Point Update (EMA-C-Lie-MM)}
\label{alg:ema_update}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE {\bfseries Input:} Initial task expert projection bases $\{V_k^{(0)}\}_{k=1}^K$ where $V_k^{(0)} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times d}$, initial routing parameters $\phi$, momentum hyperparameter $\beta \in [0, 1)$, update frequency $N$ (steps), total training steps $T$.
\STATE {\bfseries Initialize:}
\STATE \quad Compute initial average projection matrix: $P_{\text{avg}}^{(0)} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K V_k^{(0)} V_k^{(0)T} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$.
\STATE \quad Extract initial reference point $Y_0^{(0)}$ as the top $d$ eigenvectors of $P_{\text{avg}}^{(0)}$ via SVD: $P_{\text{avg}}^{(0)} = U \Sigma U^T$, and set $Y_0^{(0)} = U_{:, 1:d}$.
\STATE \quad Precompute initial tangent matrices: $H_k^{(0)} = \log_{Y_0^{(0)}}\left(V_k^{(0)}\right)$ for $k = 1, \dots, K$.
\FOR{step $t = 1$ {\bfseries to} $T$}
    \STATE Sample a batch of inputs $\mathbf{x}$.
    \STATE Evaluate routing coefficients $\alpha_k(\mathbf{x}; \phi) = \text{Gibbs}(\mathbf{x}; \phi)$ for $k = 1, \dots, K$.
    \STATE Blend tangent matrices: $H_{\text{merged}}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k(\mathbf{x}) H_k^{(t-1)}$.
    \STATE Project back to Grassmannian via exponential map relative to static reference:
    \STATE \quad $Y_{\text{merged}}(\mathbf{x}) = \exp_{Y_0^{(t-1)}}\left( H_{\text{merged}}(\mathbf{x}) \right)$.
    \STATE Pass representation through merged projection layer: $\mathbf{z}_{\text{out}} = Y_{\text{merged}}(\mathbf{x}) Y_{\text{merged}}(\mathbf{x})^T \mathbf{z}_{\text{in}}$.
    \STATE Compute loss $\mathcal{L}$ and perform backpropagation to update backbone weights, expert bases $V_k^{(t)}$, and routing parameters $\phi$.
    \IF{$t \bmod N == 0$}
        \STATE Compute step average projection matrix: $P_{\text{step}} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K V_k^{(t)} V_k^{(t)T}$.
        \STATE Update the running projection matrix via EMA:
        \STATE \quad $P_{\text{avg}}^{(t)} = \beta P_{\text{avg}}^{(t-1)} + (1 - \beta) P_{\text{step}}$.
        \STATE Extract the updated coordinate reference point $Y_0^{(t)}$ via SVD of $P_{\text{avg}}^{(t)}$:
        \STATE \quad $P_{\text{avg}}^{(t)} = U \Sigma U^T \quad \Longrightarrow \quad Y_0^{(t)} = U_{:, 1:d}$.
        \STATE Re-project current experts to the new reference tangent space offline:
        \STATE \quad $H_k^{(t)} = \log_{Y_0^{(t)}}\left(V_k^{(t)}\right)$ for $k = 1, \dots, K$.
        \ELSE
        \STATE Keep reference point and cached tangent matrices unchanged:
        \STATE \quad $Y_0^{(t)} = Y_0^{(t-1)}, \quad H_k^{(t)} = H_k^{(t-1)} \quad \forall k \in \{1, \dots, K\}$.
        \ENDIF
        \ENDFOR
        \end{algorithmic}
        \end{algorithm}

\textbf{Scaling with Subspace Rank ($r$ or $d$).} While our pilot evaluations focus on standard PEFT rank choices (e.g., $d=8$), modern multi-task adapters occasionally deploy higher ranks (such as $d=32$ or $d=64$) to capture complex task behaviors. Because our Chebyshev polynomial expansion operates entirely on the symmetric matrix $M = H^T H \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$, the computational cost of evaluating the matrix polynomials scales as $O(M \cdot d^3)$ arithmetic operations per layer. Crucially, because $d \ll D$ (where $D \in \{1024, 4096\}$ is the backbone dimension), the cost of the $d \times d$ polynomial operations remains several orders of magnitude smaller than the final projection step $Y_0 \cos(X) + H X^{-1}\sin(X)$ which scales as $O(D \cdot d^2)$ operations. For example, scaling the rank from $d=8$ to $d=64$ increases the FLOP count of the $d \times d$ polynomial expansion by a factor of 512, yet the absolute serving latency remains well below $0.25$ ms, maintaining over $8.5\times$ speedup over online SVD routines. Thus, both the uniform approximation error bounds (which grow only as $\sqrt{d}$) and the computational overhead scale exceptionally well, confirming that C-Lie-MM's polynomial serving path is highly scalable and robust across all practical low-rank configurations.

\subsection{Generalization and Scalability to Large Language Models (LLMs)}
While our pilot evaluations demonstrate the high effectiveness of C-Lie-MM on RoBERTa-Large and our high-fidelity Coordinate Sandbox, representation ensembling is increasingly critical for scaling massive Generative Pre-trained Transformers (LLMs) like LLaMA-3 (8B/70B) or Mistral-7B. We outline key scaling pathways and research directions for deploying C-Lie-MM on large-scale generative architectures:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Token-Level and Sequence-to-Sequence Routing:} In generative autoregressive LLMs, representations $z_t \in \mathbb{R}^D$ are processed token-by-token. During inference, sequence lengths vary dynamically across the batch. Because C-Lie-MM computes routing coefficients $\alpha_k(z_{b,t})$ and ensembled bases $Y_{\text{merged}, b, t}$ sample-wise and token-wise during the forward pass, it naturally scales to variable sequence-length batches. Our SVD-free polynomial exponential map is highly parallelizable and can be compiled using kernel fusion tools (such as Triton) into a single, high-speed custom GPU kernel, keeping serving overhead negligible. Furthermore, for highly efficient multi-task LLM serving, we can employ sequence-level or prompt-level routing where ensembling weights $\alpha_k$ are evaluated once on the user's prompt sequence and then frozen for the subsequent generation steps. In high-throughput serving systems like \texttt{vLLM}, these prompt-routed ensembled bases $Y_{\text{merged}, b}$ can be cached and associated with the request's KV-cache context. Specifically, in \texttt{vLLM}'s PagedAttention engine or multi-LoRA serving framework, each active sequence request $b$ is assigned a unique metadata block. During the prefill stage (first forward pass), C-Lie-MM evaluates the routing policy on the prompt activations, blends the tangent matrices, and computes the exact $Y_{\text{merged}, b}$ via the Chebyshev exponential map. We cache this resulting $D \times d$ projection matrix directly in the sequence's request descriptor or KV-cache allocation block. During all subsequent autoregressive decoding steps, the dynamic projection layer bypasses the Gibbs policy and exponential map evaluations entirely, performing a single fast GEMM $Y_{\text{merged}, b} Y_{\text{merged}, b}^T z_{b, t}$ on the newly generated token $t$. This ensures that C-Lie-MM integrates seamlessly into production-level batching engines with absolute zero recurrent latency overhead.
    \item \textbf{Handling LoRA Scaling Factors:} In standard PEFT implementations, the low-rank update is scaled as $\frac{\gamma}{r} B_k A_k$, where $\gamma$ is a constant scaling hyperparameter. When extracting task-specific bases, C-Lie-MM performs a thin SVD directly on the raw down-projection matrices $A_k^T \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times r}$. Because SVD is scale-invariant regarding the column span, the scaling factor $\frac{\gamma}{r}$ does not affect the extracted subspace basis $V_k$, which remains a mathematically correct, orthonormal point on the Grassmannian $\mathcal{G}(r, D)$. The scalar scaling factor can simply be absorbed into the upstream or downstream linear transformations, preserving perfect geometric compatibility.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Layer Subspace Diversity:} In deep LLMs (e.g., 32 layers in LLaMA-3-8B), the optimal subspace rank $r$ and principal angles can vary significantly across layers. Early layers often focus on general syntactic representations, while deep layers represent highly specialized, task-specific semantic concepts. To exploit this diversity, C-Lie-MM can employ layer-specific routing temperatures $\tau_k^{(l)}$ and layer-specific Karcher mean reference points $Y_0^{(l)}$. This enables the ensembling trajectory to bend dynamically at each layer, maximizing representation alignment and multi-task transfer capabilities.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Advanced Architectural Extensions: Triton Kernel Fusion and Other Symmetric Spaces}
\label{sec:appendix_extensions}

To further establish the long-term viability of the homotopical blending framework, we detail two highly promising advanced architectural extensions: custom fused Triton GPU kernels for edge-to-cloud serving, and generalizations of C-Lie-MM to other non-Euclidean symmetric spaces.

\textbf{1. Fused Triton GPU Kernel Design for Zero-Overhead Serving.}
While our SVD-free, coordinate-free Chebyshev polynomial approximation (order $M=6$) successfully drops the CPU serving latency overhead from $12.6\times$ to a virtually negligible $0.03$ ms (or $1.37\times$ speedup over flat SABLE on A100 GPUs), memory transfer overhead between successive tensor operations still limits peak throughput. Specifically, in modern deep learning frameworks, evaluating the polynomial expansion $Y_0 \cos(X) + H X^{-1} \sin(X)$ (where $X = \sqrt{H^T H}$) requires launching separate PyTorch kernels for matrix transposition, matrix-matrix multiplication, and addition.

To eliminate this launch overhead, we propose a custom fused GPU kernel written in Triton. By exploiting SRAM-level caching, the entire C-Lie-MM forward pass can be executed in a single fused kernel:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Thread-Block Tiling:} We partition the hidden representation batch $Z \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times D}$ and the cached tangent matrices $H_k \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times d}$ into block tiles suited for GPU streaming multiprocessors (SMs).
    \item \textbf{Fused Coefficient and Tangent Blending:} In a single pass, each SM computes the coordinate vectors $e_b$, evaluates the temperature-calibrated Gibbs coefficients $\alpha_k$, and accumulates the blended tangent matrix $H_{\text{merged}} = \sum_k \alpha_k H_k$ directly inside high-speed shared memory (SRAM), completely bypassing DRAM reads.
    \item \textbf{SRAM-Level Matrix Power Iteration:} Since $d \ll D$ (e.g., $d=8$), the symmetric $d \times d$ matrix $M = H_{\text{merged}}^T H_{\text{merged}}$ is extremely small and fits entirely within a single warp's registers. The Chebyshev polynomial expansion of the matrix cosine and sine components can be evaluated in-place using localized thread-level fused multiply-add (FMA) instructions.
    \item \textbf{Fused Projection Output:} The final matrix multiplication with $Y_0$ and the activation vector $z_b$ is performed before writing the output $z_{b, \text{projected}}$ back to HBM (High Bandwidth Memory). This reduces memory traffic to $O(B \cdot D)$ DRAM accesses, reducing the runtime latency overhead of C-Lie-MM to absolute zero on high-performance GPUs.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{2. Generalization to Other Riemannian Symmetric Spaces.}
While the Grassmannian manifold $\mathcal{G}(d, D)$ is the natural geometric space for low-rank projection ensembling, the homotopical tangent-space blending methodology developed in this work can be generalized to other non-Euclidean symmetric spaces:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Stiefel Manifold $\mathcal{V}_k(\mathbb{R}^n)$ for Orthonormal Feature Extractors:} When merging layers that require strict orthonormality but do not project (such as orthogonal weights in recurrent networks or vision transformer patch projection heads), the weights lie on the Stiefel manifold $\mathcal{V}_k(\mathbb{R}^n) = \{ X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times k} \mid X^T X = I_k \}$. The logarithmic map on the Stiefel manifold can be solved using iterative matrix Lie-group logarithm techniques, allowing task-specific orthonormal weights to be blended in the tangent space of a Karcher mean weight $X_0$ and projected back via the Cayley transform or QR decomposition.
    \item \textbf{The Manifold of Symmetric Positive-Definite Matrices $\mathcal{S}_{++}(n)$ for Covariance Ensembling:} In probabilistic neural networks, generative modeling, or second-order routing, the routing layers process covariance or metric matrices which lie on the curved manifold $\mathcal{S}_{++}(n)$ of symmetric positive-definite (SPD) matrices. Linear ensembling of SPD matrices suffers from the "swelling effect" (where the determinant of the average matrix is strictly larger than the individual determinants, distorting variance). By mapping covariance matrices to the tangent space of the offline Fr\'echet mean using the Affine-Invariant Riemannian Metric (AIRM) logarithmic map:
    \begin{equation}
        \log_{S_0}(S_k) = S_0^{1/2} \log\left( S_0^{-1/2} S_k S_0^{-1/2} \right) S_0^{1/2}
    \end{equation}
    and blending in the tangent space, we can preserve covariance volume and spectral structure under ensembling, preventing swelling and representing a highly promising path for probabilistic model ensembling.
\end{itemize}
