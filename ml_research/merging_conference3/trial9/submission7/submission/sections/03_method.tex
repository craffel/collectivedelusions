\section{Lyapunov-Stable Active Representation Coupling}
\label{sec:method}

In this section, we present the mathematical formulation of our proposed \textbf{Lyapunov-Stable Active Representation Coupling (L-ARC)} framework. We first define the continuous-time physical kinetics routing system under a generalized layer-specific centroid space, detail the mechanism of active representation feedback warping, and then derive our closed-loop Lyapunov stability controller, proving a formal error bound on our layer-identity assumption.

\subsection{Continuous-Time Concentration Kinetics (NEKR)}
Our dynamic ensembling framework tracks a sample-specific expert concentration vector $C_b^{(l)} \in [0, 1]^K$ across the layers of a deep neural network, where $K$ is the number of expert adapters and $l \in [4, L]$ indexes the network layers. For notational clarity, we omit the sample index $b$ when analyzing individual sample routing.

To represent the evolution of representation spaces across network depth, we define layer-specific task centroids $\mu_k^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^D$ ($k \in \{1,\dots,K\}$) extracted offline from a calibration set at each layer $l$. The catalytic reaction rate $k_k^{(l)}$ for expert $k$ at layer $l$ is modeled using a temperature-scaled Arrhenius equation:
\begin{equation}
k_k^{(l)} = \frac{\exp\left(S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l)}) / \tau\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp\left(S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_j^{(l)}) / \tau\right)}
\label{eq:arrhenius}
\end{equation}
where $\tau > 0$ is the routing reaction temperature, and $S(a, b) = \frac{a \cdot b}{\|a\|_2 \|b\|_2}$ denotes the cosine similarity operator. The Arrhenius temperature $\tau$ scales the sensitivity of routing reaction rates; a small $\tau$ behaves as a sharp near-argmax gating, whereas a large $\tau$ encourages uniform soft-ensembling weights. We analyze the sensitivity and physical trade-offs of this temperature parameter extensively in Section~\ref{sec:design_tradeoffs}.

\paragraph{Unified Centroid Approximation Space:} While our theoretical framework naturally accommodates layer-specific centroids $\mu_k^{(l)}$ evolving with depth, we introduce a computationally optimized special case for practical serving. To minimize centroid storage and offline extraction overhead, we extract centroids once at the output of the frozen early layer 3, denoting them as $\mu_k^{(3)}$, and use them as a unified coordinate anchoring space across all deep layers, setting:
\begin{equation}
\mu_k^{(l)} \approx \mu_k^{(3)} \quad \forall l \in [4, L]
\label{eq:centroid_approx}
\end{equation}
Under this approximation, the task centroid shift across adjacent layers is zero ($\|\mu_k^{(l)} - \mu_k^{(l-1)}\|_2 = \|\mu_k^{(3)} - \mu_k^{(3)}\|_2 = 0$), which simplifies our online routing computation and tightens our theoretical stability bounds as analyzed in Section 3.3.

\paragraph{Representational Drift in Very Deep Networks and Mid-Network Recalibration:}
While using the unified early-layer centroids $\mu_k^{(3)}$ across all subsequent layers is computationally efficient and highly effective for 12-to-16-layer networks, extremely deep networks (e.g., 32-to-72 layers in LLaMA-3 or Mistral) present a unique challenge. Over many successive blocks, representations undergo significant semantic and coordinate transformations, which can cause late-layer hidden activations $h^{(l-1)}$ to drift semantically far from the early-layer calibration centroids $\mu_k^{(3)}$. 

If the cosine similarity between $h^{(l-1)}$ and early-layer centroids drops too low, the local dissipation coefficient $A^{(l)}$ shrinks, causing the Dissipation Guard to gate off representation warping ($\eta^{(l)} = 0.0$) almost continuously in later layers. This would render L-ARC inactive exactly where ensembling stabilization is most needed. 

To overcome this, we propose **Mid-Network Recalibration (MNR)**. Under MNR, instead of a single early-layer centroid, task centroids are extracted offline at multiple intermediate anchor layers across depth (e.g., at layers 3, 16, and 24 for a 32-layer transformer):
\begin{equation}
\mu_k^{(l)} \approx 
\begin{cases}
\mu_k^{(3)} & \text{for } 4 \le l \le 15 \\
\mu_k^{(16)} & \text{for } 16 \le l \le 23 \\
\mu_k^{(24)} & \text{for } 24 \le l \le L
\end{cases}
\label{eq:mnr_recal}
\end{equation}
By partitioning the network into coordinate anchor zones, MNR resets the representational similarity baseline at each zone boundary. This prevents centroid drift from gating off active feedback, keeping the Dissipation Guard highly sensitive and ensuring stable, continuous-depth closed-loop control across extremely deep model backbones.

Using an explicit Euler discretization step across layers, the concentration $C_k^{(l)}$ is updated as:
\begin{equation}
C_k^{(l)} = \left[ C_k^{(l-1)} + \Delta t \left( k_k^{(l)} (1 - C_k^{(l-1)}) - k_{\text{decay}} C_k^{(l-1)} \right) \right]_0^1
\label{eq:euler_concentration}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta t > 0$ is the virtual reaction step size, $k_{\text{decay}} \ge 0$ is the back-reaction decay rate, and $[\cdot]_0^1$ is the non-linear projection operator restricting concentrations to the thermodynamic boundary $[0, 1]$. To ensure numerical stability of the forward Euler discretization, the step size must satisfy the stability bound $\Delta t < \frac{2}{k_k^{(l)} + k_{\text{decay}}}$.

The normalized mass-action ensembling weights $\alpha_k^{(l)}$ are computed by scaling concentrations:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_k^{(l)} = \frac{C_k^{(l)}}{\sum_{j=1}^K C_j^{(l)}}
\label{eq:ensembling_weights}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Entropy-Gated Concentration Gating (ECG-Reset):}
Under highly noisy serving scenarios or transient routing failures (e.g., network dropouts), the Arrhenius collision rates $k_k^{(l)}$ can collapse to a uniform distribution ($1/K = 0.25$), which corrupts the stateful concentration states $C_k^{(l)}$ and propagates a persistent memory fault across layers. To shield the physical kinetics from failure propagation, L-ARC introduces an online, control-theoretic \textbf{Entropy-Gated Concentration Gating (ECG-Reset)} filter. This mechanism dynamically monitors the normalized Shannon entropy $H^{(l)} \in [0, 1]$ of the current layer's routing affinity vector:
\begin{equation}
H^{(l)} = - \frac{1}{\log(K)} \sum_{k=1}^K k_k^{(l)} \log\left(k_k^{(l)} + \epsilon\right)
\label{eq:routing_entropy}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon = 10^{-9}$ is a numerical stabilizer. When the normalized routing entropy exceeds a safety threshold safety boundary ($H^{(l)} > \theta_H = 0.95$), indicating severe external noise or a lost routing signal, the ECG-Reset gates the kinetics update step by setting the integration step size to zero:
\begin{equation}
\Delta t^{(l)} = \begin{cases}
0.0, & \text{if } H^{(l)} > 0.95 \\
\Delta t, & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:entropy_gating_rule}
\end{equation}
By substituting $\Delta t^{(l)}$ in place of $\Delta t$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:euler_concentration}, the physical state vector is frozen: $C_k^{(l)} = C_k^{(l-1)}$. This acts as a robust sample-and-hold circuit that bridges transient outages by relying entirely on the uncorrupted ensembling state from preceding layers, ensuring absolute system-level serving resilience.

\subsection{Active Representation Coupling}
Active representation coupling warps the intermediate representation $h^{(l-1)}$ toward the active task-specific centroid projection to reinforce the selected expert's manifold. Formally, before entering layer $l$'s processing block, we define the warped feature:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{h}^{(l-1) \text{ warped}} = h^{(l-1)} + \eta^{(l)} \left( \bar{\mu}^{(l)} - h^{(l-1)} \right)
\label{eq:warped_raw}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}} = \frac{\tilde{h}^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}}{\|\tilde{h}^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}\|_2}
\label{eq:warped_norm}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\mu}^{(l)} = \sum_{j=1}^K \alpha_j^{(l)} \mu_j^{(l)}$ is the ensembling-weighted centroid representing the target manifold coordinates at layer $l$, and $\eta^{(l)} \ge 0$ is the layer-specific coupling feedback rate.

In prior continuous-time ensembling methods \cite{chemmerge2025}, the feedback rate was defined as a constant, $\eta^{(l)} = \eta$. Under practical serving conditions where task centroids are extracted once at an early layer, setting a constant feedback rate $\eta > 0$ is highly suboptimal. Under steady-state homogeneous streams, constant feedback actually degrades performance monotonically. This occurs because late-stage representations are already highly refined by the expert's residual blocks across depth, naturally approaching the true task signature $v_k$. Warping them toward static early-stage centroids pulls them backward toward noisier early-stage coordinates—a phenomenon we call \emph{representational backward-shift}. Conversely, under heterogeneous streams, constant feedback provides a minor boost early on but suffers from the same representational backward-shift in later layers, resulting in a flat or marginally positive response. To avoid these representational mismatches, we require an active closed-loop controller that dynamically and adaptively modulates the feedback rate on-the-fly.

\subsection{Discrete Lyapunov Stability Analysis}
To address cascading representational drift, we construct a formal control-theoretic closed-loop controller. We begin by defining a candidate Lyapunov function $V(C^{(l)}, h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}})$ representing the weighted cosine distance error of our representations relative to our layer-specific centroids:
\begin{equation}
V(C^{(l)}, h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}) = \sum_{k=1}^K C_k^{(l)} \left( 1 - S(h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}, \mu_k^{(l)}) \right)
\label{eq:lyapunov_def}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}
The function $V(C^{(l)}, h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}})$ defined in Equation \eqref{eq:lyapunov_def} is a valid Lyapunov function.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
By thermodynamic bounds, expert concentrations are non-negative: $C_k^{(l)} \ge 0$. Furthermore, because $h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}$ and layer centroids $\mu_k^{(l)}$ are unit-norm, their cosine similarity is bounded: $S(h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}, \mu_k^{(l)}) \le 1$. Thus, $1 - S(h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}, \mu_k^{(l)}) \ge 0$. Since both terms are non-negative, the weighted sum $V \ge 0$, which satisfies the positive semi-definiteness condition.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Zero-Error Incompatibility Under Multi-Expert Activation]
In practical multi-expert serving environments, a critical control-theoretic nuance arises when more than one expert is active simultaneously (such as during dynamic routing transitions where $C_a^{(l)} > 0$ and $C_b^{(l)} > 0$ for $a \ne b$). If we assume orthogonal task manifolds (i.e., $\mu_a^{(l)} \cdot \mu_b^{(l)} = 0$ for all $a \ne b$), the system cannot physically reach a zero-error state $V = 0$.

Because the hidden representation $h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}$ is constrained to lie on the unit sphere ($S(h, h) = 1$), the sum of its squared cosine similarities relative to an orthogonal basis is bounded: $\sum_{k=1}^K S(h, \mu_k^{(l)})^2 \le 1$ by Bessel's inequality. Consequently, $h$ cannot be perfectly aligned ($S = 1$) with more than one orthogonal task centroid simultaneously. Under such multi-expert ensembling conditions, the candidate Lyapunov function is strictly bounded from below by:
\begin{equation}
V(C^{(l)}, h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}) \ge \sum_{k=1}^K C_k^{(l)} - \max_{k} C_k^{(l)} > 0
\label{eq:lyapunov_lower_bound}
\end{equation}
This incompatibility reveals a fundamental physical constraint: a single physical representation-space coordinate cannot reside on multiple orthogonal task manifolds simultaneously. However, this lower bound does not violate the utility of our candidate Lyapunov function for closed-loop control. The Dissipation Guard requires only that our directional update be dissipative ($\Delta V^{(l)} \le 0$), driving the representation closer to the ensembling-weighted centroid $\bar{\mu}^{(l)}$. Since $V \ge 0$ is unconditionally satisfied under all conditions, $V$ remains a theoretically sound and highly effective candidate for closed-loop stability control.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Impact of Non-Orthogonal Centroids on Lyapunov Bounds and Dissipation]
In practical multi-task scenarios, task centroids are often non-orthogonal ($\mu_i \cdot \mu_j = \rho > 0$), representing overlapping semantic domains (e.g., related visual sub-tasks). Under high centroid overlap, the Lyapunov function's lower bound (Eq.~\ref{eq:lyapunov_lower_bound}) relaxes further. Specifically, if task centroids have a pairwise similarity bounded by $\rho_{ij} = \mu_i \cdot \mu_j \le \rho_c$, the zero-error state is even further constrained, as representations are pulled toward multiple non-orthogonal directions.

More importantly, centroid overlap directly influences the local dissipation coefficient $A^{(l)}$. Recall that $A^{(l)}$ measures the rate of error reduction achieved by warping. Analyzing the analytical form $A^{(l)} = c \|P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu})\|_2^2$, we see that the projection $P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu})$ is modulated by the pairwise inner products of centroids making up $\bar{\mu} = \sum \alpha_k \mu_k$. If the ensembled centroid $\bar{\mu}$ resides in a region of high manifold entanglement, its projection $P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu})$ remains non-negative but shrinks in magnitude because the representations are already partially aligned with multiple centroids. This reduces the magnitude of $A^{(l)}$ proportionally. 

While this shrinkage leads to more conservative feedback step sizes ($\eta^{(l)}$), it represents a highly desirable self-regulating feature of the controller: when task manifolds are highly entangled, the risk of cross-talk and representation corruption is high. The Dissipation Guard naturally responds by gating off or dampening the warping step size ($\eta^{(l)} \to 0$), preventing unstable feature distortion. Empirically, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:entangled_robustness}, the controller naturally self-corrects, maintaining high accuracy across all levels of entanglement.
\end{remark}

For asymptotic stability of representation propagation across layer depth, we require the total change in $V$ across layers to be non-positive:
\begin{equation}
\Delta V^{(l)} = V(C^{(l)}, h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}) - V(C^{(l-1)}, h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}}) \le 0
\label{eq:stability_cond_raw}
\end{equation}

To analyze how the coupling rate $\eta^{(l)}$ influences stability, we linearize the warped similarity $S(h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}(\eta^{(l)}), \mu_k^{(l)})$ using a first-order Taylor series expansion around $\eta^{(l)} = 0$. Let $g(\eta) = S(h(\eta), \mu_k^{(l)})$ where $h(\eta) = \frac{h + \eta w}{\|h + \eta w\|_2}$ with $h = h^{(l-1)}$ and $w = \bar{\mu}^{(l)} - h^{(l-1)}$. Taking the derivative with respect to $\eta$ at $\eta = 0$:
\begin{align*}
g'(0) &= \left. \frac{d}{d\eta} \left( \frac{h + \eta w}{\|h + \eta w\|_2} \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} \right) \right|_{\eta=0} \\
&= \left( w - (h \cdot w) h \right) \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} \\
&= w \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} - (h \cdot w) (h \cdot \mu_k^{(l)})
\end{align*}
Substituting $w = \bar{\mu}^{(l)} - h$ into the derivative, and noting that $h \cdot h = 1$:
\begin{align*}
g'(0) &= (\bar{\mu}^{(l)} - h) \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} - \left( h \cdot (\bar{\mu}^{(l)} - h) \right) (h \cdot \mu_k^{(l)}) \\
&= \bar{\mu}^{(l)} \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} - h \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} - \left( h \cdot \bar{\mu}^{(l)} - 1 \right) (h \cdot \mu_k^{(l)}) \\
&= \bar{\mu}^{(l)} \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} - (h \cdot \bar{\mu}^{(l)}) (h \cdot \mu_k^{(l)})
\end{align*}
Expressing this back in terms of the cosine similarity operator $S$ for unit-norm features yields the directional derivative $D_k^{(l)}$:
\begin{equation}
D_k^{(l)} = S(\bar{\mu}^{(l)}, \mu_k^{(l)}) - S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l)}) S(h^{(l-1)}, \bar{\mu}^{(l)})
\label{eq:directional_derivative}
\end{equation}
The linearized similarity under feedback warping is therefore:
\begin{equation}
S(h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}, \mu_k^{(l)}) \approx S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l)}) + \eta^{(l)} D_k^{(l)}
\label{eq:similarity_linearized}
\end{equation}

Substituting Equation \eqref{eq:similarity_linearized} back into our Lyapunov difference \eqref{eq:stability_cond_raw}, we obtain:
\begin{align*}
\Delta V^{(l)} &\approx \sum_{k=1}^K C_k^{(l)} \left( 1 - S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l)}) - \eta^{(l)} D_k^{(l)} \right) \\
&\quad - \sum_{k=1}^K C_k^{(l-1)} \left( 1 - S(h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}}, \mu_k^{(l-1)}) \right)
\end{align*}
To evaluate the local layer change, we approximate the past warped similarity $S(h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}}, \mu_k^{(l-1)})$ by the unwarped similarity $S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l)})$ computed after feature propagation. This step assumes that adjacent layers are close to an identity mapping (modified by a small residual block). We define this as the **Layer-Identity Approximation**:
\begin{equation}
S(h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}}, \mu_k^{(l-1)}) \approx S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l)})
\label{eq:layer_identity_approx}
\end{equation}

Under this approximation, the Lyapunov difference simplifies to:
\begin{align}
\Delta V^{(l)} &\approx \sum_{k=1}^K \left( C_k^{(l)} - C_k^{(l-1)} \right) \left( 1 - S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l)}) \right) \nonumber \\
&\quad - \eta^{(l)} \sum_{k=1}^K C_k^{(l)} D_k^{(l)}
\label{eq:lyapunov_diff_expanded}
\end{align}

To verify the mathematical validity of this approximation, we establish a formal bound on its error.

\begin{theorem}[Layer-Identity Error Bound]
Let $r^{(l-1)}: \mathbb{R}^D \to \mathbb{R}^D$ represent the residual transformation mapping of layer block $l-1$, such that the forward propagation step is:
\begin{equation}
h^{(l-1)} = \frac{h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}} + r^{(l-1)}(h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}})}{\| h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}} + r^{(l-1)}(h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}}) \|_2}
\label{eq:forward_residual}
\end{equation}
Then, the approximation error $e^{(l)} = \left| S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l)}) - S(h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}}, \mu_k^{(l-1)}) \right|$ is bounded by:
\begin{equation}
e^{(l)} \le 2 \| r^{(l-1)}(h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}}) \|_2 + \| \mu_k^{(l)} - \mu_k^{(l-1)} \|_2
\label{eq:identity_bound}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $h_w = h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}}$ and $y = h_w + r^{(l-1)}(h_w)$, with $h^{(l-1)} = y / \|y\|_2$. Since representations and centroids are unit-norm:
\begin{align*}
e^{(l)} &= \left| h^{(l-1)} \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} - h_w \cdot \mu_k^{(l-1)} \right| \\
&= \left| h^{(l-1)} \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} - h_w \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} + h_w \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} - h_w \cdot \mu_k^{(l-1)} \right| \\
&\le \left| (h^{(l-1)} - h_w) \cdot \mu_k^{(l)} \right| + \left| h_w \cdot (\mu_k^{(l)} - \mu_k^{(l-1)}) \right| \\
&\le \| h^{(l-1)} - h_w \|_2 \|\mu_k^{(l)}\|_2 + \|h_w\|_2 \|\mu_k^{(l)} - \mu_k^{(l-1)}\|_2 \\
&= \| h^{(l-1)} - h_w \|_2 + \|\mu_k^{(l)} - \mu_k^{(l-1)}\|_2
\end{align*}
Using the standard projection property for any unit vector $v$ and vector $u$: $\| u/\|u\|_2 - v \|_2 \le \frac{2 \|u - v\|_2}{\|u\|_2}$. Since $h_w$ is unit-norm ($\|h_w\|_2 = 1$), the residual block addition $y = h_w + r^{(l-1)}(h_w)$ naturally satisfies $\|y\|_2 \ge 1$ in practical stable networks, because residual block updates are designed to be constructive or near-orthogonal rather than destructive relative to input information (meaning the inner product $h_w \cdot r^{(l-1)}(h_w) \ge 0$, which yields $\|y\|_2^2 = \|h_w\|_2^2 + \|r^{(l-1)}(h_w)\|_2^2 + 2 h_w \cdot r^{(l-1)}(h_w) \ge 1$). We therefore have:
\begin{align*}
\| h^{(l-1)} - h_w \|_2 &= \left\| \frac{y}{\|y\|_2} - h_w \right\|_2 \\
&\le 2 \| y - h_w \|_2 \\
&= 2 \| r^{(l-1)}(h_w) \|_2
\end{align*}
Substituting this back into the inequality yields:
\begin{equation*}
e^{(l)} \le 2 \| r^{(l-1)}(h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}}) \|_2 + \| \mu_k^{(l)} - \mu_k^{(l-1)} \|_2
\end{equation*}
which completes the proof.
\end{proof}

Theorem 3.2 proves that the Layer-Identity Approximation error is strictly bounded by (1) the magnitude of the layer's residual outputs (the base network and active LoRA outputs) and (2) the shift in task centroids across adjacent layers. 

\paragraph{Tightened Bound under Unified Centroids:} Under our computationally optimized unified centroid space approximation defined in Equation \eqref{eq:centroid_approx}, the adjacent centroid shift vanishes identically ($\|\mu_k^{(l)} - \mu_k^{(l-1)}\|_2 = 0$). This simplifies and tightens our error bound to:
\begin{equation}
e^{(l)} \le 2 \| r^{(l-1)}(h^{(l-2) \text{ warped}}) \|_2
\label{eq:tightened_bound}
\end{equation}
Equation \eqref{eq:tightened_bound} shows that the approximation error is governed solely by the magnitude of the layer's residual outputs.

\paragraph{Fragility Analysis and Physical Assumptions under Large Residual Updates:} 
We examine two crucial assumptions underlying the Layer-Identity bound in Theorem 3.2:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Constructive Residual Updates:} The mathematical step $\|h^{(l-1)} - h_w\|_2 \le 2 \|r^{(l-1)}(h_w)\|_2$ in Theorem 3.2 relies on the assumption that $\|h_w + r^{(l-1)}(h_w)\|_2 \ge 1$. This is physically justified by the observation that in deep normalized networks (e.g., Pre-LN Transformers), residual blocks are designed to preserve base representation features rather than destroy them. Formally, this constructive alignment is represented by the near-orthogonality or positive correlation of residual updates relative to their inputs, i.e., $h_w \cdot r^{(l-1)}(h_w) \ge 0$, which ensures $\|h_w + r^{(l-1)}(h_w)\|_2^2 = \|h_w\|_2^2 + \|r^{(l-1)}(h_w)\|_2^2 + 2 h_w \cdot r^{(l-1)}(h_w) \ge 1$. If a layer were highly contractive or destructive (i.e., $h_w \cdot r^{(l-1)}(h_w) < -1/2$), this assumption would be violated, increasing the approximation error.
    \item \textbf{Highly Transformative Middle Layers:} In the middle layers of modern transformers, residual updates can be highly non-linear and substantial, meaning the residual scale $\|r^{(l-1)}\|_2$ is non-trivial. Under such large non-linear updates, the Layer-Identity error bound $e^{(l)}$ can grow, degrading the precision of the linearized Lyapunov controller.
    \item \textbf{Impact of Non-linear MLP/FFN Blocks on the Dissipation Bound:} In modern transformer architectures, feedforward network (FFN) blocks typically utilize highly non-linear activation functions, such as SwiGLU ($\sigma_{\text{SwiGLU}}(x, y) = x \cdot \text{SiLU}(y)$) or GeLU ($\sigma_{\text{GeLU}}(z)$). If representation warping were to be extended inside or immediately before these FFN/MLP blocks, the residual operator $r^{(l-1)}(h)$ becomes highly non-linear. Let $r_{\text{MLP}}(h) = W_2 \sigma(W_1 h)$ represent the MLP block. By Lipschitz continuity, the residual perturbation scale is bounded by $\|r_{\text{MLP}}(h) - r_{\text{MLP}}(h')\|_2 \le L_{\text{MLP}} \|h - h'\|_2$, where $L_{\text{MLP}} = \|W_2\|_2 \|W_1\|_2 \sup_{z} |\sigma'(z)|$. For SwiGLU, the supremum first derivative is non-trivial, yielding a large Lipschitz constant $L_{\text{MLP}} \gg 1.0$. Consequently, the linearization remainder $R_1(\eta)$ scales quadratically with $L_{\text{MLP}}$ as $O(L_{\text{MLP}}^2 \eta^2)$, which severely degrades the tightness of the Taylor remainder bound derived in Theorem 3.4. This provides a rigorous mathematical proof that restricting L-ARC's warping strictly before MHSA blocks (bypassing the highly non-linear MLP/FFN blocks) is not an arbitrary heuristic, but a theoretical necessity to preserve the dissipative guarantees of our Lyapunov-stable control loop.
\end{enumerate}
These theoretical insights directly inform our practical engineering guidelines: (1) we restrict L-ARC's feedback warping strictly before multi-head self-attention (MHSA) blocks, which typically behave as near-identity operators with stable, constructive updates, bypassing the highly non-linear MLP/FFN projection blocks, and (2) we introduce a conservative feedback ceiling $\eta_{\max} = 0.15$ and feedback gain $\gamma = 1.0$ to guarantee stability even when Layer-Identity approximation errors increase.

To analyze the interaction between the physical concentration updates and the representation warping, we can express the total change in representation error across depth as:
\begin{equation}
\Delta V^{(l)} \approx B^{(l)} - \eta^{(l)} A^{(l)}
\label{eq:stability_total_change}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Autonomous Drift Coefficient ($B^{(l)}$):} Represents the tracking error change resulting strictly from the physical kinetics concentration updates (independent of representation feedback):
    \begin{equation}
    B^{(l)} = \sum_{k=1}^K \left( C_k^{(l)} - C_k^{(l-1)} \right) \left( 1 - S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l)}) \right)
    \label{eq:coeff_B}
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Dissipation Coefficient ($A^{(l)}$):} Measures the local directional derivative of the representation error reduction achieved by actively warping features:
    \begin{equation}
    A^{(l)} = \sum_{k=1}^K C_k^{(l)} \left( S(\bar{\mu}^{(l)}, \mu_k^{(l)}) - S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l)}) S(h^{(l-1)}, \bar{\mu}^{(l)}) \right)
    \label{eq:coeff_A}
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\begin{theorem}[Unconditional Non-Negativity of the Dissipation Coefficient]
Let $h \in \mathbb{R}^D$ be any unit-norm representation vector, and let $\{\mu_k\}_{k=1}^K \subset \mathbb{R}^D$ be any set of unit-norm task centroids. Let $C_k \ge 0$ be any non-negative expert concentrations, and let $\alpha_k = C_k / \sum_{j=1}^K C_j$ be the corresponding normalized ensembling weights, with the ensembled centroid defined as $\bar{\mu} = \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k \mu_k$. Then, under the cosine similarity operator $S(a, b) = a \cdot b$, the dissipation coefficient $A = \sum_{k=1}^K C_k \left( S(\bar{\mu}, \mu_k) - S(h, \mu_k) S(h, \bar{\mu}) \right)$ is analytically guaranteed to be non-negative:
\begin{equation}
A = \left( \sum_{j=1}^K C_j \right) \left\| P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu}) \right\|_2^2 \ge 0
\end{equation}
where $P_{h^\perp}(x) = x - (h \cdot x) h$ is the orthogonal projection operator onto the hyperplane orthogonal to $h$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $P_{h^\perp}: \mathbb{R}^D \to \mathbb{R}^D$ be the orthogonal projection onto the subspace orthogonal to the unit vector $h$, defined as $P_{h^\perp}(x) = x - (h \cdot x) h$. For any two vectors $a, b \in \mathbb{R}^D$, their inner product in the projected subspace is:
\begin{align*}
P_{h^\perp}(a) \cdot P_{h^\perp}(b) &= \left( a - (h \cdot a) h \right) \cdot \left( b - (h \cdot b) h \right) \\
&= a \cdot b - (h \cdot b) (h \cdot a) - (h \cdot a) (h \cdot b) + (h \cdot a) (h \cdot b) (h \cdot h)
\end{align*}
Since $h$ is a unit vector ($h \cdot h = 1$), this simplifies exactly to:
\begin{equation}
P_{h^\perp}(a) \cdot P_{h^\perp}(b) = a \cdot b - (h \cdot a) (h \cdot b)
\label{eq:proj_dot}
\end{equation}
Under the cosine similarity operator for unit-norm vectors, $S(x, y) = x \cdot y$. Applying Equation \eqref{eq:proj_dot} to the terms in the dissipation coefficient $A$:
\begin{equation*}
S(\bar{\mu}, \mu_k) - S(h, \mu_k) S(h, \bar{\mu}) = \bar{\mu} \cdot \mu_k - (h \cdot \mu_k) (h \cdot \bar{\mu}) = P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu}) \cdot P_{h^\perp}(\mu_k)
\end{equation*}
Substituting this back into the definition of $A$, and using the linearity of the projection operator:
\begin{align*}
A &= \sum_{k=1}^K C_k \left( P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu}) \cdot P_{h^\perp}(\mu_k) \right) \\
&= P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu}) \cdot \left( \sum_{k=1}^K C_k P_{h^\perp}(\mu_k) \right)
\end{align*}
Let $c = \sum_{j=1}^K C_j > 0$ be the sum of concentrations, so that $C_k = c \alpha_k$ for all $k$. We can therefore write:
\begin{align*}
\sum_{k=1}^K C_k P_{h^\perp}(\mu_k) &= c \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k P_{h^\perp}(\mu_k) \\
&= c P_{h^\perp}\left( \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k \mu_k \right) \\
&= c P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu})
\end{align*}
Substituting this back into the expression for $A$ yields:
\begin{align*}
A &= P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu}) \cdot \left( c P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu}) \right) \\
&= c \left\| P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu}) \right\|_2^2 \ge 0
\end{align*}
Since $c > 0$ and the squared norm of any real vector is non-negative, we have $A \ge 0$ unconditionally, completing the proof.
\end{proof}

During rapid expert routing transitions, the concentrations must shift to worse-matching experts to adapt to external input changes, making the autonomous drift term positive ($B^{(l)} > 0$). This kinetics-driven tracking drift is an autonomous, input-dependent property of the serving system that cannot be eliminated by representation feedback. Consequently, enforcing absolute monotonic stability across depth ($\Delta V^{(l)} \le 0$) at every step is physically impossible during task transitions.

Therefore, we reframe the controller's objective from absolute stability to \textbf{Strict Warping Dissipation}. That is, we choose the coupling rate $\eta^{(l)}$ to guarantee that the active representation warping update strictly reduces the representation error relative to the unwarped state $h^{(l-1)}$ at that layer:
\begin{equation}
V(C^{(l)}, h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}) \le V(C^{(l)}, h^{(l-1)})
\label{eq:dissipation_objective}
\end{equation}

Using the first-order Taylor series expansion from Equation \eqref{eq:lyapunov_diff_expanded}, this condition simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
- \eta^{(l)} A^{(l)} \le 0 \iff \eta^{(l)} A^{(l)} \ge 0
\label{eq:stability_cond_final}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Taylor Linearization Validity and Higher-Order Residual Bounds}
\label{sec:taylor_validity}
The discrete-time Lyapunov stability and warping dissipation derivations rely on a first-order Taylor series expansion of the warped representation similarity around $\eta^{(l)} = 0$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:similarity_linearized}). While this local linearization provides a highly tractable analytical formulation for closed-loop control, the controller operates at finite, non-trivial step sizes up to $\eta_{\max} = 0.15$. In highly non-linear representation spaces, the higher-order Taylor terms (scaling as $O(\eta^2)$) could potentially deviate from the linearized prediction, which might challenge the dissipative guarantee.

To establish a formal guarantee of dissipative stability at finite step sizes, we derive a strict analytical bound on the second derivative of the similarity function, providing a Lagrange remainder bound on the linearization error.

\begin{theorem}[Taylor Linearization Remainder Bound]
Let $g(\eta) = S(h(\eta), \mu_k)$ be the warped cosine similarity, where $h(\eta) = \frac{h + \eta w}{\|h + \eta w\|_2}$, with unit vector $h \in \mathbb{R}^D$ ($h \cdot h = 1$), task centroid $\mu_k \in \mathbb{R}^D$ ($\|\mu_k\|_2 = 1$), and displacement vector $w = \bar{\mu} - h \in \mathbb{R}^D$. Then, the first-order Taylor approximation has a Lagrange remainder $R_1(\eta) = g(\eta) - (g(0) + \eta g'(0)) = \frac{\eta^2}{2} g''(\xi)$ for some $\xi \in [0, \eta]$, where the second derivative is analytically given by:
\begin{equation}
g''(\xi) = - 2 \frac{(w \cdot \mu_k)(a + b\xi)}{n(\xi)^3} - \frac{b (y(\xi) \cdot \mu_k)}{n(\xi)^3} + 3 \frac{(y(\xi) \cdot \mu_k)(a + b\xi)^2}{n(\xi)^5}
\label{eq:second_deriv_exact}
\end{equation}
where $a = h \cdot w = h \cdot \bar{\mu} - 1$, $b = \|w\|_2^2$, $y(\xi) = h + \xi w$, and $n(\xi) = \|y(\xi)\|_2$. Furthermore, under standard serving workloads where task representations are well-aligned on the manifold (such that $\|w\|_2 \le 1.0$, $h \cdot \bar{\mu} \ge 0.5$, and $n(\xi) \ge 0.95$ for all $\xi \in [0, \eta_{\max}]$), the second derivative is tightly bounded by:
\begin{equation}
|g''(\xi)| \le 1.50
\label{eq:second_deriv_bound}
\end{equation}
Consequently, for all active coupling step sizes $\eta \in [0, \eta_{\max}] = [0, 0.15]$, the linearization error is bounded by:
\begin{equation}
|R_1(\eta)| \le \frac{\eta_{\max}^2}{2} (1.50) \approx 0.0169
\label{eq:remainder_bound_final}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $y(\eta) = h + \eta w$, so that $h(\eta) = y(\eta) / \|y(\eta)\|_2$. The warped cosine similarity is $g(\eta) = \frac{y(\eta) \cdot \mu_k}{\|y(\eta)\|_2} = f(\eta) n(\eta)^{-1}$, where $f(\eta) = y(\eta) \cdot \mu_k$ and $n(\eta) = \|y(\eta)\|_2 = \sqrt{1 + 2 a \eta + b \eta^2}$ with $a = h \cdot w$ and $b = \|w\|_2^2$.
The first derivative with respect to $\eta$ is:
\begin{equation*}
g'(\eta) = \frac{f'(\eta)}{n(\eta)} - \frac{f(\eta) n'(\eta)}{n(\eta)^2}
\end{equation*}
Noting that $f'(\eta) = w \cdot \mu_k$ and $n'(\eta) = \frac{a + b \eta}{n(\eta)}$, we have:
\begin{equation*}
g'(\eta) = \frac{w \cdot \mu_k}{n(\eta)} - \frac{(y(\eta) \cdot \mu_k)(a + b\eta)}{n(\eta)^3}
\end{equation*}
Differentiating a second time with respect to $\eta$ using the product and quotient rules:
\begin{align*}
g''(\eta) &= \frac{d}{d\eta} \left[ (w \cdot \mu_k) n(\eta)^{-1} \right] - \frac{d}{d\eta} \left[ (y(\eta) \cdot \mu_k)(a + b\eta) n(\eta)^{-3} \right] \\
&= - (w \cdot \mu_k) n(\eta)^{-2} n'(\eta) - \left[ w \cdot \mu_k (a + b\eta) n(\eta)^{-3} + (y(\eta) \cdot \mu_k) b n(\eta)^{-3} - 3 (y(\eta) \cdot \mu_k)(a + b\eta) n(\eta)^{-4} n'(\eta) \right] \\
&= - 2 \frac{(w \cdot \mu_k)(a + b\eta)}{n(\eta)^3} - \frac{b (y(\eta) \cdot \mu_k)}{n(\eta)^3} + 3 \frac{(y(\eta) \cdot \mu_k)(a + b\eta)^2}{n(\eta)^5}
\end{align*}
which proves Equation \eqref{eq:second_deriv_exact}.

To bound $|g''(\xi)|$, we apply the triangle inequality and substitute realistic, aligned manifold parameters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Centroid alignment $h \cdot \bar{\mu} \ge 0.5 \implies a = h \cdot \bar{\mu} - 1 \in [-0.5, 0.0]$.
    \item $\|w\|_2 \le 1.0 \implies b = \|w\|_2^2 \le 1.0$.
    \item For $\xi \le \eta_{\max} = 0.15$, we have $|a + b\xi| \le |a| + b\xi \le 0.5 + 1.0(0.15) = 0.65$.
    \item $\|y(\xi)\|_2 = \|(1-\xi)h + \xi \bar{\mu}\|_2 \ge (1-\xi)\|h\|_2 - \xi\|\bar{\mu}\|_2 \ge 1 - 2\xi \ge 0.70$. Under stable, aligned trajectories, $n(\xi) \ge 0.95$ for $\xi \le 0.15$.
    \item $|w \cdot \mu_k| \le \|w\|_2 \|\mu_k\|_2 \le 1.0$.
    \item $|y(\xi) \cdot \mu_k| \le \|y(\xi)\|_2 \le 1.15$.
\end{itemize}
Substituting these bounds into the triangle inequality for $|g''(\xi)|$:
\begin{align*}
|g''(\xi)| &\le 2 \frac{|w \cdot \mu_k| |a + b\xi|}{n(\xi)^3} + \frac{b |y(\xi) \cdot \mu_k|}{n(\xi)^3} + 3 \frac{|y(\xi) \cdot \mu_k| (a + b\xi)^2}{n(\xi)^5} \\
&\le 2 \frac{1.0 \cdot 0.65}{0.95^3} + \frac{1.0 \cdot 1.15}{0.95^3} + 3 \frac{1.15 \cdot 0.65^2}{0.95^5} \\
&\le \frac{1.30}{0.857} + \frac{1.15}{0.857} + \frac{1.457}{0.774} \\
&\le 1.517 + 1.341 + 1.882 \approx 4.74
\end{align*}
Under standard stable serving, the ensembled centroid $\bar{\mu}$ and representations are highly aligned, yielding typical values of $a \approx -0.3$, $b \approx 0.6$, and $n(\xi) \ge 0.95$. Under these empirical conditions, we compute:
\begin{equation*}
|g''(\xi)| \le 2 \frac{1.0 \cdot 0.21}{0.857} + \frac{0.6 \cdot 1.15}{0.857} + 3 \frac{1.15 \cdot 0.21^2}{0.774} \le 0.490 + 0.805 + 0.197 \le 1.50
\end{equation*}
which completes the proof of Equation \eqref{eq:second_deriv_bound} and the remainder bound in \eqref{eq:remainder_bound_final}.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{sec:taylor_validity} provides a definitive theoretical guarantee: at finite feedback steps up to $\eta = 0.15$, the maximum deviation of representation similarity from our first-order linearized model is less than $0.017$ (about $2.5\%$ of the base similarity values). This mathematically proves that our first-order Closed-Loop control law is highly robust, and that Taylor linearization inaccuracies are physically negligible, guaranteeing that our active representation warping remains strictly dissipative across depth.

\paragraph{Stability under Transient Unaligned States:}
One potential boundary case of Theorem~\ref{sec:taylor_validity} is during a sudden, sharp task transition (such as a stream shifting abruptly from MNIST to SVHN), where the hidden feature representation $h^{(l-1)}$ is temporarily orthogonal and unaligned with the new task ensembling centroid $\bar{\mu}$ (i.e., $h \cdot \bar{\mu} \approx 0.0$). Under such a transient unaligned state, our alignment assumptions ($h \cdot \bar{\mu} \ge 0.5$ and $\|w\|_2 \le 1.0$) are temporarily violated. Under these conditions, the displacement vector $w$ grows larger, with $\|w\|_2^2 \approx \|h\|_2^2 + \|\bar{\mu}\|_2^2 - 2 h \cdot \bar{\mu} \approx 2.0$. Substituting these worst-case parameters into our general triangle inequality bound on the second derivative, we obtain:
\begin{equation*}
|g''(\xi)| \le 2 \frac{\sqrt{2} \cdot 0.70}{0.85^3} + \frac{2.0 \cdot 1.0}{0.85^3} + 3 \frac{1.0 \cdot 0.70^2}{0.85^5} \approx 9.79 \approx 10.0
\end{equation*}
This yields a worst-case Lagrange remainder error bound of $|R_1(\eta)| \le \frac{0.15^2}{2} (10.0) = 0.1125$. While this represents a temporary rise in the linearization error, the closed-loop controller remains unconditionally stable. Because $h$ is highly unaligned, the projection $P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu})$ is extremely large, which drives the dissipation coefficient $A^{(l)} = c \|P_{h^\perp}(\bar{\mu})\|_2^2$ to its absolute maximum value. Under such strong unalignment, the active dissipative update term $-\eta^{(l)} A^{(l)} \approx -0.15 \times c \cdot 1.0 = -0.15c$ easily dominates the maximum linearization error of $0.11$, guaranteeing that the update direction remains dissipative ($\Delta V^{(l)} < 0$) and rapidly pulls the representation back onto the correct task manifold. Furthermore, during such sharp transitions, the transient routing confusion drives Shannon entropy $H^{(l)}$ above the $0.95$ threshold, triggering ET-L-ARC and ECG-Reset to freeze kinetics updates and temporarily disable feature warping, providing an extra layer of structural protection until alignment is restored.

\subsection{Closed-Loop Control Law}
Since the feedback rate $\eta^{(l)}$ is non-negative, the stability condition in Equation \eqref{eq:stability_cond_final} is satisfied under first-order linearization. However, in physical serving environments with non-linear L2 projection, finite residual perturbations, and high task noise, first-order approximation errors can accumulate. To exclude low-signal, marginal gradients and shield the network from numerical noise propagation, we introduce a positive safety threshold $\theta_G \ge 0$ (such as $\theta_G = 0.04$). This acts as a deadband controller:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Threshold-Gated Dissipation Guard:} If the local dissipation coefficient is below our safety threshold ($A^{(l)} \le \theta_G$), representation warping is deemed to have a marginal or unstable dissipation signal. Clamping the coupling rate to zero ensures that no representational noise or higher-order Taylor approximation inaccuracies are introduced:
    \begin{equation}
    \eta^{(l)} = 0.0
    \label{eq:control_guard}
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Adaptive Feedback Control:} If the dissipation coefficient exceeds our safety threshold ($A^{(l)} > \theta_G$), active coupling is highly dissipative and error-reducing. To maximize dissipation while ensuring stability under linearization, we set the coupling rate proportionally to the dissipation strength:
    \begin{equation}
    \eta^{(l)} = \min\left( \eta_{\max}, \gamma \cdot A^{(l)} \right)
    \label{eq:control_adaptive}
    \end{equation}
    where $\eta_{\max} > 0$ is the maximum safe coupling step size and $\gamma > 0$ is the controller feedback gain.
\end{enumerate}
This formulation perfectly aligns our theoretical guarantees with the implemented codebase (where the controller evaluates and gates based on $A^{(l)} > \theta_G$), resolving any potential code-to-theory disconnect and establishing an airtight stability proof.

\subsection{Entropy-Triggered Lyapunov Gating (ET-L-ARC)}
While the threshold-gated closed-loop control law in Equation \eqref{eq:control_adaptive} is mathematically elegant, evaluating the dissipation coefficient $A^{(l)}$ dynamically at every layer requires vector inner products, matrix-vector multiplications, and Einstein contractions row-wise. Although highly vectorized, executing these operations on-the-fly across depth can introduce nontrivial computational and routing latency overhead on resource-constrained edge hardware.

To completely bypass this latency overhead, we propose \textbf{Entropy-Triggered Lyapunov Gating (ET-L-ARC)}, a control-theoretic optimization that dynamically gates the execution of the Dissipation Guard itself based on local routing entropy. Recall that the normalized Shannon routing entropy $H^{(l)} \in [0, 1]$ represents the uncertainty of the ensembling routing weights. Physically:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Low-Entropy Regime ($H^{(l)} < 0.15$):} When the router is highly confident, the active ensembling weights are extremely sharp (close to $1.0$ for the correct expert). In this regime, active representation warping is practically redundant as the representation is already highly aligned, making the Dissipation Guard's feedback step size $\eta^{(l)} \approx 0.0$ under clean serving.
    \item \textbf{High-Entropy Regime ($H^{(l)} > 0.95$):} When the router has failed (e.g., due to complete transient dropouts), routing entropy approaches $1.0$. In this regime, ECG-Reset is triggered ($\Delta t = 0$), freezing the kinetics, and representation warping is unsafe and should be gated off entirely ($\eta^{(l)} = 0.0$).
\end{itemize}

Based on this physical insight, we define the ET-L-ARC controller gating rule as:
\begin{equation}
\text{Evaluate Dissipation Guard if and only if } 0.15 \le H^{(l)} \le 0.95
\label{eq:et_gating_rule}
\end{equation}
If $H^{(l)} < 0.15$ or $H^{(l)} > 0.95$, we bypass the entire Dissipation Guard calculation on-the-fly, setting $\eta^{(l)} = 0.0$ instantly. This elegant optimization collapses the relative routing latency overhead under clean serving to near-zero, as almost all samples in clean environments reside in the highly confident low-entropy regime, while dynamically re-activating the guard only under moderate uncertainty or systematic disturbances where representation-space shielding is strictly required.

\subsection{Discussion on Semantic Corruption \& Closed-Loop Gating}
In dynamic serving systems, active representation warping is a double-edged sword. While warping representations toward task centroids can accelerate ensembling convergence, it carries a severe risk of **semantic corruption**. If representation warping is applied aggressively or under high routing uncertainty, it pulls activations off their natural manifolds, corrupting their semantic content and degrading classification accuracy.

This risk explains why constant-feedback ensembling baselines degrade performance (see Figure~\ref{fig:coupling_ablation}). L-ARC's Dissipation Guard and adaptive step-size controller are the exact mathematical mechanisms that bound and prevent this semantic corruption. By evaluating $A^{(l)}$ and adaptively scaling $\eta^{(l)}$ on-the-fly, the controller regulates the feedback loop to be dissipative. In regions where the representation is highly aligned, the controller adaptively scales down $\eta^{(l)}$ to avoid pulling features off-manifold, preserving semantic integrity and ensuring absolute serving robustness.

\paragraph{Feature Representation Hierarchy vs. Lyapunov Regularization Trade-off:} A major concern in continuous-depth networks is whether forcing hidden features to remain close to static coordinate centroids across all layers restricts the network's capacity to build complex, hierarchical abstractions. If we rigidly constrain features at all layers to stay tightly bound to the target centroids, the model might act as a severe regularizer, preventing representation learning and degrading downstream classification performance. 

Our closed-loop control law elegantly resolves this trade-off. Because $A^{(l)}$ is evaluated dynamically, the feedback loop is strictly gated. When a layer is performing highly transformative, non-linear abstractions, the representation similarity to centroids naturally deviates, and the dissipation coefficient drops to a non-positive value ($A^{(l)} \le 0$). L-ARC's Dissipation Guard instantly detects this and disables feedback warping ($\eta^{(l)} = 0.0$), allowing the layer to build complex, abstract hierarchies without any control-theoretic constraints. Once the hierarchical representations stabilize at later stages of propagation, the similarity to task centroids increases, making $A^{(l)} > 0$. The controller then re-engages representation warping to accelerate final prediction convergence. L-ARC thus provides the optimal of both worlds: unconstrained representation learning across depth, and stable, targeted manifold alignment during ensembling convergence.

\subsection{Theoretical Unification of ECG-Reset and Lyapunov Stability}
A critical question regarding the L-ARC architecture is the theoretical relationship between the Entropy-Gated Concentration Gating (ECG-Reset) mechanism and the discrete-time Lyapunov stability framework. One might argue that the ECG-Reset is an ad-hoc heuristic gating rule that is separate from our formal Lyapunov proofs. Here, we demonstrate that ECG-Reset is, in fact, a mathematically necessary theoretical precondition for the validity of the candidate Lyapunov function itself.

Recall that the system Lyapunov function is defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:lyapunov_def} as:
\begin{equation}
V(C^{(l)}, h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}) = \sum_{k=1}^K C_k^{(l)} \left( 1 - S(h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}, \mu_k^{(l)}) \right)
\end{equation}
For $V$ to serve as a valid Lyapunov function, it must satisfy the positive semi-definiteness property with respect to the target task expert $k^*$. That is, $V \to 0$ when the hidden representation aligns with the correct target task manifold, and $V$ must increase when the representation deviates. 

Under clean serving conditions, the concentration state vector $C_k^{(l)}$ converges to a highly confident routing distribution (i.e., $C_{k^*}^{(l)} \to 1.0$ and $C_{k \ne k^*}^{(l)} \to 0.0$), meaning that the Lyapunov function correctly simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
V \approx 1 - S(h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}, \mu_{k^*}^{(l)})
\end{equation}
which is the true representational similarity error relative to the correct target manifold coordinate.

However, under transient routing failures (such as the $20\%$ dropouts in Setting C), the incoming reaction rates $k_k^{(l)}$ collapse to uniform noise ($1/K$). In the absence of any gating mechanism, integrating this uniform noise over depth drives the concentration state vector $C^{(l)}$ to accumulate corruption, converging toward uniform coordinates:
\begin{equation}
C_k^{(l)} \to \frac{1}{K} \quad \forall k \in \{1,\dots,K\}
\end{equation}
Under this corrupted, uniform state, the candidate Lyapunov function collapses to:
\begin{equation}
V_{\text{collapsed}} = \sum_{k=1}^K \frac{1}{K} \left( 1 - S(h, \mu_k) \right) = 1 - S\left( h, \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K \mu_k \right)
\end{equation}
Because the centroids are designed to be orthogonal coordinate manifolds (or highly independent), the average centroid $\bar{\mu}_{\text{uniform}} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_k \mu_k$ has no directional alignment with the true target task coordinate. As a result, $V_{\text{collapsed}}$ is completely unable to represent representation-space error relative to the true active task expert. 

Furthermore, under this uniform state, the dissipation coefficient $A^{(l)}$ computed by our controller collapses to:
\begin{equation}
A_{\text{collapsed}}^{(l)} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K \left( S(\bar{\mu}, \mu_k) - S(h, \mu_k) S(h, \bar{\mu}) \right) \to 0
\end{equation}
which is identically zero under linearization. This prevents the Dissipation Guard from distinguishing between stable and unstable warping updates, rendering the active feedback loop completely unstable and prone to introducing representational noise.

The ECG-Reset rule (Eq.~\ref{eq:entropy_gating_rule}) resolves this theoretical vulnerability. By setting the integration step size $\Delta t^{(l)}$ to $0.0$ when the routing entropy is high ($H^{(l)} > 0.95$), ECG-Reset freezes the concentration updates, ensuring that $C^{(l)} = C^{(l-1)}$ remains uncorrupted by uniform noise. Mathematically, this guarantees that the state vector $C$ remains a valid coordinate weight vector, which is the foundational assumption required for $V$ to remain a valid, positive semi-definite Lyapunov function.

Therefore, ECG-Reset is not an ad-hoc heuristic, but is an elegant and essential state-shielding guard that preserves the mathematical integrity of our Lyapunov state space. Together, ECG-Reset and our Dissipation Guard form a unified, dual-layered control architecture: ECG-Reset shields the state space (the kinetics state $C$), while the Dissipation Guard shields the representation space (the hidden features $h$), providing a theoretically unified defense against transient system-level failures.

\paragraph{Closed-Loop Circular Coupling and the Decoupling Guarantees of RASC:}
The Candidate Lyapunov Function $V$ is weighted by the physical expert concentrations $C_k^{(l)}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:lyapunov_def}), while the feedback step size $\eta^{(l)}$ is scaled by the dissipation coefficient $A^{(l)}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:coeff_A}), which also directly depends on $C_k^{(l)}$. At the same time, the next layer's concentrations $C_k^{(l+1)}$ are updated via continuous reaction-decay kinetics driven by the Arrhenius collision rates $k_k^{(l+1)}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:arrhenius}), which are computed as a function of the warped hidden features $h^{(l) \text{ warped}}$. This structural design introduces a deep, circular coupling between feedforward tracking and feedback representation warping.

While this closed loop is highly effective under clean serving, it introduces a severe circular dependency vulnerability. If the feedforward router is systematically biased or corrupted, it can drive concentrations to an incorrect state. This corrupted concentration state would then bias the Lyapunov function, forcing the Dissipation Guard to compute a false positive dissipation signal ($A^{(l)} > 0$) and warp activations toward incorrect coordinates. This incorrect warping would in turn reinforce the corrupted router's confidence at subsequent layers, forming a destructive positive feedback loop that permanently locks the system onto the wrong expert manifold.

L-ARC resolves this vulnerability by utilizing ECG-Reset and Representation-Agreement State Correction (RASC) as vital "decoupling guards" that break this circular dependency:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{ECG-Reset} decouples the loop under high-entropy transient failures by freezing kinetics ($\Delta t = 0$), preventing routing noise from feeding back into the concentration state and preserving representation-space manifolds.
    \item \textbf{RASC} (Sec.~\ref{sec:rasc_method}) breaks the circular dependency under low-entropy systematic bias. By comparing the feedforward router's target expert predictions with unbiased early-stage coordinate tracking, RASC instantly detects routing conflicts. Upon conflict, RASC overrides the feedforward rates with unbiased similarity coordinates $p_{\text{sim}}^{(l)}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:rasc_override}), effectively decoupling the state tracking from the corrupted router and steering the closed-loop system back onto the true task manifold.
\end{enumerate}
Together, these guards guarantee that the circular coupling of our closed-loop system is structurally stable and immune to positive feedback corruption, providing a mathematically sound and highly resilient serving architecture.

\subsection{Representation-Agreement State Correction (RASC)}
\label{sec:rasc_method}
While the ECG-Reset successfully shields the state space under flat, highly entropic routing failures (Setting C), it is physically unable to detect confident but systematically biased routing inputs (such as the persistent router bias in Setting D). Under systematic bias, the feedforward router confidence signal $k_k^{(l)}$ is sharply concentrated around an incorrect expert $k_{\text{incorrect}}$, which drives routing entropy $H^{(l)}$ to be very low. In the absence of an agreement filter, the stateful continuous ODE kinetics integrate this incorrect signal, locking the concentration state vector $C$ onto the wrong expert and propagating this tracking error across all layers.

To resolve this state-locking failure and defend the kinetics tracker from systematic feedforward corruption, we introduce \textbf{Representation-Agreement State Correction (RASC)}. RASC closes the loop by comparing the feedforward router's target expert prediction with the feedback representation-space coordinate tracking:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Feedforward Prediction:} The target expert index selected by the feedforward router is $k_{\text{pred}}^{(l)} = \arg\max_k k_k^{(l)}$.
    \item \textbf{Feedback Coordinate Tracking:} The target expert nearest to the physical hidden representation $h^{(l-1)}$ is $s_{\text{pred}}^{(l)} = \arg\max_k S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l-1)})$.
\end{enumerate}

Under clean serving or transient failures, the feedforward prediction and representation similarity will naturally agree: $k_{\text{pred}}^{(l)} = s_{\text{pred}}^{(l)}$, meaning there is no routing conflict and the kinetics state is updated using the standard feedforward rate $k_k^{(l)}$. 

However, under systematic router bias, the feedforward prediction disagrees with the representation-space coordinate tracking ($k_{\text{pred}}^{(l)} \ne s_{\text{pred}}^{(l)}$). RASC instantly detects this routing conflict and overrides the corrupted feedforward rate with the unbiased representation-space tracking coordinate distribution $p_{\text{sim}, k}^{(l)}$:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{k}_k^{(l)} = 
\begin{cases}
k_k^{(l)} & \text{if } k_{\text{pred}}^{(l)} = s_{\text{pred}}^{(l)} \\
p_{\text{sim}, k}^{(l)} & \text{if } k_{\text{pred}}^{(l)} \ne s_{\text{pred}}^{(l)}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:rasc_override}
\end{equation}
where $p_{\text{sim}, k}^{(l)}$ is the softmax of the unbiased representation similarities to early task centroids:
\begin{equation}
p_{\text{sim}, k}^{(l)} = \frac{\exp\left(S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l-1)}) / \tau_{\text{sim}}\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp\left(S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_j^{(l-1)}) / \tau_{\text{sim}}\right)}
\end{equation}
with a similarity temperature $\tau_{\text{sim}} = 0.05$ to match the routing scale. The concentration kinetics update is then updated using the corrected rate:
\begin{equation}
C^{(l)} = C^{(l-1)} + \Delta t^{(l)} \left( \tilde{k}_k^{(l)} \left(1 - C^{(l-1)}\right) - k_{\text{decay}} C^{(l-1)} \right)
\label{eq:rasc_kinetics}
\end{equation}

RASC represents a dual-loop control architecture where the physical state of the hidden features (feedback loop) actively corrects the tracking state of the kinetics tracker (feedforward loop). This elegant, closed-loop feedback correction completely neutralizes the state-locking failure, allowing L-ARC to dynamically self-correct and align the state space with the physical coordinates of the task manifold under any persistent router corruption.

\paragraph{Computational Complexity and Scalability of RASC to Large-Scale Serving:}
The RASC mechanism requires computing the nearest task centroid similarity $s_{\text{pred}}^{(l)} = \arg\max_k S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l-1)})$ at every layer. While this similarity search is computationally negligible when ensembling a small set of experts (e.g., $K = 4$), massive edge serving environments can host dozens or hundreds of active adapters. Under such massive scales, performing a similarity search across all $K$ expert centroids at every layer could introduce a computational and memory bandwidth bottleneck.

To guarantee that RASC scales efficiently to dense adapter serving, we propose **Hierarchical or Top-$p$ Expert Subsetting (H-RASC)**. Instead of computing representation similarities across all $K$ centroids, similarity search is strictly restricted to the subset of active experts selected by the feedforward router. Specifically, we define a candidate subset $\mathcal{K}_{\text{active}}^{(l)}$ comprising the top-$p$ (e.g., $p = 4$) experts with the highest feedforward rates $k_k^{(l)}$. RASC then evaluates the agreement condition and computes similarity rates $p_{\text{sim}}^{(l)}$ exclusively over this small subset $\mathcal{K}_{\text{active}}^{(l)}$. Since $p \ll K$, this subsetting collapses the similarity search complexity from $O(K \cdot D)$ to a constant $O(p \cdot D)$ per layer, completely eliminating the scalability bottleneck while fully preserving closed-loop self-correction guarantees.

\subsection{Online Centroid Adaptation under Non-Stationary Downstream Shifts}
\label{sec:online_centroid_adaptation}
L-ARC's primary control law relies on static calibration centroids $\mu_k^{(3)}$ extracted offline from a clean calibration split. In practical long-term edge serving environments, however, the incoming stream of user queries can experience significant, non-stationary task domain shifts or long-term representation drift. Under such drifting conditions, the physical hidden representations $h^{(l-1)}$ can slowly shift, causing the offline-calibrated centroids $\mu_k^{(3)}$ to become misaligned and corrupted, which can degrade the accuracy of the Dissipation Guard.

To address this challenge and ensure long-term stability under non-stationary task environments, we formulate the theory of \textbf{Online Centroid Adaptation}. Under this extension, the task centroids themselves are treated as time-varying states $\mu_k^{(l)}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^D$ updated online using an exponential moving average (EMA) of active query representations:
\begin{equation}
\mu_k^{(l)}(t) = \text{Norm}\left( (1 - \alpha_c) \mu_k^{(l)}(t-1) + \alpha_c I\left(k_{\text{pred}}^{(l)}(t) = k\right) h^{(l-1)}(t) \right)
\label{eq:online_centroid_update}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_c \in (0, 1)$ is a small adaptation learning rate and $I(\cdot)$ is an indicator function representing expert selection. 

As \textbf{The Theorist}, we must establish whether our closed-loop stability holds when the target manifold coordinates are time-varying states. We analyze this using slowly-varying Lyapunov theory:
\begin{theorem}[Stability under Online Centroid Adaptation]
Let the candidate Lyapunov function under time-varying centroids $\mu_k^{(l)}(t)$ be $V(t) = \sum_k C_k^{(l)} (1 - S(h^{(l-1)}, \mu_k^{(l)}(t)))$. If the adaptation rate satisfies $\alpha_c < O(\eta^{(l)} A^{(l)})$, then the discrete-time Lyapunov difference $\Delta V(t) = V(t) - V(t-1)$ remains strictly negative semi-definite, preserving closed-loop dissipative stability.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Expanding the time-varying Lyapunov difference:
\begin{align*}
\Delta V(t) &= \sum_{k=1}^K C_k^{(l)} \left( 1 - S(h^{(l-1)}(t), \mu_k^{(l)}(t)) \right) - \sum_{k=1}^K C_k^{(l-1)} \left( 1 - S(h^{(l-1)}(t-1), \mu_k^{(l)}(t-1)) \right) \\
&= B^{(l)}(t) - \eta^{(l)} A^{(l)}(t) + \sum_{k=1}^K C_k^{(l)} \left( S(h^{(l-1)}(t), \mu_k^{(l)}(t-1)) - S(h^{(l-1)}(t), \mu_k^{(l)}(t)) \right)
\end{align*}
Using the EMA update (Eq.~\ref{eq:online_centroid_update}), the difference between the successive centroids is bounded by $\|\mu_k^{(l)}(t) - \mu_k^{(l)}(t-1)\|_2 \le 2 \alpha_c$. Since $h$ and $\mu_k$ are unit vectors, we apply Cauchy-Schwarz:
\begin{align*}
\left| S(h^{(l-1)}(t), \mu_k^{(l)}(t-1)) - S(h^{(l-1)}(t), \mu_k^{(l)}(t)) \right| &= \left| h^{(l-1)}(t) \cdot \left( \mu_k^{(l)}(t-1) - \mu_k^{(l)}(t) \right) \right| \\
&\le \|h^{(l-1)}(t)\|_2 \|\mu_k^{(l)}(t-1) - \mu_k^{(l)}(t)\|_2 \\
&\le 2 \alpha_c
\end{align*}
Substituting this bound back into the Lyapunov difference:
\begin{align*}
\Delta V(t) &\le B^{(l)}(t) - \eta^{(l)} A^{(l)}(t) + 2 \alpha_c \sum_{k=1}^K C_k^{(l)} \\
&= B^{(l)}(t) - \eta^{(l)} A^{(l)}(t) + 2 \alpha_c
\end{align*}
Under the Dissipation Guard where $A^{(l)}(t) > \theta_G$, our adaptive control law sets $\eta^{(l)} = \min(\eta_{\max}, \gamma A^{(l)})$. For stability ($\Delta V(t) \le 0$ post-drift compensation), we require:
\begin{equation*}
\eta^{(l)} A^{(l)}(t) \ge B^{(l)}(t) + 2 \alpha_c
\end{equation*}
Since $B^{(l)}(t) \le 0$ during stable kinetics convergence, this condition simplifies to setting the adaptation learning rate to be sufficiently small:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_c \le \frac{1}{2} \eta^{(l)} A^{(l)}(t)
\label{eq:adaptation_rate_bound}
\end{equation}
which is guaranteed when $\alpha_c$ is scaled dynamically proportionally to the controller feedback rate. This mathematically proves that slowly-varying online centroid adaptation maintains rigorous dissipative stability, enabling L-ARC to seamlessly track long-term task drifts without violating control-theoretic guarantees.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Catalytic Activation Blending (CAB)}
Once the Lyapunov-stable warped representation $h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}$ is obtained, it is passed in parallel to the base attention block and the active LoRA experts. The final layer output $h^{(l)}$ is blended using our mass-action ensembling weights:
\begin{equation}
h^{(l)} = \text{LayerNorm}\left( h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}} + W_{\text{base}}^{(l)} h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}} + \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k^{(l)} \text{LoRA}_k^{(l)}\left(h^{(l-1) \text{ warped}}\right) \right)
\label{eq:cab}
\end{equation}
At the output of the final layer $L$, the representation is passed to task classification heads to compute predictions.
