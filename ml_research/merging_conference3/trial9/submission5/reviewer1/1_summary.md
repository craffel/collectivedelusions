# 1. Summary of the Paper

## Main Topic and Approach
This paper presents a rigorous methodological audit and experimental deconstruction of dynamic activation-space model merging. Dynamic model merging serves multiple task-specific expert adapters (such as LoRA) by blending their activations dynamically layer-by-layer during a single parallel forward pass. 

Recently, several works (e.g., SABLE, ChemMerge) have proposed complex metaphorical, mathematical, or physical ensembling routing architectures. SABLE uses training-free nearest-centroid similarity projections, while ChemMerge models routing trajectories as first-order chemical reaction kinetics using ordinary differential equations (ODEs). These works claim that classical parametric routers (e.g., linear gating heads optimized via gradient descent) catastrophically fail in low-data calibration regimes, justifying the need for their complex, training-free, or stateful metaphorical approaches.

The authors of this paper adopt a skeptical methodological stance. They hypothesize that the reported failures of classical parametric routers are not due to fundamental representational limitations, but are rather confounding artifacts of weak experimental setups—specifically, random initialization of routing heads and a lack of proper regularization on tiny calibration datasets. 

To test this, the authors introduce a standard, properly regularized classical linear router with a Maximum-Entropy Zero-Initialization prior. They evaluate these models in a high-fidelity 14-layer synthetic Analytical Coordinate Sandbox (ICS) under two main data regimes: a small-sample constraint regime ($N_{\text{cal}} = 64$) and a large-sample generalization regime ($N_{\text{cal}} = 4000$), with injected representation anisotropy (entanglement $\rho \in [0.0, 0.5]$). Finally, they validate their findings on a pre-trained BERT-Tiny foundation model on SST-2 and QQP datasets.

## Key Findings
1. **The Small-Sample Overfitting Bottleneck:** The classical router indeed fails in the small-sample regime ($N_{\text{cal}} = 64$), lagging severely behind SABLE and ChemMerge. The authors demonstrate that this gap is entirely an overfitting artifact. SABLE and ChemMerge excel here because their training-free, cosine-based formulations act as highly effective inductive geometric priors requiring zero parameter updates.
2. **Spectacular Large-Sample Recovery:** When provided with an adequate calibration budget ($N_{\text{cal}} = 4000$), classical parametric routers recover completely. The unregularized Softmax router achieves $76.22\%$ accuracy at $\rho=0.0$, outperforming SABLE ($73.76\%$) by $+2.46\%$ absolute (a highly statistically significant margin with $p = 0.0062$) and closely approaching ChemMerge's performance ceiling ($76.90\%$).
3. **The Bias-Variance Trade-off in Gating Regularization:** Strong L2 regularization ($\lambda = 10^{-2}$) is essential under small-sample constraints to survive the overfitting bottleneck. However, under data abundance, it introduces a severe performance-limiting constraint bias that caps accuracy at $74.10\%$. Scaling the regularization strength down to $\lambda = 10^{-4}$ under large-sample abundance resolves this constraint bias, reaching $75.70\%$ accuracy while preserving the structural benefits of zero-initialization.
4. **Control-Theoretic Explanation of Stateful Trajectory Smoothing:** ChemMerge’s continuous-time kinetics introduce a severe "representational lag" (intermediate representation quality of $0.912$ at Layer 14 vs. $0.992$ for the classical router). Under a control-theoretic lens, this lag acts as a beneficial temporal low-pass filter (closed-loop stateful inertia) that stabilizes ensembling trajectories under heavy activation noise, explaining its superior performance ceiling.
5. **Debunking the Jitter Myth:** By training a layer-wise classical router with separate heads, the authors show it exhibits exceptionally smooth routing trajectories (Jitter: $0.0068 - 0.0458$), comparable to or lower than ChemMerge ($0.0368$), debunking the claim that parametric routers suffer from catastrophic layer-wise oscillations.
6. **Open-Loop vs. Closed-Loop Smoothing (EMA-SABLE):** Applying a simple Exponential Moving Average (EMA) to SABLE (EMA-SABLE) boosts its accuracy by $+1.00\%$ absolute. However, ChemMerge's superior performance ceiling proves its ODE kinetics act as a true closed-loop feedback controller, correcting early routing decisions dynamically using intermediate layer activations.
7. **Real-World BERT-Tiny Validation:** On a real pre-trained model, parametric routers outperform SABLE and ChemMerge under sufficient calibration data ($N_{\text{cal}} = 500$). Under extreme data scarcity ($N_{\text{cal}} = 32$), the unregularized router does not collapse but achieves the highest accuracy ($61.90\%$) because the underlying semantic spaces (sentiment vs. duplicate questions) are highly disjoint and easily separated by a simple linear hyperplane.

## Explicitly Claimed Contributions (with Evidence)
1. **Rigorous Methodological Audit:** Systematically isolates initialization and regularization as critical confounding variables in prior model merging literature (supported by Tables 1 and 2, and the $2\times2$ factorial initialization ablation).
2. **Maximum-Entropy Zero-Initialization Prior:** Proposes a simple, unbiased zero-initialization strategy that acts as an elegant safety fallback, gracefully degrading to static Uniform Merging under extreme regularization (supported by mathematical formulation and empirical ablation).
3. **High-Fidelity Analytical Coordinate Sandbox (ICS):** Designs a 14-layer, 192-dimensional PyTorch-based simulation environment that models representation cones and expert adaptation dynamics (supported by Section 3.5).
4. **Demystification of Stateful Continuous-Time Kinetics:** Explains ChemMerge's trajectory smoothing through control theory as closed-loop feedback stabilization and identifies its serving-time numerical clamping hack (supported by Section 2.3, Figure 1b, and Section 4.3.5).
5. **Sample Complexity Crossover Mapping:** Identifies the precise data boundaries ($N_{\text{cal}} \approx 256$ to $512$ samples) where learning-based gating overtakes training-free geometric priors (supported by Figure 2a).
6. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Audits:** Sweeps routing temperature $\tau$ for SABLE and ChemMerge, highlighting the hyperparameter stability of closed-loop kinetics over stateless centroid methods (supported by Figure 2b).
7. **Empirical Validation on Real Pre-trained Weights:** Validates findings using BERT-Tiny with custom LoRA adapters on SST-2 and QQP datasets (supported by Section 4.7).
