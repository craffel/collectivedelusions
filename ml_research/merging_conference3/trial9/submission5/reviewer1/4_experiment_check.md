# 4. Experiment Check

## Critical Evaluation of Experimental Setup
The experimental setup of this methodological audit is exceptionally thorough and rigorous:
* **Exhaustive sweeps:** Evaluating performance across 6 representation entanglement levels ($\rho \in [0.0, 0.5]$) and 2 distinct data regimes ($N_{\text{cal}} = 64$ and $N_{\text{cal}} = 4000$) provides a comprehensive picture of router robustness.
* **Statistical Rigor:** All reported accuracies are averages across 5 independent random evaluation seeds, accompanied by standard deviations. The authors also conduct a paired t-test to confirm the statistical significance of their classical router's $+2.46\%$ performance gain over SABLE (yielding a highly significant $p = 0.0062$ at $\rho=0.0$).
* **Baselines:** The baselines are strong and highly relevant, including Uniform Merging, SABLE (stateless nearest-centroid), and ChemMerge (stateful ODE continuous-time routing).

## Do the Results Support the Claims?
Yes, the experimental results provide overwhelming support for the authors' claims:

1. **The Small-Sample Overfitting Bottleneck (Claim Supported):** Table 1 clearly reproduces the reported collapse of classical routers when data is scarce ($N_{\text{cal}} = 64$), where the best Softmax configuration achieves only $68.14\%$ accuracy, severely lagging behind SABLE ($73.76\%$) and ChemMerge ($76.90\%$). This supports the claim that the overfitting bottleneck is a fundamental sample-complexity constraint.
2. **Spectacular Large-Sample Recovery (Claim Supported):** Table 2 shows that when the calibration set is expanded to $N_{\text{cal}} = 4000$ samples, the unregularized Softmax Router achieves $76.22\%$ accuracy, significantly outperforming SABLE ($73.76\%$) and closely approaching ChemMerge ($76.90\%$). This proves that classical linear routers possess robust representational capacity once data is abundant.
3. **The Bias-Variance Trade-off in Gating Regularization (Claim Supported):** The experiments sweep weight decay strengths ($\lambda \in \{0.0, 10^{-4}, 10^{-2}\}$). In Table 1 ($N_{\text{cal}} = 64$), strong regularization ($\lambda = 10^{-2}$) is mandatory. In Table 2 ($N_{\text{cal}} = 4000$), strong regularization limits performance to $74.10\%$ due to constraint bias, while weak regularization ($\lambda = 10^{-4}$) resolves this bias to reach a near-optimal $75.70\%$ accuracy. This beautifully demonstrates the need to scale regularization inversely with calibration dataset size.
4. **Control-Theoretic Explanation of ChemMerge (Claim Supported):** Tracking layer-wise intermediate representation quality (Figure 1b) reveals that ChemMerge introduces a representational lag (similarity of $0.912$ at Layer 14 vs. $0.992$ for the classical router). This lag acting as a closed-loop temporal low-pass filter explains why ChemMerge stabilizes trajectories and achieves its superior performance ceiling under the sandbox's highly unbalanced activation noise (where SVHN has a massive noise variance of $\sigma_3 = 1.20$).
5. **Debunking the Jitter Myth (Claim Supported):** The layer-wise vs. layer-invariant ablation study (Table 4) shows that a true layer-wise classical router achieves highly smooth routing trajectories (Jitter: $0.0068 - 0.0458$) comparable to ChemMerge's kinetics ($0.0368$), empirically debunking the claim that parametric routers suffer from severe layer-wise weight oscillations.
6. **Open-Loop vs. Closed-Loop Stabilization (Claim Supported):** Table 5 shows that while simple Exponential Moving Average smoothing (EMA-SABLE) boosts SABLE's accuracy by $+1.00\%$ absolute, ChemMerge maintains a $+2.60\%$ absolute performance premium ($77.50\%$). This proves that ChemMerge's continuous ODE kinetics function as a true closed-loop feedback controller, correcting routing weights dynamically.
7. **Real-World BERT-Tiny Validation (Claim Supported):** Table 6 confirms that under $N_{\text{cal}}=500$, parametric routers outperform SABLE and ChemMerge on real natural language text. Crucially, under $N_{\text{cal}}=32$, the unregularized classical router does not collapse but achieves the highest accuracy ($61.90\%$). This supports the nuanced claim that the overfitting bottleneck is task-dependent: when tasks are semantically disjoint, classical parametric routers can easily locate a stable separating hyperplane with minimal data.
