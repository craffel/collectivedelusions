# 2. Novelty and Originality Check

## Originality and Positioning
The paper's positioning is highly original, critical, and refreshing. Rather than proposing yet another mathematically complex or metaphorical architecture (which has been a prominent trend in the activation-space model merging literature), it adopts the skeptical perspective of **The Methodologist**. The core novelty of this paper is not the introduction of a new model, but rather a **rigorous scientific audit and empirical deconstruction** of existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) claims.

Methodological audits are a crucial and highly valuable genre of machine learning research (similar to audits in recommender systems, graph neural networks, and neural ODEs). This paper successfully:
1. Deconstructs the widely accepted narrative that classical parametric routers "catastrophically fail" in low-data regimes.
2. Formulates a simpler, elegant alternative: **Maximum-Entropy Zero-Initialization with Proper L2 Regularization**, which serves as an intuitive and mathematically sound baseline.
3. Provides a novel **control-theoretic re-interpretation** of the complex chemical kinetics in ChemMerge, reframing "representational lag" (a potential defect in feedforward routing) as "closed-loop feedback stabilization."

## Comparison to Related Work
- **SABLE (Liang et al., 2024):** SABLE is stateless and uses training-free nearest-centroid similarity. The paper properly identifies SABLE's true value as an inductive geometric prior that is highly sample-efficient rather than representationally superior. This is a crucial distinction that was glossed over in SABLE's original publication.
- **ChemMerge (Weber et al., 2025):** ChemMerge uses continuous-time ordinary differential equations based on chemical reaction kinetics to smooth ensembling trajectories. The paper validates that ChemMerge provides a significant performance ceiling under extreme noise, but reframes its mechanism as a closed-loop controller (low-pass filter) rather than an optimal representation system.
- **Sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) Literature:** The paper successfully connects its findings (zero-initialization and proper L2 weight decay) to the well-established sparse MoE router literature (e.g., Shazeer et al., 2017). This connection is highly valuable because it demonstrates that preventing router collapse via regularization is a foundational principle in deep learning that was simply forgotten or omitted in the model merging sub-field, reinforcing the paper's role as a methodological audit.

## Novelty Gaps and Potential Redundancy
While the paper is highly original in its critique, there are a few minor gaps in its positioning and connections to prior literature:
1. **Static vs. Dynamic Baselines:** The paper uses Uniform Merging as the static baseline. However, other static baseline alternatives (e.g., Fisher Merging, Task Arithmetic, or TIES-Merging) are widely used in parameter-merging. While activation-space ensembling is fundamentally different, comparing the final performance of the best dynamic router with more advanced static parameter-merging methods would help contextualize the exact trade-offs (compute vs. accuracy) of dynamic serving.
2. **The Limitation of Binary Task Exclusivity in BERT-Tiny:** The BERT-Tiny validation evaluated on actual GLUE validation splits of SST-2 (sentiment analysis) and QQP (duplicate question detection). However, both SST-2 and QQP are binary classification tasks (with exactly 2 output classes). In Section 4.9 (BERT-Tiny implementation), the model evaluates joint serving by blending the classifiers' logits directly:
   $$\text{logits} = \alpha_0 \cdot \text{classifier}_0(\text{pooled}) + \alpha_1 \cdot \text{classifier}_1(\text{pooled})$$
   This direct blending runs successfully *only* because both tasks have the exact same number of classes (2). If the multi-task setup included a task with 3 classes (e.g., MNLI) or 10 classes, this direct logit blending would crash due to shape mismatches. The authors have now transparently discussed this limitation in Section 4.9 and Section 5.3, which is highly commendable.
3. **Stateless Nature of Embedding Routing:** In the real-world validation on BERT-Tiny, the parametric router uses pooled Layer 0 (embedding-level) features to make its routing decision: `test_reps_emb = get_pooler_embeddings(test_ids, test_att)`. It does not ingest any intermediate activations. This represents a stateless, layer-invariant design. While this reduces serving overhead, it does not evaluate how a classical parametric router would perform if it were trained on intermediate hidden activations, which would be an interesting extension.
