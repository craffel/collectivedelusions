# 5. Presentation, Impact, and Recommendations

## Major Strengths
- **Highly Practical Motivation:** The paper tackles a very real and critical bottleneck in edge-AI deployment—namely, the post-training quantization (PTQ) heterogeneity across different target compilers and accelerators (such as mobile TPUs, DSPs, and runtime compilers). 
- **Zero-Overhead Inference:** The proposed solution is training-free, requires no hardware metadata, and adds absolutely zero inference-time latency, memory, or computational overhead, as the optimized continuous coefficients are merged and compiled into a standard quantized model.
- **Empirical Accuracy Improvements:** Although the full framework is over-complicated, the inclusion of scale and zero-point noise perturbation during test-time adaptation successfully drives clear accuracy improvements over the naive and standard Q-Merge baselines across all five target accelerators.

## Areas for Improvement (Minimalist Recommendations)
- **Embrace Parsimony and Simplify the Method:** The authors should simplify their framework by focusing on the simpler, more elegant variant: standard single-operator optimization with Scale and Zero-Point Noise Perturbation (SZNP Only). The ablation study (Table 2) demonstrates that this simpler model achieves better average performance (**50.45%**) than the full, more complex OmniMerge framework (**50.33%**) that combines both SZNP and Stochastic Operator Sampling (SOS). Removing SOS eliminates the need to build and maintain a discrete pool of multiple quantization operators, dramatically simplifying the implementation while actually improving final accuracy.
- **Simplify the Regularization Objective:** The Task-Consensus Regularization (TCR) objective in Equation (20) is over-parameterized, introducing two separate penalty terms and two hyperparameters ($\beta = 0.1, \gamma = 0.5$). The authors should simplify this into a single, elegant distance penalty (e.g., penalizing only the absolute deviation from starting uniform coefficients) to reduce hyperparameter tuning complexity and improve the method's elegance.
- **Adopt a Realistic and Concise Experimental Setup:** The authors should replace the over-engineered setup of using a pre-trained `ViT-Tiny` (5.7M parameters) on upsampled $28 \times 28$ MNIST/FashionMNIST images. A much more appropriate and elegant setup would be evaluating standard convolutional backbones (e.g., ResNet) on realistic multi-task/domain adaptation benchmarks, or evaluating the Vision Transformer on tasks where it is actually standard and necessary.
- **Ensure Symmetric, Fair Experimental Controls:** The authors must address the asymmetric learning rate tuning. Comparing OmniMerge with a learning rate of $\eta = 2 \times 10^{-2}$ against baselines restricted to $\eta = 10^{-2}$ under a highly restricted 15-step budget is methodologically unsound. The authors should run all methods to full convergence (e.g., 50 or 100 steps) and compare them under symmetric hyperparameter settings to isolate the true performance gains.
- **Temper Unsupported Speculative Claims:** The authors should remove the speculative and statistically unsupported claim that "discrete weight rounding acts as a beneficial noise filter." The tiny 0.39% absolute difference (exactly 4 correct predictions out of 1024) between quantized and unquantized OmniMerge is statistically insignificant and well within random binomial noise.

## Overall Presentation Quality
- **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is clearly written, and the overall narrative flow is easy to follow. The introduction, related work, and experiment structure are well-thought-out.
- **Mathematical Over-Specification:** The overall presentation quality is degraded by mathematical padding. The inclusion of standard textbook formulas for asymmetric, symmetric, and double quantization (Equations 5-18) takes up excessive space. These should be heavily condensed or replaced with a brief, elegant description and references, keeping the methodology focused on the key novel contributions.
- **Missing Statistical Rigor:** The paper lacks error bars, standard deviations, or multi-run seed evaluations, which are critical for validating a highly stochastic test-time adaptation framework running under a very short 15-step optimization window.

## Potential Impact and Significance
- **Constrained Practical Significance:** The potential impact of this work is severely limited by the extremely weak performance of the base task experts. SVHN expert performance is only 28.91% (barely above random guessing), and the overall full-precision ensembling ceiling is restricted to 38.67% average accuracy across tasks. It is highly doubtful whether practitioners will adopt a merging framework where the underlying task models are so poorly trained and non-functional.
- **Potential Utility if Simplified:** If the authors address these limitations, simplify their framework to the elegant and performant SZNP-only baseline, and validate it using fully converged, realistic task experts on standard benchmarks, this research could have a significant and highly beneficial impact on edge MLOps and robust model-deployment pipelines.
