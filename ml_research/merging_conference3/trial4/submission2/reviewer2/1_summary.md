# 1. Summary of the Paper

## Main Topic and Approach
This paper addresses a practical and critical bottleneck in edge-computing deep learning deployment: **Cross-Schema Performance Degradation** in weight-space model merging. Specifically, when merging specialized task-specific experts fine-tuned from a shared pre-trained backbone, existing quantization-aware model merging methods (such as Q-Merge) optimize layer-wise blending coefficients ($\Lambda$) under a single simulated post-training quantization (PTQ) operator (usually Symmetric Per-Channel). However, edge-computing fleets are highly heterogeneous and run compilers/accelerators that implement distinct, incompatible PTQ schemas (e.g., Symmetric Per-Tensor, Asymmetric Per-Channel, or Double Quantization). This causes the optimized blending coefficients to overfit to the specific rounding boundaries of the simulated training operator, leading to notable performance drops when deployed on mismatched target hardware.

To resolve this, the authors introduce **OmniMerge**, a training-free, multi-schema stochastic co-optimization framework designed to produce hardware-invariant merging coefficients. OmniMerge operates during a short test-time adaptation phase over a small calibration stream of unlabeled data ($N_{\text{cal}} = 64$ images per task) using entropy minimization combined with Task-Consensus Regularization (TCR). The key mechanisms of OmniMerge are:
1. **Stochastic Operator Sampling (SOS):** Rather than keeping the quantization operator static, the active quantization schema is stochastically sampled at each optimization step from a discrete pool of four hardware-standard operators: $\mathcal{Q} = \{Q_{\text{sym, tens}}, Q_{\text{sym, chan}}, Q_{\text{asym, tens}}, Q_{\text{asym, chan}}\}$. This acts as a parameter-space data augmentation to prevent boundary-overfitting.
2. **Scale and Zero-Point Noise Perturbation (SZNP):** Multiplicative and additive Gaussian noise is dynamically injected into the scale and zero-point parameters during the forward pass of the test-time optimization phase to smooth the non-differentiable loss landscape and help the optimizer escape brittle local minima.
3. **Task-Consensus Regularization (TCR):** A penalty that prevents single-task dominance and encourages merging coefficients across different tasks to remain near their group consensus mean.

Gradients are propagated back to the continuous coefficients using the Straight-Through Estimator (STE) during a rapid 15-step on-device adaptation. Crucially, the noise perturbation is inactive during inference, and the optimized coefficients are used to compile standard quantized models, introducing zero inference-time computational or memory overhead.

## Key Findings
1. **Cross-Schema Overfitting:** Optimizing merging coefficients strictly under a single quantization schema (e.g., Symmetric Per-Channel) results in performance degradation when evaluated on mismatched schemas (e.g., Symmetric Per-Tensor).
2. **Superior Generalization:** OmniMerge successfully closes this generalization gap, achieving superior and robust multi-task classification accuracy across five distinct target post-training quantization schemas (Symmetric Per-Tensor, Symmetric Per-Channel, Asymmetric Per-Tensor, Asymmetric Per-Channel, and Double Quantization) compared to Naive merging, Quantized AdaMerging, and Q-Merge.
3. **Out-of-Pool Generalization:** OmniMerge generalizes to completely unseen target operators, such as Double Quantization, which was not part of the active stochastic operator pool during optimization.
4. **Regularization through Weight Discretization:** The authors observe that weight-space discretization (quantization rounding) can act as a beneficial regularizer (similar to noise filtering or hard-thresholding), occasionally causing certain quantized configurations to slightly outperform their full-precision ceilings.

## Claimed Contributions and Evidence
- **Identification of Cross-Schema Performance Degradation:** The paper demonstrates that single-operator optimization via STE causes boundary-overfitting. This is backed by evidence showing a performance drop of Q-Merge (optimized under Symmetric Per-Channel) to 45.90% when evaluated on Symmetric Per-Tensor.
- **Introduction of OmniMerge (SOS + SZNP):** A novel, hardware-invariant model merging framework. The paper provides mathematical formulations for asymmetric, symmetric, and double quantization, along with the co-optimization details.
- **Empirical Demonstration of SOTA Performance:** Evaluated on a `ViT-Tiny` backbone across MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, and SVHN, the authors show that OmniMerge achieves up to 50.78% average accuracy (Table 1), outperforming both Q-Merge and Quantized AdaMerging across all 5 target compilers.
- **Discussion of the Denoising Regularization of Discretization:** The authors provide control experiments showing that quantized OmniMerge achieves 50.78% accuracy (under Symmetric Per-Channel), which is +0.39% higher than its unquantized FP16 counterpart (50.39%) under the exact same coefficients.
