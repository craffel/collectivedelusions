\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis of Scale and Zero-Point Perturbation Noise}
We conduct a sensitivity analysis on the scale and zero-point noise standard deviation parameters ($\sigma_{\text{scale}}$ and $\sigma_{\text{zero}}$) used during test-time adaptation. We evaluate the average multi-task accuracy across all five target hardware post-training quantization (PTQ) schemas under robust 8-bit quantization. The results are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:noise_sensitivity}.

Our empirical findings validate that adding moderate noise acts as an effective parameter-space landscape smoother, smoothing out local discontinuities in the rounding boundaries and allowing the gradient-based optimizer to escape fragile minima. Specifically, when noise is entirely omitted ($\sigma_{\text{scale}} = 0.0, \sigma_{\text{zero}} = 0.0$), the average accuracy drops to 48.24\% due to localized boundary overfitting. Moderate-noise configurations recover some robustness, while our recommended configuration ($\sigma_{\text{scale}} = 0.01, \sigma_{\text{zero}} = 0.02$) achieves the optimal average multi-task accuracy of 50.33\%. Conversely, when the noise standard deviation is excessively high (e.g., $\sigma_{\text{scale}} = 0.10, \sigma_{\text{zero}} = 0.20$), performance degrades severely to 47.15\% because the gradient signals propagated by the Straight-Through Estimator (STE) become corrupted by high-variance stochastic noise, destabilizing the optimization path.
