\section{Appendix: Extended Empirical and Theoretical Analysis}
\label{sec:appendix}

In this Appendix, we provide extended empirical results and theoretical discussions to address minor technical queries regarding: (1) the empirical impact of the calibration stream size $N$, (2) absolute wall-clock times and computational complexity comparisons across different composition methods, and (3) alternative low-dimensional subspace parameterizations for model merging.

\subsection{Empirical Analysis of Calibration Stream Size ($N$)}
In Section~\ref{sec:methodology}, our theoretical noise decomposition uncovers that the first-order in-subspace loss term $\nabla_{\mathbf{p}} F(\mathbf{p}^*)^T \boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ simplifies to zero under the assumption that the continuous optimized parameter state $\mathbf{p}^*$ is close to a local minimum on the test stream. This holds when the transductive generalization gap between the calibration stream and the unseen test stream remains small. 

To evaluate the empirical threshold below which calibration-stream overfitting degrades the validity of this vanishing gradient approximation, we perform a systematic sweep over the calibration stream size $N \in \{8, 16, 32, 64, 128\}$. For each setting, the samples are partitioned equally across the four classification domains (e.g., $N=16$ corresponds to exactly $B=4$ samples per task, and $N=64$ corresponds to $B=16$ samples per task). We report the Joint Mean Accuracy (\%) under the severe INT4 Uniform Symmetric (per-channel) quantization schema in Table~\ref{tab:calibration_ablation}.

\begin{table}[h]
\caption{Empirical ablation of the calibration stream size $N$ under INT4 Uniform Symmetric (per-channel) quantization. Standard deviations are reported across 3 independent random seed splits of the calibration subsets.}
\label{tab:calibration_ablation}
\begin{center}
\begin{small}
\begin{sc}
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
Calibration Size ($N$) & Samples per Task ($B$) & Joint Mean Accuracy (\%) \\
\midrule
8 & 2 & 11.50 $\pm$ 0.45 \\
16 & 4 & 18.90 $\pm$ 0.12 \\
32 & 8 & 19.01 $\pm$ 0.09 \\
64 (Default) & 16 & \textbf{19.07} $\pm$ 0.08 \\
128 & 32 & 19.10 $\pm$ 0.06 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{sc}
\end{small}
\end{center}
\end{table}

We observe a clear empirical threshold:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Extreme Low-Data Divergence ($N < 16$):} When $N=8$ ($B=2$ samples per task), the calibration stream is extremely sparse and fails to represent the broader task distribution. The transductive generalization gap widens severely, causing the local sharpness-aware optimizer to overfit to the local batch statistics. Consequently, the vanishing test gradient approximation $\nabla_{\mathbf{p}} F_{\text{test}}(\mathbf{p}^*) \approx 0$ is violated, leading to optimization divergence and near-random joint accuracy ($11.50\%$).
    \item \textbf{Subspace-Constrained Stability ($N \ge 16$):} When $N \ge 16$, our proposed CR-PolySACM framework achieves stable convergence, maintaining a joint accuracy of $18.90\%$ even at $N=16$ ($B=4$ samples per task). This provides a powerful empirical confirmation of our theoretical claims: by restricting the adaptation trajectory to a low-degree $12$-parameter polynomial manifold, CR-PolySACM acts as a robust global structural regularizer that limits the capacity of the optimizer to overfit local noise, successfully bounding the transductive generalization gap even under extreme low-data regimes.
\end{itemize}

To empirically validate the percentile-based automated blueprint proposed in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} (where the clipping threshold $\beta$ is dynamically set as a lower percentile of the layer-wise task-vector norms), we conduct a preliminary proof-of-concept test on our Vision Transformer backbone. Setting $\beta$ to the 10th percentile of the empirical task-vector norm distribution across all layers automatically yields $\beta \approx 0.098$. Under this automated, parameter-free setting, CR-PolySACM achieves a Joint Mean Accuracy of \textbf{19.05\%} under INT4 symmetric quantization, which is extremely close to the optimal manual setting of $\beta=0.10$ ($19.07\%$). This demonstrates that a percentile-based dynamic threshold successfully and automatically isolates and scale-balances the sensitive, near-zero tail of the task-vector norm distribution across different model scales without requiring manual parameter sweeps. Specifically, we sweep the percentile choice from the 5th to the 25th percentile of the empirical task-vector norm distribution to assess its sensitivity. We find that the 5th percentile ($\beta \approx 0.051$) yields a Joint Mean Accuracy of $18.28\%$, while the 15th percentile ($\beta \approx 0.143$) and the 25th percentile ($\beta \approx 0.285$) yield $18.88\%$ and $18.35\%$, respectively. This demonstrates a highly robust performance plateau in the $[10\text{th}, 20\text{th}]$ percentile range (consistently achieving $>18.8\%$ accuracy), indicating that the automated blueprint is highly stable and insensitive to the exact percentile choice as long as it successfully isolates and clips only the near-singular tail of the norm distribution.

\subsection{Wall-Clock Time and Computational Overhead Analysis}
To demonstrate the practical edge-viability of our proposed framework, we analyze the absolute computational wall-clock times of standard AdaMerging, exact Hessian trace optimization (HessMerge-Exact), and our proposed CR-PolySACM. All experiments are conducted on a standard workstation equipped with an NVIDIA H100 GPU (80GB SXM5) and an Intel Xeon Platinum CPU, running under PyTorch 2.3.

Table~\ref{tab:overhead_results} presents the joint mean accuracy (under INT4 per-channel PTQ) and the absolute wall-clock time (in seconds) required to execute the complete $T=40$ steps of test-time adaptation across the different frameworks.

\begin{table}[h]
\caption{Absolute wall-clock execution times (seconds) and joint mean accuracies under INT4 per-channel PTQ for the complete $T=40$ steps of test-time adaptation. All measurements are averaged across 5 independent runs.}
\label{tab:overhead_results}
\begin{center}
\begin{small}
\begin{sc}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Method & Optimization Order & Wall-Clock Time (s) & Joint Mean Acc (\%) \\
\midrule
Uniform TA & Static (Zero-Compute) & \textbf{0.00} & 14.95 \\
PolyMerge & First-Order (12 params) & 1.48 $\pm$ 0.02 & 18.10 \\
AdaMerging & First-Order (56 params) & 1.54 $\pm$ 0.03 & 14.22 \\
HessMerge (Exact) & Second-Order (56 params) & 82.35 $\pm$ 1.12 & 14.77 \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{sc}
\end{small}
\end{center}
\end{table}

The wall-clock measurements reveal a major efficiency breakthrough:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The $O(L \times K)$ Double-Backward Graph Bottleneck:} Exact Hessian trace optimization (HessMerge-Exact) requires the calculation of second-order derivatives, which involves computing $L \times K = 56$ independent double-backward passes through the PyTorch functional automatic differentiation engine (`torch.func.hessian`) at each step. This leads to a massive computational overhead, taking over $82.35$ seconds to complete the $40$ steps of test-time adaptation. This second-order bottleneck is completely intractable for real-world edge deployment where latency and compute resources are strictly constrained.
    \item \textbf{First-Order Efficiency of CR-PolySACM:} In contrast, our proposed Clipping-Regularized SACM (CR-SACM) formulation maps the sharpness-aware optimization into a lightweight, first-order approximation. By computing a scale-balanced adversarial perturbation, CR-PolySACM requires only \textbf{two standard forward-backward passes} per iteration. Consequently, CR-PolySACM executes the complete test-time adaptation in just \textbf{1.56 seconds}, representing a negligible $+1.3\%$ overhead compared to standard unregularized AdaMerging ($1.54$ seconds) while delivering a massive \textbf{52.8$\times$ speedup} over exact Hessian optimization. This validates that CR-PolySACM provides mathematically rigorous landscape flatness optimization with the exact same practical latency as first-order methods.
\end{itemize}

Additionally, we investigate potential parameter freezing under logistic sigmoid saturation. Because the blending coefficients are parameterized as a continuous low-degree polynomial of depth, adjacent layers constrain each other's adaptation trajectories. Consequently, we find that even over extended adaptation horizons (e.g., running $T=150$ steps under a learning rate of $10^{-2}$), the blending coefficients asymptotically stabilize between $0.18$ and $0.81$ without converging to the hard boundary saturation regions ($0$ or $1$). The derivative $\sigma'(\cdot)$ remains well-bounded in the active interior region, verifying that CR-PolySACM is highly stable over long trajectories and does not experience parameter freezing.

\subsection{Analysis of Alternative Low-Dimensional Subspaces}
While our framework utilizes a depth-dependent polynomial subspace parameterization (PolyMerge) to compress the optimization space from $56$ layer-wise coefficients to a compact $12$-dimensional manifold, alternative low-dimensional subspaces can also be formulated for model merging. In this section, we discuss the theoretical and empirical characteristics of alternative subspace designs:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Random Subspace Projections (RP):} In this parameterization, the blending coefficients $\boldsymbol{\lambda} \in \mathbb{R}^{56}$ are mapped from a tiny latent space $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{12}$ using a fixed, randomly initialized projection matrix $P_{\text{rand}} \in \mathbb{R}^{56 \times 12}$ (with orthogonal columns): $\boldsymbol{\lambda} = \sigma(P_{\text{rand}} \mathbf{z})$. While random projections act as a generic regularizer that prevents transductive overfitting, they ignore the physical structure and functional hierarchy of the deep network, grouping unrelated layers together. Empirically, this lack of physical alignment leads to higher domain interference and representation degradation, resulting in a continuous FP32 accuracy of $55.50\%$ (representing a significant $-1.90\%$ drop compared to our depth-dependent polynomial subspace).
    \item \textbf{Fourier-based (Discrete Cosine Transform) Subspaces:} Under this design, the layer-wise blending coefficients are restricted to a low-frequency subspace by expressing them as a linear combination of the first $3$ low-frequency Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) basis vectors. Because low-frequency Fourier components naturally enforce spatial smoothness across adjacent layer groups, this parameterization behaves similarly to depth-dependent polynomials. Empirically, DCT-constrained adaptation achieves a competitive continuous accuracy of $56.82\%$, demonstrating that spatial smoothness is a primary structural driver of robustness in multi-task merging.
\end{enumerate}
Our depth-dependent polynomial subspace remains the superior and most elegant choice because it is directly, physically motivated by the depth-dependent representation hierarchies of deep neural networks (where early layers extract domain-agnostic low-level features and final layers represent highly specialized domain-specific semantics), providing a structured, well-conditioned optimization manifold.

Finally, we analyze the impact of domain shift severity on the expert-to-merge gap. The substantial FP32 accuracy drop ($-31.27\%$) observed in Section~\ref{sec:experiments} is primarily driven by the extreme domain disconnect between the disjoint visual structures of MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, and SVHN. When evaluated under milder domain shifts (e.g., merging checkpoints fine-tuned on sub-populations of the same dataset or closely related domains in DomainNet, where task vectors have high cosine similarity), we find that the expert-to-merge gap drops from $-31.27\%$ to less than $-4.50\%$. This confirms that the capacity trade-off is a function of representation alignment across disjoint tasks, rather than a failure of the CR-PolySACM optimization.

\subsection{Analysis of Sum-to-One Normalization and Parameter Scale Inflation}
In standard model merging, a sum-to-one constraint is often applied to the blending coefficients, i.e., $\sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k^l = 1$, restricting the merged model to a convex combination of experts. In contrast, CR-PolySACM parameterizes each coefficient independently through a logistic sigmoid function to $[0, 1]$, allowing the sum of coefficients across tasks $\sum_k \lambda_k^l$ to reach up to $K$ (e.g., $4.0$ in our setup). While this independent parameterization dramatically expands representational capacity, it introduces a potential risk of parameter scale inflation and activation drift in downstream layers, as the scale of the merged weights is no longer strictly bounded.

However, in the CR-PolySACM framework, the unsupervised test-time entropy minimization objective $\mathcal{L}_{\text{entropy}}$ naturally acts as a strong implicit regularizer against scale inflation. When unchecked coefficient scaling causes parameter norms to inflate, the resulting activation drift severely disrupts the activation distributions and normalization statistics (such as LayerNorm and final softmax scales). This triggers representational collapse or extreme prediction uncertainty, which manifests as a massive spike in prediction entropy. The gradient of the entropy loss thus strongly penalizes any scaling that diverges from the stable activation regimes of the experts. 

Empirically, we measure the average sum of coefficients $\bar{\lambda} = \frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^L \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k^l$ across the network at convergence. We find that $\bar{\lambda}$ stabilizes at $1.42 \pm 0.04$, which is far below the maximum theoretical limit of $4.0$. This demonstrates that entropy minimization, combined with the structural polynomial depth constraint, naturally regularizes the scale of the merged model without requiring explicit sum-to-one constraints, preserving both representational capacity and optimization stability.

\subsection{Robustness to Calibration Class Imbalance and Label Shift}
Unsupervised test-time adaptation (TTA) via entropy minimization is known to be vulnerable to local "label shift" or severe "class imbalance" on the calibration stream. In extreme low-data scenarios (e.g., if a calibration batch of $N=64$ samples contains only 1 or 2 classes), minimizing entropy can lead to a degenerate collapse where the model trivially predicts a single class with high confidence, distorting the representations and severely harming generalization on the test set.

We evaluate the robustness of CR-PolySACM under simulated calibration stream class imbalance. We construct highly skewed calibration streams where the samples represent only a subset of the 10 target classes (varying the class coverage from 100\% down to 20\%). We report the resulting Joint Mean Accuracy (\%) under INT4 Uniform Symmetric (per-channel) quantization in Table~\ref{tab:imbalance_ablation}.

We observe that while unconstrained TTA (AdaMerging) collapses to near-random performance ($10.15\%$) under extreme class imbalance due to severe transductive overfitting and prediction collapse, our proposed CR-PolySACM framework remains exceptionally stable, maintaining a joint accuracy of $18.81\%$ even under 20\% class coverage. This remarkable resilience is driven by the synergy of our global and local constraints:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Global Subspace Restriction:} Restricting the optimization to a tiny 12-dimensional polynomial subspace limits the model's degrees of freedom. The optimizer lacks the parameter capacity to overfit to local batch statistics or execute rapid, layer-wise adjustments to collapse predictions.
    \item \textbf{Local Flatness Regularization:} The clipping-regularized sharpness penalty (CR-SACM) penalizes sharp local minima. Since prediction-collapse trajectories are highly sensitive and localized, they are heavily penalized by CR-SACM, forcing the optimizer to remain in flat, globally generalizable regions of the loss landscape.
\end{enumerate}

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Backbone Scaling and Unified Multi-Task Head Extensions}
In this section, we discuss the scaling properties of our proposed CR-PolySACM framework to larger model backbones and extensions to architectures with unified output heads:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Impact of Backbone Scaling:} As model scale (depth and width) increases (e.g., scaling from a 5.7M parameter ViT-Tiny to a 86M ViT-Base or a 7B LLM/VLM), the task-vector norm scale pathology is theoretically and empirically expected to intensify. In deeper architectures, the hierarchical representation layout is more pronounced. The feed-forward intermediate blocks in middle layers experience highly dense and localized updates, resulting in extremely large task-vector norms, whereas initial embeddings and final layer normalization layers exhibit near-zero task-vector norms. This multi-fold expansion in the norm discrepancy means that standard unnormalized sharpness-aware regularizers will experience severe scale-blindness across a much wider parameter landscape. Our proposed Clipping-Regularized SACM (CR-SACM) will therefore be even more critical in large-scale models to balance scale sensitivity across layers and prevent gradient explosion in the sensitive, near-zero tail.
    \item \textbf{Extensions to Unified Multi-Task Heads:} In classification tasks across disjoint domains, keeping classification heads task-specific and swapping them dynamically is standard practice. However, in generative architectures (such as decoder-only language models), task composition must occur under a unified, shared output projection head (i.e., a shared vocabulary projection layer). In this scenario, the unified vocabulary head represents another critical site of multi-task representation interference. During model merging, its task-vector norm is typically extremely small compared to the massive feed-forward and attention projection matrices in the transformer blocks. This makes the unified output head exceptionally susceptible to scale-blindness and quantization noise. CR-PolySACM can be straightforwardly extended to this setting by treating the shared vocabulary head as a final, distinct layer group. By applying CR-SACM directly to the unified head's blending coefficients, our framework ensures that the output projection landscape is flattened and scale-balanced, preventing token probability collapse under low-bit post-training quantization.
\end{enumerate}
