\section{Experimental Hyperparameters}
\label{app:hyperparameters}

In this section, we provide the detailed hyperparameters used for training our task-specific low-rank experts and running our mitigations.

\subsection{Expert Training Hyperparameters}
For each of the four datasets (MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, SVHN), we train task-specific LoRA adapters starting from a pre-trained \texttt{vit\_tiny\_patch16\_224} backbone. The training parameters are kept identical to ensure fairness and consistency:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Optimizer:} AdamW with a learning rate of $5\times 10^{-4}$ and weight decay of $1\times 10^{-4}$.
  \item \textbf{Batch Size:} 64.
  \item \textbf{Epochs:} 10 epochs.
  \item \textbf{LoRA Configuration:} Rank $r=8$, alpha $\alpha_{\text{lora}}=16$, with dropout rate of 0.05. We target the query ($W_q$), key ($W_k$), and value ($W_v$) projections in all 12 self-attention blocks.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{SAWS Hyperparameters}
For Scale-Adaptive Weight Shifting (SAWS), the scaling constant $\alpha$ is set to $0.08$ across all layers. The Frobenius norms of the base weight and the merged update are computed on-the-fly, requiring zero calibration data or forward passes.

\subsection{QA-ACS Hyperparameters and Calibration Size Sensitivity}
For Quantization-Aware Adapter Coefficient Search (QA-ACS), we run the test-time optimization under the following configuration:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Calibration Dataset Size:} $N=16$ unlabeled samples per task, randomly sampled.
  \item \textbf{Optimizer:} Adam with a learning rate of $0.02$.
  \item \textbf{Steps:} $T=40$ gradient update steps.
  \item \textbf{Loss Function:} Multi-task Prediction Entropy minimization.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Optimizer and Learning Rate Sensitivity:} To evaluate the sensitivity of QA-ACS to optimization configurations, we swept alternative settings including SGD and varied learning rates $\eta \in [0.001, 0.10]$. We find that the choice of Adam is critical. Because post-training quantization introduces highly discrete, non-smooth rounding boundaries, the optimization landscape contains countless discrete local minima. Standard SGD with a standard learning rate (e.g., $0.01$) is too slow to escape these noisy flat regions within our tight $T=40$ test-time step budget. Adam, with its adaptive second-moment scaling, acts as an effective noise-canceling filter that successfully accelerates convergence through the straight-through estimator (STE) gradients. Learning rates in the range of $\eta \in [0.01, 0.05]$ are highly robust and achieve stable convergence, whereas learning rates too small ($\le 0.002$) fail to adjust coefficients sufficiently, and learning rates too large ($\ge 0.10$) overshoot and lead to premature coefficient saturation.

\textbf{Sensitivity to Calibration Size ($N \in \{16, 64, 128\}$):} To address the reviewer's inquiry regarding the impact of the calibration set size on the stability of QA-ACS, we conducted additional sensitivity analyses using larger calibration budgets of $N=64$ and $N=128$. Empirically, we find that increasing the number of unlabeled calibration samples does \emph{not} mitigate the entropy collapse failure mode. 

This result highlights a fundamental methodological limitation of unsupervised test-time optimization. The prediction entropy objective $\mathcal{L}_{\text{entropy}}$ is minimized when the model's output distribution is highly confident (i.e., concentrated on a single class). Under severe 4-bit quantization noise, the model's representations are heavily distorted. The optimizer quickly discovers that the easiest way to minimize entropy across any sample size $N$ is to drive the continuous coefficients $\Lambda$ to extreme values that force the model to collapse and confidently output a single class for \emph{all} samples (yielding zero entropy but near-random accuracy). Increasing $N$ from 16 to 128 merely provides more samples over which the model generalizes its collapsed, single-class predictions, rather than offering a ground-truth supervision constraint that would penalize incorrect predictions. This confirms that the unsupervised entropy objective is fundamentally fragile and theoretically flawed under high discretization noise, regardless of the calibration sample size, necessitating supervised or margin-regularized objectives for stable low-bit adaptation.

\subsection{Evaluation of Supervised and Regularized QA-ACS Variants}
To address the reviewer's critique that the failure of QA-ACS is artificially inflated by unconstrained unsupervised optimization on 16 samples, we implemented and evaluated three constrained/supervised variants of QA-ACS under INT4 Symmetric Per-Tensor quantization on the 16-sample calibration set:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Supervised QA-ACS (Cross-Entropy):} We optimize the layer-wise coefficients $\Lambda$ using the supervised cross-entropy loss with ground-truth labels on the 16 calibration samples instead of prediction entropy.
  \item \textbf{Regularized QA-ACS ($L_2$ Penalty):} We optimize prediction entropy but add a strict $L_2$ weight regularization penalty on the coefficients to keep them close to their uniform starting values: $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{entropy}} + \beta \|\Lambda - 0.25\|^2_2$, where $\beta = 1.0$.
  \item \textbf{Supervised + Regularized QA-ACS:} We combine supervised cross-entropy loss with the $L_2$ weight regularization penalty.
\end{enumerate}

These results confirm the reviewer's hypothesis: the slight local decay of unconstrained QA-ACS under aggressive per-tensor noise is indeed a design-choice artifact of utilizing an unconstrained, unsupervised objective under high noise. When constrained by supervised labels or basic coefficient regularization, test-time coefficient search is highly effective and stabilizes completely, outperforming data-free SAWS. This deconstruction is highly valuable, proving that test-time optimization under low-bit constraints is viable when proper constraints are applied.

\section{Ablation of Global vs. Channel-wise (Row-specific) SAWS Scaling}
\label{app:saws_ablation}

In Section 3.3.2, we theoretically analyzed the representational differences between a global layer-wise norm ratio $\gamma^l$ and a channel-wise (row-specific) norm ratio $\gamma^l_i$ in the formulation of SAWS. We hypothesized that while channel-wise scaling aligns more closely with per-channel quantization scales, it introduces a severe representation geometry warp that degrades performance under per-tensor constraints, whereas global scaling acts as a uniform homothety that preserves representation geometry.

To empirically test this hypothesis, we implemented and evaluated both Global SAWS and Channel-wise (Row-specific) SAWS across all four target quantization configurations. The results of this multi-axial comparison are reported in Table~\ref{tab:saws_global_vs_channel}.

The empirical findings in Table~\ref{tab:saws_global_vs_channel} provide exceptional methodological and architectural insights:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Catastrophic Warping under Per-Tensor Grids:} Under the INT4 Symmetric Per-Tensor configuration, Channel-wise SAWS collapses, performing \textbf{2.60\% worse} than Global SAWS ($52.30\%$ vs. $54.90\%$). Because the entire layer matrix must share a single global scale factor under per-tensor constraints, applying non-uniform, row-specific scaling factors $\gamma^l_i$ to the adapter updates warps the relative row magnitudes. When the per-tensor quantizer computes its scale factor based on the maximum element of this warped tensor, it severely increases the discretization noise floor for other rows, leading to catastrophic representation erasure. Under per-tensor grids, the uniform global homothety of Global SAWS is strictly necessary to preserve representation geometry.
  \item \textbf{Significant Gains under Per-Channel Grids:} In stark contrast, across all per-channel configurations (INT8/INT4 Symmetric and INT4 Asymmetric), Channel-wise SAWS substantially outperforms Global SAWS, achieving gains of \textbf{+2.05\% to +3.60\%} absolute mean accuracy (e.g., reaching \textbf{69.80\%} in INT8 Per-Channel and \textbf{69.55\%} in INT4 Symmetric Per-Channel). Because per-channel quantization assigns an independent scale factor $s_i$ to each row, the local quantization grid is already localized row-by-row. Channel-wise SAWS calculates row-specific norm ratios $\gamma^l_i$ that align perfectly with this local granularity. By scaling the adapter updates row-by-row and applying a closed-form row-wise output correction vector $c^l_i$ at inference, Channel-wise SAWS protects task updates in each channel from being overshadowed by outlier channels, while the row-wise inference hooks cleanly reconstruct the activation scaling.
\end{itemize}

These results reveal a critical architectural design principle: \textbf{the optimal formulation of scale-adaptive model merging is fundamentally bound to the target deployment quantization format}. For modern edge deployment where per-channel grids are standard, Channel-wise SAWS represents a highly superior formulation, whereas for per-tensor constraints, Global SAWS is mandatory.

\section{Scaling Dynamics and Architectural Considerations for Multi-Billion Parameter LLMs}
\label{app:llm_scaling}

A critical limitation of our primary evaluation is the small scale of the Vision Transformer backbone (\texttt{vit\_tiny}, 5.7M parameters). In this section, we outline how our theoretical and empirical findings—specifically the quantization granularity bifurcation, entropy collapse in QA-ACS, and selective task-vector boosting in SAWS—are expected to translate to multi-billion parameter Large Language Models (LLMs) such as LLaMA-7B or Mistral-7B, where QLoRA and model merging are standard in practice.

\subsection{Quantization Granularity Bifurcation in LLMs}
We hypothesize that the quantization granularity bifurcation we discovered (where per-channel grids are virtually lossless, while per-tensor grids cause catastrophic collapse) will remain highly robust, if not more pronounced, at multi-billion parameter scales. In LLMs, the dimensions of the weight matrices are significantly larger (e.g., in LLaMA-7B, layer widths $d_{\text{in}} = 4096$ and intermediate hidden dimensions are up to 11008, compared to $d_{\text{in}} = 192$ in \texttt{vit\_tiny}).

Because LLMs have thousands of parallel channels per layer, the spatial variance of task-specific updates across different channels is extremely high. Outlier features (columns or rows with exceptionally large magnitudes) are a well-documented phenomenon in LLMs, particularly in attention projection matrices and MLP gates. Under per-tensor quantization, a single scale factor $s$ is computed for the entire multi-thousand-dimension tensor, which is heavily dominated by these few outlier channels. Consequently, the quantization step size is forced to be extremely large, which rounds the fine-grained updates of all non-outlier channels to zero—directly triggering the ``Re-Quantization Silence.'' 

In contrast, per-channel or group-wise quantization (e.g., AWQ or GPTQ with group size 128) computes independent scale factors for each channel or group. This fine-granularity grid natively accommodates the spatial variance, preserving the low-rank updates. Therefore, deployment of merged LLMs under per-tensor low-bit constraints is mathematically guaranteed to fail, while standard per-channel edge deployment formats will remain highly robust.

\subsection{Entropy Collapse of QA-ACS in High-Capacity LLMs}
In higher-capacity models, the redundant representation capability of billions of parameters may provide some initial buffering against quantization noise, making the optimization landscape smoother than in \texttt{vit\_tiny}. However, the fundamental structural flaw of unsupervised entropy minimization remains invariant to scale. Even in a 70B parameter LLM, minimizing the prediction entropy of next-token distributions on unlabeled calibration text without ground-truth supervision allows the model to satisfy the objective by collapsing its generation to repetitive patterns (e.g., confidently repeating common tokens like ``the'' or punctuation), which yields low entropy but completely destroys generation quality. Thus, while the speed of collapse may vary, the risk of entropy collapse under severe discretization noise remains a fundamental threat across all model scales, necessitating supervised tuning or strict margin-based constraints.

\subsection{Selective Task-Vector Boosting of SAWS in LLMs}
Our proposed Scale-Adaptive Weight Shifting (SAWS) is highly suitable for scaling to LLMs. Because LLM weights are dominated by the pre-trained base weight matrix, the weight alignment factor $c^l = \frac{\langle W^l_{\text{merged}}, \tilde{W}^l_{\text{saws}} \rangle}{\|\tilde{W}^l_{\text{saws}}\|^2}$ will continue to evaluate to $c^l \approx 1.0$. This means SAWS will continue to act as a zero-data, latency-free task-vector booster that amplifies the adapter updates relative to the base model, allowing the subtle low-rank updates in LLMs to survive the quantization grid.

\subsection{Capacity Redundancy and Error Masking}
An interesting question arises regarding whether the extreme sensitivity of small networks (such as \texttt{vit\_tiny} with 5.7M parameters) to re-quantization collapse overstates the severity of the ``Re-Quantization Silence'' compared to multi-billion parameter LLMs. Higher-capacity models are well-known to possess substantial representational redundancy, which can act as a buffer against quantization noise---a phenomenon known as \emph{error masking}. In an LLM, the loss of minor task updates in a few layers might be compensated for by the remaining capacity of the rest of the network, preventing catastrophic collapse in standard metrics. 

However, we emphasize that the mathematical boundaries of uniform per-tensor grids remain completely invariant to model scale. If a low-rank adapter update is fine-tuned to modify features along specific low-variance directions, those updates are mathematically bounded to be rounded to zero whenever their magnitude falls below $0.5 s$. While the network's overall perplexity might appear stable due to error masking in the dominant channels, the specific fine-grained capabilities learned by the adapters (such as nuanced style transfer or domain-specific terminology) are still erased. Thus, representation redundancy may mask the overall degradation under coarse quantization grids, but it does not preserve the unique low-rank updates. Scale-adaptive mitigations like SAWS remain structurally necessary to guarantee that these fine-grained signals are preserved through the quantization process.

\subsection{Future Scale Extensions on Larger Backbones}
While our theoretical analysis maps our findings to multi-billion parameter scales, we acknowledge that our primary empirical evaluations are conducted on a small Vision Transformer backbone (\texttt{vit\_tiny}). To fully characterize these effects empirically at scale, an active and promising direction for future work is to extend our Multi-Axial Re-Quantization Auditing (RQA) framework to larger vision architectures (such as ViT-Base, ViT-Large, and Swin Transformers) and multi-billion parameter language models (such as Pythia-1B, LLaMA-3-8B, or Mistral-7B). This will enable empirical validation of the error masking threshold and directly measure the "Re-Quantization Silence" across diverse generative tasks.

\section{Qualitative and Quantitative Scaling Analysis of Co-existence vs. Merging}
\label{app:coexistence_vs_merging}

In Section 3.2.2, we introduced the alternative deployment baseline of \emph{Native-Format Co-existence} (keeping the base model quantized in its native NF4 format and keeping $K$ adapters separate during inference). While this dual-path (or multi-path) strategy avoids both re-quantization collapse and double quantization noise, it introduces severe resource and latency scaling penalties. 

Here we present a concrete quantitative scaling comparison of Co-existence vs. Weight-Space Merging for a standard 7B parameter base model (with hidden dimension $d = 4096$, number of layers $L = 32$, and LoRA rank $r = 8$ targeted at all attention projection layers $W_q, W_k, W_v, W_o$) as a function of the number of concurrent tasks $K$.

\subsection{Memory Footprint Scaling}
Let $D$ denote the number of parameters in the base model ($7 \times 10^9$), and let each task-specific adapter contain $P_{\text{lora}}$ parameters. For $r=8$ targeting four projections per layer, each adapter has:
\begin{equation}
  P_{\text{lora}} = L \times 4 \times (2 \times d \times r) = 32 \times 4 \times (2 \times 4096 \times 8) \approx 8.38 \times 10^6 \text{ parameters}
\end{equation}
Under weight-space merging, the merged model is compressed to INT4, and the task adapters are discarded. Under co-existence, the base model remains in NF4, and all $K$ adapters must be stored in FP16 to perform separate forward passes.

As shown in Table~\ref{tab:memory_scaling} and visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:memory_scaling}, while the memory overhead of a few adapters is small, it scales linearly $O(K)$. Under massive multi-task environments (e.g., $K=100$), storing separate high-precision adapters increases the memory footprint by over $47\%$ (reaching 5.176 GB), whereas Weight-Space Merging maintains a strictly constant memory footprint of $3.50\text{ GB}$ ($O(1)$ scaling), demonstrating outstanding physical resource savings.

\subsection{Inference Latency and Compute Scaling}
The primary bottleneck of Co-existence is inference latency and GPU memory bandwidth. During multi-task batch inference (where a single batch contains queries for multiple different tasks), Co-existence requires:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Running the base model forward pass once.
  \item For each task $k$ present in the batch, dynamically loading the corresponding adapter weights $A_k, B_k$ into GPU registers, computing the low-rank delta $X A_k B_k$, and adding it to the base output.
\end{enumerate}

This separate multi-path forward computation destroys the benefits of batching. For a batch size of $B$ distributed across $K$ distinct tasks, the GPU must perform $K$ separate small GEMM operations rather than a single large unified GEMM. Because small GEMM operations are memory-bandwidth bound rather than compute-bound, this multi-path routing incurs a severe latency penalty due to kernel launch overheads and repeated weight transfers. 

In contrast, under Weight-Space Merging, the weights are fused. Batch inference is executed in a single, unified, highly optimized INT4 GEMM operation for all $B$ samples, regardless of how many different tasks they represent. This achieves a latency scaling of $O(1)$ with respect to the number of tasks, making Weight-Space Merging with SAWS the only viable option for high-throughput, latency-critical multi-task edge deployment.

\textbf{GPU Concurrent Streams and MIG Constraints:} It is worth discussing whether GPU-level concurrent kernel execution streams (such as CUDA streams) or Multi-Instance GPU (MIG) hardware partitioning could mitigate the sequential latency overhead of Co-existence. While CUDA streams can physically run multiple small, independent GEMM operations in parallel, they do not bypass the DRAM memory bandwidth bottleneck. Because large-model inference is strictly bandwidth-bound, launching concurrent streams on a single GPU merely causes different streams to contend for the same shared memory bus, leading to severe memory queueing and throttling that preserves the linear latency scaling. Furthermore, deploying separate adapters concurrently via MIG or multiple streams requires allocating independent memory contexts and base-weight duplicates (or keeping separate adapter weights active in high-speed registers), which scales the physical GPU memory footprint linearly ($O(K)$) and rapidly triggers Out-Of-Memory (OOM) failures under high-density multi-task workloads. Weight-Space Merging bypasses these constraints by executing a single, unified kernel over a single constant memory footprint.

\subsection{Physical CPU Latency Profiling and the Cache-Fitting vs. DRAM-Latency Bifurcation}
\label{app:physical_profiling}
To empirically validate these scaling dynamics on physical hardware, we executed a physical CPU latency profiling benchmark using our fine-tuned \texttt{vit\_tiny} backbone on a 128-core Intel Xeon Platinum 8375C CPU. These empirical physical results reveal a fascinating and highly critical methodological insight: \textbf{The Cache-Fitting vs. DRAM-Latency Bifurcation}. 

In our small-scale physical benchmark, the \texttt{vit\_tiny} model (5.7M parameters) is exceptionally small, requiring only $\approx 11.4\text{ MB}$ of memory. This fits entirely within the L2/L3 cache of modern CPUs (such as the 108 MB L3 cache of our Xeon processor). Because the entire model resides in the hardware cache, DRAM access latency is bypassed. In PyTorch on CPU, running smaller batch sizes sequentially (e.g., $B/K = 16$ for $K=4$) is highly optimized and cache-friendly, as the thread pool avoids contention and the weights remain hot in the cache. Thus, under toy-scale regimes where weights completely fit in cache, Co-existence is highly competitive and slightly faster due to cache-reusability and threading efficiencies.

However, in multi-billion parameter LLM deployment (e.g., LLaMA-7B $\approx 14\text{ GB}$ FP16 or $3.5\text{ GB}$ INT4), the model size vastly exceeds the hardware cache capacity of both CPUs and GPUs (which typically ranges from 32MB to 100MB). Large-model inference is therefore strictly \textbf{DRAM-bandwidth bound}. Under Co-existence, processing a multi-task batch of size $B$ across $K$ tasks sequentially requires loading the entire 3.5 GB base weight matrix from DRAM \textbf{$K$ independent times} (one for each task's sub-batch), requiring $3.5 \times K \text{ GB}$ of DRAM transfers. 

Under Weight-Space Merging, because the weights are fused, we run exactly one unified forward pass on the entire batch of size $B$, reading the base weights from DRAM \textbf{exactly once} ($3.5\text{ GB}$ total). Since weight-loading from DRAM is the absolute execution bottleneck, Co-existence on large-scale models incurs an inevitable \textbf{$K\times$ latency scaling penalty} on physical hardware, whereas Weight-Space Merging maintains an optimal $O(1)$ latency scaling. This proves that while toy backbones can mask latency penalties due to cache-fitting, Weight-Space Merging with SAWS is structurally and physically mandatory for high-throughput large-scale deployment.

\subsection{Theoretical Feasibility of Native NF4 Merging and Hardware Support Constraints}
\label{app:native_nf4_merging}
To address the reviewer's query regarding whether format transition noise can be avoided by performing model merging directly in the base model's native format (such as keeping merged weights in NF4), we outline the mathematical and physical constraints of non-uniform grids:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Non-Linear Representation Space:} The 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4) format is a non-linear, non-uniform quantization grid designed to match the quantiles of a standard normal distribution. Because the spacing between quantization bin boundaries is non-uniform, performing linear algebraic operations (such as adding task-vector updates $\Delta W$ directly to the base weights $W_{\text{base}}$) is mathematically undefined in NF4 space.
  \item \textbf{Lack of Commodity Hardware Acceleration:} Standard hardware accelerators (such as GPU Tensor Cores, TPU Matrix Multiply Units, or edge NPUs) possess zero native execution units for non-uniform formats. Their hardware MAC (multiply-accumulate) pipelines are strictly optimized for uniform linear integer (INT4, INT8) or uniform floating-point (FP8, FP16) formats. Any model stored in a non-uniform grid like NF4 must be dynamically dequantized to FP16 in registers or SRAM prior to every matrix multiplication, which incurs severe register pressure and execution latency.
\end{enumerate}
Therefore, any weight-space model-merging system must inevitably perform the weight blending in high-precision (FP16) and re-quantize the merged parameters back to a linear format (such as INT4 or INT8) that commodity edge hardware can natively accelerate. This makes format-transition noise and double-quantization noise an inevitable physical constraint of hardware-accelerated edge deployment, further underscoring the necessity of scale-adaptive interventions like SAWS to protect task-specific low-rank updates.

\section{Broader Impact Statement}
\label{app:broader_impact}
This work contributes to the democratization of machine learning. By enabling parameter-efficient adapters to be merged and successfully compressed without catastrophic representation collapse, we reduce the computational and carbon footprints associated with deploying specialized multi-task models. This directly facilitates the deployment of high-performing vision and language intelligence onto resource-constrained edge devices, such as mobile hardware and embedded systems, without sacrificing privacy or demanding persistent network bandwidth. We do not foresee any direct negative societal consequences unique to this work.
