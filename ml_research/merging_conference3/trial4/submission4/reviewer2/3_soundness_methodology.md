# Evaluation Phase 3: Soundness and Methodology Evaluation

## 1. Description Clarity and Reproducibility
* **Clarity:** The description of the methodology (Section 3) is mathematically precise and clear. The forward DCT-II and inverse IDCT-III transformations are well-formulated, and the boundary condition derivative proofs are elegantly presented. However, the writing style is overly verbose, repetitive, and heavily saturated with academic hype (e.g., over-explaining the virtual boundary derivative behavior multiple times across different sections).
* **Reproducible Details:** The authors provide concrete details such as the number of random seeds (30), sample sizes ($M \in \{5, 10, 20, 50\}$), specific baselines, and optimizer configurations (Adam, 30-150 steps). However, a critical reproducibility detail is missing: the **learning rates** used for optimizing the spectral coordinates vs. the spatial coordinate baselines. Because different coordinate spaces have different scales, the optimization speed and convergence are highly sensitive to the learning rate. Without specifying the learning rates, reproducing these exact optimization speeds is difficult.

## 2. Technical and Methodological Flaws (Critical Review)

### Critique A: Global Spectral Smoothing across Extreme Architectural Heterogeneity
The core formulation of SpectralMerge treats the sequence of layer coefficients $\vec{\alpha}_k \in \mathbb{R}^L$ as a single, continuous 1D spatial signal across network depth. 
* **The Flaw:** For modern architectures like ResNet-18 or Vision Transformers, layers are highly heterogeneous. ResNet-18 contains initial convolutional layers, block-wise convolutional filters (with different kernel sizes and channel depths), downsampling convolutional layers, batch normalization layers (with running means, variances, and affine parameters), and a final fully connected classification head. These layers serve fundamentally different mathematical operations and operate on completely different parameter distributions and scales.
* **The Contradiction:** While the authors mathematically formulate "Block-wise and Layer-type Spectral Merging" in Section 3.5 to address this architectural heterogeneity, they only evaluate it on a simple synthetic MLP. In their real-world pre-trained ResNet-18 evaluations (Section 4.6), **they optimized all 18 layers globally under a single 1D DCT-II**. This directly contradicts their own architectural arguments! Forcing convolutional filters, batch normalization parameters, and the classification head to lie on a single continuous, smooth trigonometric curve is physically and architecturally nonsensical. Doing a global spectral smoothing across these highly disjoint components is a major methodological flaw.

### Critique B: Exaggerated Vandermonde Ill-Conditioning Arguments
The authors repeatedly criticize PolyMerge's polynomial parameterization, claiming that its standard power-series basis is highly collinear and leads to an extremely ill-conditioned Vandermonde matrix, which represents a "catastrophic bottleneck at scale."
* **The Flaw:** While a Vandermonde matrix is indeed ill-conditioned for high degrees $d$, PolyMerge typically operates at very low degrees (such as $d=2$ or $d=3$). For $L=12$ or $18$ layers, the condition number of a $L \times 3$ Vandermonde matrix with normalized depth inputs in $[0, 1]$ is very small (typically $< 100$). This level of mild collinearity is trivial for modern first-order optimizers like Adam with momentum, which easily bypasses such Hessian distortions.
* **The Mathematical Overstatement:** The authors state that "optimizing the polynomial weights involves solving a system of equations governed by the Vandermonde matrix." This is mathematically incorrect for model merging. In PolyMerge, we do NOT solve a system of linear equations or invert any matrices. The polynomial coefficients are updated using standard first-order backpropagation (gradient descent) based on the validation loss. The Vandermonde matrix only acts as a forward transformation matrix. Thus, claiming that this ill-conditioning causes "derivative-free simplex search to fail completely" or acts as a "catastrophic bottleneck" is an extreme exaggeration designed to inflate the benefits of the DCT-II's orthonormality.

### Critique C: Major Baseline Omission — Direct Spatial Smoothness Regularization
To prevent high-frequency oscillations and smooth the trajectory of merging coefficients, one does not need to transition to the frequency domain. 
* **The Omission:** The most standard, simple, and direct approach in signal processing and trajectory optimization is to add a spatial smoothness penalty (first-order or second-order finite differences) directly to the validation loss objective:
  $$\mathcal{R}_{spatial}(\Theta_{spatial}) = \gamma \sum_{k=1}^K \sum_{l=2}^L \bigl( \alpha_k(l) - \alpha_k(l-1) \bigr)^2$$
* **Why this is a critical gap:** A spatial difference penalty acts as an implicit low-pass filter, forcing adjacent layers to have similar coefficients, and is trivial to implement in the spatial coordinate space. This simple baseline is completely omitted. The authors compare SpectralMerge against PolyMerge (polynomial trajectories) and unconstrained spatial search, but fail to compare it against direct spatial smoothness regularization. Without this baseline, the paper fails to prove that optimizing in the frequency domain is fundamentally superior to simply regularizing the spatial trajectory in the spatial domain.
