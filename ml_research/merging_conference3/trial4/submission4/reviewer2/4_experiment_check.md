# Evaluation Phase 4: Experimental Evaluation and Baselines Check

## 1. Over-Reliance on Synthetic Simulation Landscapes
The paper presents extensive, multi-axial evaluations across standard and adversarial conditions (extreme label shift, bursty streams, small batch noise), sample complexity sweeps ($M \in [5, 50]$), and validation target selection bias sweeps.
* **The Critique:** **All** of these systematic, rigorous analyses are conducted **entirely** on the synthetic Vision Transformer (ViT-B/32) simulation landscape (Model II). This simulation is a highly simplified mathematical proxy (a calibrated quadratic sensitivity model) rather than a real, physical neural network. 
* **The Concern:** A quadratic simulation landscape is inherently smooth and convex. It completely fails to capture the highly non-convex, non-linear, and high-dimensional optimization dynamics of actual deep neural network weight merging. While the authors argue that this simulation allows them to decouple optimization from hardware/software noise, drawing sweeping methodological conclusions and claiming to "completely resolve" the *Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox* based primarily on a toy mathematical proxy is speculative and lacks empirical depth.

## 2. Weakness and Artificiality of the Physical Checkpoint Evaluation (CIFAR-10)
To validate the method on physical parameters, the authors construct a multi-task ResNet-18 merging setup on CIFAR-10.
* **Toy-Scale Protocol:** The task-specific experts are trained on a tiny, extremely data-scarce subset of only **120 samples per task** for just 4 epochs. The validation tuning set is restricted to **15 samples per task** ($M=15$). Sourcing a pre-trained ResNet-18 from PyTorch Hub to fine-tune on 120 samples on two artificial binary tasks (Vehicles vs. Animals) is a highly artificial, toy-scale setup that does not represent real-world model-merging scenarios.
* **Lack of Real-World Benchmarks:** In the established model-merging literature, competitive methods (such as AdaMerging, RegCalMerge, TIES-Merging, and PolyMerge) are evaluated on full-scale multi-task consolidation pools (e.g., merging 5 to 8 diverse vision classification models fine-tuned on full datasets like CIFAR-100, SVHN, RESISC45, etc., or merging Large Language Models on GLUE/MMLU benchmarks). By restricting their physical evaluations to a toy-scale ResNet-18 and a 12-layer MLP on synthetic data, the authors fail to demonstrate the scalability and practical utility of SpectralMerge in realistic, large-scale deep learning workflows.

## 3. Poor Absolute Performance of the Merged Model
* **Analysis of Table 5:** The pre-trained ResNet-18 expert for Task 0 achieves an individual accuracy of **86.00%**, and the expert for Task 1 achieves **65.00%**. When merged post-hoc:
  * The proposed **SpectralMerge-Reg ($\mu=1.0$)** achieves a multi-task test accuracy of **54.00%**.
  * While this is significantly better than unconstrained spatial search and PolyMerge (which catastrophically collapse to a majority-guessing baseline of $29.00\%$) and exceeds the Uniform baseline ($41.00\%$), the absolute performance is still extremely poor.
* **The Critique:** An average accuracy of $54.00\%$ on a joint set consisting of two binary classification tasks is barely above random guessing ($50.00\%$). It represents a massive performance drop from the original expert capabilities ($86.00\%$ and $65.00\%$). This indicates that while SpectralMerge-Reg is the best among the evaluated merging methods, the resulting consolidated model is still practically unusable. The authors' enthusiastic tone ("spectacular", "blowout improvement") glosses over the fact that post-hoc model merging under this protocol still suffers from catastrophic performance degradation, and SpectralMerge only acts as a stabilizer against validation collapse rather than a solution to task interference.

## 4. Complete Failure of Hard-Cutoff Variants on Physical Checkpoints
* **The Collapse:** In the ResNet-18 evaluation (Table 5), the hard-cutoff variant **SpectralMerge-LP ($F=3$)** and the dynamic **LP-Adaptive** scheduler collapse completely to **29.00%** (the same majority-class guessing state as the overfitted baselines).
* **The Explanation:** The authors explain that because only the final block (`layer4` and `fc`) was fine-tuned, a sharp "PEFT-induced step-function discontinuity" is introduced. Because step functions have infinite frequency support, the hard-cutoff low-pass filters catastrophically underfit, leading to complete optimization failure.
* **The Critique:** While this mathematical explanation is sound, it exposes a major vulnerability of their primary proposed method. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (such as fine-tuning only deep layers or classification heads) is the dominant paradigm in real-world deep learning due to resource constraints. If SpectralMerge-LP and LP-Adaptive fail completely under this standard protocol, their practical utility is severely limited. Additionally, the authors' claim in Section 4.5 that LP-Adaptive is "highly effective" on the physical MLP is directly contradicted by its complete failure ($29.00\%$) on the pre-trained ResNet-18, a crucial caveat that the authors downplay in their conclusions.
