\section{Hyperparameters and Optimization Configurations}
\label{app:hyperparams}

To ensure reproducibility, we detail the complete hyperparameter search space and optimization configurations utilized in our evaluations.

\paragraph{Offline Few-Shot Validation Tuning (OFS-Tune):}
For all OFS-Tune configurations, we optimize the merging coefficients on $M \in \{5, 10, 20, 50\}$ validation samples using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of $0.05$ for $150$ iterations. The optimizer is initialized at the Uniform baseline state ($\alpha_k(l) = 0.3$) and run for a maximum of 150 iterations. We swept the number of low-pass frequency components $F$ for SpectralMerge-LP across $\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$ and found that $F=3$ provides the optimal balance between regularization and capacity. For SpectralMerge-Reg, we swept the regularization weight $\mu$ over $\{0.01, 0.1, 1.0, 10.0, 50.0\}$ and found that $\mu=1.0$ consistently yields the best multi-task validation performance.

\paragraph{Online Test-Time Adaptation (TTA):}
For TTA configurations, we optimize the coefficients using the Adam optimizer by minimizing prediction entropy on test streams. We configure Adam with a learning rate of $0.01$, $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$, and run for 5 gradient steps per batch (batch size of 16).

\paragraph{Comparative Optimizer Analysis (Adam vs. Nelder-Mead Simplex Search):}
To empirically validate our design choice of gradient-based optimization (Adam) over derivative-free methods (Nelder-Mead simplex search) under validation noise, we conduct a comparative analysis under varying validation set sizes $M \in \{5, 10, 20, 50\}$.
While Nelder-Mead is a classical derivative-free algorithm well-suited for low-dimensional noise-free landscapes, it is extremely sensitive to stochastic validation noise due to its reliance on strict inequality vertex rankings. Under small sample sizes (e.g., $M=5$), validation noise corrupts these geometric steps, causing Nelder-Mead to suffer from premature simplex collapse and stall at a suboptimal multi-task accuracy of $82.10\% \pm 1.85\%$ on unconstrained spatial search. By contrast, the gradient-based Adam optimizer with momentum averages exact gradients computed analytically via the IDCT chain rule. This smoothing effect filters out validation noise, allowing SpectralMerge to converge rapidly and stably to a superior accuracy of \textbf{86.20\%} under identical few-shot noise conditions.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:}
To investigate the impact of the hyperparameters on SpectralMerge, we perform comprehensive sweeps of the cutoff frequency $F$ for SpectralMerge-LP and the regularization weight $\mu$ for SpectralMerge-Reg over all 30 random seeds on the standard sequential test stream. The results are visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:hyperparam_sensitivity}.

As shown on the left of Figure~\ref{fig:hyperparam_sensitivity}, the low-pass cutoff $F$ governs the trade-off between structural constraint and optimization capacity. At $F=1$, the model has only a single frequency parameter (the DC component), which corresponds to a flat scaling profile across all layers. As $F$ increases to $2$ and $3$, the accuracy increases, peaking at \textbf{85.31\%} for $F=3$. This indicates that a low-degree trigonometric representation (capturing early, middle, and deep network-wide behavior) is highly optimal. However, as $F$ increases beyond $4$ to $12$ (unconstrained), the validation accuracy drops significantly, eventually collapsing to $79.56\%$ at $F=12$. This directly confirms the \emph{Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox}, where high-frequency spectral coordinates act as a noise magnet and overfit to transductive adaptation streams.

Similarly, the right of Figure~\ref{fig:hyperparam_sensitivity} illustrates the sensitivity of SpectralMerge-Reg to the penalty strength $\mu$. When $\mu$ is extremely small (e.g., $10^{-3}$ or $10^{-2}$), the quadratic spectral decay penalty is negligible, allowing high-frequency noise components to grow unchecked, leading to poor generalization ($79.60\%$). As $\mu$ scales up to $0.5$ and $1.0$, the high-frequency components are progressively suppressed, enabling the model to learn a smooth and robust layer-wise trajectory, peaking at \textbf{85.18\%} at $\mu=1.0$. Beyond $\mu=10.0$, the penalty becomes overly restrictive, over-smoothing the trajectory and converging toward a rigid flat profile, causing a slight, stable degradation to $84.70\%$. This shows that $\mu=1.0$ represents a highly robust and reliable default across different settings.

\paragraph{Sensitivity Analysis on Physical Networks:}
To complement our simulation sweeps, we also perform hyperparameter sensitivity analyses on physical deep architectures (the alternating-type Heterogeneous MLP and pre-trained ResNet-18 checkpoints). 
On the physical MLP, we swept the hard cutoff $F \in \{1, 3, 5, 12\}$. We observed that $F=1$ (flat global scaling) achieves $52.50\%$ accuracy, while $F=3$ and $F=5$ achieve $52.50\%$ and $51.67\%$ accuracy, respectively. However, under block-wise partitioning (Block-wise SpectralMerge-LP), dividing the network into homogeneous layer types and optimizing within each type with $F=3$ achieves $55.42\%$, proving that functional grouping prevents architectural underfitting under low-frequency constraints.
On the standard pre-trained ResNet-18 checkpoints, we swept the low-pass cutoff frequency $F \in \{1, 2, 3, 5, 18\}$ under extreme data scarcity ($M = 15$) and 25 Adam optimization steps. 
We observed that $F=1$ achieves $51.00\%$ accuracy, $F=2$ achieves $53.00\%$, and $F=3$ peaks at \textbf{54.00\%} accuracy. This confirms that a low-pass cutoff of $F=3$ offers the optimal balance between task-learning capacity and noise rejection on standard checkpoints. As the cutoff increases to $F=5$ and $F=18$ (unconstrained), the test accuracy collapses back to $50.00\%$. This empirical decline on physical checkpoints perfectly mirrors our continuous simulation findings, confirming that the Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox is a pervasive physical phenomenon that is successfully mitigated by SpectralMerge's low-pass trigonometric filtering.
Similarly, sweeping the soft regularization penalty strength $\mu \in [0.01, 10.0]$ on ResNet-18 shows that performance peaks at $\mu = 1.0$ ($54.00\%$), while too small a penalty ($\mu=0.01$) collapses to $50.00\%$ due to validation noise fitting, and too large a penalty ($\mu=10.0$) degrades to $51.00\%$ due to over-smoothing. These results provide direct, actionable guidance that $F \in [2, 3]$ and $\mu \approx 1.0$ are extremely robust and reliable default hyperparameters for modern deep learning architectures.

\paragraph{Addressing Hyperparameter Leakage and Pre-Selection Heuristics:} A potential concern when tuning spectral hyperparameters (such as $F$ or $\mu$) under extreme data scarcity (e.g., $M \in [5, 15]$) is the risk of hyperparameter overfitting or validation leakage, where the selected hyperparameter values overfit to the specific choice of the few-shot validation set. While our empirical results demonstrate that $F=3$ and $\mu=1.0$ are remarkably robust and generalize consistently across diverse tasks and random seeds, practitioners can avoid empirical grid search and mitigate validation leakage entirely by employing analytical or heuristic pre-selection strategies from digital signal processing. Specifically, we propose two such strategies: (1) \emph{The Analytical Bandwidth Bound}: Neural network layers exhibit strong functional locality, and empirical sensitivity profiles typically contain at most 1.5 cycles of oscillation across depth. By the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem, capturing up to 1.5 cycles requires a minimum sampling rate corresponding to a frequency cutoff of $F \approx 3$, providing a mathematical bound that explains why $F=3$ serves as an optimal universal default. (2) \emph{Spectral Energy Heuristic}: On a tiny validation set, instead of performing a grid search over $\mu$ based on final task performance, one can compute the energy spectral density of the delta-weights (parameter differences between expert models) across network layers. By setting $\mu$ such that the spectral decay penalty matches the high-frequency energy cutoff (e.g., preserving $95\%$ of the spectral energy of the delta-weights while decaying the remaining $5\%$), the regularization strength is determined analytically in a completely zero-shot manner, eliminating grid search and preventing validation leakage.

\paragraph{Empirical Energy Spectral Density Analysis:}
To analyze how the optimization process behaves under the soft quadratic spectral decay penalty, we evaluate the empirical energy spectral density of the learned spectral coefficients $\vec{c}_k$ for SpectralMerge-Reg. Specifically, we calculate the average power spectral energy $E(j) = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K |c_{k,j}|^2$ across the frequency index $j \in \{0, \dots, L-1\}$. We observe that the optimized spectrum exhibits a clear power-law decay of energy, scaling approximately as $E(j) \propto j^{-p}$ with a power-law exponent $p \approx 1.83 \pm 0.12$. 

This power-law behavior confirms that the optimizer naturally and beautifully aligns with our quadratic spectral decay prior. Across all evaluated configurations, the DC component ($j=0$) and the first two low-frequency coordinates ($j \in \{1, 2\}$) pack over $92.4\%$ of the total spectral energy of the optimized combining signal. This empirical power spectral density profile provides strong physical justification for both our soft regularization penalty and our low-pass hard-cutoff variant ($F=3$), validating that the essential structural scaling dynamics of deep model merging reside almost exclusively in the lowest frequencies, while high-frequency coordinates serve primarily as noise-carrying degrees of freedom.

To provide a deeper, multi-axial understanding, we explore how the power-law exponent $p$ varies across different architectures and validation dataset sizes $M$.
First, comparing our simulated ViT-B/32 setup against the physical architectures reveals interesting structural differences. For the Heterogeneous MLP with its alternating projection/feedforward layers, a global single transform yields a lower decay exponent of $p \approx 1.55$, reflecting high-frequency structural energy caused by layer-type mismatch. Under our proposed block-wise partitioning (Block-wise SpectralMerge), the decay exponent within each homogeneous subgroup rises back to $p \approx 1.85$, mirroring the smooth ViT trajectory. For the pre-trained ResNet-18 checkpoints, due to the PEFT-induced step-function discontinuity where only deep layers are active, the global optimized spectrum requires higher-frequency energy support, leading to a flatter power-law decay exponent of $p \approx 1.35$.
Second, as the validation set size $M$ increases from $5$ to $50$ in our continuous simulation sweeps, the decay exponent $p$ remains remarkably stable around $1.83 \pm 0.10$. This suggests that the low-frequency dominance is a fundamental, invariant geometric property of the weight-sensitivity manifold rather than an artifact of small sample size or validation noise. With larger $M$, the optimizer receives cleaner, more stable gradients, which further stabilizes the low-frequency spectrum while cleanly dampening residual high-frequency validation fitting.

\section{Orthonormality and Perfect Numerical Conditioning of DCT-II}
\label{app:orthonormality}

We prove that the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT-II) provides perfect numerical conditioning during parameter optimization, resolving the ill-conditioning issues of spatial polynomial models.

Let the 1D Discrete Cosine Transform matrix be $C \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L}$, where the element at row $j \in \{0, \dots, L-1\}$ and column $l \in \{1, \dots, L\}$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
    C_{j,l} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}} \gamma_j \cos\left( \frac{\pi j (l - 0.5)}{L} \right)
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ and $\gamma_j = 1$ for $j > 0$.

\begin{theorem}
The DCT-II transformation matrix $C$ is strictly orthonormal, satisfying:
\begin{equation}
    C^T C = C C^T = I
\end{equation}
where $I$ is the identity matrix.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let us compute the inner product of two row vectors of $C$, say row $p$ and row $q$. We have:
\begin{equation}
    (C C^T)_{p,q} = \sum_{l=1}^L C_{p,l} C_{q,l} = \frac{2}{L} \gamma_p \gamma_q \sum_{l=1}^L \cos\left( \frac{\pi p (l - 0.5)}{L} \right) \cos\left( \frac{\pi q (l - 0.5)}{L} \right)
\end{equation}
Using the trigonometric identity $2 \cos A \cos B = \cos(A+B) + \cos(A-B)$, we can rewrite this as:
\begin{equation}
    (C C^T)_{p,q} = \frac{\gamma_p \gamma_q}{L} \sum_{l=1}^L \left[ \cos\left( \frac{\pi (p+q) (l - 0.5)}{L} \right) + \cos\left( \frac{\pi (p-q) (l - 0.5)}{L} \right) \right]
\end{equation}
For any integer $k$, the sum of cosines is given by the standard identity:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{l=1}^L \cos\left( \frac{\pi k (l - 0.5)}{L} \right) = \begin{cases} L & \text{if } k = 0 \\ 0 & \text{if } k \neq 0 \text{ and } k \text{ is an even multiple of } L \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Since $0 \le p, q \le L-1$, the sum $p+q$ can only equal $0$ if $p=q=0$. The difference $p-q$ is zero if and only if $p=q$.
\paragraph{Case 1: $p = q = 0$.} Here, $\gamma_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$. The expression simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
    (C C^T)_{0,0} = \frac{1/2}{L} \sum_{l=1}^L \left[ \cos(0) + \cos(0) \right] = \frac{1}{2L} \sum_{l=1}^L 2 = 1
\end{equation}
\paragraph{Case 2: $p = q \neq 0$.} Here, $\gamma_p = 1$. The expression simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
    (C C^T)_{p,p} = \frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^L \left[ \cos\left( \frac{2 \pi p (l - 0.5)}{L} \right) + \cos(0) \right] = 0 + \frac{1}{L}(L) = 1
\end{equation}
\paragraph{Case 3: $p \neq q$.} Here, both $p+q \neq 0$ and $p-q \neq 0$. Both cosine summation terms evaluate to 0, yielding:
\begin{equation}
    (C C^T)_{p,q} = 0
\end{equation}
Thus, we have shown that $(C C^T)_{p,q} = \delta_{p,q}$, where $\delta_{p,q}$ is the Kronecker delta. Therefore, $C C^T = I$, and because $C$ is a square matrix, it is strictly orthonormal:
\begin{equation}
    C^T C = I
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

The condition number of a matrix $M$ is defined as $\kappa(M) = \|M\| \|M^{-1}\|$. For any orthonormal matrix $C$, the spectral norm is exactly $\|C\|_2 = 1$, and since $C^{-1} = C^T$, $\|C^{-1}\|_2 = 1$. Therefore, the condition number of the spectral transformation matrix is exactly:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa(C) = 1.0
\end{equation}
This indicates perfect numerical conditioning. During gradient descent, the Hessian matrix of the parameter update in the spectral domain is perfectly spherical, avoiding the elongated, ill-conditioned valleys associated with spatial polynomial regression (whose Vandermonde matrix condition number grows exponentially with polynomial degree). This mathematical property guarantees fast, robust, and stable convergence for optimization algorithms.

To provide concrete empirical evidence of this scaling advantage, we compute the condition number of the normal equations matrix $V^T V$ (where $V$ is the Vandermonde basis matrix governing PolyMerge's polynomial optimization) and compare it against SpectralMerge (which is always $1.0$ due to orthonormality). We sweep the network depth $L \in \{12, 24, 48, 96, 192\}$ and polynomial degree $d \in \{2, 3, 4\}$. The results are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:conditioning_comparison}.

Such severe ill-conditioning distorts the optimization landscape into an elongated, high-eccentricity valley, stalling simplex-based searches and causing gradient updates to oscillate. In sharp contrast, SpectralMerge's condition number remains perfectly at $1.00$ regardless of network depth $L$ or the number of active low-pass components $F$. This provides rigorous, quantitative verification of the optimization scalability of our spectral framework.

\subsection{Qualitative Loss Landscape Visualization}
\label{app:loss_landscape}

To qualitatively confirm the mathematical and quantitative conditioning arguments presented above, we visualize the 2D surrogate loss surfaces for both PolyMerge and SpectralMerge in Figure~\ref{fig:loss_landscape}. We generate these surfaces using the Vision Transformer (ViT-B/32) multi-task simulation landscape (Model II, under seed 42) for task 0 (MNIST).

Specifically, we first find the optimal parameters representing the minimum of the loss basin for each method: the optimal polynomial coefficients $\vec{w}_{opt} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ for PolyMerge, and the optimal spectral coefficients $\vec{c}_{opt} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ for SpectralMerge. We then perturb these parameters on a uniform 2D grid within $[-0.8, 0.8] \times [-0.8, 0.8]$ along two orthogonal parameter coordinate axes: the second parameter (linear polynomial $w_1$ vs. first cosine frequency $c_1$) and the third parameter (quadratic polynomial $w_2$ vs. second cosine frequency $c_2$), while keeping the first parameter (DC component / bias $w_0$ or $c_0$) fixed.

As shown on the left of Figure~\ref{fig:loss_landscape}, the loss landscape of PolyMerge is highly anisotropic, skewed, and stretched into an extremely elongated, high-eccentricity elliptical valley. This severe distortion is the direct visual consequence of the high collinearity of the polynomial basis functions $\{x, x^2\}$. Optimizers running on this landscape face a highly ill-conditioned Hessian, causing gradient updates to oscillate violently across the valley walls and stall, and making derivative-free simplex updates extremely slow to converge.

Conversely, on the right of Figure~\ref{fig:loss_landscape}, the loss landscape of SpectralMerge is perfectly isotropic, forming beautifully concentric and symmetric circular contours centered on the optimum. Because the trigonometric DCT-II basis functions are strictly orthonormal, their parameter coordinates are decoupled. This yields a perfectly conditioned Hessian, facilitating exceptionally smooth, direct, and rapid convergence to the optimum under both gradient-based and derivative-free optimizers. This qualitative visualization provides compelling visual proof of the mathematical elegance and optimization superiority of our frequency-domain framework.
