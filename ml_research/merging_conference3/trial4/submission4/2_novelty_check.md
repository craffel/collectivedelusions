# Review Step 2: Novelty and Originality Check

## 1. Assessment of Conceptual Novelty
The paper presents an exceptionally high level of originality and conceptual novelty. While post-hoc model merging has become a highly active research field, existing literature has focused primarily on two paradigms:
1. Static parameter-space or sign-and-magnitude-based merging heuristics (e.g., TIES-Merging, DARE, Task Arithmetic) which rely on pre-defined heuristics and lack data-driven adaptability.
2. Parameterized layer-wise or block-wise merging optimized on small validation or test data (e.g., AdaMerging, RegCalMerge) which treat layer-wise combining coefficients as disjoint, independent parameters, making them highly susceptible to overfitting.

This paper breaks away from both paradigms by introducing a **frequency-domain formulation via the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT-II)**. Treating combining coefficients as a continuous 1D spatial signal across network depth and modeling its spectral distribution is a highly creative, elegant, and paradigm-shifting contribution to the model-merging literature.

## 2. Key Theoretical and Conceptual Contributions
The paper's conceptual novelty is supported by several highly insightful sub-contributions:

* **The Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox:** The authors formalize a crucial and common failure mode in parameterized merging—namely, that optimizing combining coefficients on very small data streams fits local, transductive validation noise. Rather than treating this simply as "overfitting," the authors conceptualize it as the optimization of high-frequency spatial noise. This conceptualization naturally motivates frequency-domain filtering.
* **Spectral-LP and Spectral-Reg Formulations:** The mathematical formulations of these two constraints are elegant and intuitive. The hard low-pass cutoff (LP) physically reduces the search-space dimensionality (e.g., from 48 parameters to 12 parameters) while the soft quadratic spectral decay penalty (Reg) scales progressively with frequency index squared ($\lambda_j = \mu \cdot j^2$). This soft decay is an extremely clean, continuous alternative to discrete band-filtering.
* **PEFT-Induced Step-Function Discontinuity:** This is a major, highly insightful theoretical contribution. The authors identify that when task-specific experts are trained using localized adaptation (such as PEFT or fine-tuning only the deep blocks like `layer4` in ResNet-18), the parameter sensitivity across depth forms a sharp step-function discontinuity. Because a step-function has infinite frequency support, a rigid low-pass cutoff (like SpectralMerge-LP) is mathematically incapable of representing it, resulting in underfitting. This explains why soft regularization (SpectralMerge-Reg) is required for PEFT-style models. This insight demonstrates a deep, fundamental understanding of both signal processing and modern transfer learning.
* **Vandermonde Ill-Conditioning and Polynomial Limitations:** The paper conducts a rigorous mathematical critique of PolyMerge (the closest spatial-smoothing baseline). It highlights that standard power-series polynomials suffer from severe collinearity, resulting in an ill-conditioned Vandermonde matrix whose condition number grows exponentially with polynomial degree. In contrast, because the DCT-II basis functions are strictly orthonormal on uniform grids, SpectralMerge achieves perfect numerical conditioning ($\kappa \approx 1.0$) across all scales, regardless of network depth or frequency components. The paper also discusses the limitations of other orthogonal polynomials (Chebyshev, Legendre), pointing out Runge's boundary run-away phenomenon and uniform-grid limitations.
* **Boundary Derivative and DST Comparison:** The paper justifies why DCT-II is selected over DFT and DST. In particular, it demonstrates that DST assumes an odd symmetric boundary extension that forces the virtual boundary coefficients to zero, which severely underfits critical physical boundary layers (like input layers or task-specific heads) and introduces gradient spikes. By contrast, DCT-II's even extension smoothly handles boundary spatial derivatives, allowing the network endpoints to adapt freely.
* **Block-wise/Layer-type Decompositions:** The authors address architectural heterogeneity (the fact that MHA and MLP layers behave differently) by partitioning the network and applying independent DCT-II transforms within each homogeneous family. This generalizes spectral merging to modern, heterogeneous architectures.

## 3. Distinction from Prior Work
The paper positions itself very clearly and fairly relative to prior works:
* It acknowledges that static methods like Task Arithmetic, TIES-Merging, and DARE are powerful but static, and explains that SpectralMerge is conceptually orthogonal and can operate in synergy with them (e.g., after sign consensus and magnitude pruning are applied).
* It distinguishes itself from AdaMerging and RegCalMerge by identifying their high spatial dimensionality and susceptibility to overfitting on small data streams.
* It compares directly to PolyMerge, demonstrating superior optimization convergence and numerical conditioning on deep networks ($L \ge 48$).

## 4. Refined Reviewer Insights (LoRA vs. Localized Fine-Tuning)
As an expert reviewer, we can introduce a highly valuable conceptual distinction that further elevates the paper's novelty:
* **The LoRA Contrast:** Under Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning via LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation), adapter weights are added to attention query/key/value projections at *every* transformer layer. Consequently, the task-specific parameter updates are distributed uniformly across the entire network depth rather than localized to specific deep layers (like the ResNet-18 fine-tuning setup). This suggests that for LoRA-merged models, the layer-wise sensitivity trajectory will be highly continuous and smooth across depth, making the hard-cutoff SpectralMerge-LP ($F=3$) or LP-Adaptive extremely effective and enabling severe parameter compression (e.g., optimizing only 3 spectral parameters per task). Conversely, localized fine-tuning (e.g., head-only or deep-block-only tuning) induces a step-function discontinuity that necessitates SpectralMerge-Reg. This distinction provides a highly valuable guideline for practitioners when choosing between the LP and Reg variants based on their fine-tuning protocol.

## 5. Rating of Originality
**Excellent.** The transition to the frequency domain using the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT-II) is highly creative, theoretically well-justified, and elegantly implemented. The conceptual frameworks developed (such as the Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox and the PEFT-Induced Step-Function Discontinuity) provide profound and lasting insights that are highly likely to influence future model-merging and parameter-management research.
