\begin{abstract}
Post-hoc model merging has emerged as a powerful paradigm for consolidating multiple task-specific expert models into a single multi-task network without the prohibitive cost of joint retraining. 
However, existing parameterized merging approaches optimize layer-wise combining coefficients strictly within the physical, spatial depth coordinate space. 
We argue that this spatial paradigm is fundamentally limited: layer-wise parameter sensitivity is inherently multi-scale and highly collinear, causing unconstrained spatial optimization to behave as high-frequency noise that overfits catastrophically to small streams or validation sets---a phenomenon we term the \emph{Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox}. 

In this work, we present \textbf{SpectralMerge}, a paradigm-shifting framework that rethinks model merging by transitioning parameter consolidation into the frequency domain. 
By applying the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT-II) to the spatial layer coefficient signal, SpectralMerge maps the merging trajectory to an orthogonal frequency space. 
We propose two elegant frequency-domain constraints: 
(1) \textbf{SpectralMerge-LP}, which restricts trainable parameters to the lowest-frequency spectral coordinates (acting as an analytical low-pass filter), and 
(2) \textbf{SpectralMerge-Reg}, which optimizes the full spectrum under a quadratic Spectral Decay Penalty. 

To validate our framework under controlled optimization and generalization conditions, we evaluate SpectralMerge on a mathematically calibrated Vision Transformer (ViT-B/32) multi-task simulation landscape across 30 random seeds. 
We demonstrate that SpectralMerge-LP ($F=3$) and SpectralMerge-Reg achieve state-of-the-art simulated accuracies of $85.32\%$ and $85.17\%$. 
Under extreme data sparsity (few-shot validation sets with $M \le 10$), SpectralMerge completely resolves the Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox, achieving $86.46\%$ accuracy and outperforming unconstrained spatial search by over $2.6\%$. 
Finally, we show that frequency-domain model merging is remarkably resilient to extreme validation target selection bias and adversarial stream noise, establishing a robust, transparent, and mathematically elegant foundation for spectral parameter consolidation.
\end{abstract}
