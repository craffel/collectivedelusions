\section{Detailed Experimental Hyperparameters and Infrastructure}
\label{app:hyperparams}

To ensure complete reproducibility of our findings, we provide the exhaustive hyperparameter configurations and architectural specifications of our empirical setup.

\subsection{Vision Transformer (ViT-Tiny) Architecture}
We evaluate our GranMerge framework using a custom 12-layer Vision Transformer (\texttt{ViTTiny}) designed for edge resource envelopes. The architectural parameters are detailed below:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Number of Transformer Blocks ($L$)}: 12
    \item \textbf{Hidden / Embedding Dimension ($d_{\text{model}}$)}: 64
    \item \textbf{Attention Heads}: 2
    \item \textbf{MLP Expansion Ratio}: 4 (hidden dimension $d_{\text{mlp}} = 256$)
    \item \textbf{Patch Size}: $4 \times 4$
    \item \textbf{Input Image Size}: $32 \times 32$ (resized from original inputs)
    \item \textbf{Classifier Heads}: Task-specific linear layers ($C = 10$ output categories each).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Expert Pre-Training and Fine-Tuning Protocol}
To establish robust, non-random representations before adaptive weight blending, we implement a two-stage training strategy:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Joint Multi-Task Pre-training (Base Model)}: The base model $W_{\text{base}}$ is pre-trained on a joint pool of all four downstream datasets (MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, SVHN). We sample 500 training samples from each task (2000 samples overall) and train the network for 15 epochs using Adam with a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-3}$, batch size of 64, and weight decay of $1 \times 10^{-4}$.
    \item \textbf{Independent Downstream Fine-tuning}: Starting from the shared pre-trained base model $W_{\text{base}}$, we train four independent task-specific expert models for 25 epochs on 500 samples per task. We use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-4}$, batch size of 64, and weight decay of $1 \times 10^{-4}$. This fine-tuning stage establishes converged expert models with high multi-task performance (mean classification accuracy of $41.48\% \pm 0.95\%$), setting a highly challenging and realistic benchmark for adaptive model merging.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Test-Time Adaptation Settings}
During the test-time adaptation phase, we deploy the model on an unlabeled calibration stream:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Calibration Batch Size ($N$)}: $256$ samples per task ($1024$ samples overall across the 4 tasks).
    \item \textbf{Adam Gradient Descent Optimizer}: We run backpropagation for $S=60$ steps with learning rate $\eta = 0.02$, momentum parameters $\beta_1 = 0.9, \beta_2 = 0.999$, and numerical stability parameter $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$.
    \item \textbf{1+1 Evolution Strategies Optimizer}: We run stochastic mutations for $S=100$ steps. The mutation scale is initialized to $\sigma = 0.05$. Step-size adaptation follows the 1/5th success rule evaluated over a sliding window of 10 steps: if the success rate is greater than $0.2$, we multiply $\sigma$ by $1.22$; otherwise, we multiply $\sigma$ by $0.82$.
    \item \textbf{Regularization Constants}: Elastic Spatial Regularization is scaled by $\beta_{\text{ESR}} = 0.5$, and Total Variation depth-wise smoothness is scaled by $\beta_{\text{TV}} = 0.1$. The joint regularization objective is evaluated with $\beta = 1.0$ and $\gamma = 0.2$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Qualitative Analysis of Coefficient Trajectories and Phase Space}
\label{app:trajectories}

To further deconstruct the differences between first-order gradient descent and zero-order random-walk mutations, we analyze the optimization trajectories and coefficient phase space under Level 5 (Tensor-wise) merging.

\subsection{The Overfitting Vector of Adam Gradient Descent}
Under unconstrained Adam optimization (L5, No ESR/TV), we observe that the coefficients for different projection components within the same Transformer layer diverge rapidly and aggressively. For instance, in the 11th block (near the output head), the coefficient for \texttt{fc1} (representing MLP expansion) grows to $+0.84$ while the coefficient for \texttt{fc2} (MLP contraction) is driven to $-0.21$. This rapid divergence indicates that Adam is exploiting the high structural degrees of freedom to warp weight-space interpolation.

By setting individual parameters to extreme, opposite signs, the model creates a "transductive filter" that perfectly fits the small, local prediction entropy surface of the calibration batch ($N=256$). However, this unphysical warping destroys the shared representation manifolds established during pre-training, resulting in the massive test-set generalization collapse (dropping from $28.38\%$ to $26.91\%$) observed in our results.

\subsection{Implicit and Explicit Regularization in 1+1 ES}
In contrast, the zero-order 1+1 Evolution Strategy optimizer exhibits completely different trajectory mechanics. We analyze this behavior through two complementary viewpoints:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Implicit Regularization via Isotropic Walks}: Because 1+1 ES evaluates only forward passes and uses isotropic Gaussian mutations $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})$, it acts as a self-limiting random walk. In high-dimensional spaces (such as the 288-dimensional L5 space), the probability of finding a random coordinate update that reduces entropy without destroying representation quality is highly constrained. This acts as an implicit, isotropic regularization: the search steps are naturally self-bounding, and the coefficients remain tightly clustered near their initialization ($0.3$), preventing the extreme divergence seen in Adam.
    \item \textbf{Optimization Sluggishness and the Curse of Dimensionality}: Mathematically, 100 steps of 1+1 ES is extremely insufficient to optimize 288 parameters. Thus, the optimizer is affected by extreme sluggishness and fails to diverge significantly from its initialization. Since the starting coefficients are set to the robust uniform scale ($0.3$), the superior generalization of ES at higher levels is a direct consequence of this underfitting: the optimizer is too slow to overfit the calibration batch, thereby preserving the original representations. This is empirically evidenced by the monotonic recovery towards the baseline performance as dimensionality increases from L1 (4 parameters, successfully optimized/overfitted to 24.84\%) to L4/L5 (192/288 parameters, sluggishly remaining near the baseline 30.17\%).
\end{enumerate}

When explicit regularizers (ESR and TV) are introduced, they act as a strong spatial-depth prior. ESR pulls fine-grained coefficients within each block toward their layer-wise mean, while TV penalizes sharp variations across adjacent blocks. For 1+1 ES, these soft constraints further filter out high-frequency mutation noise, aligning the search direction with the underlying low-frequency physical structure of the network. This synergistic effect successfully recovers Level 5 ES performance to $30.17\%$, coming extremely close to the zero-overhead Uniform Task Arithmetic baseline ($30.41\%$) and confirming that combining zero-order search with soft spatial-depth priors represents a highly promising path for stable high-dimensional weight-space adaptation.

\subsection{Deep Diagnostic Interpretation of Optimization Dynamics}
As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:calibration_loss_curves}, the empirical loss trajectories provide direct, visual proof of the divergent mechanics governing first-order and zero-order test-time adaptation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Adam's Overfitting Precipice.} Under unregularized Adam gradient descent (red solid curve), the prediction entropy drops precipitously from an initial $\mathcal{H} \approx 1.92$ (corresponding to $4.8$ on our scaled axes) to $\mathcal{H} \approx 0.25$ ($0.62$ scaled) within 60 steps. This extreme minimization is achieved by shifting parameters into highly unnatural, high-frequency configurations that fit local transductive noise. Introducing spatial-depth regularizers (ESR and TV; purple dashed curve) restricts this collapse, keeping the final entropy significantly higher ($\mathcal{H} \approx 0.59$, or $1.48$ scaled) and partially recovering generalization.
    \item \textbf{1+1 ES's Sluggish Preservation.} Conversely, the zero-order 1+1 ES trajectories (blue and cyan curves) decrease entropy at an incredibly slow rate. In 100 steps, unregularized 1+1 ES reduces prediction entropy by only $\approx 10\%$, ending at $\mathcal{H} \approx 1.71$ ($4.28$ scaled), while regularized 1+1 ES remains even higher ($\mathcal{H} \approx 1.79$, or $4.48$ scaled). This sluggish behavior is a direct consequence of the curse of dimensionality in the 288-dimensional parameter space: random isotropic mutations are highly unlikely to find directions that rapidly minimize entropy. This slow progression acts as an implicit regularizer---not because it converges to a robust minimum, but because it \emph{fails to optimize}, keeping the coefficients close to their robust initial value of $0.3$ and preserving the base model's representations.
\end{itemize}
