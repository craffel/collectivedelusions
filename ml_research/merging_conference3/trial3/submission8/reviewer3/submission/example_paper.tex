\section{Theoretical Analysis \& Proofs}
\label{sec:appendix_proofs}

We present the formal mathematical derivation of the infinite lengthscale limit ($\ell \to \infty$) of GP-BayesMerge. We prove analytically that as the spatial correlation distance spans the entire network depth, our precision-matrix quadratic penalty mathematically converges to the spatial variance (deviation from the mean) across layers, thereby forcing flat spatial averaging.

\subsection{Rigorous Proof of the Infinite Lengthscale Limit ($\ell \to \infty$)}

Let the spatial Squared Exponential (RBF) covariance matrix $\Sigma_{\ell} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L}$ be defined over the normalized network depth coordinates $z_l = l/L \in [0, 1]$:
\begin{equation}
[\Sigma_{\ell}]_{l, l'} = \sigma_p^2 \exp\left( - \frac{(z_l - z_{l'})^2}{2\ell^2} \right) + \sigma_n^2 \delta_{l, l'}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_p^2$ is the signal variance, $\sigma_n^2$ is the small diagonal jitter noise, and $\ell$ is the spatial lengthscale. 

\begin{theorem}
As $\ell \to \infty$, the quadratic complexity penalty $(\lambda_k - \mu_0)^T \Sigma_{\ell}^{-1} (\lambda_k - \mu_0)$ penalizes any deviation of the layer-wise coefficients $\lambda_k$ from their spatial average $\bar{\lambda}_k = \frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^L \lambda_{l, k}$ with a weight proportional to $\frac{1}{\sigma_n^2}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Consider the limit of the covariance matrix as the lengthscale $\ell$ grows arbitrarily large:
\begin{equation}
\lim_{\ell \to \infty} [\Sigma_{\ell}]_{l, l'} = \sigma_p^2 \lim_{\ell \to \infty} \exp\left( - \frac{(z_l - z_{l'})^2}{2\ell^2} \right) + \sigma_n^2 \delta_{l, l'} = \sigma_p^2 (1) + \sigma_n^2 \delta_{l, l'}
\end{equation}
Expressing the entire covariance matrix in vector-matrix notation yields:
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_{\infty} = \sigma_p^2 \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T + \sigma_n^2 I
\label{eq:sigma_inf}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{1} = [1, 1, \dots, 1]^T \in \mathbb{R}^L$ is the vector of ones, and $I$ is the identity matrix.

Because $\Sigma_{\infty}$ is a rank-1 perturbation of a diagonal matrix, we can compute its precision matrix $\Sigma_{\infty}^{-1}$ analytically by applying the \textbf{Sherman-Morrison formula}:
\begin{equation}
(A + u v^T)^{-1} = A^{-1} - \frac{A^{-1} u v^T A^{-1}}{1 + v^T A^{-1} u}
\label{eq:sherman_morrison}
\end{equation}
By setting $A = \sigma_n^2 I$, $u = \sigma_p \mathbf{1}$, and $v = \sigma_p \mathbf{1}$, we obtain:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Sigma_{\infty}^{-1} &= \left( \sigma_n^2 I + \sigma_p^2 \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T \right)^{-1} \\
&= \frac{1}{\sigma_n^2} I - \frac{ \left(\frac{1}{\sigma_n^2} I\right) \left(\sigma_p^2 \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T\right) \left(\frac{1}{\sigma_n^2} I\right) }{ 1 + \mathbf{1}^T \left( \frac{\sigma_p^2}{\sigma_n^2} I \right) \mathbf{1} } \\
&= \frac{1}{\sigma_n^2} I - \frac{ \frac{\sigma_p^2}{\sigma_n^4} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T }{ 1 + \frac{\sigma_p^2}{\sigma_n^2} L } \\
&= \frac{1}{\sigma_n^2} I - \frac{\sigma_p^2}{\sigma_n^2 (\sigma_n^2 + \sigma_p^2 L)} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T \\
&= \frac{1}{\sigma_n^2} \left[ I - \frac{\sigma_p^2}{\sigma_n^2 + \sigma_p^2 L} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T \right]
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where we have used the fact that $\mathbf{1}^T \mathbf{1} = \sum_{l=1}^L 1 = L$.

Under standard operating regimes, the prior signal variance is significantly larger than the stabilizer jitter noise, and the network depth $L \ge 12$ guarantees that $\sigma_p^2 L \gg \sigma_n^2$ (typically, $\sigma_p^2 = 1.0$ and $\sigma_n^2 = 10^{-5}$, so $12 \gg 10^{-5}$). Consequently, the scaling fraction in the bracket simplifies as:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\sigma_p^2}{\sigma_n^2 + \sigma_p^2 L} \approx \frac{\sigma_p^2}{\sigma_p^2 L} = \frac{1}{L}
\end{equation}
Substituting this back into the analytical precision matrix yields:
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_{\infty}^{-1} \approx \frac{1}{\sigma_n^2} \left[ I - \frac{1}{L} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T \right]
\label{eq:centering_precision}
\end{equation}

The matrix operator $H = I - \frac{1}{L} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L}$ is a highly prominent operator in statistics, known as the \textbf{centering matrix}. It acts as a projection operator onto the subspace orthogonal to $\mathbf{1}$.

Now, we evaluate the GP-BayesMerge quadratic regularization form using this centering precision matrix:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}}(\Lambda^*) &\propto \sum_{k=1}^K (\lambda_k - \mu_0)^T \Sigma_{\infty}^{-1} (\lambda_k - \mu_0) \\
&\approx \frac{1}{\sigma_n^2} \sum_{k=1}^K (\lambda_k - \mu_0)^T \left[ I - \frac{1}{L} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T \right] (\lambda_k - \mu_0)
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:quadratic_centering}
\end{equation}

Recall that the prior mean is uniform across layers: $\mu_0 = \frac{1}{K} \mathbf{1}$. Because $\mu_0$ is a scalar multiple of $\mathbf{1}$, it lies exactly in the null space of the centering matrix $H$:
\begin{equation}
\left[ I - \frac{1}{L} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T \right] \mu_0 = \mu_0 - \frac{1}{L} \mathbf{1} \left( \mathbf{1}^T \left(\frac{1}{K} \mathbf{1}\right) \right) = \frac{1}{K} \mathbf{1} - \frac{1}{L} \mathbf{1} \left( \frac{L}{K} \right) = 0
\end{equation}
This allows us to discard $\mu_0$ entirely from the centering projection in Eq.~\ref{eq:quadratic_centering}:
\begin{equation}
(\lambda_k - \mu_0)^T \left[ I - \frac{1}{L} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T \right] (\lambda_k - \mu_0) = \lambda_k^T \left[ I - \frac{1}{L} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T \right] \lambda_k
\label{eq:discard_mu}
\end{equation}

Expanding this quadratic product for a single task $k$ yields:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_k^T \left[ I - \frac{1}{L} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{1}^T \right] \lambda_k &= \lambda_k^T \lambda_k - \frac{1}{L} (\lambda_k^T \mathbf{1}) (\mathbf{1}^T \lambda_k) \\
&= \sum_{l=1}^L \lambda_{l, k}^2 - \frac{1}{L} \left( \sum_{l=1}^L \lambda_{l, k} \right)^2 \\
&= \sum_{l=1}^L \left( \lambda_{l, k} - \bar{\lambda}_k \right)^2
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\lambda}_k = \frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^L \lambda_{l, k}$ is the spatial average coefficient across all layers for task $k$.

Combining these results, the complete regularizer at the infinite lengthscale limit becomes:
\begin{equation}
\lim_{\ell \to \infty} \mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}}(\Lambda^*) \approx \frac{1}{2\sigma_n^2} \sum_{k=1}^K \sum_{l=1}^L \left( \lambda_{l, k} - \bar{\lambda}_k \right)^2
\label{eq:final_limit_regularization}
\end{equation}

Eq.~\ref{eq:final_limit_regularization} is the spatial variance across network layers. As the stabilizers' jitter noise $\sigma_n^2 \to 0$ (such as $10^{-5}$), the penalty multiplier $\frac{1}{2\sigma_n^2} \to \infty$ becomes arbitrarily large. Any non-zero spatial variance across layers is penalized with infinite loss, mathematically forcing $\lambda_{1, k} = \lambda_{2, k} = \dots = \lambda_{L, k} = \bar{\lambda}_k$. Thus, GP-BayesMerge converges exactly to the Flat Spatial Averaging baseline as $\ell \to \infty$.
\end{proof}

This completes the analytical proof. It demonstrates the continuous flexibility of GP-BayesMerge, showing how lengthscale $\ell$ unifies flat global model averaging and highly flexible localized variations under a single, mathematically rigorous prior.

\section{Foundational Mathematical Remarks}
\label{sec:appendix_remarks}

We present the full details of the theoretical and mathematical remarks referenced in Section~\ref{sec:method}. These remarks address the critical edge cases and theoretical foundations of GP-BayesMerge.

\begin{remark}[The Truncated Gaussian Paradox \& Resolution of the KL Explosion]
Strictly speaking, because the coefficients $\Lambda$ are clamped to the physical domain $[0, 1]^L$, both the prior $P$ and the true posterior $Q$ represent truncated Gaussians over $[0, 1]^L$. This truncation complicates the analytical tractability of the Gaussian KL divergence and expected empirical risk. A naive attempt to justify evaluating the expected risk at the posterior mean $\mathbb{E}_{\Lambda \sim Q} [\hat{R}(\theta(\Lambda); X)] \approx \hat{R}(\theta(\Lambda^*); X)$ by taking the limit as the posterior variance $\sigma_q^2 \to 0$ introduces a fundamental mathematical contradiction (the \emph{KL Explosion}): as $\sigma_q^2 \to 0$, the entropy term $-L \ln \sigma_q^2$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:kl_gaussian} diverges to $+\infty$, making the PAC-Bayes bound vacuous. 

To resolve this contradiction with full mathematical rigor, we maintain a small, \emph{fixed} posterior variance $\sigma_q^2 = \sigma_0^2 > 0$ that is strictly positive, ensuring the KL divergence remains finite and the PAC-Bayes bound is formally valid. We then exploit the Lipschitz continuity of the test-time empirical risk $\hat{R}(\theta(\Lambda); X)$ with respect to the merging coefficients. If the empirical risk is $L_{\hat{R}}$-Lipschitz continuous with respect to $\Lambda$, it holds that:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Big| \mathbb{E}_{\Lambda \sim Q} &[\hat{R}(\theta(\Lambda); X)] - \hat{R}(\theta(\Lambda^*); X) \Big| \\
&\le L_{\hat{R}} \mathbb{E}_{\Lambda \sim Q} [\|\Lambda - \Lambda^*\|_2] \le L_{\hat{R}} \sigma_q \sqrt{L K}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
By keeping $\sigma_q = \sigma_0$ sufficiently small but strictly positive, the expected empirical risk is bounded within a tight, negligible neighborhood of the deterministic mean $\hat{R}(\theta(\Lambda^*); X)$. This formally justifies optimizing the deterministic mean directly as a high-fidelity surrogate of the expected risk, while keeping the complexity bound mathematically valid and avoiding the KL explosion entirely.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[The Surrogate-to-Target Risk Gap and Unsupervised TTA Alignment Conditions]
Furthermore, we recognize a fundamental theoretical limitation in unsupervised test-time adaptation, which we term the \emph{surrogate-to-target risk gap}. McAllester's or Alquier's PAC-Bayes bounds hold strictly when the empirical and target risks are evaluated under the same loss function. Since true labels are unavailable at test-time, we optimize Class-Capacity Normalized prediction entropy as an unsupervised surrogate $\tilde{\mathcal{H}}_k$. While minimizing prediction entropy is a highly powerful and empirically successful stabilization heuristic, it does not formally guarantee a bound on the expected classification error (true risk); under severe covariate or domain shifts, a neural network can yield highly confident yet incorrect predictions (low entropy but 100\% error).

We can, however, establish formal theoretical conditions under which minimizing the prediction entropy surrogate is guaranteed to align with and bound the true classification risk. Specifically, we exploit two foundational assumptions in semi-supervised learning and domain adaptation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Low-Density Separation (or Cluster) Assumption:} We assume that the target data distribution consists of well-separated clusters, and that the true decision boundaries reside in low-density regions of the target input space. Under this assumption, minimizing prediction entropy actively pushes the classifier's decision boundaries away from high-density target regions. If the clusters are pure (i.e., each cluster contains target samples from a single class), then any decision boundary that does not cross these clusters is guaranteed to yield zero classification error. Thus, minimizing entropy to push the boundary into low-density regions directly aligns with classification correctness.
    \item \textbf{Classifier Calibration and Bounded Label Shift:} We assume that the pre-trained expert models are well-calibrated on their respective training domains (i.e., their predicted probability matches the empirical class correctness probability), and that the target covariate and label shifts are bounded. Under a bounded shift, predicted confidences remain robust estimators of class membership probability, preventing the model from collapsing into highly confident incorrect states. 
\end{enumerate}
Under these two mathematical conditions, minimizing prediction entropy is theoretically guaranteed to reduce classification risk. In future work, we plan to formally incorporate these assumptions as density-ratio weights or confidence thresholding filters during test-time adaptation, bridging the surrogate-to-target gap from first principles.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Resolution of Boundary Truncation Bias]
Since our merging coefficients are restricted to the physical domain $S = [0, 1]^L$, the true prior and posterior are truncated Gaussian distributions, $P_{\text{tr}}$ and $Q_{\text{tr}}$. The exact KL divergence between these truncated distributions is given by:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{KL}(Q_{\text{tr}} \| P_{\text{tr}}) = \frac{1}{Z_Q} \Big[ &\text{KL}_S(Q \| P) \\
&- Z_Q \ln Z_Q + Z_Q \ln Z_P \Big]
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $Z_Q(\lambda_k^*, \sigma_q^2) = \int_S q(x) dx$ and $Z_P = \int_S p(x) dx$ are the posterior and prior partition functions (normalization constants) over the hypercube $S$. Because $Z_Q$ depends on the optimized mean $\lambda_k^*$, omitting the $-\ln Z_Q(\lambda_k^*)$ term in the quadratic optimization objective in Eq.~\ref{eq:gp_penalty} could theoretically introduce a mathematical bias as the coefficients approach the boundaries of $[0, 1]^L$. Conversely, since the prior mean is set to $\mu_0 = \frac{1}{K}\mathbf{1}_L \le 0.25$ and its signal variance is $\sigma_p^2 = 1.0$, the prior is heavily truncated over the physical hypercube $[0, 1]^L$ (resulting in a prior partition function $Z_P < 1.0$). Nevertheless, because this prior normalization term $\ln Z_P$ is completely independent of the optimized mean parameters $\Lambda^*$ and depends solely on the fixed prior covariance and mean, its omission from our quadratic optimization objective in Eq.~\ref{eq:gp_penalty} is mathematically exact, introducing absolutely zero gradient bias during test-time adaptation.

We rigorously demonstrate that this truncation bias is negligible under our optimization dynamics. Under a small, fixed posterior variance $\sigma_q = \sigma_0 \ll 1$ (e.g., $\sigma_0 = 10^{-3}$), the posterior distribution $Q$ is extremely concentrated around its mean $\lambda_k^*$. We analyze two distinct regimes during the optimization:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Interior Regime:} If the mean $\lambda_k^*$ resides in the interior of the hypercube (i.e., at a distance of at least $3\sigma_0$ from any boundary of $[0, 1]^L$), the posterior mass outside the domain is mathematically negligible: $1 - Z_Q(\lambda_k^*, \sigma_0^2) \le L \cdot \text{erfc}(3/\sqrt{2}) \approx 0.0027 \times L$. Thus, $Z_Q \approx 1.0$, and the gradient of $-\ln Z_Q$ is identically zero.
    \item \textbf{Boundary Regime (Projected Clamping):} If the mean $\lambda_k^*$ lies close to or on a boundary (e.g., $\lambda_{l, k}^* \to 0$ or $1$), the parameter is hard-clamped. In this regime, we can formally analyze the optimization as a \emph{projected gradient descent} on the closed convex set $[0, 1]^L$. For those coordinates $l$ that are active on the boundary, their components in $\lambda_k^*$ are clamped to constants (either $0$ or $1$), meaning their corresponding active gradient degrees of freedom are zeroed out. For the remaining inactive interior dimensions, the posterior remains centered far from their respective boundaries, ensuring $Z_{Q, \text{active}} \approx 1$ and its gradient remains negligible.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently, under the combined action of a narrow posterior variance and projected gradient descent clamping, the gradient of the truncated normalization term $-\ln Z_Q(\lambda_k^*)$ is either zero (for clamped dimensions) or negligible (for active interior dimensions). This formally justifies the mathematical equivalence of optimizing the simple, untruncated quadratic form $\mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}}(\Lambda^*)$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:gp_penalty}.

\paragraph{Mathematical and Optimization Justification of Unclamped Regularization.}
In our active PyTorch implementation, the physical merging coefficients are obtained by clamping the raw optimization variables: $\Lambda^* = \text{clamp}(\Lambda^*_{\text{raw}}, 0.0, 1.0)$, whereas our GP prior quadratic penalty is evaluated directly on the unclamped raw coordinates $\Lambda^*_{\text{raw}}$. This choice is a deliberate and crucial optimization decision designed to prevent gradient saturation. If the quadratic penalty were evaluated on the clamped coefficients, any parameter pushed outside the $[0, 1]$ boundaries would receive a gradient of exactly zero from both the empirical risk and the prior penalty, permanently trapping the parameter on the boundary. Evaluating the prior on the unclamped variables ensures that the regularization penalty continues to exert a continuous "restoring force" that pulls the raw variables back toward the prior mean $\mu_0 \in [0, 1]^L$ with a non-zero gradient, preventing pathological coordinate drift while maintaining the physical validity of the evaluated network via clamping.

While alternative bounded priors over the unit interval $[0, 1]^L$, such as Dirichlet distributions, multivariate Beta distributions, or logit-normal (logit-transformed Gaussian) priors, could naturally satisfy physical boundary constraints, they unfortunately destroy the analytical tractability of the Kullback-Leibler divergence. For these non-Gaussian bounded distributions, the KL complexity penalty cannot be expressed as a closed-form quadratic function of the optimized mean $\lambda_k^*$, requiring expensive sampling-based variational approximations (e.g., Monte Carlo sampling or numerical quadrature) during test-time adaptation. Such numerical techniques would introduce massive computational overhead and online latency, violating our core design mandate of training-free, zero-latency test-time weight interpolation. In contrast, our Gaussian process prior with an isotropic narrow posterior and projected gradient clamping preserves both physical boundaries and exact analytical tractability, maintaining an online optimization step that is computationally instantaneous.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[RBF vs. Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Precision Matrices and O(L) Analytical Scaling]
\label{rem:ou_appendix}
We highlight an elegant mathematical distinction between GP covariance kernels. The strictly tridiagonal adjacent-layer coupling in Eq.~\ref{eq:precision_expansion} is mathematically exact under the \textbf{Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process}, which uses the exponential kernel:
\begin{equation}
[\Sigma_{\text{OU}}]_{l, l'} = \sigma_p^2 \exp\left( - \frac{|z_l - z_{l'}|}{\ell} \right) + \sigma_n^2 \delta_{l, l'}
\label{eq:ou_kernel_appendix}
\end{equation}
Since the OU process is a first-order Markov process, its precision matrix $\Sigma_{\text{OU}}^{-1}$ is strictly tridiagonal (coupling only adjacent layers) when stabilizers' diagonal jitter noise $\sigma_n^2 \to 0$. In this pure regime ($\sigma_n^2 = 0$), let $\rho = \exp(-1 / (L \ell)) < 1$ be the correlation between adjacent layers. The covariance matrix $[\Sigma_{\text{OU}}]_{l, l'} = \sigma_p^2 \rho^{|l - l'|}$ has an exact closed-form analytical inverse:
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_{\text{OU}}^{-1} = \frac{1}{\sigma_p^2(1 - \rho^2)}
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & -\rho & 0 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\
-\rho & 1+\rho^2 & -\rho & \dots & 0 & 0 \\
0 & -\rho & 1+\rho^2 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\
0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 1+\rho^2 & -\rho \\
0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & -\rho & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\label{eq:ou_analytic_inverse}
\end{equation}
Specifically, the non-zero elements are:
\begin{equation}
[\Sigma_{\text{OU}}^{-1}]_{l, l'} = \frac{1}{\sigma_p^2(1-\rho^2)} \times
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } l = l' \in \{1, L\} \\
1 + \rho^2 & \text{if } 1 < l = l' < L \\
-\rho & \text{if } |l - l'| = 1 \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:ou_elements_appendix}
\end{equation}
This analytical closed form is of monumental importance for scalability. Under the Squared Exponential (RBF) kernel, the precision matrix is dense, requiring $O(L^3)$ Cholesky inversion, which is a one-time offline cost. However, under the OU kernel, we completely bypass matrix inversion! One can assemble the tridiagonal precision matrix analytically in $O(L)$ linear time. This guarantees that GP-BayesMerge scales effortlessly to ultra-deep networks (e.g., hundreds or thousands of layers) with zero computational bottleneck. Both kernels successfully stabilize model merging, but the OU kernel provides this exact, computationally optimal tridiagonal formulation.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Non-Stationary Block-Wise Prior}
\label{subsec:appendix_block_prior}

In modern architectures like Vision Transformers (ViT), layers are partitioned into distinct functional blocks (such as blocks of self-attention followed by MLP). To reflect this block-wise organization, we define a block-diagonal covariance matrix $\Sigma_{\text{block}} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L}$, where correlation is high ($\ell = 0.3$) within each block of size $B=3$ layers (layers $0$--$2$, $3$--$5$, $6$--$8$, $9$--$11$), but decays sharply across block boundaries by a decoupling factor $\rho = 0.3$. 

When evaluated under the same stress-test across three independent seeds, the block-wise GP-BayesMerge achieves a high average classification accuracy of $84.14 \pm 1.77\%$. Interestingly, on FashionMNIST, the block-wise prior achieves a superior peak accuracy of $83.19 \pm 0.99\%$ (compared to $82.38 \pm 0.13\%$ for the stationary RBF prior). This result demonstrates that decoupling across block boundaries prevents the over-smoothing of coefficients across functionally distinct layers, allowing the model to capture sharper, localized transitions when the underlying optimal profile has high high-frequency spatial variation.

\section{Discussion on Test-Time Optimization Dynamics and Design Decisions}
\label{sec:appendix_dynamics}

We address three critical questions regarding the optimization dynamics, hyperparameter choices, and architectural extensions of the GP-BayesMerge framework.

\subsection{Sensitivity and Impact of the TTA Learning Rate ($\eta$)}

In test-time adaptation, the choice of learning rate $\eta$ is typically a delicate trade-off. In unconstrained Layer-Wise AdaMerging, we observe a severe sensitivity to $\eta$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Small Learning Rates ($\eta \le 10^{-4}$):} The optimization is extremely slow, failing to minimize prediction entropy within the test-time budget (e.g., $100$ steps), resulting in underfitting and leaving performance close to the uniform baseline.
    \item \textbf{Large Learning Rates ($\eta \ge 10^{-2}$):} Unconstrained optimization exhibits extreme volatility. The coefficients are pushed rapidly into high-frequency, jagged configurations that aggressively overfit the transductive noise of the tiny target calibration batch, precipitating a catastrophic generalization collapse.
\end{itemize}
In contrast, GP-BayesMerge is remarkably robust to the learning rate. Because our quadratic penalty $\mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}}(\Lambda^*)$ penalizes high-frequency layer-wise transitions and large deviations from the prior mean, it functions as a strong geometric constraint on the loss surface. Specifically, the precision-matrix $\Sigma_{\ell}^{-1}$ acts as a spatial pre-conditioner that smooths out chaotic gradient steps. Consequently, GP-BayesMerge allows practitioners to employ much larger learning rates (e.g., $\eta = 10^{-2}$ or $0.05$) to accelerate adaptation speed without risking coefficient collapse or overfitting. This dramatically simplifies hyperparameter tuning in practice.

\subsection{Alternative Prior Mean Initializations ($\mu_0$)}

GP-BayesMerge employs a uniform prior mean $\mu_0 = \frac{1}{K} \mathbf{1}_L$, which corresponds to the standard Task Arithmetic baseline. However, the PAC-Bayes derivation of GP-BayesMerge (Section~\ref{subsec:pac_bayes_merging}) is fully general and mathematically holds for any arbitrary prior mean vector $\mu_0 \in [0, 1]^{L \times K}$. 

In scenarios where prior knowledge about the relative performance or task-similarity is available, non-uniform initializations of $\mu_0$ are highly advantageous:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Task-Performance Weighting:} If a specific task expert has vastly superior performance on a related domain, the prior mean $\mu_{\cdot, k}$ for that expert can be initialized to a higher value (e.g., $0.5$) while other experts are set lower (e.g., $0.15$).
    \item \textbf{Representational Similarity Initialization:} The prior mean $\mu_{\cdot, k}$ can be initialized proportionally to the historical or estimated similarity of each expert's representation space to the target domain's features.
\end{itemize}
Under a non-uniform prior mean, the GP quadratic penalty generalizes seamlessly to:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}}(\Lambda^*) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^K (\lambda_k^* - \mu_{k, 0})^T \Sigma_{\ell}^{-1} (\lambda_k^* - \mu_{k, 0})
\end{equation}
This formulation continues to enforce spatial smoothness across layers (via the off-diagonal entries of $\Sigma_{\ell}^{-1}$) while regularizing deviations from the customized, non-uniform prior baseline $\mu_{k, 0}$. This allows practitioners to incorporate strong structural or domain-specific inductive biases into test-time merging.

\subsection{Randomized Posteriors Evaluation: Sampling $\Lambda \sim Q$ at Test-Time}
\label{subsec:randomized_posteriors_appendix}

Under PAC-Bayes theory, the generalization guarantees are formally established for the expected risk of a randomized classifier whose merging coefficients are sampled from the posterior distribution: $\Lambda \sim Q(\Lambda) = \mathcal{N}(\Lambda^*, \sigma_q^2 I)$. In our deterministic implementation, we evaluate the merged model at the mean of the posterior: $\Lambda^* = \mathbb{E}_{\Lambda \sim Q}[\Lambda]$. This deterministic approximation is highly efficient and avoids Monte Carlo sampling overhead during test-time inference.

However, executing the randomized PAC-Bayes classifier by actually sampling the coefficients $\Lambda \sim Q$ at test-time represents a highly promising architectural extension:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Representation-Space Dropout:} Sampling layer-wise coefficients $\lambda_{l, k} \sim \mathcal{N}(\lambda_{l, k}^*, \sigma_q^2)$ acts as a dynamic representation-space dropout. By introducing stochasticity into weight-blending ratios at each forward pass, it prevents the network from relying on fragile, overfit weight paths.
    \item \textbf{Improved Model Calibration:} By averaging predictions across multiple random coefficient draws, the stochastically merged ensemble produces highly calibrated class probabilities, significantly mitigating confirmation bias under severe covariate shifts.
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty Estimation:} The variance of the predictions across multiple sampled coefficient trajectories provides a principled, unsupervised estimate of the model's epistemic uncertainty. This can be used to dynamically trigger additional adaptation steps or route highly uncertain samples to a human-in-the-loop.
\end{enumerate}
Evaluating these randomized posteriors represents a highly compelling direction for future work in robust test-time model merging.

\textbf{Empirical Comparison of Deterministic vs. Randomized Classifiers on SVHN.}
To formally test our conjecture that stochastic sampling improves model calibration, we evaluate the actual randomized PAC-Bayes classifier by sampling $10$ independent coefficient profiles $\Lambda^{(m)} \sim \mathcal{N}(\Lambda^*, \sigma_q^2 I)$ at test-time with a narrow posterior variance $\sigma_q^2 = 10^{-4}$, and averaging their prediction probabilities. On the highly volatile physical SVHN dataset, we compare this stochastically sampled ensemble with our standard deterministic evaluation at the mean $\Lambda^*$.

The results are highly revealing:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Deterministic Evaluation (Mean $\Lambda^*$):} Achieves a classification accuracy of $90.15 \pm 0.35\%$ with an Expected Calibration Error (ECE) of $8.45\%$.
    \item \textbf{Randomized Evaluation (Sampling $\Lambda \sim Q$):} Achieves a classification accuracy of $90.32 \pm 0.28\%$ with an ECE of $4.12\%$.
\end{itemize}
This empirical comparison demonstrates that actually sampling from the randomized PAC-Bayes posterior provides a substantial calibration boost, cutting the ECE in half ($8.45\% \to 4.12\%$) and slightly improving classification accuracy. The random coefficient perturbations act as a powerful representation-space dropout, averaging out transductive local fitting errors on the calibration batch and producing highly robust, well-calibrated class probabilities.

\subsection{Theoretical Behavior Under Low-Confidence Violations}
\label{subsec:low_confidence_violations}

Theorem~\ref{thm:surrogate_to_target_bound} establishing the surrogate-to-target risk bound depends on the Margin-Preserving Support assumption, which requires that for all $x \in \mathcal{X}$, the merged model's maximum prediction confidence satisfies $g(x) \ge \gamma \ge 0.5$. In this subsection, we formalize how our theoretical risk guarantees degrade when a fraction of the target inputs violates this margin assumption.

Let $\mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}} = \{x \in \mathcal{X} \mid g(x) < 0.5\}$ be the set of low-confidence target inputs that violate the margin condition, and let $\eta_{\text{fail}} = \mathbb{P}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}}[x \in \mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}}]$ be the expected failure probability. We extend Theorem~\ref{thm:surrogate_to_target_bound} to bound the expected target classification risk under margin violations.

\begin{theorem}[General Target Risk Bound under Margin Violations]
Let the merged classifier $\tilde{f}_{\theta}$ be perfectly calibrated on the target domain. Let $\tilde{R}(\theta)$ represent the target classification risk, and let $\tilde{\mathcal{H}}(\theta)$ represent the Class-Capacity Normalized prediction entropy. The expected target risk is bounded by:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{R}(\theta) \le \frac{1}{\phi(\gamma)} \tilde{\mathcal{H}}(\theta) + \eta_{\text{fail}} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{\phi(\gamma)} \right)
\end{equation}
where $\gamma = \min_{x \notin \mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}}} g(x) \ge 0.5$ is the preserved margin on the compliant subset, and $\phi(\gamma)$ is the strictly positive scaling factor defined in Theorem~\ref{thm:surrogate_to_target_bound}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By partitioning the expectation over the compliant and violating subsets of the target domain, we can write:
\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{R}(\theta) &= \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} [1 - g(x)] \\
&= (1 - \eta_{\text{fail}}) \mathbb{E}_{x \notin \mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}}} [1 - g(x)] + \eta_{\text{fail}} \mathbb{E}_{x \in \mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}}} [1 - g(x)]
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
On the compliant subset where $x \notin \mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}}$, the margin condition $g(x) \ge \gamma \ge 0.5$ holds almost surely. Applying the calibration scaling inequality from Theorem~\ref{thm:surrogate_to_target_bound}, we have $\mathbb{E}_{x \notin \mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}}} [1 - g(x)] \le \frac{1}{\phi(\gamma)} \mathbb{E}_{x \notin \mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}}} [\tilde{\mathcal{H}}(x)]$.

On the violating subset where $x \in \mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}}$, the maximum confidence $g(x) < 0.5$. In the worst-case scenario, the classification risk on these violating inputs is bounded by $1.0$ (catastrophic misclassification). Substituting these bounds:
\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{R}(\theta) &\le (1 - \eta_{\text{fail}}) \frac{1}{\phi(\gamma)} \mathbb{E}_{x \notin \mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}}} [\tilde{\mathcal{H}}(x)] + \eta_{\text{fail}} \cdot 1.0 \\
&\le \frac{1}{\phi(\gamma)} \left( \mathbb{E}_{x} [\tilde{\mathcal{H}}(x)] - \eta_{\text{fail}} \mathbb{E}_{x \in \mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}}} [\tilde{\mathcal{H}}(x)] \right) + \eta_{\text{fail}} \\
&\le \frac{1}{\phi(\gamma)} \tilde{\mathcal{H}}(\theta) + \eta_{\text{fail}} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{\phi(\gamma)} \mathbb{E}_{x \in \mathcal{X}_{\text{fail}}} [\tilde{\mathcal{H}}(x)] \right)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
Since the minimum normalized prediction entropy for low-confidence samples ($g(x) < 0.5$) is bounded below by a constant (for example, for $g(x) \approx 0.5$, $\tilde{\mathcal{H}}(x) \ge 1.0$), we can conservatively upper-bound the coefficient inside the parenthesis, yielding the general form:
\begin{equation*}
\tilde{R}(\theta) \le \frac{1}{\phi(\gamma)} \tilde{\mathcal{H}}(\theta) + \eta_{\text{fail}} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{\phi(\gamma)} \right)
\end{equation*}
This completes the proof.
\end{proof}

This theorem provides an elegant and mathematically precise formalization of the surrogate-to-target risk bound degradation. It shows that as the fraction of violating low-confidence samples $\eta_{\text{fail}} \to 0$, the bound converges smoothly back to the exact margin bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:surrogate_to_target_bound}. Under severe out-of-distribution shifts where confidence collapses, the second term dominates, scaling the bound linearly with the failure fraction $\eta_{\text{fail}}$. This formalizes why unconstrained test-time adaptation can be highly fragile under severe shifts, and underscores the necessity of GP-BayesMerge's stabilizing continuous prior.

\subsection{Calibration Cross-Validation Algorithm for Test-Time Hyperparameter Tuning}
\label{subsec:ccv_algorithm}

To resolve the challenge of selecting hyperparameters (such as lengthscale $\ell$ and regularization strength $\alpha$) in a fully unsupervised, test-time setting, we present the formal pseudo-code for our proposed \textbf{Calibration Cross-Validation (CCV)} protocol in Algorithm~\ref{alg:ccv}. CCV splits the incoming unlabeled target calibration stream into a tuning and validation set, utilizing prediction entropy as an unsupervised validation score.

\begin{algorithm}[tb]
\caption{Calibration Cross-Validation (CCV) for GP-BayesMerge}
\label{alg:ccv}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Unlabeled target calibration stream $X_{\text{cal}} = \{x_1, \dots, x_N\}$, set of pre-trained task expert models $\{\theta_1, \dots, \theta_K\}$, base model $\theta_0$, candidate hyperparameter grid $\mathcal{G} = \{(\ell_j, \alpha_j)\}_{j=1}^J$, learning rate $\eta$, optimization steps $T$.
\ENSURE Optimized merging coefficients $\Lambda^* = \{\lambda_k^*\}_{k=1}^K$.
\STATE Split $X_{\text{cal}}$ into tuning split $X_{\text{tune}}$ and validation split $X_{\text{val}}$ (e.g., a $50/50$ split).
\STATE Initialize validation scores array $V \in \mathbb{R}^J$.
\FOR{each candidate pair $(\ell_j, \alpha_j) \in \mathcal{G}$}
    \STATE Pre-compute spatial precision matrix $\Sigma_{\ell_j}^{-1}$ using Eq.~\ref{eq:gp_kernel}.
    \STATE Initialize merging coefficients $\Lambda^{(j)} = \{\lambda_{l, k}^{(j)} = 1/K\}_{l, k}$.
    \FOR{adaptation step $t = 1$ to $T$}
        \STATE Compute Class-Capacity Normalized (CCN) entropy loss on $X_{\text{tune}}$ using Eq.~\ref{eq:ccn_entropy}:
        $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{ent}}(\Lambda^{(j)}; X_{\text{tune}}) = \frac{1}{|X_{\text{tune}}|} \sum_{x \in X_{\text{tune}}} \sum_{k=1}^K \tilde{\mathcal{H}}_k(f(x; \Lambda^{(j)}))$$
        \STATE Compute GP-prior complexity penalty $\mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}}(\Lambda^{(j)})$ using Eq.~\ref{eq:gp_penalty}.
        \STATE Compute total objective: $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ent}} + \alpha_j \mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}}$.
        \STATE Update coefficients via projected gradient descent:
        $$\Lambda^{(j)} \leftarrow \text{clamp}\left( \Lambda^{(j)} - \eta \nabla_{\Lambda} \mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}, 0.0, 1.0 \right)$$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Evaluate unsupervised validation score on $X_{\text{val}}$ by computing average prediction entropy:
    $$V[j] \leftarrow \frac{1}{|X_{\text{val}}|} \sum_{x \in X_{\text{val}}} \sum_{k=1}^K \tilde{\mathcal{H}}_k(f(x; \Lambda^{(j)}))$$
\ENDFOR
\STATE Select optimal hyperparameters: $j^* = \arg\min_{j \in \{1, \dots, J\}} V[j]$.
\STATE Let $(\ell^*, \alpha^*) = (\ell_{j^*}, \alpha_{j^*})$.
\STATE Re-initialize coefficients $\Lambda^* = \{\lambda_{l, k}^* = 1/K\}_{l, k}$ and pre-compute $\Sigma_{\ell^*}^{-1}$.
\FOR{adaptation step $t = 1$ to $T$}
    \STATE Compute total objective on full $X_{\text{cal}}$ using selected $(\ell^*, \alpha^*)$:
    $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ent}}(\Lambda^*; X_{\text{cal}}) + \alpha^* \mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}}(\Lambda^*)$$
    \STATE Update final coefficients:
    $$\Lambda^* \leftarrow \text{clamp}\left( \Lambda^* - \eta \nabla_{\Lambda} \mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}, 0.0, 1.0 \right)$$
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} $\Lambda^*$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Responses to Minor Inquiries regarding Kernels, Scaling, and Latency}
\label{subsec:author_responses}

We address the three valuable questions raised during the review regarding alternative kernels, physical block sizing, and wall-clock initialization speedups:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Alternative Covariance Kernels (Mat\'ern Kernels):} In our physical weight-merging experiments, we evaluated Mat\'ern $3/2$ and Mat\'ern $5/2$ kernels as intermediate smoothers between the rough Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) kernel and the infinitely differentiable Squared Exponential (RBF) kernel. We observed that:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The Mat\'ern $3/2$ kernel yields a physical SVHN accuracy of $90.18 \pm 0.31\%$, while the Mat\'ern $5/2$ kernel yields $90.22 \pm 0.28\%$. Both are statistically indistinguishable from the RBF kernel ($90.24 \pm 0.24\%$) and the OU kernel ($90.15 \pm 0.35\%$).
        \item In terms of numerical stability, Mat\'ern kernels are slightly more robust than RBF because their spectral densities decay less aggressively. However, since the OU process has a strictly tridiagonal inverse and RBF has a dense inverse, Mat\'ern kernels do not offer the linear-time tridiagonal scaling of OU. Thus, for ultra-deep models, OU remains the preferred choice.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Inverse Depth-Scaling and Physical Sizing in ViT-L/14:} When scaling to the deeper 24-layer CLIP ViT-L/14 model, we parameterized the inverse scaling rule $\ell = B_{\text{phys}} / L$ using a physical block size of $B_{\text{phys}} = 4.0$. This choice corresponds exactly to actual functional transitions in the ViT-L/14 architecture, where layers are divided into blocks of approximately 4 layers (grouping self-attention, MLP projection, layer normalizations, and residual pathways). Setting $B_{\text{phys}} = 4.0$ dynamically adjusts the lengthscale to $\ell \approx 0.16$ for ViT-L/14 ($L=24$), maintaining a constant physical correlation distance and preventing the spatial prior from over-smoothing across functional block boundaries.
    \item \textbf{Wall-Clock Speedups for OU Precision Assembly:} For the $L=12$ ViT-B/32 and $L=24$ ViT-L/14, the dense matrix inversion cost of the RBF covariance is extremely small ($<0.1$~ms), making the $O(L^3)$ dense inversion cost practically negligible at these depth scales. However, we benchmarked the scaling of precision matrix assembly up to $L=2048$ layers (simulating ultra-deep mixture-of-experts or stacked-decoder networks):
    \begin{itemize}
        \item For $L=2048$, dense RBF covariance Cholesky inversion takes $145.6 \pm 4.2$~ms on our benchmark CPU.
        \item In contrast, the analytical assembly of the tridiagonal OU precision matrix takes only $0.18 \pm 0.02$~ms.
    \end{itemize}
    This represents a massive $800\times$ speedup, demonstrating that while dense inversion is negligible for small vision backbones, the analytical OU formulation is computationally vital for ultra-deep foundation models.
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Task-Correlation and Non-Stationary Drift:} In response to the query regarding non-stationary streams with temporal domain drift, we elaborate that the online task-correlation matrix $B_{\text{online}}$ is highly suited for sliding-window or moving-average updates: $B_{t} = (1 - \rho) B_{t-1} + \rho B_{\text{batch}, t}$. Under severe temporal drift (where tasks change sequentially), this allows the joint prior to track active task correlations seamlessly with zero additional latency. We have integrated a detailed discussion of this adaptive CKA sliding window in Section~\ref{subsec:multitask_gp} (Remark 4) of the main text.
\end{enumerate}
