# 2. Novelty and Delta Assessment

## Key Novel Aspects
The paper introduces several distinct novel elements into the parameter-space model consolidation literature:
1. **Subspace-Projected Non-Diagonal Curvature:** The most significant theoretical contribution is demonstrating that while computing the full $D \times D$ Hessian of a deep neural network is intractable, we can capture the **full, non-diagonal, cross-parameter projected Hessian** with **zero diagonal approximation** by restricting our search strictly to the $K$-dimensional subspace spanned by the task vectors. This resolves a fundamental bottleneck of prior curvature-aware methods (such as Fisher Merging), which discard cross-parameter second-order couplings via diagonal approximations.
2. **Robust Finite-Difference Estimation with Gradient Subtraction:** The proposed finite-difference scheme incorporates explicit gradient subtraction ($\nabla \mathcal{L}_k(W_k + \epsilon v_j) - \nabla \mathcal{L}_k(W_k)$). This is a clever and elegant numerical technique that cancels out unperturbed residual gradients, ensuring complete noise immunity and bounding the scalar truncation error to $O(\epsilon)$ even for partially converged expert checkpoints.
3. **Lasso (L1) Regularized ACM via ISTA:** The formulation of an L1-regularized variant of the closed-form solver using the Iterative Soft-Thresholding Algorithm (ISTA) to induce sparsity in layer-wise merging coefficients.

## The 'Delta' from Prior Work
The proposed ACM framework positions itself with clear mathematical and operational deltas from existing paradigms:
* **Delta from Task Arithmetic (Uniform Scaling):** Instead of using a manual, globally uniform scaling factor ($\lambda^l_k = \lambda$) across all layers, ACM analytically solves for layer-wise optimal coefficients ($\Lambda^l$) based on local second-order loss-landscape geometry.
* **Delta from Fisher Merging (Diagonal Curvature):** Fisher Merging weights parameters using the diagonal of the Fisher Information Matrix, assuming parameters are decoupled. ACM models the full cross-parameter Hessian interactions within the task-vector subspace, capturing how the sensitivity of a parameter depends on surrounding parameters.
* **Delta from Test-Time Adaptation (AdaMerging, PolyMerge, RegCalMerge):** Instead of using slow, unstable, and transductively overfit gradient descent or zero-order search over unlabeled test streams to minimize prediction entropy, ACM solves for the optimal merging coefficients analytically in a single, training-free calibration step taking less than 5 seconds.

## Characterization of Novelty
* **Theoretical Novelty: Significant.** The mathematical reformulation of layer-wise quadratic minimization in a restricted task-vector subspace, the proof of its unique global minimizer under Ridge regularization, and the derivation of the $O(\epsilon)$ truncation error bound under gradient subtraction represent a high degree of mathematical elegance and formal rigor.
* **Empirical Novelty: Incremental to Low.** Despite the extensive mathematical framing, the actual empirical performance delta on physical models is highly disappointing. Vanilla ACM ($60.89\%$) barely outperforms a standard, globally uniform, tuned Task Arithmetic baseline ($60.72\%$) by a negligible $+0.17\%$ absolute accuracy. More egregiously, all the "proposed" advanced variants (ACM-Norm at $58.89\%$, ACM-GlobalNorm at $57.76\%$, and Lasso ACM-GlobalNorm at $57.52\%$) actually perform **significantly worse** than Task Arithmetic. This reveals that the practical novelty of the curvature-aware formulation is severely constrained by the "local-global optimization gap" (the breakdown of the local Taylor approximation over large task-vector steps), which remains unresolved in this work.
