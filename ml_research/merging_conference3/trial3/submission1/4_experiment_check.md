# 4. Experimental Validation and Results Check

This paper features an exceptionally thorough empirical design, evaluating several models and configurations across four multi-axial directions. While the results are compelling and support most of the authors' claims, a highly critical examination reveals a few potential experimental flaws and interpretation biases.

---

## 1. Key Strengths of the Experimental Validation

* **Exhaustive Calibration Stream Size Sweep:** Axis 1 sweeps calibration sizes from $N=1$ to $N=64$. The discovery that larger calibration sets fail to resolve performance collapse, and that standard FP16 search followed by post-hoc quantization (**Quantized AdaMerging** at 30.00%) substantially outperforms direct quantization-aware optimization (**Q-Merge** at 26.25%), is a robust and highly high-signal result that directly challenges the field.
* **Intelligent Cross-Schema Matrix:** Axis 2 covers 20 different source-target evaluation combinations. This systematic approach allows the authors to identify which specific quantization parameters (e.g., granularity vs. symmetry) trigger the most severe overfitting.
* **Rigorous Optimizer Comparison:** Axis 3 evaluates unregularized STE, TV-regularized STE, and 1+1 ES. This comparison clearly exposes the "Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox" — that derivative-free search (1+1 ES) achieves the highest source performance (20.75%) but overfits the most intensely, expanding the generalization gap.
* **Realistic Stream Stress-Tests:** Axis 4 evaluates OOD input noise and severe Gini label skew. Integrating randomized smoothing concepts to mathematically explain why input noise acts as a stochastic regularizer is brilliant.
* **Decoupling Data Scarcity from Entropy Collapse:** Proposing a supervised cross-entropy baseline at $N=16$ (achieving 35.00% clean and 23.75% skewed) is a vital control experiment. It proves that the collapse under small streams or class skew is a failure of the unsupervised entropy objective, not a pure data-size limit.

---

## 2. Key Weaknesses & Potential Empirical Flaws

* **Subspace Representation Degradation and the Generalization Illusion in PoCs:** In Section 4.5, the authors evaluate whether architectural choices (ResNet-18) and subspace-constrained merging (LoRA-like low-rank projection via SVD) can mitigate the Cross-Schema Generalization Gap. They report a dramatically smaller gap for ResNet-18 ($-4.25\%$) and a positive gap for the Low-Rank Subspace ($+0.50\%$) compared to full-parameter ViT-Tiny ($-7.76\%$), concluding that low-rank constraints act as powerful structural defenses.
  * *Critical Flaw:* While the authors successfully trained and configured these models to stay above the 10.00% random-guess floor (ResNet-18 at 21.25% matched / 17.00% mismatched; Low-Rank at 13.00% matched / 13.50% mismatched), the absolute performance of the Low-Rank Subspace model is exceptionally poor (13.00% matched accuracy). This represents a catastrophic loss of information compared to the unquantized FP16 Task Arithmetic baseline (35.12%) and the individual experts (>90%).
  * *The "Low-Capacity" Generalization Illusion:* Performing a global SVD projection on full-parameter task vectors to project them into a rank-4 subspace is highly destructive, stripping away vital task-specific representation directions. The resulting model is so heavily degraded that its output probability distributions are nearly flat (approaching random noise). A model with such low representational capacity and small dynamic range is naturally less sensitive to quantization-operator mismatches because its activations have minimal variance. Thus, the closed generalization gap (+0.50%) is likely a "low-capacity generalization illusion" rather than an active, robust alignment of expert weights.
  * *Proxy Inadequacy:* Post-hoc global SVD task vector projection is a poor proxy for actual natively-trained Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (such as LoRA, where $W = W_0 + BA$). Natively-trained LoRA models preserve high performance (>80%) under low rank because their low-rank adapters are optimized end-to-end. The authors must evaluate actual natively-trained LoRA experts to confirm whether the Cross-Schema Generalization Gap remains closed as performance scales, or if the overfitting reappears once model capacity is restored.
* **The High-Interference Ceiling Bias:** The Task Arithmetic baseline in this paper is **35.12%** (compared to individual experts at $>90\%$), representing an extremely fractured weight-space landscape. 
  * While this is a highly challenging setup, it remains unclear whether the catastrophic cross-schema collapse is a fundamental property of quantization-aware model merging in general, or is primarily an artifact of this extreme task-interference regime. If the experts were pre-aligned (e.g., using weight decay or joint training, which the authors briefly noted yields 18.50% target accuracy) or fine-tuned using parameter-efficient adapters, the initial merged model would start from a much higher ceiling. Evaluating the cross-schema matrix under a "high-cooperation" (low-interference) regime would clarify whether the generalization gap remains severe when weight-space alignment is already strong.
* **Absence of Quantitative Verification on Larger Backbones:** Although the paper includes a lengthy discussion in Section 5 about how these findings scale to multi-billion parameter LLMs/VLMs and alternative post-training quantization schemes (such as AWQ, GPTQ, or SmoothQuant), there is zero quantitative data on larger models. Even a moderate-scale experiment (e.g., on a `ViT-Base` or `DeiT-S` backbone) would strengthen the scaling arguments and prove that cross-schema collapse is not an artifact of the lightweight `ViT-Tiny` backbone.
