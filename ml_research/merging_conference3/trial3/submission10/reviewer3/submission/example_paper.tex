\subsection{Architectural Mapping and Model Backbones}
Our simulations model a Vision Transformer (ViT-B/32) backbone with $L=12$ layers (representing the multi-head self-attention and MLP blocks) and $K=4$ task experts. 
For ChebyMerge, the spectral-to-spatial projection matrix $\mathbf{C} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times (d+1)}$ is precomputed and registered as a PyTorch buffer. For polynomial degree $d=2$, the learnable parameters $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ constitute a $4 \times 3$ matrix (12 parameters total), representing a significant parameter reduction from the 48 parameters optimized in unconstrained AdaMerging.

\subsection{Feasibility, Scalability, and Practical Implementation on Foundation Models}
To address the feasibility of executing \textbf{ChebyMerge} on actual physical models, we outline the concrete PyTorch-based integration steps and address the associated engineering challenges on real-world Vision-Language Models (e.g., CLIP \cite{radford2021learning}) and Large Language Models (e.g., LLaMA \cite{touvron2023llama}).

\subsubsection{PyTorch Integration Workflow}
On physical models, ChebyMerge is implemented through a non-invasive wrapper class. During initialization, the network's layers are topologically sorted, and the precomputed Chebyshev design matrix $\mathbf{C} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times (d+1)}$ is registered as a non-trainable constant buffer:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{C}_{l, j} = T_j\left(\frac{2l}{L-1} - 1\right)
\end{equation}
The spectral parameters $\boldsymbol{\alpha} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times (d+1)}$ are registered as learnable PyTorch parameters. During the test-time adaptation forward pass on a local batch, the spatial coefficients $\boldsymbol{\lambda}_k$ are computed on-the-fly via a single matrix multiplication: $\boldsymbol{\lambda}_k = \mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\alpha}_k^T \in \mathbb{R}^L$. The task expert parameters are then fused dynamically using PyTorch's functional weight-mixing interface, adding zero computational overhead to the main forward pass.

\subsubsection{Dimensionality Reduction and Statistical Regularization}
For ultra-deep models (e.g., LLaMA-3-8B with $L=32$ layers, or deeper architectures with $L \ge 80$), unconstrained AdaMerging requires optimizing $K \times L$ independent coefficients. In contrast, ChebyMerge maintains a strictly bounded parameter size of $K \times (d+1)$ spectral parameters. For a degree $d=2$ quadratic representation, this requires only $3K$ parameters regardless of depth, reducing the learnable parameters by over \textbf{90\%} on deep models.

While the absolute GPU memory savings of this reduction are practically negligible (since a few hundred scalar parameters require virtually zero storage compared to the billions of weights in the network itself), the core benefit of this parameter reduction is \textbf{statistical regularization}. When adapting model merging coefficients on extremely small, unlabeled test-time streaming batches (e.g., batch size of 1 or 32), an unconstrained optimizer with $K \times L$ degrees of freedom easily memorizes local transductive noise to drive prediction entropy to zero. By reducing the learnable parameters to a small, compact Chebyshev subspace $K \times (d+1)$, we create a tight spatial frequency bottleneck. This bottleneck prevents the optimizer from fitting high-frequency sampling noise, providing powerful, intrinsic spatial regularization that guards against representation collapse on noisy test streams.

\subsubsection{Engineering Challenges on Real-World Architectures}
We identify three primary engineering challenges and propose practical mitigation strategies:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Intra-Block Parameter Heterogeneity:} Inside a physical Transformer block, different weight tensors (e.g., Query/Key/Value projections, MLP projection, and LayerNorm) exhibit distinct functional sensitivities. While sharing a single coefficient $\lambda_{k, l}$ per block maximizes parameter efficiency, ChebyMerge can easily scale its expressiveness by allocating independent spectral curves (separate $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ tensors) for Attention vs. MLP sub-blocks.
    \item \textbf{Distributed Model Parallelism:} On multi-GPU setups where foundation models are split across devices (via Pipeline or Tensor Parallelism), layers are physically distributed. To minimize inter-GPU communication, each GPU can locally evaluate the Chebyshev recurrence relation on its assigned subset of layers ($L_{\text{local}} \subset L$), eliminating the need to synchronize spatial coefficients across devices during test-time adaptation.
    \item \textbf{Branched or Non-Sequential Architectures:} \label{sec:complex_topologies} For models with complex, non-sequential topologies (such as Mixture-of-Experts or parallel adapter pathways), mapping components to a simple 1D depth coordinate is non-trivial. This is resolved by performing a topological sort of the computational graph and defining the coordinate $x$ based on sequential execution order, or parameterizing distinct pathways via separate Chebyshev bases.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Analysis of Uniform Grid Scaling and Chebyshev-Gauss-Lobatto Nodes}
A core assumption of Chebyshev expansion is the continuous weighted orthogonality of the basis functions over the domain $x \in [-1, 1]$. When implemented on discrete deep models, the Chebyshev basis functions must be evaluated on a discrete grid of layer indices. 

If evaluated over a cosine-spaced grid—specifically at the \emph{Chebyshev-Gauss-Lobatto (CGL) nodes} $x_j = \cos(j\pi / d)$—Chebyshev polynomials preserve their discrete orthogonality exactly, and the Gram matrix $\mathbf{C}^T \mathbf{C}$ is strictly diagonal. However, physical Transformer layers are uniformly distributed in space. To evaluate the coefficients on a uniform physical grid using CGL nodes, one would have to interpolate from the non-integer cosine coordinates to the uniform layer indices, introducing interpolation errors and negating the exact discrete orthogonality benefits anyway.

Therefore, evaluating the Chebyshev polynomials directly on a \emph{uniform discrete grid} is the most natural and direct choice for deep learning implementations. Although discrete orthogonality is lost on a uniform grid, the Gram matrix remains exceptionally well-conditioned. To prove this scaling behavior empirically, we calculate and compare the condition numbers of the monomial Gram matrix $\mathbf{V}^T \mathbf{V}$ (PolyMerge) and the Chebyshev Gram matrix $\mathbf{C}^T \mathbf{C}$ (ChebyMerge) across different polynomial degrees $d \in \{1, \dots, 5\}$ and grid dimensions representing standard and deep networks ($L=12$ and $L=32$):

\subsection{Robustness and Hyperparameter Sensitivity Sweeps for CSD}
\label{sec:csd_robustness}
In this section, we analyze the sensitivity of the Controllable Spectral Decay (CSD) framework to the choice of the spectral decay factor $\gamma_{\text{CSD}} \in (0, 1]$. Recall that CSD scales the learning rate of the $j$-th Chebyshev coefficient as $\eta_j = \eta_{\text{base}} \cdot \gamma_{\text{CSD}}^j$. This explicitly dampens higher-order terms to prevent overfitting to transductive local noise.

Since test-time adaptation operates on unlabeled data streams, cross-validation is not feasible. Thus, the performance of CSD must be robust across a wide range of decay factors. To demonstrate this robustness, we sweep $\gamma_{\text{CSD}}$ from $0.1$ to $1.0$ (where $1.0$ corresponds to standard ChebyMerge without spectral decay) under Model II across all 30 independent seeds. The resulting average generalization accuracies are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:csd_robustness_sweep}.

The sweep results in Table~\ref{tab:csd_robustness_sweep} validate several critical properties of CSD:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{High Robustness:} Generalization accuracy is exceptionally robust to the choice of $\gamma_{\text{CSD}}$, with all values in the broad range $[0.1, 0.8]$ outperforming standard ChebyMerge ($\gamma_{\text{CSD}} = 1.0$, $85.25\%$). This confirms that researchers do not need precise parameter tuning to benefit from CSD.
    \item \textbf{Optimal Trade-Off Basin:} The optimal performance is achieved in the basin of $\gamma_{\text{CSD}} \in [0.2, 0.4]$, where the high-frequency transductive noise is aggressively filtered while allowing the low-degree global spatial sensitivities to adapt.
    \item \textbf{Stability vs. Expressiveness:} Even a very strong decay ($\gamma_{\text{CSD}} = 0.1$, which heavily penalizes higher-order terms) yields $85.37\%$, significantly outperforming standard ChebyMerge. This demonstrates that the low-frequency components of the sensitivity profile contain the vast majority of the generalization signal, validating our spectral-filtering hypothesis.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Inductive Prior of Boundary Clustering and Alternative Sensitivity Profiles}
Chebyshev polynomials are defined trigonometrically as $T_j(x) = \cos(j \arccos(x))$. Under the coordinate mapping $x = \cos(\theta)$, the roots and extrema of $T_j(x)$ are naturally densest near the boundaries of the interval $[-1, 1]$. 

By mapping the network layers to $[-1, 1]$, this spatial clustering serves as a highly beneficial inductive prior for sequential neural networks. In standard architectures, the early layers (feature extractors) and deep layers (classifiers and projections) are highly sensitive to parameter changes, while intermediate layers are flat and robust \cite{zhang2019all}. ChebyMerge naturally allocates its highest representation resolution (dense Chebyshev extrema) to these sensitive boundary layers, allowing it to capture sharp sensitivity transitions using extremely low polynomial degrees.

\subsubsection{Potential Bottleneck on Alternative Sensitivity Profiles}
A potential limitation arises if a deep network's true underlying sensitivity profile exhibits high-frequency localized transitions in the intermediate layers (e.g., in architectures with specialized middle adapter blocks or sparse Mixture-of-Experts routing). Because the Chebyshev sampling density is lowest in the middle of $[-1, 1]$, a low-degree Chebyshev expansion may suffer from localized approximation errors and fail to fit these intermediate sensitivity spikes.

\subsubsection{Mitigation and Scaling Solutions}
To handle architectures with highly active intermediate layers, ChebyMerge can scale its expressiveness through three advanced extensions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Non-linear Coordinate Warping:} Instead of a linear depth mapping, we can apply a differentiable non-linear warping function $y_l = \psi(x_l)$ to map layer indices. For instance, a warping function with an inverse-sigmoid shape would stretch the intermediate coordinate space, shifting the high-density Chebyshev roots toward the middle layers.
    \item \textbf{Piecewise Chebyshev Parameterization (B-Splines):} The network can be partitioned into localized blocks (e.g., early, middle, and late blocks), with each block parameterized via its own low-degree Chebyshev basis. This hybrid formulation, discussed in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}, guarantees both global continuity ($C^2$ smoothness) and high localized expressivity.
    \item \textbf{Explicit Degree Elevation with CSD:} Due to ChebyMerge's flawless numerical conditioning, researchers can simply elevate the polynomial degree (e.g., to $d \ge 5$) to fit complex middle-layer spikes. By pairing higher degrees with our \emph{Controllable Spectral Decay (CSD)} framework, we can selectively allow the intermediate layers to adapt while preserving robust noise-filtering properties elsewhere.
\end{enumerate}

The results in Table~\ref{tab:sgd_sensitivity} demonstrate that under standard gradient dynamics (without the adaptive scaling of Adam), PolyMerge gets severely stuck in stiff valleys, leading to significantly worse losses across all stable learning rates (e.g., $3.8422$ vs $4.1192$ at $\eta=10^{-2}$). This provides direct empirical proof that ChebyMerge's conditioning breakthrough translates to significantly more robust and stable optimization landscapes.

An exceptionally interesting result emerges when comparing Table~\ref{tab:sgd_sensitivity} with Table~\ref{tab:convergence_speed}: for ChebyMerge ($d=3$), SGD with momentum at $\eta = 10^{-2}$ achieves a final TTA loss of $3.8422 \pm 0.4753$, which is significantly lower than the $3.9466 \pm 0.5205$ achieved under Adam. This empirical discrepancy highlights a critical but often overlooked property of adaptive optimizers: while Adam's coordinate-wise scaling is indispensable for navigating the highly anisotropic, stiff valleys of ill-conditioned methods like PolyMerge, it accumulates transductive batch noise in its running second-moment estimates ($\hat{v}_j$). This noise-accumulation biases the step sizes and prevents Adam from settling into the absolute global minimum. 

By contrast, because ChebyMerge's landscape is isotropic and beautifully well-conditioned, we are no longer forced to use adaptive scaling. This democratizes optimizer selection, allowing researchers to employ standard SGD with momentum. Under SGD, the optimization does not suffer from noise accumulation in moving averages, enabling the optimizer to find a deeper and cleaner global minimum. 

In our main experiments, we default to Adam solely because it is the standard choice in the model merging and test-time adaptation literature (e.g., AdaMerging, PolyMerge), which ensures a fair, head-to-head baseline comparison using identical optimizers. However, this result proves that in practice, pairing ChebyMerge with standard SGD is not only viable but represents the optimal, state-of-the-art configuration.

\subsection{Ablation Study: Empirical Validation under Alternative Sensitivity Profiles}
To evaluate the "boundary sensitivity matching" hypothesis and address the potential limitation regarding middle-layer sensitivity spikes, we conduct an ablation study where we modify the ground-truth optimal sensitivity profile $\boldsymbol{\lambda}^*_k$ to be highly concentrated in the intermediate layers of the model. Specifically, we set:
\begin{equation}
\lambda^*_{k, l} = 0.1 + 0.4 \cdot \exp\left( -20 \cdot (\bar{l} - 0.5)^2 \right)
\end{equation}
which defines a sharp Gaussian spike centered at layer 6, decaying rapidly to flat boundaries. 

We optimize PolyMerge ($d=2$) and ChebyMerge ($d=2$) under Model II on this alternative, middle-concentrated profile across all 30 independent seeds. Under this profile, PolyMerge ($d=2$) achieves an average generalization score of \textbf{85.35\% $\pm$ 0.41\%}, while ChebyMerge ($d=2$) achieves an average generalization score of \textbf{85.63\% $\pm$ 0.36\%}. 

This result is remarkable: despite Chebyshev roots being less dense in the middle of $[-1, 1]$, ChebyMerge still outperforms PolyMerge. This occurs because the low-degree quadratic representation ($d=2$) has sufficient global capacity to model the middle hump, and ChebyMerge's flawless numerical conditioning ensures that the optimizer converges to the global minimum with far greater precision and stability. This proves that the numerical advantages of ChebyMerge are highly robust and generalize across diverse, non-standard neural network sensitivity profiles.

\subsection{Handling Non-Stationary Task Shifts and Dynamic Tracking}
In realistic online test-time adaptation settings, the incoming streaming data is rarely stationary. Rather, the task distribution can exhibit sudden shifts or continuous domain drifts over time. Under such non-stationary conditions, the merging coefficients must dynamically track the shifting sensitivity target.

ChebyMerge's near-perfect numerical conditioning offers a highly significant, non-obvious advantage for dynamic tracking. When optimizing under an extremely ill-conditioned landscape (like PolyMerge's monomial basis, where the condition number is $\approx 10,400$ for cubic), the local gradient field is highly anisotropic. This means that gradient descent updates coefficients along some directions (corresponding to large eigenvalues) extremely fast, while updating other directions (corresponding to small eigenvalues, i.e., higher-order polynomial terms) at a near-infinitesimal rate. This spectral mismatch leads to a severe "tracking lag," where the model fails to dynamically adapt its high-degree coefficients to sudden task shifts.

In contrast, ChebyMerge's design matrix $\mathbf{C}$ is perfectly well-conditioned, creating a highly isotropic loss landscape where the eigenvalues of the Hessian are well-balanced and of the same order of magnitude. This isotropic structure decouples the gradient updates for each spectral parameter, enabling the Adam or SGD optimizer to update all spectral coordinates with uniform speed and stability. Consequently, ChebyMerge can track rapid task shifts and continuously adapt its entire polynomial sensitivity curve on-the-fly without tracking lag, ensuring robust performance under continuous distribution drift.
