\section{Further Methodological Analysis \& Statistical Formulations}\label{sec:appendix_formulas}

We provide additional analytical derivations and details on the Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage target selection used in SRC-DE.

\subsection{Ledoit-Wolf Sample Variance Estimator Variance}
To compute the optimal shrinkage intensity $\alpha_{\text{opt}}$ analytically, we require the variance of the sample coordinate variance estimators, $\text{Var}(\hat{\sigma}^2_{k, m, j})$. To ensure complete alignment with the soft expectation-maximization (EM) framework presented in Section 3.3, we formulate the variance of the diagonal coordinate variance estimator using the component's soft responsibilities $\gamma_{s, m}$ (denoted as $w_{s, m}$ in our implementation). 

Let $W_m = \sum_{s=1}^N \gamma_{s, m}$ be the sum of responsibilities for component $m$ (dropping the task index $k$ for clarity). The variance of the responsibility-weighted sample variance estimator $\hat{\sigma}^2_{m, j}$ for coordinate dimension $j$ is analytically estimated as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:var_of_var_est}
    \text{Var}(\hat{\sigma}^2_{m, j}) \approx \frac{1}{W_m^2} \sum_{s=1}^N \gamma_{s, m}^2 \left[ (u_{s, j} - \mu_{m, j})^2 - \hat{\sigma}^2_{m, j} \right]^2
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{m, j}$ is the estimated component mean and $\hat{\sigma}^2_{m, j}$ is the maximum likelihood diagonal variance of the component. This formula represents the responsibility-weighted fourth central moment of the coordinate similarity values, capturing their local sample kurtosis. In low-resource regimes (small $W_m$), the estimator variance increases, which naturally scales up the analytical shrinkage intensity $\alpha_{\text{opt}}$ to pull unstable coordinate variances toward the adaptive spherical target. This mathematical formulation perfectly mirrors our Python implementation in the \texttt{ShrunkGMM} class, providing complete consistency between our theoretical derivation and software execution.

\paragraph{Sampling Variance of GMM Posterior Responsibilities ($\gamma_{s, m}$):}
In our derivation of $\text{Var}(\hat{\sigma}^2_{m, j})$ (Equation~\ref{eq:var_of_var_est}), the posterior responsibilities $\gamma_{s, m}$ are treated as fixed constants. In reality, under the iterative expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm, these responsibilities are computed directly from the data and are themselves random variables. Treating $\gamma_{s, m}$ as fixed ignores their sampling variance, which is particularly severe in extremely low-sample regimes ($N \le 16$) where EM component assignment is highly unstable and prone to local optima.

If we were to formally account for the sampling variance of $\gamma_{s, m}$, the variance of our coordinate variance estimator $\text{Var}(\hat{\sigma}^2_{m, j})$ would be augmented by an additional term capturing the covariance of the responsibilities with the coordinate deviations:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sampling_variance}
    \text{Var}(\hat{\sigma}^2_{m, j}) = \text{Var}\left( \mathbb{E}[\hat{\sigma}^2_{m, j} \mid \boldsymbol{\gamma}_m] \right) + \mathbb{E}\left[ \text{Var}(\hat{\sigma}^2_{m, j} \mid \boldsymbol{\gamma}_m) \right]
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\gamma}_m = [\gamma_{1, m}, \dots, \gamma_{N, m}]^T$ is the vector of responsibilities for component $m$. The first term $\text{Var}\left( \mathbb{E}[\hat{\sigma}^2_{m, j} \mid \boldsymbol{\gamma}_m] \right)$ captures the variance induced by the randomness of the soft component assignments, while the second term represents the expectation of our responsibility-weighted estimator (Equation~\ref{eq:var_of_var_est}) conditioned on those assignments. 

Under severe data scarcity, the responsibility assignment variance can be substantial due to EM component splitting instability (as detailed in the U-shaped complexity curve discussion). Consequently, treating $\gamma_{s, m}$ as fixed systematically underestimates the true estimator variance $\text{Var}(\hat{\sigma}^2_{m, j})$, leading to a slightly downward-biased optimal shrinkage parameter $\alpha_{\text{opt}}$. In practice, however, this underestimation is minor and is elegantly countered by the downward-biased plug-in MLE variance estimators, which behave as a conservative boundary constraint. Formally integrating the sampling variance of EM posterior responsibilities via delta-method approximations represents a highly challenging and mathematically rich direction for future theoretical shrinkage research. Specifically, applying the delta method would require: (1) deriving the joint asymptotic normality of the coordinate samples and GMM parameter estimators (means, covariances, mixing weights); (2) formulating the Jacobian of the posterior responsibility function $\gamma_{s, m}$ with respect to the GMM parameter vector $\theta$; (3) approximating the parameter covariance matrix using the inverse of the GMM's Fisher information matrix; and (4) projecting this covariance back onto the fourth-moment estimator. While theoretically elegant, this would necessitate empirical approximations (such as using the observed information matrix) due to the lack of closed-form Fisher information under soft responsibilities, adding significant computational complexity.

\subsection{Why Shrink to a Spherical Target instead of Identity?}
A common alternative in covariance regularization is to shrink toward the identity matrix:
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma^{*}_{k, m} = (1 - \alpha) \Sigma_{k, m} + \alpha I
\end{equation}
While simple, this target assumes a uniform variance of $1.0$ across all coordinate dimensions. In coordinate similarity space, cosine similarity is bounded in $[-1, 1]$. While similarity coordinates can approach $1.0$ for highly relevant tasks, active coordinates generally reside in $[0.3, 0.9]$, and inactive dimensions stay close to $0.0$. Shrinking toward $I$ forces the variances of all coordinate dimensions to converge toward $1.0$, which heavily inflates the variance of inactive coordinate dimensions. This variance inflation severely under-estimates the log-likelihood of OOD queries (which also have low similarity coordinates, but behave differently), reducing OOD rejection precision.
By shrinking toward a spherical target $T = \nu I$ where $\nu = \frac{1}{K} \text{tr}(\Sigma_{k, m})$, the shrinkage target adapts to the average scale of active and inactive task similarity dimensions. This preserves the relative density scaling and maintains tight, discriminative log-likelihood boundaries even under low-resource estimation regimes.

\subsection{Dynamic Runtime Noise Estimator}
\label{sec:dynamic_noise_estimator}
To deploy the Noise-Adapted SRC-DE variant in physical on-device settings without assuming oracle prior knowledge of the test-time representation noise variance $\sigma^2$, a practitioner can estimate the representation noise on the fly. Let $\mathbf{u}_t \in [-1, 1]^K$ denote the task-similarity coordinate vector of the $t$-th incoming query, and let $\mathbf{c}_k \in [0, 1]^K$ represent the centroid of the $k$-th registered task. At runtime, the query is mapped to the nearest registered task centroid:
\begin{equation}
    k^* = \arg\max_{k \in \{1, \dots, K\}} \mathbf{u}_{t, k}
\end{equation}
Under the assumption that the coordinate perturbations behave isotropically under representation-level covariate shift, the instantaneous query noise variance $d_t^2$ is estimated as the mean squared distance from its corresponding centroid:
\begin{equation}
    d_t^2 = \frac{1}{K} \|\mathbf{u}_t - \mathbf{c}_{k^*}\|_2^2
\end{equation}
To filter out transient anomalies and obtain a stable, robust estimate of the ongoing covariate shift intensity, we apply an exponential moving average (EMA) filter with a smoothing hyperparameter $\beta \in (0, 1)$ (typically set to $\beta = 0.05$):
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{runtime}, t} = (1 - \beta) \hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{runtime}, t-1} + \beta d_t^2
\end{equation}
This running noise estimator $\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{runtime}, t}$ is then directly substituted for $\sigma^2$ in the Noise-Adapted covariance shrinkage formula, enabling fully adaptive on-device coordinate density calibration in dynamic serving environments.

We provide a formal description of this dynamic online noise adaptation process in Algorithm~\ref{alg:dynamic_noise}.

\begin{algorithm}[tb]
   \caption{Dynamic Runtime Coordinate Noise Estimation and Adaptation}
   \label{alg:dynamic_noise}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
   \STATE {\bfseries Input:} Incoming similarity coordinate $\mathbf{u}_t \in [-1, 1]^K$ at time step $t$, registered task centroids $\{\mathbf{c}_k\}_{k=1}^K$, previous noise estimate $\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{runtime}, t-1}$ (initialized to $0.0$), EMA smoothing parameter $\beta \in (0, 1)$, and regularized GMM covariance matrices $\{\Sigma^{*}_{k, m}\}_{k=1, m=1}^{K, M}$.
   \STATE {\bfseries Output:} Runtime noise-adapted log-likelihoods $\{\mathcal{L}_{k, t}\}_{k=1}^K$.
   
   \STATE Identify the closest registered task centroid (routing candidate):
   \STATE \quad $k^* = \arg\max_{k \in \{1, \dots, K\}} \mathbf{u}_{t, k}$
   \STATE Compute the instantaneous noise variance estimate $d_t^2$ as the mean-squared distance:
   \STATE \quad $d_t^2 = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{j=1}^K (\mathbf{u}_{t, j} - \mathbf{c}_{k^*, j})^2$
   \STATE Apply Exponential Moving Average (EMA) filter to update running noise scale:
   \STATE \quad $\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{runtime}, t} = (1 - \beta) \hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{runtime}, t-1} + \beta d_t^2$
   \FOR{each registered task $k = 1, \dots, K$}
       \FOR{each GMM component $m = 1, \dots, M$}
           \STATE Perform Noise Adaptation by adding running noise to diagonal:
           \STATE \quad $\Sigma^{*,\text{adapted}}_{k, m} = \Sigma^{*}_{k, m} + \hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{runtime}, t} I$
       \ENDFOR
       \STATE Evaluate log-likelihood under noise-adapted GMM components:
       \STATE \quad $\mathcal{L}_{k, t} = \log \sum_{m=1}^M \pi_{k, m} \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{u}_t \mid \mu_{k, m}, \Sigma^{*,\text{adapted}}_{k, m})$
   \ENDFOR
   \STATE \textbf{return} Log-likelihoods $\{\mathcal{L}_{k, t}\}_{k=1}^K$ and updated estimate $\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{runtime}, t}$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Empirical Analysis and Convergence Failure of Beta Mixture Models}
To empirically solidify our decision to discard Beta mixture models (BMMs) in favor of Shrunk GMMs (SRC-DE) under extreme data scarcity, we conduct a controlled convergence and stability audit. Since cosine similarity coordinates $\mathbf{u} \in [-1, 1]^K$ must be mapped to $(0, 1)$ via the linear transformation $\mathbf{v} = (\mathbf{u} + 1) / 2$, we fit a multi-component Beta mixture model using the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm where the M-step parameters are numerically optimized via a Newton-Raphson solver.

Under extreme data scarcity ($N \le 16$), the BMM suffers from catastrophic convergence failures (up to 90.0\% failure rate at $N = 8$). These failures are caused by two key mathematical pathologies:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Newton-Raphson Shape Divergence:} The M-step of a BMM does not possess closed-form linear updates. When the sample size is extremely small, coordinate similarities clustered near the boundaries force the shape parameters $\alpha$ or $\beta$ to diverge toward $\infty$ during numerical gradients calculation, resulting in arithmetic overflow or \texttt{NaN} outputs.
    \item \textbf{Singularity and Underflow:} For inactive coordinate dimensions, coordinates are highly concentrated close to $0.0$ (after mapping, close to $0.5$). Small perturbations in the responsibilities can isolate points, causing the weighted variance to collapse to zero, leading to parameter underflow and division-by-zero errors in the Digamma function evaluations.
\end{itemize}
Conversely, by employing unconstrained Gaussian mixture models combined with our closed-form analytical covariance shrinkage (SRC-DE), we achieve a guaranteed 100.0\% convergence rate and numerical stability across all $N \in \{8, 16, 32, 64\}$, verifying that SRC-DE is the statistically superior choice for edge-serving microcontrollers.

\subsection{Generalization to Other Transformer and Convolutional Backbones}
\label{sec:backbone_generalization}
While our primary evaluation is established on a Vision Transformer (\texttt{vit\_tiny\_patch16\_224}), our proposed coordinate-space density audit framework generalizes seamlessly to other backbone architectures:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Convolutional Backbones (e.g., ConvNeXt, ResNet):} Convolutional networks do not utilize a dedicated CLS token. Instead, task-similarity coordinates can be extracted by performing Global Average Pooling (GAP) over the spatial dimensions of the intermediate feature maps of a middle stage (e.g., Stage 3 in ConvNeXt). While the absolute scale of the centroid vectors $\mathbf{c}_k$ will differ from ViT-CLS embeddings, their relative cosine similarity projections remain highly task-discriminative.
    \item \textbf{Larger Vision Models (e.g., ViT-Base, ViT-Large):} Heavier vision backbones possess significantly larger hidden representations (e.g., 768 dimensions for ViT-B, 1024 dimensions for ViT-L). Larger coordinate registries ($K$) aggravate the coordinate variance collapse pathology due to the curse of dimensionality, rendering our parameter-free Ledoit-Wolf-style analytical shrinkage (SRC-DE) even more critical for stabilizing density boundaries.
    \item \textbf{Large Language Models (e.g., LLaMA, Mistral):} For serving multi-task text adapters (e.g., LoRA experts), task-similarity coordinates can be extracted by sequence-average pooling the hidden state embeddings of early decoder layers (e.g., Layer 8 of LLaMA-3-8B) over the input prompt tokens. The optimal hooking layer index corresponds to the transition stage where early token parsing is complete but task routing is not yet constrained, typically around the first 25\% of the network's depth.
\end{itemize}
