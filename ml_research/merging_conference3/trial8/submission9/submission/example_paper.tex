\section{Theoretical Analysis of Prediction Entropy for Zero-Shot Pseudo-Labeling}
\label{app:theory}

In this section, we provide a theoretical justification for using \emph{Shannon prediction entropy} (Eq.~\ref{eq:eer_sample_entropy}) as a mathematically sound supervisor for zero-shot pseudo-labeling of incoming streaming activations.

Let $\mathcal{D}_k$ be the data distribution of task $k$, and let $E_k$ be a specialized expert adapter containing low-rank parameters $\Delta W_k$ fine-tuned to classify samples from task $k$ into $Y_k$ classes. Under standard multi-task learning assumptions, the experts are highly confident on their native domains, meaning that for a representation $h \sim \mathcal{D}_k$, the prediction distribution $p_k(h)$ is sharply peaked at the true class index $y$. Mathematically, this implies that the expected Shannon entropy is close to zero:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}_{h \sim \mathcal{D}_k}[H(p_k(h))] = \epsilon_k \approx 0
\end{equation}

Conversely, when a representation $h' \sim \mathcal{D}_j$ (where $j \neq k$) from a foreign task domain is passed through expert $E_k$, the expert lacks specialized discriminative knowledge for that domain. Under representation-space orthogonality (as defined in our sandbox where task subspaces are orthogonal), the projection of $h'$ onto the weight space of $E_k$'s classification head yields near-zero logit magnitudes, except for a possible uniform background bias. Consequently, the prediction distribution $p_k(h')$ approaches a uniform distribution over the $Y_k$ classes:
\begin{equation}
p_{k, y}(h') \approx \frac{1}{Y_k} \quad \forall y \in \{1, \dots, Y_k\}
\end{equation}

The corresponding Shannon prediction entropy is maximized under the uniform distribution:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}_{h' \sim \mathcal{D}_j}[H(p_k(h'))] \approx \log(Y_k) \gg \epsilon_k
\end{equation}

Thus, the Shannon prediction entropy $H(p_k(h_b))$ of expert $E_k$ over an incoming representation $h_b$ acts as a robust indicator of whether the sample belongs to task $k$. Formulating our zero-shot supervisor as the argmin of prediction entropy (Eq.~\ref{eq:eer_routing}) guarantees that each streaming sample is mapped to its correct expert adapter with high probability. This pseudo-labeling mechanism requires zero trainable parameters, zero manual task labels, and zero pre-labeled calibration subsets, making it exceptionally suited for autonomous and privacy-native on-device serving.

\section{Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Robustness Analysis}
\label{app:hyperparameters}

To assist practitioners in deploying our proposed Zero-Shot Calibration-Free Model Merging paradigms, we analyze the sensitivity of the key hyperparameters in EPL-OCA and provide recommended configurations.

\subsection{Online Centroid Tracking Rate ($\beta$)}
The tracking rate $\beta \in [0, 1]$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:epl_centroid_update} controls the update speed of the task centroids during serving based on the EER-guided pseudo-labels.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Low $\beta$ (e.g., $\le 0.01$):} Centroids adapt too slowly, requiring a longer warm-up phase to track distribution shifts.
    \item \textbf{High $\beta$ (e.g., $\ge 0.2$):} Centroids are overly sensitive to individual sample noise and class-specific orthogonality, leading to high-frequency geometric jitter and representation collapse.
\end{itemize}
We find that $\beta = 0.05$ provides an optimal balance, allowing the EMA centroids to stabilize and track representation drift under linear domain shifts without being over-sensitized to isotropic Gaussian noise.

\subsection{Routing Temperature ($\tau$)}
The temperature $\tau > 0$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:epl_soft_routing} controls the sharpness of the expert ensembling weights $\alpha_{k, b}$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{As $\tau \to 0$:} The routing becomes hard (one-hot), executing only the single nearest expert adapter. This minimizes inter-task parameter interference, which is highly suited for orthogonal task manifolds.
    \item \textbf{As $\tau \to \infty$:} The routing approaches a uniform distribution ($\alpha_{k, b} \approx 1/K$), which collapses ensembling performance toward the Uniform Merging baseline.
\end{itemize}
We find that setting a sharp temperature of $\tau = 0.001$ (as used in our experiments) acts as a high-confidence selector, ensuring that inputs are routed cleanly to their native experts.

\subsection{Algorithmic Complexity and Scaling to Large $K$}
In scenarios where the expert registry scales to a massive number of streaming tasks (e.g., $K \gg 10$), the computational complexity of task alignment or routing becomes a critical systems consideration. For instance, if a global bipartite matching algorithm (such as the Hungarian method with $\mathcal{O}(K^3)$ complexity) is used to align discovered cluster centroids with expert adapters, the cubic time complexity can introduce non-trivial latency overhead on resource-constrained edge devices. 

To mitigate this scaling bottleneck, several highly efficient alternatives can be deployed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Greedy Max-Min Selection:} Instead of globally optimizing the bipartite matching cost matrix, expert adapters can be assigned to centroids greedily based on maximum confidence (minimum Shannon entropy) first. This reduces the assignment complexity from $\mathcal{O}(K^3)$ to $\mathcal{O}(K^2)$ while maintaining near-identical routing accuracy.
    \item \textbf{Approximate Bipartite Matching:} Algorithms such as the Auction algorithm can find near-optimal bipartite matchings in $\mathcal{O}(K^2 \log K)$ time, serving as a highly scalable intermediate solution.
    \item \textbf{Direct Routing (EER) Linear Scaling:} Our proposed EER paradigm bypasses centroid-to-expert matching entirely by computing entropy directly over the expert heads, scaling linearly as $\mathcal{O}(K)$ forward passes. Under the Amortized Pseudo-Labeling scheme ($N_{\text{amortize}} = 10$), this makes the ensembling pipeline extremely scalable even for large expert registries.
\end{itemize}

\section{Geometric Metrics for Representational Sparsity and Adaptive Amortization Policies}
\label{app:geometric_adaptive}

In this section, we expand on the constructive feedback from academic reviewers to provide formal geometric metrics and dynamic systems policies that further ground and optimize Zero-Shot Calibration-Free Model Merging.

\subsection{Topological Metrics for the Representational Sparsity Paradox}
To quantitatively track and diagnose the \emph{Representational Sparsity Paradox} (the phenomenon where centroid-based ensembling fails due to intra-task class orthogonality), we formulate two geometric metrics of the representation space:

\begin{definition}[Cosine-Similarity Dispersion]
Let $\mathcal{H}_k = \{h_b \mid \hat{y}_b = k\}$ be the set of intermediate representations mapped to task $k$, and let $\mu_k$ be the tracked centroid for task $k$. The \emph{Cosine-Similarity Dispersion} (CSD) of task $k$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\text{CSD}_k = 1 - \frac{1}{|\mathcal{H}_k|} \sum_{h \in \mathcal{H}_k} \frac{h \cdot \mu_k}{\|h\|_2 \|\mu_k\|_2}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Subspace Overlap Index]
Let $S_i, S_j \subset \mathbb{R}^D$ be the representation subspaces of tasks $i$ and $j$, respectively. The \emph{Subspace Overlap Index} (SOI) between tasks $i$ and $j$ is computed as:
\begin{equation}
\text{SOI}(i, j) = \frac{\|\mathbf{P}_i \mathbf{P}_j\|_F^2}{\min(\dim(S_i), \dim(S_j))}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{P}_i$ and $\mathbf{P}_j$ are the orthogonal projection matrices onto $S_i$ and $S_j$, and $\|\cdot\|_F$ is the Frobenius norm.
\end{definition}

Under the ideal task orthogonality assumed in our simulation sandbox, $\text{SOI}(i, j) \approx 0$ for $i \neq j$. However, within a task $k$, the representation manifold is composed of $C_k$ orthogonal class-specific sub-manifolds. The centroid $\mu_k = \frac{1}{C_k} \sum_{c=1}^{C_k} \mu_{k, c}$ represents the average of these orthogonal vectors, lying in a representational void where sample density is practically zero. 

As a result, any incoming class representation $h \in \mathcal{H}_k$ satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\frac{h \cdot \mu_k}{\|h\|_2 \|\mu_k\|_2} \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{C_k}}
\end{equation}
This bounds the dispersion metric from below: $\text{CSD}_k \ge 1 - 1/\sqrt{C_k}$. For $C_k = 10$ classes (as in our CIFAR-10 and SVHN tasks), $\text{CSD}_k \approx 0.684$, indicating extreme spatial sparseness. Centroids fail to lie on the true activation manifolds and instead jitter erratically between class prototypes, which explains why centroid-based EPL-OCA drops to 49.88\% accuracy compared to direct EER.

\subsection{Adaptive Amortization via Volatility-Triggered Recalibration}
To make Amortized EER robust to highly volatile sequential streams where temporal task locality changes dynamically, we propose an \emph{Adaptive Amortization Policy} that monitors routing coordinate volatility.

Instead of evaluating expert prediction entropy at a static interval $N_{\text{amortize}}$, we maintain a running sliding window $W$ of the past $M$ ensembling weights $\alpha_b \in \mathbb{R}^K$. We define the \emph{Ensembling Volatility} $V_b$ at step $b$ as:
\begin{equation}
V_b = \frac{1}{M-1} \sum_{m=1}^{M-1} \|\alpha_{b-m} - \alpha_{b-m-1}\|_2^2
\end{equation}

The serving engine operates as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Homogeneous Stream (Low Volatility):} If $V_b \le \theta_{\text{drift}}$, the stream is stable. The engine skips the EER evaluation and safely reuses the cached routing weights $\alpha_b \approx \alpha_{b-1}$, extending the amortization interval beyond $N_{\text{amortize}}$ to conserve power.
    \item \textbf{Task Boundary Transition (High Volatility):} If $V_b > \theta_{\text{drift}}$, a distribution shift or task change is detected. The engine immediately triggers a fresh EER forward pass over the $K$ parallel low-rank paths to compute Normalized Shannon Entropy, recalibrating the routing coordinates.
\end{itemize}
By dynamically tuning the caching window based on stream volatility, the serving engine guarantees high ensembling robustness under rapid domain shifts while minimizing systems overhead under stable streams.

\section{Theoretical Bounds on Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Task Rejection under Covariate Drift}
\label{app:ood_rejection_proofs}

In Section 4.7, we discuss the integration of density safety checks, such as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), to reject out-of-distribution (OOD) tasks and prevent catastrophic misclassifications in open-world serving. Here, we present a rigorous first-principles mathematical analysis to derive the fundamental bounds and limits of density-based thresholding on orthogonal task subspaces when the streaming data is subjected to severe linear covariate drift.

\subsection{Mathematical Framework and Density Modeling}
Let the representation space be $\mathbb{R}^D$. Suppose we have a set of $K$ in-distribution tasks. Each task $k \in \{1, \dots, K\}$ is modeled as a low-dimensional orthogonal subspace $S_k \subset \mathbb{R}^D$ with dimensionality $d_k \ll D$, where the subspaces are mutually orthogonal: $S_i \perp S_j$ for all $i \neq j$. 

We model the density of the intermediate representations $h \in \mathbb{R}^D$ belonging to task $k$ using a multivariate Gaussian distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu_k, \mathbf{\Sigma}_k)$, where the mean $\mu_k \in S_k$ is the task-specific centroid. Under the assumption of isotropic covariance within the task subspace and a small isotropic background noise variance $\sigma_\perp^2$ in the orthogonal complement $S_k^\perp$, the covariance matrix $\mathbf{\Sigma}_k$ is formulated as:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\Sigma}_k = \sigma^2 \mathbf{P}_k + \sigma_\perp^2 (\mathbf{I}_D - \mathbf{P}_k)
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{P}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$ is the orthogonal projection matrix onto the subspace $S_k$, $\sigma^2$ is the variance within the task manifold, and $\sigma_\perp^2 \ll \sigma^2$ is the out-of-subspace noise variance.

The Mahalanobis distance of an arbitrary representation $h$ to the task centroid $\mu_k$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
D_M^2(h, \mu_k) = (h - \mu_k)^T \mathbf{\Sigma}_k^{-1} (h - \mu_k)
\end{equation}
For a sample belonging to task $k$, the Mahalanobis distance follows a Chi-squared distribution: $D_M^2(h, \mu_k) \sim \chi^2_D$. A standard density-based OOD rejection policy rejects the sample as OOD for task $k$ if its Mahalanobis distance exceeds a threshold $\gamma_k$:
\begin{equation}
\text{Reject } h \iff D_M^2(h, \mu_k) > \gamma_k
\end{equation}

\subsection{Impact of Covariate Drift on Mahalanobis Distance}
Let $h_0$ be an in-distribution sample of task $k$ with mean $\mathbb{E}[h_0] = \mu_k$. During serving, the sample is subjected to a severe linear covariate drift of magnitude $\delta \ge 0$ along a random unit direction vector $w \in \mathbb{R}^D$ ($\|w\|_2 = 1$):
\begin{equation}
h_t = h_0 + \delta w
\end{equation}
The expected Mahalanobis distance of the drifted sample $h_t$ to the centroid $\mu_k$ is:
\begin{align}
\mathbb{E}[D_M^2(h_t, \mu_k)] &= \mathbb{E}[(h_0 + \delta w - \mu_k)^T \mathbf{\Sigma}_k^{-1} (h_0 + \delta w - \mu_k)] \\
&= \mathbb{E}[D_M^2(h_0, \mu_k)] + \delta^2 w^T \mathbf{\Sigma}_k^{-1} w + 2\delta \mathbb{E}[(h_0 - \mu_k)^T] \mathbf{\Sigma}_k^{-1} w
\end{align}
Since $\mathbb{E}[h_0 - \mu_k] = 0$, the linear expectation term vanishes, yielding:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:expected_drift_distance}
\mathbb{E}[D_M^2(h_t, \mu_k)] = D + \delta^2 w^T \mathbf{\Sigma}_k^{-1} w
\end{equation}

We analyze two critical geometric orientations of the drift vector $w$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{In-Subspace Drift ($w \in S_k$):} The drift vector lies entirely within the task subspace, meaning $\mathbf{P}_k w = w$. Then $w^T \mathbf{\Sigma}_k^{-1} w = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}$. The expected distance increases slowly:
    \begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[D_M^2(h_t, \mu_k)] = D + \frac{\delta^2}{\sigma^2}
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Out-of-Subspace/Orthogonal Drift ($w \in S_k^\perp$):} The drift vector is orthogonal to the task manifold, meaning $\mathbf{P}_k w = 0$. Then $w^T \mathbf{\Sigma}_k^{-1} w = \frac{1}{\sigma_\perp^2}$. Because $\sigma_\perp^2 \ll \sigma^2$, the expected distance increases catastrophically fast:
    \begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[D_M^2(h_t, \mu_k)] = D + \frac{\delta^2}{\sigma_\perp^2} \gg D
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Fundamental Limits of Density-Based OOD Rejection}
To guarantee that drifted in-distribution samples of task $k$ are not falsely rejected (maintaining robust serving under a maximum in-subspace drift magnitude $\delta_{\max}$), the rejection threshold $\gamma_k$ must be set to accommodate the expected drifted distance:
\begin{equation}
\gamma_k \ge D + \frac{\delta_{\max}^2}{\sigma^2}
\end{equation}

Now consider an actual OOD task $j$ (where $j \neq k$) whose centroid $\mu_j$ lies on the orthogonal subspace $S_j \perp S_k$. The squared Euclidean distance between the centroids is $\|\mu_j - \mu_k\|_2^2 > 0$. The Mahalanobis distance of the OOD task centroid $\mu_j$ evaluated under task $k$'s density model is:
\begin{equation}
D_M^2(\mu_j, \mu_k) = (\mu_j - \mu_k)^T \mathbf{\Sigma}_k^{-1} (\mu_j - \mu_k)
\end{equation}
Since $\mu_j \in S_j$ and $\mu_k \in S_k$ are orthogonal, we have $\mathbf{P}_k (\mu_j - \mu_k) = -\mu_k$, and $(\mathbf{I}_D - \mathbf{P}_k) (\mu_j - \mu_k) = \mu_j$. Substituting these projections into the distance metric yields:
\begin{equation}
D_M^2(\mu_j, \mu_k) = \frac{\|\mu_k\|_2^2}{\sigma^2} + \frac{\|\mu_j\|_2^2}{\sigma_\perp^2}
\end{equation}

The OOD task $j$ will fail to be rejected (yielding a catastrophic false acceptance) if its distance falls below the threshold: $D_M^2(\mu_j, \mu_k) \le \gamma_k$. Under the necessary threshold configuration for drift-robustness, this condition becomes:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\|\mu_k\|_2^2}{\sigma^2} + \frac{\|\mu_j\|_2^2}{\sigma_\perp^2} \le D + \frac{\delta_{\max}^2}{\sigma^2}
\end{equation}

Rearranging terms, we establish the following theorem defining the fundamental theoretical limits of density-based OOD rejection under covariate drift:

\begin{theorem}[Fundamental Limit of Drift-Robust OOD Rejection]
Under mutually orthogonal task subspaces $S_k$ and $S_j$, any single density-thresholding scheme (such as GMM or Mahalanobis boundary rejection) fails to differentiate drifted in-distribution samples from OOD task samples if the maximum expected in-subspace drift magnitude $\delta_{\max}$ satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ood_limit_bound}
\delta_{\max} \ge \sqrt{\|\mu_k\|_2^2 + \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma_\perp^2} \|\mu_j\|_2^2 - \sigma^2 D}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $\gamma_k$ be the decision threshold. To avoid rejecting drifted in-distribution samples of task $k$ up to drift $\delta_{\max}$, we require $\gamma_k \ge D + \frac{\delta_{\max}^2}{\sigma^2}$. To guarantee the rejection of OOD task $j$, we require $D_M^2(\mu_j, \mu_k) > \gamma_k$. Combining these two requirements yields:
\begin{equation}
D_M^2(\mu_j, \mu_k) > D + \frac{\delta_{\max}^2}{\sigma^2} \implies \frac{\|\mu_k\|_2^2}{\sigma^2} + \frac{\|\mu_j\|_2^2}{\sigma_\perp^2} > D + \frac{\delta_{\max}^2}{\sigma^2}
\end{equation}
Multiplying both sides by $\sigma^2$ and isolating $\delta_{\max}^2$:
\begin{equation}
\|\mu_k\|_2^2 + \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma_\perp^2} \|\mu_j\|_2^2 - \sigma^2 D > \delta_{\max}^2
\end{equation}
Taking the square root defines the strict upper bound on the allowable covariate drift $\delta_{\max}$ for which a mathematically perfect decision boundary exists. If Eq.~\ref{eq:ood_limit_bound} is violated, the in-distribution drifted density support and the OOD task density support mathematically overlap, rendering them fundamentally indistinguishable to any density-thresholding classifier.
\end{proof}

This theorem provides an absolute geometric and statistical guarantee for safety-critical edge serving: if the expected domain drift on the edge is highly severe (violating Eq.~\ref{eq:ood_limit_bound}), practitioners cannot rely solely on simple static density thresholds. Instead, they must deploy dynamic tracking mechanisms—such as our proposed online centroid refinement (EPL-OCA) or direct entropy-routing (EER)—to continuously track the task manifold and prevent catastrophic OOD acceptance.

\section{Hierarchical Task Registries for Mitigating Subspace Capacity Degradation at Scale}
\label{app:hierarchical_registries}

In Section 4.10, we observe a natural decay in ensembling accuracy as the task registry size scales from $K=4$ to $K=12$. This decay is mathematically driven by \emph{subspace capacity degradation}: because the base model's representation dimension $D$ is fixed (e.g., $D=192$), partitioning the space into $K$ orthogonal task subspaces limits the dimension of each subspace to $d_k = D / K$. When $K$ becomes large, $d_k$ shrinks rapidly (e.g., $d_k = 16$ at $K=12$), reducing the representational capacity of each expert's specialized manifold and making task boundaries highly vulnerable to cross-task noise and activation blending interference.

To mitigate this scaling bottleneck and enable calibration-free model merging to scale to massive registries (e.g., $K \gg 50$) without modifying the base model's architecture, we propose a \emph{Hierarchical Task Routing} architecture.

\subsection{Two-Stage Hierarchical Routing Formulation}
Instead of performing a flat routing decision over all $K$ experts simultaneously, we organize the expert registry into a hierarchy of $G \ll K$ distinct semantic task groups, where each group $g \in \{1, \dots, G\}$ contains a subset of related tasks $\mathcal{G}_g \subset \{1, \dots, K\}$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stage 1: Coarse Group-Level Routing.} The incoming sample $h_b$ is first routed to one of the $G$ task groups. This decision operates over larger group-level subspaces of dimension $D_g = D / G \gg D / K$, providing substantial representation capacity.
    \item \textbf{Stage 2: Fine-Grained Intra-Group Routing.} Once the active group $g^*$ is selected, a fine-grained routing decision is executed strictly within $\mathcal{G}_{g^*}$ to route the sample to the final specialized expert adapter.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hierarchical Group-Level Entropy Routing (HG-EER)}
To implement Hierarchical Routing completely calibration-free, we extend our prediction entropy framework to the coarse routing stage. We define the \emph{Group Minimum Entropy} $H_G(g, h_b)$ for group $g$ as the minimum Normalized Shannon Entropy across all experts belonging to group $g$:
\begin{equation}
H_G(g, h_b) = \min_{k \in \mathcal{G}_g} \bar{H}(p_k(h_b))
\end{equation}

The first-stage group routing coefficients $\alpha_g \in [0, 1]$ are computed by applying a Softmax over group minimum entropies with temperature $\tau_{\text{group}} > 0$:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_g = \frac{\exp\left(-H_G(g, h_b) / \tau_{\text{group}}\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^G \exp\left(-H_G(j, h_b) / \tau_{\text{group}}\right)}
\end{equation}

If coarse routing is hard ($\tau_{\text{group}} \to 0$), we select the active group index $g^* = \operatorname{argmin}_g H_G(g, h_b)$. We then compute the fine-grained intra-group ensembling coefficients $\alpha_k$ for $k \in \mathcal{G}_{g^*}$:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_k = \frac{\exp\left(-\bar{H}(p_k(h_b)) / \tau\right)}{\sum_{i \in \mathcal{G}_{g^*}} \exp\left(-\bar{H}(p_i(h_b)) / \tau\right)}
\end{equation}

For tasks outside the active group ($k \notin \mathcal{G}_{g^*}$), we set $\alpha_k = 0$. Serving is then performed using SPS Activation-Space Blending restricted to the active experts in $\mathcal{G}_{g^*}$.

\subsection{Systems-ML Scaling Advantages}
Hierarchical Task Routing offers three outstanding systems-level benefits for edge deployment:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Resolution of Subspace Capacity Degradation:} Because group-level routing operates on groups of size $D_g = D / G$, the representational capacity of the task manifolds is preserved. For instance, in a registry of $K=48$ tasks grouped into $G=4$ semantic domains, coarse routing operates on 48-dimensional manifolds rather than extremely degraded 4-dimensional manifolds.
    \item \textbf{Cubic-to-Linear Latency Mitigation:} While flat routing over $K$ experts requires evaluating $K$ parallel LoRA paths, Hierarchical Routing can prune entire groups. If group-level task boundaries are distinct, we can dynamically execute EER only for experts in the top-1 group $g^*$, reducing fine-grained entropy evaluations from $\mathcal{O}(K)$ to $\mathcal{O}(K / G)$ forward passes. Under Amortized Pseudo-Labeling, this brings edge serving latency virtually identical to single-pass execution.
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Registries:} Hierarchical partitioning allows edge devices to dynamically register or deregister new expert adapters by simply adding them to their respective semantic groups without having to pre-calculate, pre-align, or calibrate centroids across the entire global task space.
\end{enumerate}

By structuring massive registries hierarchically, Hierarchical Task Routing breaks the flat dimensionality bottleneck, paving a clear mathematical and systems roadmap for deploying hundreds of specialized low-rank experts on resource-constrained edge systems.
