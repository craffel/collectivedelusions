\section{Proofs and Mathematical Derivations}
\label{sec:appendix_proofs}

We present the theoretical derivations and properties of LoRA Subspace Projection Routing (LSPR) in detail.

\subsection{Proof of Scale-Invariance of the Subspace Energy Score}
\label{subsec:proof_scale_invariance}

We prove that the subspace alignment score $u_{k, b}$ is perfectly scale-invariant under scaling of the input activation vector $h_b$.

\begin{theorem}
Let $h_b \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times D}$ be an activation vector and $Q_k \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times r}$ be a semi-orthogonal matrix satisfying $Q_k^T Q_k = I_r$. The subspace projection energy score defined as:
$$u_{k, b} = \frac{\| h_b Q_k \|_2}{\| h_b \|_2}$$
is invariant to any non-zero scalar scaling of $h_b$, i.e., $u_{k, b}(c \cdot h_b) = u_{k, b}(h_b)$ for all $c \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $\tilde{h}_b = c \cdot h_b$ be the scaled activation vector, where $c \neq 0$. Substituting $\tilde{h}_b$ into the definition of $u_{k, b}$, we have:
$$u_{k, b}(\tilde{h}_b) = \frac{\| \tilde{h}_b Q_k \|_2}{\| \tilde{h}_b \|_2} = \frac{\| (c \cdot h_b) Q_k \|_2}{\| c \cdot h_b \|_2}$$
By the properties of vector norms, for any scalar $c$ and vector $v$, $\| c \cdot v \|_2 = |c| \cdot \| v \|_2$. Applying this to both the numerator and the denominator, we get:
$$u_{k, b}(\tilde{h}_b) = \frac{\| c \cdot (h_b Q_k) \|_2}{\| c \cdot h_b \|_2} = \frac{|c| \cdot \| h_b Q_k \|_2}{|c| \cdot \| h_b \|_2}$$
Since $c \neq 0$, $|c| > 0$. We can divide both the numerator and denominator by $|c|$:
$$u_{k, b}(\tilde{h}_b) = \frac{\| h_b Q_k \|_2}{\| h_b \|_2} = u_{k, b}(h_b)$$
This completes the proof.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Relationship to Cosine of Subspace Angle}
\label{subsec:subspace_angle}

Let the representational subspace of Expert $k$ be denoted as $\mathcal{S}_k = \text{col}(Q_k) \subset \mathbb{R}^D$, which is an $r$-dimensional subspace. The orthogonal projection matrix onto $\mathcal{S}_k$ is given by $P_k = Q_k Q_k^T \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$.
For any activation vector $h_b^T \in \mathbb{R}^D$, its orthogonal projection onto $\mathcal{S}_k$ is:
$$\text{proj}_{\mathcal{S}_k}(h_b^T) = P_k h_b^T = Q_k Q_k^T h_b^T$$
The angle $\theta_k$ between the vector $h_b^T$ and the subspace $\mathcal{S}_k$ is defined by:
$$\cos(\theta_k) = \frac{\| \text{proj}_{\mathcal{S}_k}(h_b^T) \|_2}{\| h_b^T \|_2} = \frac{\| Q_k Q_k^T h_b^T \|_2}{\| h_b^T \|_2}$$
Using the properties of the semi-orthogonal matrix $Q_k$ ($Q_k^T Q_k = I_r$), the L2-norm of the projected vector can be simplified:
$$\| Q_k Q_k^T h_b^T \|_2^2 = (Q_k Q_k^T h_b^T)^T (Q_k Q_k^T h_b^T) = h_b Q_k Q_k^T Q_k Q_k^T h_b^T = h_b Q_k (I_r) Q_k^T h_b^T = h_b Q_k Q_k^T h_b^T = \| h_b Q_k \|_2^2$$
Taking the square root of both sides:
$$\| Q_k Q_k^T h_b^T \|_2 = \| h_b Q_k \|_2$$
Thus, we obtain:
$$\cos(\theta_k) = \frac{\| h_b Q_k \|_2}{\| h_b \|_2} = u_{k, b}$$
This proves that the coordinate $u_{k, b}$ is precisely the cosine of the angle $\theta_k$ between the activation vector $h_b$ and the $r$-dimensional subspace $\mathcal{S}_k$. By definition of the cosine of an angle, $u_{k, b} \in [0, 1]$ for any non-zero activation vector, providing a highly stable, geometrically bounded, and bounded-interval similarity coordinate.
