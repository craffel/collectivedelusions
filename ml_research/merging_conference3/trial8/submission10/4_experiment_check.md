## Empirical Evaluation and Verification
The empirical evaluation is highly comprehensive, consisting of both a high-fidelity synthetic sandbox (Isolating Coordinate Sandbox - ICS) and a proof-of-concept physical model verification on real-world datasets using a pre-trained Vision Transformer backbone.

### Key Strengths of the Evaluation
1. **Exhaustive Stress-Testing in the Sandbox:** The ICS sandbox is thoroughly calibrated based on empirical ViT-Tiny measurements. The paper sweeps multiple key dimensions, including scaling domain noise, task similarity (mutualism), deployment stream batch sizes, and destructive interference weights. ESM-LVC consistently demonstrates superior performance and robust noise resilience.
2. **Honest and Transparent Disclosures:** The authors are exceptionally candid about the sandbox's stylized assumptions, particularly that it abstracts away physical interference via a smooth power-law surrogate, making the destructive interference sweeps in Section 4.5 and the physical validation in Section 4.8 absolutely critical.
3. **Physical Model Verification (Section 4.8):** 
   - The paper extracts actual CLS token activations from Layer 12 of a pre-trained ViT-Tiny across four real-world datasets (MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10, SVHN).
   - This provides a highly challenging, non-orthogonal testbed ($\rho_{\text{CIFAR-10, SVHN}} = 0.8488$).
   - It introduces a joint 40-class evaluation setup to penalize incorrect task routing, bridging the gap to downstream classification.

### Critical Empirical Gaps & Weaknesses
1. **Routing Accuracy Equivalence & Attractor Bottleneck:** In the physical routing accuracy benchmark (Table 4), SABLE, SPS-ZCA, and single-centroid ESM-LVC variants exhibit *exactly identical* routing accuracies across all noise scales. The authors honestly disclose that this is because they share the same Zero-Shot Centroid Alignment (ZCA) affinity coordinates, and the recurrent solver acts as an attractor that focuses the ensembling coefficients without changing the argmax. This reveals that under pure hard-gated routing where only a single adapter is activated, the continuous solver does not alter the decision boundary; its true benefits are realized under soft blending and multi-modal scaling (GMC).
2. **GMC-BSC as an Empirical Breakthrough:** The paper successfully resolves the single-centroid attractor bottleneck by implementing and evaluating the **Gaussian Mixture Centroids (GMC)** extension. GMC breaks the attractor equivalence, boosting clean routing accuracy to **93.50%** and outperforming the parametric Fully-Optimized Linear Router under extreme noise ($\sigma=2.0$) by **+4.75% absolute**. This is an outstanding empirical result that validates the scalability of non-parametric approaches.
3. **Low Downstream Classification Accuracies:** The downstream classification accuracies (Table 5) are low (20.75% to 28.75%). The authors honestly explain that the individual classification probes trained on tiny 64-sample calibration sets have low accuracy due to severe data starvation and the frozen, out-of-domain features of a tiny ViT backbone. While this transparency is laudable, it highlights that the physical setup is highly constrained.
4. **The Missing End-to-End Active Adapter Blending:** The physical verification is conducted offline on pre-extracted activations. The paper does not train actual physical LoRA/PEFT adapters, nor does it serve them end-to-end to measure physical multi-task classification accuracy under active adapter blending. While the paper outlines a highly detailed systems-level roadmap and integrates with Punica/S-LoRA, the lack of an online physical deployment remains the primary empirical limitation of the work.
