# Peer Review of Conference Submission

## Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a comprehensive and technically rigorous study of **Zero-Shot Calibration-Free Model Merging** for dynamic test-time model ensembling of Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) experts on resource-constrained edge devices. 

Existing dynamic activation ensembling frameworks (such as SPS-ZCA and SABLE) rely on pre-computing task-routing centroids from offline, labeled calibration splits ($|\mathcal{C}_k|=64$ annotated samples per task). This requirement introduces a severe operational bottleneck in privacy-restricted, zero-downtime, or streaming edge environments where data cannot be uploaded, manual labels are unavailable, and plug-and-play operation is mandatory. To resolve this bottleneck, the authors systematically formulate and compare two primary dynamic serving paradigms under streaming conditions:
1. **Zero-Shot Expert Entropy Routing (EER) [Accuracy-First]:** A direct-routing paradigm that routes incoming samples on-the-fly to the expert exhibiting the minimum scale-invariant **Normalized Shannon prediction entropy** (maximum confidence).
2. **Entropy-Pseudo-Labeled Online Centroid Adaptation (EPL-OCA) [Efficiency-First]:** A centroid-routing paradigm that maintains running task centroids on-the-fly to enable single-pass ensembling. It updates the task centroids after the prediction step using EER-guided pseudo-labels to avoid chronological data leakage.

In a controlled synthetic 192-dimensional representation space sandbox (modeling MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, and SVHN), EER achieves an outstanding **71.38% Joint Mean accuracy**, outperforming the supervised SOTA baseline (SPS-ZCA) by **+4.62%** absolute, and maintains complete robustness under extreme representation drift ($d=0.45$, delivering **71.18%** Joint Mean). EPL-OCA achieves only **49.88%** Joint Mean due to the **Representational Sparsity Paradox** (class-specific orthogonality within task subspaces causes centroids to jitter). However, softening the ensembling temperature to $\tau=0.5$ (EPL-OCA Soft) acts as a spatial regularizer, lifting accuracy to **61.62%** (+11.74% absolute).

Crucially, the paper evaluates these paradigms on real-world 512-dimensional ResNet-18 embeddings. Here, they discover a critical methodological limitation—the **Entropy Calibration Discrepancy** (ECD)—where simpler experts (like MNIST) exhibit severe out-of-distribution (OOD) overconfidence, causing direct entropy routing (EER) to drop to **35.38%** accuracy and causing EPL-OCA to completely collapse (**27.45%** Hard, **31.52%** Soft) due to a self-referential pseudo-label feedback loop. 

To resolve this, the authors evaluate **Centroid-Gated Entropy Routing (CG-EER)**, achieving **61.50%** Joint Mean accuracy (outperforming supervised SPS-ZCA by **+0.70%**). They explicitly classify CG-EER as a hybrid, semi-supervised framework because it relies on pre-computed offline task centroids ($|\mathcal{C}_k|=64$) to restrict entropy routing to candidate experts near the sample. Attempts to make this gating completely calibration-free via unsupervised online centroids (**UCG-EER**) collapse to **28.45%** across all warm-up windows, demonstrating the fundamental limits of pure unsupervised online adaptation under severe calibration discrepancy.

Finally, the authors conduct a physical edge-device serving overhead and CPU wall-clock latency profiling under post-activation divergence, demonstrating that **Amortized EER** ($N_{\text{amortize}} = 10$) reduces CPU latency to a highly practical **$1.57\times$ overhead** ($0.2211$ ms per sample) and slashes the edge energy footprint by **$4.14\times$** while preserving high accuracy (**71.20%** Joint Mean on coherent streams with temporal locality).

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths
1. **Outstanding Practical Significance:** The paper targets a critical, real-world engineering hurdle in edge AI—the storage, memory, and serving footprint of Swapping Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) experts. SWOT analysis of model serving indicates that swappable adapters are heavily bottlenecked by disk-to-memory latency. Dynamic activation ensembling (SPS-ZCA) is an elegant solution, but its reliance on offline labeled calibration splits makes it impractical for on-device plug-and-play streaming. This work is directly and compellingly motivated by these real-world constraints.
2. **Exemplary "Honest" Systems-ML Engineering:** Rather than hiding or glossing over negative results, the authors present a highly thorough, complete, and honest evaluation of where and why unsupervised online centroid adaptation (EPL-OCA) fails. Their discovery and characterization of the **Entropy Calibration Discrepancy** and the **Self-Referential Pseudo-Label Corruption Loop** on real embeddings is an outstanding, high-signal contribution. It saves practitioners weeks of wasted engineering and guides them toward viable hybrid architectures.
3. **Rigorous Systems-Level Analysis:** The authors head-on address the physical systems reality of *post-activation divergence* in Vision Transformers (where base network computation can no longer be shared once activations pass through different LoRA matrices). They analyze the physical FLOP serving complexity ($0.25 + 0.75K$) and propose a practical, lightweight mitigation—**Amortized Pseudo-Labeling**—which reduces CPU latency to only **$1.57\times$ overhead** (from $6.52\times$) and slashes the energy footprint by **$4.14\times$** on low-power edge nodes.
4. **Exhaustive Experimental Sweeps:** The paper is packed with highly informative sweeps, including Softmax temperature ablations (revealing soft blending acts as a spatial regularizer), warm-up window sensitivity (proving EPL-OCA Soft centroids stabilize in just **10 steps**), registry scalability sweeps ($K \in \{4, 8, 12\}$), temporal task locality (block size $B_{\text{block}}$) analyses, and actual CPU latency wall-clock runtimes.
5. **Robust Baseline Comparison:** The paper compares the proposed methods against an exceptionally rich and appropriate set of baselines, including static parameter merging (Uniform, Task Arithmetic), non-parametric routing (PFSR, SPS-ZCA, ZCR), unsupervised online clustering (Streaming K-Means), and gradient-based TTA (TENT), providing a complete and convincing context.

### Weaknesses
1. **Semi-Supervised Dependency on Real Features:** Although the authors propose EER and EPL-OCA as fully unsupervised and calibration-free, both methods fail to outperform static Uniform Weight Merging on real ResNet-18 features due to Entropy Calibration Discrepancy. The only method that successfully outperforms SPS-ZCA on real embeddings is **CG-EER** (61.50% vs 60.80%), which is a *hybrid semi-supervised* method because it relies on pre-computed offline task centroids (requiring 64 samples per task offline). Thus, the paper does not deliver a fully unsupervised, calibration-free solution that works on real features.
2. **Idealized Synthetic Sandbox Assumptions:** The synthetic sandbox's assumptions of perfect task subspace orthogonality and class prototype orthogonality are highly simplified compared to real image embeddings, which exhibit significant topological overlaps. While the authors honestly acknowledge this, testing on more complex real representation spaces (e.g., fine-tuned ViT-B/16 or BERT embeddings on GLUE/ImageNet) would further strengthen the work.
3. **SVHN Extreme Noise Scale:** SVHN's noise scale of $0.56$ is exceptionally high, calibrating its stand-alone expert accuracy to $39.44\%$. While this serves as an aggressive stress-test for noise rejection, it severely drags down the Joint Mean accuracy for all models. Providing a clean 3-task Joint Mean (MNIST, F-MNIST, CIFAR-10) was an excellent ablation in Section 4.1 to address this, but it highlights how sensitive the Joint Mean is to this single task.

## Detailed Evaluation Ratings

### Soundness: Excellent
The submission is technically exceptionally sound. The mathematical formulations are clear and precise, and the chronological correct, non-leaking centroid update is highly correct and realistic for actual streaming applications. The authors are extremely honest about the assumptions of their synthetic sandbox and the background chance probability of $\approx 10\%$ introduced by overlapping class namespaces. The identification and characterization of the Entropy Calibration Discrepancy is mathematically and empirically rigorous, and the proposed hybrid CG-EER is an appropriate and elegant way to neutralize OOD overconfidence.

### Presentation: Excellent
The paper is exceptionally clearly written and well-structured. The logical flow from introduction to methodology, synthetic experiments, and real-world embeddings is natural and easy to follow. Figures and tables are dense with high-signal information (e.g., Figure 3 plotting amortization intervals vs block sizes, Figure 4 plotting registry scales, Table 8 detail on ResNet-18 embeddings). The authors do an outstanding job of contextualizing their work relative to prior parameter-merging, dynamic ensembling, and test-time adaptation literature. Academic transparency is maintained throughout, particularly around the classification of CG-EER as hybrid semi-supervised and the overlapping namespace evaluation bias.

### Significance: Excellent
The paper addresses an important, highly relevant problem in machine learning and edge computing. Swapping Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) experts is a critical deployment bottleneck, and this paper provides a concrete, engineering-grounded roadmap for deploying dynamic LoRA ensembling on resource-constrained devices. The proposed Amortized Pseudo-Labeling and Normalized Shannon Entropy are highly generalizable techniques that can be directly applied to larger architectures (like LLM MoEs or Vision-Language models), making the potential impact on future applications and research substantial.

### Originality: Excellent
The paper provides highly original and valuable insights. While existing dynamic ensembling methods require pre-computed task-routing centroids, this is the first study to systematically explore completely calibration-free, zero-shot test-time model merging and online centroid adaptation. The formulation of the Entropy Calibration Discrepancy and the identification of the self-referential pseudo-label corruption loop provide deep theoretical and empirical insights into the fundamental limits of pure unsupervised online adaptation under severe calibration discrepancy, establishing a highly valuable conceptual foundation for the community.

## Overall Recommendation

**Rating:** **5: Accept**
*(Technically solid paper, with high impact on at least one sub-area of AI, with excellent evaluation, resources, reproducibility, and no unaddressed ethical considerations.)*

### Justification for Rating
This paper represents an exemplary, high-signal systems-ML contribution. It is exceptionally well-written, mathematically sound, and rigorously evaluated. Rather than hiding or glossing over negative results, the authors present a complete and honest evaluation of where and why unsupervised online centroid adaptation (EPL-OCA) fails. Their discovery and characterization of the *Entropy Calibration Discrepancy* and the *Self-Referential Pseudo-Label Corruption Loop* on real embeddings is an outstanding contribution. Furthermore, their propose hybrid semi-supervised approach (*CG-EER*) successfully resolves this overconfidence, achieving **61.50%** accuracy on real ResNet-18 features (outperforming supervised SPS-ZCA by **+0.70%**), while using extremely small calibration data ($|\mathcal{C}_k|=64$). 
From a systems standpoint, the proposed *Amortized Pseudo-Labeling* is a highly practical, practitioner-first optimization that reduces CPU latency to only **$1.57\times$ overhead** ($0.2211$ ms per sample) and slashes the energy footprint by **$4.14\times$** on edge devices. 
While the paper's fully unsupervised methods (EER and EPL-OCA) fail to outperform static Uniform Weight Merging on real features, the authors' honest disclosure of these limitations and their highly robust hybrid solutions make this paper exceptionally valuable and mature for publication.

## Constructive Questions and Suggestions for the Authors
1. **Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) Gating:** To resolve the Entropy Calibration Discrepancy (OOD overconfidence) on real features in an entirely calibration-free manner, have the authors considered implementing a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) or simple Mahalanobis distance density check? Since the representation space of the base model is frozen, fitting an unsupervised, online running GMM over incoming embeddings might allow the system to detect when a sample is far from any active expert cluster and fall back strictly to the frozen base model or a uniform weight merge, preventing catastrophic misclassifications in open-world settings.
2. **Evaluation on Pre-Trained Transformers:** While the synthetic sandbox is modeled after a Vision Transformer and the real-world validation is conducted on ResNet-18, actual edge production pipelines are increasingly transitioning to Vision Transformers (e.g., fine-tuned ViT-B/16 on ImageNet) or LLMs (e.g., fine-tuned LLaMA-1B/3B on GLUE or Alpaca). Do the authors plan to evaluate their paradigms on real, high-dimensional Transformer embeddings to see if the Representational Sparsity Paradox and the Entropy Calibration Discrepancy manifest similarly?
3. **Sliding-Window Confidence Smoothing:** In Section 4.3, the authors mention that SVHN's high noise scale (0.56) pollutes cleaner task spaces, driving up routing variance for CIFAR-10. They suggest *sliding-window confidence smoothing* as an elegant systems-level mitigation. Have they empirically evaluated this smoothing, and does it successfully stabilize routing and reduce accuracy standard deviations?
