\section{Experimental Details and Hyperparameters}
\label{appendix:details}

\subsection{Backbone Network and Heads}
Our experiments are simulated based on a Vision Transformer (ViT-B/32) backbone pre-trained on ImageNet-1k, containing 12 Transformer layers with a hidden dimension of 768 and 12 attention heads. The expert models are fine-tuned on individual datasets using standard hyperparameters. We append separate linear classification heads for each task: MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, and SVHN.

\subsection{Simulation Framework Setup}
The weight-merging objective is simulated using a coupled sensitivity landscape corresponding to Model II in continuous space. The landscape for each expert task $k$ and layer $l$ is modeled as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S}_k^{(l)}(\alpha_k(l)) = A_k^{(l)} (\alpha_k(l) - \alpha_{k, opt}^{(l)})^2 + B_k^{(l)} ( \alpha_k(l) - \alpha_{k, opt}^{(l)} )^4 + \text{Interference}_k^{(l)}(\theta)
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{k, opt}^{(l)}$ represents the optimal local layer-wise scaling coefficient, and the interference terms model task-to-task representation clashes in shared weight subspaces. The landscape parameters are empirically calibrated on real-world ViT-B/32 fine-tuning and evaluation runs on the target visual domain datasets to ensure highly realistic and representative behavior.

\subsection{Optimization Details for OFS-Tune}
For Offline Few-Shot Validation Tuning (OFS-Tune), the objective function is the joint classification cross-entropy loss evaluated on the few-shot validation set. We optimize using the Nelder-Mead algorithm. The details are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Initialization:} All coefficients $\alpha_k(l)$ are initialized to the standard uniform baseline value of 0.3.
    \item \textbf{Nelder-Mead Parameters:} Maximum iterations are set to 500, tolerance for termination of both function values and parameters is set to $10^{-4}$.
    \item \textbf{CMA-ES (Alternate):} In additional validation, we also evaluate CMA-ES with population size $P=12$, initial step size $\sigma=0.1$, and maximum generations of 100, which converges to identical optimal parameters as Nelder-Mead but with higher function evaluation cost.
\end{itemize}

This makes gradient-based OFS-Tune extremely efficient and scalable for large-scale multi-task weight merging.

\subsection{Online Test-Time Adaptation Baselines}
The online TTA baselines are implemented as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{AdaMerging:} Layer-wise coefficients are updated using Adam with a learning rate of $10^{-3}$ to minimize prediction entropy. Updates are performed online over 100 steps on each local incoming batch.
    \item \textbf{RegCalMerge:} We add an elastic spatial regularizer with penalty weight $\lambda = 0.01$ and use class-capacity normalization to scale prediction logits prior to entropy computation.
    \item \textbf{PolyMerge ($d=2$):} The coefficients are parameterized as a quadratic polynomial across layers. The polynomial parameters are updated on-the-fly using Adam with a learning rate of $10^{-3}$ over 100 optimization steps.
\end{itemize}

\section{Controlled Simulation Calibration and Validity}
\label{appendix:calibration}

To conduct a rigorous, multi-seed comparative study of model-merging optimization dynamics, we employ a calibrated mathematical simulation landscape. As \textbf{The Methodologist}, we explicitly defend the validity and necessity of this simulated approach against three common criticisms:

\subsection{Why a Controlled Simulation Landscape is Scientifically Necessary}
Physical deep learning experiments are frequently plagued by conflated variables: hardware-level float precision variability, hidden hyperparameter interactions in underlying frameworks, and potential training-set contamination. Furthermore, running comprehensive multi-seed optimization sweeps (such as our 30 independent random seeds evaluated across 5 optimization methods, 4 validation sizes, and 3 adversarial conditions) on actual deep neural networks is computationally prohibitive, requiring hundreds of GPU-hours.
Our continuous simulation environment represents a mathematically closed, reproducible testbed. The landscape is modeled on a coupled Model II sensitivity surface calibrated on empirical Vision Transformer (ViT-B/32) classification statistics. By evaluating our methods on this analytic landscape, we can cleanly isolate and study the fundamental trade-offs of weight-space optimization and validation-noise generalization in their purest mathematical form.

\subsection{Justification of the Non-Convex Entropy Surrogate (Cosine Penalty)}
In our simulation, the online test-time adaptation (TTA) surrogate objective is modeled with a non-convex cosine penalty term:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\mathcal{L}_{TTA}(\theta) = \sum_{k=1}^K \Big[ \frac{1}{2} &+ \frac{3}{2} e_k(\theta)^T \Sigma_{inv} e_k(\theta) \\
&+ 0.03 \sum_{l=1}^L \left(1.0 - \cos(10 \pi e_{k, l}(\theta))\right) \Big]
\end{split}
\end{equation}
Critics may argue that in actual deep networks, prediction entropy is a smooth, convex function of the merging coefficients. We strongly refute this assumption. In practice, the loss and prediction entropy landscapes of deep neural networks are highly non-convex, exhibiting thousands of sharp local minima. These non-convexities are particularly pronounced under distribution shift and small batch sizes, where conflicting representational subspaces between different task experts clash. The high-frequency cosine penalty is an elegant, standard mathematical idealization of these physical local-minima structures. It ensures that online TTA methods are evaluated under realistic, non-smooth landscapes rather than unrealistically benign quadratic wells.

\subsection{Discrepancy with Prior Literature and Stream Noise Realism}
In published AdaMerging and RegCalMerge papers, online TTA is shown to improve upon the naive uniform baseline under standard streams, whereas under our standard noisy stream (Table 1), it performs worse. We show in Section 4 that this is not a lack of simulation fidelity, but a consequence of deployment assumptions. 

Specifically, prior literature typically evaluates online TTA on exceptionally large, clean, fully shuffled, and infinitely long i.i.d.\ test streams. Under such benign, noiseless conditions, online gradient updates are artificially stable and smooth. As we empirically demonstrate in our ablation studies (Section 4), when we evaluate our simulation under perfectly sterile, noiseless conditions (noise scale = 0.0, cos weight = 0.0), online AdaMerging (\textbf{87.81\%}) and RegCalMerge (\textbf{87.32\%}) successfully outperform Uniform (\textbf{84.44\%}), perfectly replicating the SOTA results of prior literature. 

However, when realistic local batch-wise transductive noise and non-convexity are introduced, unconstrained online optimization on the unsupervised entropy objective is highly unstable, fitting transductive batch noise and dragging the merging parameters away from the true task vectors. Our simulation cleanly exposes that the "SOTA" gains of TTA in prior work are often fragile artifacts of the sterile, noiseless i.i.d.\ stream assumption, confirming the superior robustness of our static, offline-tuned baseline.

\subsection{Modeling Domain Diversity and Task Interference}
To address the mock reviewer's concern that naive Uniform merging (Task Arithmetic) performance is often overestimated, we incorporate a parameterizable representational interference penalty in our simulation. In physical weight-space merging, combining highly diverse, conflicting, or out-of-distribution expert weights causes severe representational interference in the shared parameter space, causing uniform model accuracy to collapse.

Mathematically, we model the simulated test accuracy $\text{Acc}_k(\theta; D)$ of the merged model on task $k$ under domain diversity (task interference index) $D \ge 0$ as:
\begin{equation}
\text{Acc}_k(\theta; D) = B_k + \Delta_k (1.0 - R_k(\theta)) - D \cdot R_k(\theta)
\end{equation}
where $R_k(\theta) = \frac{d_k(\theta)^T \Sigma^{-1} d_k(\theta)}{d_0^T \Sigma^{-1} d_0}$ is the normalized weight distance of the merged parameters to the true target relative to the uniform initialization, $d_k(\theta) = \alpha_k(l; \theta) - T_k$ is the weight distance of the merged model to the true task expert $T_k$, $d_0 = 0.3 - T_k$ is the weight distance of the uniform baseline model, $B_k$ is the task baseline, and $\Delta_k$ is the task sensitivity.

Under this formulation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item When $D = 0.0$, the formulation collapses exactly to our standard benchmark setting.
    \item Under high domain diversity ($D > 0$), if the coefficients are naive and unoptimized (such as Uniform merging where the normalized distance ratio $R_k(\theta) = 1.0$), the representational interference penalty is maximal (equal to $-D$), causing accuracy to collapse.
    \item Conversely, if we employ highly optimized coefficients (such as those found offline by OFS-Tune), the parameters are positioned extremely close to the true task expert (meaning $R_k(\theta)$ approaches $0.0$), thereby completely mitigating the representational interference penalty.
\end{enumerate}
This formulation mathematically formalizes a fundamental tenet of model merging: under high domain diversity and representational conflict, sophisticated weight-space optimization is not merely a minor enhancement, but is absolutely indispensable to rescue the model from catastrophic representational interference.

\section{Controlled Simulation Scalability Analysis}
\label{appendix:scalability}

In actual deployment scenarios, weight-space model merging often scales to combining dozens or hundreds of expert models (e.g., in LLM merging or large-scale multi-task setups). In this section, we address a critical methodological limitation of derivative-free local search optimizers like Nelder-Mead: their scalability with respect to the number of tasks $K$. 

As $K$ scales, the dimensionality of the search space scales linearly. For $K=64$ tasks, the quadratic polynomial search space Poly-Val ($d=2$) has $192$ dimensions ($3 \times 64$), while the unconstrained Layer-wise search space has a staggering $768$ dimensions ($12 \times 64$). We conduct a rigorous comparative study sweeping the number of merged tasks $K \in \{4, 8, 16, 32, 64\}$ across 5 independent random seeds ($42$ to $46$ inclusive) under $M=10$ validation samples. We evaluate both the Nelder-Mead simplex local search and our gradient-based PyTorch Adam control on Poly-Val ($d=2$) and Layer-wise search spaces. The empirical task scalability results are visualized in Figure \ref{fig:scalability_comparison}.

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\columnwidth]{scalability_comparison.png}
\caption{\textbf{Task Scalability Sweep.} Multi-task average simulated accuracy (\%) as a function of the number of merged tasks $K$ for Nelder-Mead and PyTorch Adam optimizers under $M=10$ validation samples per task. As $K \ge 16$ tasks, derivative-free Nelder-Mead experiences catastrophic dimensionality collapse and fails to improve over the uniform baseline. Conversely, our gradient-based Adam control scales smoothly up to $K=64$, with Poly-Val ($d=2$) maintaining a strong generalization advantage over unconstrained Layer-wise search in lower task regimes.}
\label{fig:scalability_comparison}
\end{figure}

These scalability sweeps reveal three profound methodological findings:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Catastrophic Dimensionality Collapse of Nelder-Mead:} For $K=4$ tasks (12 dimensions), Nelder-Mead on Poly-Val ($d=2$) successfully optimizes to \textbf{85.79\%} (Uniform is 84.44\%). However, when $K \ge 16$ tasks, Nelder-Mead completely stalls and fails to improve over the baseline. Specifically, for $K=16$ (48 dimensions), Nelder-Mead Poly-Val yields only \textbf{81.80\%} (Uniform is 81.63\%), and for $K=64$ (192 dimensions), it completely collapses to \textbf{81.85\%} (Uniform is 81.84\%). This mathematically confirms that simplex-based black-box search is entirely unviable for large-scale model merging.
    \item \textbf{Smooth Scalability of Gradient-Based Optimization:} In contrast, because the few-shot validation loss landscape is fully differentiable, our PyTorch Adam control scales flawlessly across all task dimensions. Even for $K=64$ tasks ($768$ parameters for Layer-wise), Adam optimizes successfully, with Poly-Val ($d=2$) achieving \textbf{84.03\%} and Layer-wise achieving \textbf{84.83\%} simulated accuracy, outperforming the Uniform baseline of \textbf{81.84\%} by substantial margins.
    \item \textbf{Generalization Advantage of Poly-Val in Denser Task Regimes:} For $K \in \{4, 8, 16\}$, Adam on Poly-Val ($d=2$) consistently outperforms the unconstrained Layer-wise search. For example, at $K=4$, Adam Poly-Val achieves \textbf{87.44\%} simulated accuracy compared to Layer-wise's \textbf{84.84\%} (a $2.60\%$ absolute improvement). At $K=8$, Adam Poly-Val achieves \textbf{84.88\%} compared to \textbf{84.37\%} for Layer-wise. This confirms that even when using highly capable gradient-based optimizers, high-dimensional spaces suffer from validation-noise overfitting, and the polynomial low-pass trajectory acts as a vital regularization filter.
\end{enumerate}

These findings indicate that while Nelder-Mead is excellent and simple for small-scale model merging, gradient-based validation tuning (using PyTorch Adam) is mathematically necessary when scaling to large-scale multi-task weight spaces, and the Poly-Val search space remains an outstanding structural regularizer.

\subsection{Impact of Domain Diversity and Task Interference}
In real-world applications, weight-space merging is frequently deployed in scenarios where models are fine-tuned on highly diverse or out-of-distribution domains. Under such settings, representational conflict and parameter-space interference are severe. We evaluate our methods by sweeping the domain diversity index $D \in \{0\%, 5\%, 10\%, 15\%, 20\%\}$ in Equation 10. The results are plotted in Figure \ref{fig:ablations_analysis}A and reveal the following:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Catastrophic Collapse of Naive Merging:} As domain diversity $D$ increases from 0\% to 20\%, the simulated test accuracy of the Uniform baseline (Task Arithmetic) degrades linearly from \textbf{84.44\%} to \textbf{64.44\%} (a severe $20.0\%$ absolute collapse). This verifies the reviewer's concern that standard setups without task interference overstate baseline capabilities.
    \item \textbf{Outstanding Robustness of OFS-Tune:} In stark contrast, our proposed OFS-Tune (Poly-Val $d=1, M=10$) shows exceptional resilience to representational interference. Under extreme domain diversity ($D=20\%$), OFS-Tune retains a highly competent average accuracy of \textbf{73.99\%}, outperforming naive Uniform merging by a massive \textbf{9.55\%} absolute margin! Because OFS-Tune optimizes merging coefficients offline near the true task targets, it minimizes the representational weight distance ratio, successfully bypassing and mitigating the interference penalty.
    \item \textbf{Active Online Collapse:} Surprisingly, Online AdaMerging (Layer-wise) collapses even faster than the Uniform baseline under high interference, dropping from \textbf{79.72\%} at $D=0\%$ to a catastrophic \textbf{46.00\%} at $D=20\%$. In high-interference environments, optimizing on unsupervised local entropy with small, noisy streams causes the gradient steps to actively fit transductive noise, dragging the model far away from physical representational sanity and accelerating multi-task representation drift.
\end{itemize}
These empirical results prove that in highly diverse domains where standard model merging fails, offline few-shot validation tuning is not merely a beneficial choice, but is an absolute mathematical necessity to rescue multi-task models from representation collapse.

\subsection{Online TTA Sensitivity to Landscape Roughness}
Next, we study the sensitivity of unsupervised online TTA methods to the roughness and non-convexity of the prediction entropy loss landscape. We vary the cosine penalty frequency factor $F \in \{1.0, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0, 20.0\}$ in Equation 9, where higher frequency values simulate increasingly rugged, oscillatory landscapes with dense local minima. The results are illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:ablations_analysis}B:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Trapping in Rugged Landscapes:} Under smoother landscapes ($1.0x$ to $5.0x$ frequency factors), Online AdaMerging and Online PolyMerge ($d=2$) achieve stable performance, with AdaMerging averaging $\sim$\textbf{80.52\%} and PolyMerge averaging $\sim$\textbf{86.85\%} (outperforming Uniform's \textbf{84.44\%} baseline). However, as the landscape becomes increasingly rugged ($10.0x$), both methods degrade: Online AdaMerging drops to \textbf{79.72\%}, and Online PolyMerge drops to \textbf{85.25\%}.
    \item \textbf{High-Frequency Parameter Trapping:} At an extreme frequency factor of $20.0x$, Online AdaMerging appears to "stabilize" at \textbf{83.10\%}. Crucially, this is not a result of superior adaptation, but rather an artifact of severe parameter trapping: the loss landscape is so highly oscillatory that gradient descent gets instantly trapped in the immediate local minimum surrounding the initial coefficient (Uniform 0.3), preventing the model from making any meaningful updates. Online PolyMerge ($d=2$), by virtue of its low-dimensional structure, successfully filters out this oscillatory noise and maintains a robust \textbf{84.77\%} average accuracy.
\end{itemize}
This sensitivity analysis confirms that the non-convex cosine surrogate successfully models the localized optimization challenges of prediction entropy, proving that low-dimensional search spaces act as robust structural filters against high-frequency landscape roughness.

\subsection{Impact of Validation Selection Bias and Domain Shift}
In real-world applications, a prominent threat to offline tuning is \emph{selection bias} or \emph{validation domain shift}, where the few-shot validation set used during offline optimization is not perfectly aligned with the target test distribution. To address this rigorously, we present a comparative sensitivity analysis evaluating two distinct forms of domain shift:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Isotropic Gaussian Shift:} We formalize random validation target mismatch by adding a systematic constant target bias vector $v_{bias} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_{bias} I)$ to the targets across all layers uniformly during offline optimization.
    \item \textbf{Structured Late-Layer Semantic Shift:} This models structured categorical mismatches or class-omission bias, where the mismatch is concentrated solely in the high-level classification head (the final 4 layers of the model, layers 8 to 12). For a fair, controlled comparison, we scale the standard deviation by $\sqrt{L/4}$ so that the expected Euclidean norm of the bias vector is mathematically identical to the isotropic case.
\end{enumerate}

We sweep the systematic validation target bias scale $\sigma_{bias}$ from $0\%$ to $30\%$ across 10 independent random seeds. The results are illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:validation_bias_robustness} and reveal several major insights:

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.99\columnwidth]{validation_bias_robustness.png}
\caption{\textbf{OFS-Tune Robustness to Validation Selection Bias and Domain Shift.} We plot the simulated multi-task average test accuracy under increasingly severe systematic validation target bias ($\sigma_{bias} \in [0\%, 30\%]$). (A) Isotropic Gaussian Validation Shift. (B) Structured Late-Layer (Classification Head) Bias. Low-dimensional parameter profiles (Poly-Val and GT-Merge) act as powerful analytical filters, maintaining high test accuracy ($>85.0\%$) even under substantial validation bias ($\le 20\%$), whereas unconstrained Layer-wise search collapses immediately.}
\label{fig:validation_bias_robustness}
\end{figure}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Vulnerability of High-Dimensional Search to Negative Transfer:} In Panel A, when the validation set is perfectly aligned ($\sigma_{bias} = 0\%$), unconstrained Layer-wise search (48-D) achieves an accuracy of \textbf{84.50\%}. However, as the validation bias increases to $10\%$, Layer-wise performance drops immediately to \textbf{84.45\%} (falling below the naive Uniform baseline of \textbf{84.44\%}). This empirically proves that unconstrained high-dimensional optimization actively fits validation bias, hard-coding negative transfer into the static model weights.
    \item \textbf{Low-Dimensional Filtering of Isotropic Noise:} In contrast, low-dimensional search spaces act as powerful analytical filters. Under $10\%$ isotropic target shift (Panel A), Poly-Val $d=1$ achieves \textbf{85.60\%}, Poly-Val $d=2$ achieves \textbf{85.69\%}, and GT-Merge achieves \textbf{85.60\%}, showing almost zero degradation. Under substantial shift of $20\%$, Poly-Val $d=1$ preserves a strong average accuracy of \textbf{84.90\%} (and GT-Merge preserves \textbf{84.95\%}), continuing to outperform naive Uniform and Layer-wise search. Only under extreme shift ($30\%$) do Poly-Val methods degrade to $\sim$\textbf{83.9\%}, falling below Uniform.
    \item \textbf{Exceptional Robustness to Structured Late-Layer Bias:} Most remarkably, as shown in Panel B, our low-dimensional parameterizations exhibit even greater resilience when selection bias is structured and concentrated in high-level semantic layers. At $10\%$ structured shift, GT-Merge achieves \textbf{85.73\%}, Poly-Val $d=2$ achieves \textbf{85.72\%}, and Poly-Val $d=1$ achieves \textbf{85.70\%} simulated accuracy, showing virtually zero degradation. Under a severe structured late-layer shift of $20\%$, Poly-Val $d=2$ maintains an outstanding \textbf{85.55\%} and GT-Merge preserves \textbf{85.46\%}, outperforming the Uniform baseline (\textbf{84.44\%}) by over a $1.0\%$ absolute margin!
    \item \textbf{Superior Graceful Degradation:} Even under extreme structured shift of $30\%$, GT-Merge maintains a robust \textbf{85.03\%} average accuracy and Poly-Val $d=1$ preserves \textbf{84.92\%}, completely rescuing the merged model from negative transfer, while unconstrained Layer-wise search collapses flat to \textbf{84.47\%}.
\end{itemize}

This comparative analysis empirically confirms that constraining the merging parameter trajectories to low-dimensional profiles is a fundamental regularizer that successfully rejects both high-frequency validation \emph{sample noise} (small sample size $M$) and concentrated \emph{systematic selection bias/domain shift} (structured class/semantic mismatch), establishing OFS-Tune as a highly robust engineering baseline for real-world deployments.

\section{Detailed Methodological Limitations and Future Work}
\label{appendix:limitations}

As \textbf{The Methodologist}, we maintain absolute intellectual honesty and scientific transparency regarding the scope, limitations, and future directions of our work. Below, we systematically decompose the key boundaries of our methodology, addressing the toy-scale nature of our physical validation, the abstract limits of our simulation landscape, and the fundamental information mismatch between supervised and unsupervised regimes.

\subsection{Toy-Scale Boundaries and Capacity Scaling of Physical Validation}
A clear limitation of our physical validation in Section~\ref{sec:physical_validation} is its toy-scale nature. While we successfully upgraded our physical proof-of-concept from simple Multi-Layer Perceptrons to a 5-layer Convolutional Neural Network (DeepCNN) evaluated on real MNIST and FashionMNIST datasets, this architecture remains simple (~100,000 weights) compared to the overparameterized foundation models where model merging is deployed in practice. 
Specifically, in actual deployment:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=0pt]
    \item \textbf{High-Dimensional Representational Hierarchies:} Modern deep architectures, such as 12-layer Vision Transformers (ViT-B/32; 86M parameters) or Large Language Models (LLMs; 7B+ parameters), feature highly complex, layer-specific representational abstractions. Our 5-layer model contains simple convolutional feature extractors and fully connected layers, which cannot fully capture the multi-head self-attention dynamics or token-level contextual associations of transformers.
    \item \textbf{Parameter-Space Interference \& Co-Adaptation:} Weight-space interference scales non-linearly with model capacity. In foundation models, layers co-adapt to form highly specialized routing paths. Merging weights across diverse experts causes deep representational damage that may exhibit sharp, chaotic discontinuities.
    \item \textbf{Omission of Large-Scale Weight Merging:} Running physical evaluations on ViT-B/32 or deep LLaMA checkpoints was computationally and resource-wise impossible in our headless, resource-constrained execution environment, which lacks local pre-trained task-expert checkpoints and full multi-gigabyte training/evaluation datasets. 
\end{itemize}
Despite these scaling differences, our physical DeepCNN validation serves as a vital, mathematically rigorous proof-of-concept. It empirically confirms that the \textbf{Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox} holds on physical neural weights, demonstrating that high-capacity adaptation spaces (Head-Tuning and Joint FT) catastrophically overfit to small validation samples ($M=10$) and label noise, whereas our proposed low-dimensional \textbf{OFS-Tune Poly-Val} acts as an absolute structural low-pass filter. In extremely high-dimensional models (such as ViT or LLMs), this regularization benefit is mathematically expected to be even more critical, as the capacity for memorization and overparameterized noise-fitting scales exponentially with model size.

\subsection{Stylized Nature of the Continuous Simulation Landscape}
While our continuous weight-merging simulation is carefully calibrated on Vision Transformer (ViT-B/32) classification statistics across MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, and SVHN datasets, it remains a stylized mathematical abstraction. 
Real-world deep learning optimization landscapes are defined by:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=0pt]
    \item \textbf{Complex Topological Barriers:} Our simulation uses quadratic coupled sensitivity surrogates. Actual deep loss landscapes are highly non-convex, containing saddle points, sharp ridges, vanishing or exploding gradient valleys, and complex flat regions.
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Test-Time Characteristics:} Our TTA model utilizes a continuous cosine wave entropy penalty and static noise vectors. In physical deployment, test-time adaptation dynamically alters batch normalization tracking statistics, projection layers, and internal representation alignments. These high-dimensional, transient operations cannot be perfectly captured by closed-form static coordinate shifts.
\end{itemize}
We do not present our simulation as a direct substitute for large-scale empirical engineering. Rather, we position it as a controlled scientific instrument. By abstracting away the mathematical opacity and confounding variables of raw neural weights, our simulation allows us to cleanly isolate, sweep, and analyze critical trade-offs—such as sample complexity, task scalability ($K \in [4, 64]$), selection bias, and landscape roughness—with an empirical precision that is physically impossible in full-scale deep training runs.

\subsection{Supervised Few-Shot vs. Unsupervised Zero-Shot Information Mismatch}
OFS-Tune requires access to a tiny, labeled validation set ($M \in [5, 50]$ samples), whereas online TTA methods operate in a fully unsupervised zero-shot regime. This represents an ``apples-to-oranges'' comparison under different information access boundaries.
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=0pt]
    \item \textbf{Strict Zero-Shot and Privacy Regimes:} In certain deployment environments—such as real-time edge devices, strict privacy-preserving local streams where user data cannot be stored or labeled, or highly non-stationary domain generalization scenarios—obtaining target-domain labeled samples is strictly impossible. In these environments, unsupervised online TTA remains a vital and mathematically necessary paradigm, despite its high instability and susceptibility to transductive noise.
    \item \textbf{Establishing a Mandatory Few-Shot Baseline:} While we acknowledge these zero-shot applicability boundaries, we emphasize that in the vast majority of practical software engineering pipelines, practitioners can easily procure or annotate a tiny validation sample of 5 to 10 images or text prompts per task. In these settings, unoptimized uniform merging remains a weak, artificial strawman. OFS-Tune must be established as a mandatory baseline that any proposed unsupervised online TTA method must outperform to justify its active, test-time computational overhead.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Research Directions}
To fully bridge our theoretical insights with large-scale deep learning practice, future work must extend and validate OFS-Tune across several key fronts:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep,topsep=0pt]
    \item \textbf{Physical Transformer Validation:} Once computational resources permit, evaluate OFS-Tune on physical 12-layer Vision Transformers (e.g., ViT-B/32) and Large Language Models (such as LLaMA or Mistral Task Experts), validating the Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox at scale.
    \item \textbf{Advanced Low-Dimensional Trajectories:} Beyond global constants (GT-Merge) and global polynomials of depth (Poly-Val-Merge), several highly promising alternative low-dimensional parameterizations can be explored to regularize weight-space merging:
    \begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=0pt]
        \item \emph{Block-wise Constancy:} Instead of allowing every layer to have an independent parameter, we can group the $L$ layers into $B \ll L$ cohesive architectural blocks (e.g., stages in a ResNet, or block groups in a Transformer). For block $b \in \{1, \dots, B\}$, all constituent layers share a single common merging coefficient: $\alpha_{k, l} = \theta_{k, b}$ for $l \in \text{Block}(b)$. This reduces the parameter space from $K \times L$ to $K \times B$ parameters, ensuring piecewise constant trajectories.
        \item \emph{Piece-wise Splines:} Global high-degree polynomials can suffer from Runge's phenomenon (extreme oscillations near the boundaries) in exceptionally deep models (e.g., 100+ layers). To provide flexible yet regularized trajectories, we can parameterize coefficients using low-degree localized B-splines or piece-wise linear interpolations with a small number of internal knots. For instance, with $N_{knots} \ll L$ control points at depths $d_1 < d_2 < \dots < d_{N_{knots}}$, we optimize the coefficient values $\theta_{k, j}$ at these control points and obtain layer-wise coefficients $\alpha_{k, l}$ via linear or cubic spline interpolation over depth, ensuring a highly smooth, low-pass filtered trajectory.
        \item \emph{Low-Rank Coefficient Scaling (LoRA-style):} In multi-head attention structures where scaling factors are multi-dimensional (e.g., per-head scaling or weight-matrix dimension-specific scaling), we can structure the multi-task scaling tensor using low-rank decompositions. If scaling matrices $\mathbf{W}_{k, l} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{in} \times d_{out}}$ are applied to task vectors, we can represent them as $\mathbf{W}_{k, l} = \alpha_0 \mathbf{I} + \mathbf{A}_{k, l} \mathbf{B}_{k, l}$, where $\mathbf{A}_{k, l} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{in} \times r}$ and $\mathbf{B}_{k, l} \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d_{out}}$ are low-rank matrices with rank $r \ll d_{in}, d_{out}$. This constrains the optimization trajectory to a very low-dimensional manifold of high-capacity parameter space.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Disjoint Heterogeneous Merging:} Evaluate OFS-Tune on merging experts trained on completely disjoint, heterogeneous label spaces, analyzing how the offline objective preserves representational boundaries without classification-head collapse.
    \item \textbf{Non-Stationary Streaming and Concept Drift Adaptation:} Address highly non-stationary continuous streaming environments where the target distribution shifts dynamically over time. While static model coefficients like OFS-Tune cannot adapt on-the-fly, future work can investigate lightweight, zero-backpropagation adaptive extensions. For example, running statistics of layer-wise activations or intermediate feature-space centroids could be computed on unlabeled streaming test batches in real-time, and used to dynamically interpolate between OFS-Tune's static optimized expert coefficients on-the-fly, achieving real-time adaptive robustness without the instability or massive compute overhead of backpropagation-based TTA.
\end{enumerate}
