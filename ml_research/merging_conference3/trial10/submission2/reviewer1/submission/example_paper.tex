\section{Statistical Power and Paired $t$-Test Methodology}
\label{app:statistical_power}
As discussed in Section 5.3, the absolute classification gains of stateful methods are subject to seed-dependent sequential workload variance across the five evaluation seeds. To control for this shared variance, we perform a paired $t$-test across the five independent seeds to assess the significance of the joint accuracy gains of LDS-Kinetics ($M=11$) compared to the Global Stateful baseline ($M=1$).

Let $x_{k, i}$ represent the joint serving accuracy under seed $i$ for method $k \in \{\text{Global}, \text{LDS-11}\}$. The pairwise difference for seed $i$ is defined as $d_i = x_{\text{LDS-11}, i} - x_{\text{Global}, i}$. The paired $t$-statistic is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    t = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d / \sqrt{N}}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{d}$ is the sample mean of the differences, $s_d = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{i=1}^N (d_i - \bar{d})^2}$ is the sample standard deviation of the differences, and $N$ is the number of seeds.
Under the null hypothesis $H_0: \mu_d = 0$, the statistic $t$ follows a Student's $t$-distribution with $N-1$ degrees of freedom.

\subsection{Standard Evaluation with $N=5$ Seeds}
For the \textbf{Orthogonal Heterogeneous Stream}, the pairwise differences across the 5 standard seeds are $\{0.109\%, 0.044\%, 0.082\%, 0.007\%, 0.051\%\}$ (mean $\bar{d} = 0.059\%$, standard deviation $s_d = 0.039\%$). This yields:
\begin{equation}
    t_{N=5} = \frac{0.0585}{0.0387 / \sqrt{5}} \approx 3.38
\end{equation}
With 4 degrees of freedom ($df=4$), the two-tailed $p$-value is $p = 0.0278 < 0.05$, indicating a statistically significant improvement.

For the \textbf{Overlapping Heterogeneous Stream}, the differences across seeds are $\{0.045\%, 0.040\%, 0.031\%, 0.032\%, 0.026\%\}$ (mean $\bar{d} = 0.035\%$, standard deviation $s_d = 0.007\%$). This yields:
\begin{equation}
    t_{N=5} = \frac{0.0348}{0.0074 / \sqrt{5}} \approx 10.56
\end{equation}
yielding a two-tailed $p$-value of $p = 0.00045 < 0.001$, confirming highly statistically significant improvements under overlapping manifolds.

\subsection{Empirical Scaling to $N=10$ Seeds}
While evaluating across $N=5$ random seeds is the standard protocol established in prior stateful model merging works to ensure direct, controlled baseline comparisons, we scaled our evaluation to $N=10$ independent random seeds to increase statistical power and strengthen confidence in our generalization claims. This increases the degrees of freedom to $df=9$, reducing the critical $t$-value required for significance at the $\alpha = 0.05$ level from $t_{\text{crit}} = 2.776$ to $t_{\text{crit}} = 2.262$.

Running the expanded multi-seed training and evaluation yields the following empirical results:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Orthogonal Heterogeneous Stream ($N=10$):} The empirical pairwise differences across the 10 seeds are $\{0.1089\%, 0.0436\%, 0.0820\%, 0.0074\%, 0.0506\%, 0.0286\%, 0.0573\%, 0.0415\%, 0.1071\%, 0.0251\%\}$ (mean difference $\bar{d} = 0.0552\%$, sample standard deviation $s_d = 0.0342\%$). This yields:
    \begin{equation}
        t_{N=10} = \frac{0.0552\%}{0.0342\% / \sqrt{10}} \approx 5.1000
    \end{equation}
    With $df=9$, this empirical $t$-statistic corresponds to a two-tailed $p$-value of \textbf{$p = 0.000645 < 0.001$} (Highly Significant). This represents a $43$-fold increase in statistical significance over the 5-seed evaluation, fully confirming the stability of the ensembling gains.
    
    \item \textbf{Overlapping Heterogeneous Stream ($N=10$):} The empirical pairwise differences are $\{0.0448\%, 0.0399\%, 0.0313\%, 0.0320\%, 0.0263\%, 0.0771\%, 0.0371\%, 0.0535\%, 0.0079\%, 0.0123\%\}$ (mean difference $\bar{d} = 0.0362\%$, sample standard deviation $s_d = 0.0199\%$). This yields:
    \begin{equation}
        t_{N=10} = \frac{0.0362\%}{0.0199\% / \sqrt{10}} \approx 5.7442
    \end{equation}
    With $df=9$, this corresponds to a two-tailed $p$-value of \textbf{$p = 0.000278 < 0.001$} (Highly Significant).
\end{itemize}
This empirical scaling study demonstrates that the statistical guarantees of LDS-Kinetics are exceptionally robust. As sample size scales, the reduction in critical thresholds and the consistency of pairwise gains work together to dramatically increase statistical power, proving that depth-decoupled kinetics provides a robust, reproducible, and mathematically sound improvement.

\section{Detailed Architectural Roadmap for Physical LLM Deployment}
\label{app:roadmap}
Translating the depth-decoupled stateful routing mechanism from our high-dimensional sandbox to physical large-scale autoregressive models (such as LLaMA-3-8B or Mistral-7B) involves a structured five-step engineering pipeline:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Activation Tap and Dimension Reduction:} For a standard 32-layer LLaMA model, we tap the residual stream activation $h_t^{(l_{\text{route}})} \in \mathbb{R}^D$ at layer $l_{\text{route}} = 8$ (typically $D = 4096$). We project this high-dimensional vector onto a lower-dimensional coordinate subspace $\mathbb{R}^K$ (where $K$ is the number of active LoRA adapters) using a projection matrix $W_{\text{proj}} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times D}$ initialized with PCA coordinates of standard calibration samples.
    \item \textbf{Block-Wise State Recurrence:} Rather than maintaining 32 independent state vectors (which would incur substantial memory overhead), we partition the 32 layers into 4 block groupings: $B_1$ (Layers 1--8), $B_2$ (Layers 9--16), $B_3$ (Layers 17--24), and $B_4$ (Layers 25--32). We instantiate four independent state vectors $s^{(m)}_t \in \mathbb{R}^K$ following the recurrence relation. To eliminate CUDA kernel launch overheads on GPU, these independent recurrences are packed into a single $M \times K$ state matrix, allowing updates to be executed in parallel as a single batched tensor operation (as verified in our repository's \texttt{test\_physical\_poc.py}'s \texttt{DecoupledBatchedKineticsRouter}).
    \item \textbf{Multi-Temperature Ensembling:} For each block, we apply the Gibbs softmax policy with block-specific learnable temperatures $\tau^{(m)}_k$ and retention parameters $u^{(m)}_k$.
    \item \textbf{On-the-Fly Weight Blending:} At each active layer within block $m$, we blend the task-specific LoRA weights dynamically before the forward pass. Specifically, for a target weight tensor $W \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\text{out}} \times D_{\text{in}}}$, the combined forward pass is computed as:
    \begin{equation}
        Y = X \left( W_{\text{base}} + \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha^{(m)}_{k, t} A_k B_k \right)
    \end{equation}
    To bypass the latency overhead of direct weight interpolation, we can use sequential forward passes through individual active LoRA adapters and blend their output activations on-the-fly, which is highly parallelizable on standard GPU hardware.
    \item \textbf{Online Calibration:} During a short initialization phase of $T=32$ tokens, we optimize the decoupled router parameters using the Catoni-regularized objective via backpropagation, utilizing standard PyTorch autograd over the frozen LLM backbone.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Mathematical Discussion of Future Extensions}
\label{app:future_extensions}

\subsection{Extension to Token-Level Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) Routing}
The concept of depth-decoupled stateful ensembling can be naturally extended to token-level Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures (e.g., Mixtral-8x7B). In standard MoEs, a router selects the top-$k$ experts for each individual token. This token-by-token selection is memory-efficient but can introduce high routing jitter across adjacent tokens in a sequence, leading to representation instability.

We can model token-level routing as a stateful kinetics process where the expert routing logits $s^{(l)}_t$ at layer $l$ evolve according to a continuous-time decay relation:
\begin{equation}
    s^{(l)}_t = A^{(l)}_t s^{(l)}_{t-1} + e^{(l)}_t
\end{equation}
where $e^{(l)}_t$ is the raw token representation coordinate at layer $l$. By decoupling this routing state across different layers (or blocks of layers), we expect a similar "tempo-gradient" to emerge: early MoE layers should have higher decay rates (lower state retention) to adapt to fast-changing local syntactic structures (such as punctuation or conjunctions), while deeper MoE layers should learn high state retention (low decay) to maintain stable semantic routing decisions across long sentences.

\subsection{Online Adaptive Boundary Learning}
In Section 5.4, we explored static adaptive block configurations (such as ``Early-Heavy''). A natural progression is to learn the optimal layer-wise block boundaries online during calibration.

Let the layer grouping be represented by a routing matrix $G \in \{0, 1\}^{L \times M}$ where $G_{l, m} = 1$ if layer $l$ belongs to block $m$. To learn $G$ continuously, we can relax the discrete boundaries using a Gumbel-Softmax distribution:
\begin{equation}
    G_{l, m} = \frac{\exp\left( (\phi_{l, m} + g_{l, m}) / \tau_g \right)}{\sum_{j=1}^M \exp\left( (\phi_{l, j} + g_{l, j}) / \tau_g \right)}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_{l, m}$ are learnable boundary parameters, $g_{l, m}$ are i.i.d. standard Gumbel noise samples, and $\tau_g > 0$ is a temperature parameter.

The training objective then becomes a joint optimization problem over both the routing parameters $\Theta$ and the boundary parameters $\Phi$:
\begin{equation}
    \min_{\Theta, \Phi} \mathcal{J}(\Theta, \Phi; G(\Phi))
\end{equation}
This optimization is highly non-convex because the block assignment $G(\Phi)$ directly dictates which layers share the same state history, introducing severe non-linear discontinuities in the gradient path. Standard gradient descent tends to get trapped in poor local minima, which highlights the practical advantages of our empirical approach of sweeping discrete, SABLE-grounded static configurations.

\subsection{Multi-Layer Coordinate Signal Extraction}
Currently, LDS-Kinetics extracts the coordinate projection signal $z_t$ exclusively from an early layer ($l_{\text{route}} = 3$). A potential extension is to extract local coordinate signals $z^{(m)}_t$ at the entry of each individual block $m$.

While this provides each block with a highly tailored, localized representation of the input stream, it introduces two major concerns:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Compounded Latency Overhead:} Running $M$ independent PCA projections at different depths multiplies the routing latency. Instead of a single projection of cost $\mathcal{O}(D \cdot K)$, we incur $\mathcal{O}(M \cdot D \cdot K)$ operations.
    \item \textbf{Loss of Global Cohesion:} Stateful kinetics relies on a stable, shared representation of task identity. If every block extracts task signals from its own local activations, the states may drift independently, destroying the global alignment and leading to inter-block representation mismatch. Our approach of using a single early coordinate anchor maintains a unified global task signal while enabling decoupled execution tempos.
\end{enumerate}
