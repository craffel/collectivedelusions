# Soundness and Methodology Evaluation

## 1. Technical Flaws and Hidden Assumptions

### A. Unrealistic Data Footprint in ZipIt! Coordinate Alignment
The paper evaluates the proposed methods on actual Vision Transformer (ViT-B/16) checkpoints by merging two experts fine-tuned on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.
- **The Issue**: In Section 4.5, the authors state that because estimating the $768 \times 768$ activation covariance matrices on only 10 samples per task would result in rank-deficient sample covariances, they "utilized a separate, unlabelled calibration footprint of 100 samples per task strictly to compute stable covariance statistics for ZipIt! coordinate alignment."
- **The Critique**: This is a major methodological loophole. The paper claims to operate under a highly sample-efficient "10-shot calibration budget" (10 samples per task). However, the overall pipeline actually consumes **110 samples per task** (100 for alignment + 10 for coefficient tuning). If 100 unlabeled samples are available, the motivation for extremely restricted 10-shot trajectory optimization is severely weakened. Furthermore, the authors do not evaluate how unconstrained layer-wise ensembling (Offline Unconstrained) or Globally-Scaled ensembling would perform if optimized on this larger pool of 110 samples. If unconstrained optimization were given 110 samples, it would likely be much more stable, potentially eroding the advantage of the highly restricted spectral trajectory class. While the authors suggest using "Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage" in the appendix to eliminate this auxiliary footprint, they did not actually execute their experiments using it; their primary real-world results still rely on this inflated data budget.

### B. Vacuous and Hidden Depth-Scaling in the Generalization Bridge
In Section 3.5 and Appendix Section 1.4, the authors derive a downstream prediction generalization bound via covering numbers of the trajectory-parameterized network class (Eq. 16 / Eq. 34), claiming that the bound is "completely independent of the parameter count of the underlying deep network! The capacity is bounded solely by the trajectory dimension $d$."
- **The Issue**: The covering number bound depends on the parameter-Lipschitz constant $L_{\Theta}$, which is the Lipschitz constant of the model's output with respect to the trajectory parameter vector $\Theta$.
- **The Critique**: Because the ensembling coefficients $\Theta$ enter the network multiplicatively across all $L$ layers, the final output $f(x; \Theta)$ is a high-degree polynomial of $\Theta$. Consequently, the parameter-Lipschitz constant $L_{\Theta}$ will scale exponentially with network depth $L$ (i.e., $L_{\Theta} = \mathcal{O}(C^L)$ where $C$ is a constant related to layer weights and representations) for standard neural network architectures. This means that the exponential scaling of the bound with network depth $L$ is merely hidden inside the variable name $L_{\Theta}$. For deep networks (such as modern LLMs with 32 to 80 layers, which the authors claim their method scales to), $L_{\Theta}$ will be extremely large or infinite, rendering the covering number bound completely vacuous. Presenting this bound as a "rigorous guarantee" that "proves spectral trajectory optimization avoids the curse of overparameterization" is highly misleading as it glosses over the standard, unresolved deep network Lipschitz scaling pathology.

### C. Structural Flaw of Fourier Trajectories (RB-FTM)
The authors acknowledge in Section 3.9 that by adopting a periodic integer-frequency Fourier basis, RB-FTM mathematically forces a periodic boundary identity at $z=0$ (the first layer) and $z=1$ (the last layer), forcing identical ensembling weights: $\alpha_k(0) = \alpha_k(L-1)$.
- **The Critique**: Forcing identical merging coefficients at the first layer (low-level feature extraction) and the last layer (high-level classification projection) represents a fundamental structural flaw. These layers perform completely different functional roles. While the authors resolve this boundary identity by introducing the Discrete Cosine Trajectory (RB-DCTM) variant, they still present the fundamentally flawed RB-FTM as a primary contribution of the paper and show that it performs well in Table 1, which is theoretically counterintuitive.

## 2. Appropriateness of Methods

### A. The Artificial Sandbox vs. The "Static Uniform Dominance Paradox"
The primary quantitative evaluation of this paper is conducted in the "Analytical Coordinate Sandbox" (ACS). Inside this sandbox, the parameter-free, zero-tuning **Static Uniform** baseline consistently and significantly outperforms the proposed methods (by ~11% to 15% categorical accuracy). The authors frame this as a "paradox" and analyze it as an "anisotropic representation shearing pathology".
- **The Critique**: The sandbox is highly artificial (a purely linear dynamical system of coordinate recurrence without non-linearities, convolutions, or self-attention) and completely coordinate-aligned. If the sandbox is so artificial and idealized that a zero-parameter static baseline consistently crushes their proposed methods, then the sandbox itself is a highly questionable benchmark for evaluating adaptive layer-wise ensembling. By design, the sandbox removes the very phenomena (non-linear activation coupling, scale shifts, coordinate misalignment) that make adaptive layer-wise ensembling necessary in the first place! Evaluating adaptive merging in an environment where any adaptation is mathematically guaranteed to be counterproductive represents a major methodological flaw. Why spend so much space on a simulated environment where the proposed methods fail to beat a simple static baseline, rather than dedicating the core paper to actual checkpoint merging over multiple diverse models?

## 3. Reproducibility
- **Mathematical Derivations**: The mathematical proofs are highly detailed and presented step-by-step in the appendix, which is excellent.
- **Optimization Protocol**: The step-by-step deployment protocol in Section 5.9 and the pseudocode/simulation descriptions are thorough, which helps with reproducibility. However, the exact datasets or seeds for the synthetic sandbox are not fully specified in the main text.
- **Code Availability**: The authors discuss the automatic differentiation engine and Adam optimization steps, but do not provide a link to a public repository containing the code for reproducibility.
