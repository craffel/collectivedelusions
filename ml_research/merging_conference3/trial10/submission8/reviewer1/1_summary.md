# Comprehensive Summary: Rademacher-Bounded Fourier Trajectory Merging

## 1. Main Topic
The paper addresses the challenge of post-hoc weight-space model merging and adaptive layer-wise ensembling. When combining multiple task-specific expert models fine-tuned from a shared pre-trained base model, tuning ensembling coefficients independently for each network layer on small calibration datasets (e.g., few-shot or test-time adaptation) results in a high-dimensional search space that is highly susceptible to transductive overfitting. This overfitting manifests as severe representation shearing and out-of-distribution generalization collapse. The paper proposes to constrain the ensembling coefficients across depth to follow continuous, low-frequency spectral trajectories.

## 2. Methodology and Approach
The authors introduce **Rademacher-Bounded Fourier Trajectory Merging (RB-FTM)** and its non-periodic counterpart **Rademacher-Bounded Discrete Cosine Trajectory Merging (RB-DCTM)**.
- **Trajectory Parameterization**: Instead of treating each layer's ensembling coefficient $\alpha_k(l)$ as an independent parameter (which scales as $K \times L$ where $K$ is the number of tasks and $L$ is the number of layers), the authors parameterize the trajectory as a continuous curve across depth. This curve is represented as a low-frequency continuous Fourier series (sinusoids) or half-period Cosine series (DCT) with a spectral cutoff frequency $F \ll L$.
- **Empirical Rademacher Complexity Bounds**: The authors derive mathematically rigorous bounds on the empirical Rademacher complexity of both the Fourier ($\mathcal{H}_F$) and DCT ($\mathcal{H}_F^{\text{DCT}}$) trajectory classes over depth coordinates:
  - Fourier Trajectory Complexity: $\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_L(\mathcal{H}_F) \le C_0 \sqrt{\frac{2 \ln(4F+2)}{L}}$
  - DCT Trajectory Complexity: $\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_L(\mathcal{H}_F^{\text{DCT}}) \le C_0 \sqrt{\frac{2 \ln(2F+2)}{L}}$
  These bounds demonstrate that the trajectory's capacity to fluctuate is strictly limited by the cutoff frequency $F$ and network depth $L$, independent of the parameter count of the underlying neural network.
- **Spectral Lasso ($L_1$) Regularization**: To physically enforce this complexity bound, the authors introduce a Spectral Lasso penalty applied strictly to the harmonic (non-DC) trajectory coefficients: $\gamma \sum_k \|\theta_{k,\text{harm}}\|_1$. This penalty constrains trajectory fluctuations and promotes spectral sparsity by driving redundant high-frequency coefficients to zero.
- **Boundary Properties**: The use of a half-period cosine basis (RB-DCTM) resolves the boundary runaway characteristic of prior polynomial trajectories (e.g., RBPM, $d=2$) and eliminates the periodic boundary identity forced by standard Fourier bases. It introduces an implicit homogeneous Neumann boundary condition ($h'(0) = h'(1) = 0$), acting as a "boundary buffer" that stabilizes feature-extraction and classification layers.

## 3. Key Findings
- **Static Uniform Dominance Paradox**: In perfectly coordinate-aligned representation spaces (such as the main sandbox evaluation), the parameter-free, zero-tuning **Static Uniform** baseline ($1/K$) is a robust consensus upper bound (85.10% on CNN, 83.75% on CLIP) because any parameter adaptation induces "anisotropic representation shearing" that degrades generalization.
- **Spectral Trajectory Robustness**: Under adaptation constraints (e.g., representation coordinate rotations and projections), the proposed spectral methods (RB-FTM and RB-DCTM) serve as highly effective regularized optimizers, outperforming all other tuned/adaptive baselines (Globally-Scaled, Offline Unconstrained, and RBPM).
- **Real-World Proof-of-Concept**: In merging two actual Vision Transformer (ViT-B/16) checkpoints fine-tuned on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 aligned with ZipIt!, **RB-DCTM (F=2)** achieves a joint average test accuracy of **74.90%**, outperforming Static Uniform (71.30%), Globally-Scaled (72.50%), Offline Unconstrained (69.80%), and RBPM (70.70%).
- **Automated Cutoff Selection**: The Spectral Lasso regularizer successfully functions as a sparse spectral selector, pruning higher-frequency coefficients (e.g., $f=3, 4, 5$) to zero during Adam optimization starting from $F_{\max}=5$, dynamically balancing trajectory complexity.

## 4. Explicitly Claimed Contributions and Evidence
1. **Trigonometric/Cosine Trajectory Representation**: Continuous projection of ensembling coefficients onto sinusoidal/cosinusoidal bases. Evidence: Formal formulation in Section 3.2 and 3.9, and trajectory profile visualizations showing smooth parameter curves across depth.
2. **Learning-Theoretic Complexity Guarantees**: Complete proofs for trajectory-space Rademacher complexity bounds and a downstream prediction generalization bridge via covering numbers. Evidence: Theorem 3.1, Theorem 3.4, and detailed derivations in Appendix Sections 1.1–1.6.
3. **Spectral Lasso ($L_1$) Regularization**: Formulating a practical optimization objective that couples the learning-theoretic bound to a physical regularizer on harmonic coefficients. Evidence: Section 3.8 and Appendix 5.7 demonstrating automated high-frequency pruning to absolute zero during optimization.
4. **Boundary Runaway Mitigation and Runge's Phenomenon Mitigation**: Overcoming the extreme boundary oscillations of polynomial trajectory baselines. Evidence: Table 1 (Deep12LayerCNN where RBPM drops to 39.30% due to boundary sensitivity while RB-FTM achieves 70.70%), Table 3 (ViT-B/16 where RBPM gets 70.70% while RB-DCTM gets 74.90%), and theoretical analysis of Neumann boundary flat-derivatives in Section 3.9.
