# Novelty Assessment: SPS-ZCA

## Delta from Prior Work
The proposed SPS-ZCA framework is situated in the context of parameter-efficient multi-expert serving and dynamic model merging. The primary "delta" compared to existing state-of-the-art baselines includes:
1. **Dynamic Routing Space (vs. PFSR):** While Parameter-Free Subspace Routing (PFSR) routes inputs by projecting features against late-stage classification heads, SPS-ZCA shifts the routing operation to an early-stage representation space (specifically Layer 3 of the pre-trained backbone). This is designed to bypass task-specific vocabulary asymmetries and resolve the temporal circular dependency (routing paradox).
2. **Execution Flow (vs. MBH):** Micro-Batch Homogenization (MBH) partitions heterogeneous batches and executes sequential forward passes, resulting in a linear latency penalty $O(K)$. SPS-ZCA proposes to execute a single, parallel forward pass and blend the task-specific activations sample-wise on-the-fly, transforming the latency profile back to constant $O(1)$.
3. **Calibration & OOD Detection:** The introduction of Unit-Norm Calibration (which is mathematically identical to standard cosine similarity), Intra-Task Dispersion Calibration (a heuristic division by expected similarity), and a diagonal Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) fitted over low-dimensional routing coordinates for out-of-distribution rejection.

## Characterization of Theoretical Novelty
From a theoretical and mathematical perspective, the conceptual novelty of SPS-ZCA is characterized as **incremental to modest**. The framework is highly engineered, combining well-established, classical statistical and machine learning building blocks to address systems-ML overheads rather than introducing new theoretical paradigms or mathematical frameworks:

- **Zero-Shot Centroid Routing:** Pre-computing centroids via sample averages and computing routing coefficients using cosine similarity (presented as "Unit-Norm Calibration") are standard practices in classical nearest-centroid classification (e.g., Rocchio classifier) and representation learning (e.g., Prototypical Networks). Applying them at Layer 3 of a Transformer is a practical architectural choice but lacks a formal theoretical foundation or rigorous justification for why Layer 3 is universally optimal.
- **Activation Blending (SPS):** Linear activation blending of LoRA adapters (Equation 5) is functionally a convex combination of low-rank updates. This formulation is highly related to existing linear weight-merging and activation-fusion techniques (such as AdapterFusion). The novel aspect is performing this on-the-fly sample-wise inside a single parallel pass rather than statically, which is an engineering/implementation contribution rather than a theoretical breakthrough.
- **Manifold Dispersion Calibration (IDC):** The division of raw similarities by their expected calibration mean (Equation 9) is a heuristic normalization. While empirically effective at balancing routing under asymmetric task variances, it lacks a rigorous probabilistic or statistical proof showing that this specific normalization corresponds to a valid density scaling or coordinate transformation.
- **Coordinate GMM Rejection:** Fitting a diagonal 2-component Gaussian Mixture Model over $K$-dimensional similarity coordinates is a standard density estimation technique. The application of standard PAC learning complexity bounds is mentioned qualitatively (Section 4.7) but is not formally proved for this specific coordinate density estimator.

## Conclusion on Novelty
The novelty of the paper lies primarily in its **systems-level integration, architectural execution layout, and thorough empirical evaluation of edge CPU performance bottlenecks**, rather than in its theoretical foundations. The mathematical formulations are straightforward adaptations of standard statistical operations (sample averages, cosine similarity, diagonal GMMs, Softmax) applied to deep learning activations. While the practical utility and co-designed compiler roadmap are highly valuable for edge deployment, the paper does not advance the theoretical understanding of representation spaces or provide formal mathematical guarantees of routing correctness or error propagation.
