# Systematic Mock Review: 2. Novelty Check

## 2.1 Theoretical and Methodological Novelty
The core novelty of the paper lies in introducing a **non-parametric Bayesian routing framework** for post-hoc dynamic model merging. While Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) routing layers are well-established, they typically rely on co-designing and jointly training the backbone representations and routing parameters from scratch or through extensive pre-training. Applying these techniques post-hoc to merge independent expert models under extreme calibration data constraints ($N \le 64$) is a relatively new and challenging frontier.

Specifically, the paper introduces two key novel formulations:
1. **GP-DR (Gaussian Process Dynamic Routing):** Instead of using parametric gating networks (e.g., MLPs, linear layers) trained with backpropagation, the authors model the dynamic merging coefficients $\alpha(\psi_*)$ using a Gaussian Process posterior. By treating the tiny calibration split of frozen representations as spatial "landmarks", GP-DR calculates the blending weights as a closed-form posterior mean. This represents a highly original synthesis of classical non-parametric Bayesian regression and post-hoc model merging, completely bypassing the need for training loops and gradient-descent optimization.
2. **Uncertainty-Guided OOD Fallback:** The paper derives an exact, closed-form posterior predictive variance $\sigma^2(\psi_*)$ to measure epistemic uncertainty for each test sample. This allows the merged model to trigger a safe fallback to a uniform prior blend when a test sample is highly out-of-distribution (OOD), preventing destructive parameter activations.

## 2.2 Comparison with SOTA Dynamic Merging Systems
The paper positions GP-DR against several recent state-of-the-art frameworks, highlighting its distinct advantages:
* **vs. Parametric Routers (L3-Linear, TSAR, QWS-Merge):** Standard linear routers or Task-Space Anchor Regularization (TSAR) train small gating networks using gradient descent on the calibration split. Under extreme data scarcity (e.g., $N=64$), these methods suffer heavily from the *Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox*, memorizing representation noise. GP-DR bypasses this completely by being entirely training-free and resolving routing coefficients in a single, stable closed-form matrix forward pass.
* **vs. PFSR (Parameter-Free Subspace Routing):** PFSR is a concurrent training-free, non-parametric approach that projects latent representations onto task subspaces via cosine similarity. While PFSR is robust to overfitting, it relies entirely on heuristic similarity thresholds and a sharp step-function (temperature $T=0.001$) to select experts. PFSR lacks any formal Bayesian uncertainty quantification and is completely blind to OOD inputs, making it highly fragile under extreme covariate shifts. GP-DR builds directly on PFSR’s coordinate projection, but replaces the heuristic projection with GPR, adding mathematically rigorous OOD detection.
* **vs. Classic GP Mixture-of-Experts:** Historically, infinite mixtures of GP experts (e.g., Rasmussen et al., 2001) used GPs as gating networks. However, those models required complex, non-analytical posterior approximations (like Laplace or Expectation Propagation) and iterative numerical optimization. GP-DR maintains a mathematically exact, analytical closed-form solution by making a continuous Gaussian likelihood approximation over discrete targets, reducing the online computational cost of GPR to a lightweight $O(NK) = O(N \cdot K)$ vector-matrix multiplication.

## 2.3 Novelty of Micro-Batch Homogenization (MBH)
To address the streaming deployment challenge, the paper proposes **Micro-Batch Homogenization (MBH)**. In production streaming environments, batches are highly heterogeneous. Standard neural network execution averages features across the batch (vectorization), which forces dynamic routing coefficients to collapse to uniform averages ("vectorization collapse"). 

While dynamic batching or sorting-based batching is common in systems engineering to improve throughput or minimize padding in sequence models, applying it at the routing level to **prevent representation-space collapse and preserve localized parameter-merging integrity** is highly original. MBH groups inputs into homogeneous micro-batches based on their predicted expert routing preferences, isolating representational spaces during the forward pass.

## 2.4 Overall Novelty Rating: Excellent
The paper does not merely apply an off-the-shelf algorithm to a new dataset. Instead, it carefully analyzes the mathematical and geometric limitations of current model merging routers, maps them to classical Bayesian formulations, and derives closed-form, training-free solutions with provable Lipschitz guarantees. The combination of non-parametric Bayesian routing, mathematical OOD thresholds, and a system-level batching solution (MBH) represents a highly cohesive, rigorous, and novel contribution to the field of modular deep learning.
