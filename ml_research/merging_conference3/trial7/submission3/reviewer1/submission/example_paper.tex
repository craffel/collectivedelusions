\section{Exact Derivation of Closed-Form GP-DR Equations}
\label{app:gp_derivation}

We present the complete step-by-step derivation of the posterior predictive distribution for Gaussian Process Dynamic Routing.

Let $\mathbf{\Psi}_{\text{cal}} = [\psi_1, \dots, \psi_N]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ be the landmark coordinates, and let $\mathbf{y}_k = [y_{1, k}, \dots, y_{N, k}]^T \in \mathbb{R}^N$ be the target column vector for a specific task $k \in \{1, \dots, K\}$. Let $\mathbf{m}_k = [1/K, \dots, 1/K]^T \in \mathbb{R}^N$ be the prior mean vector. 

Under the Gaussian Process prior, the latent function values $\mathbf{f}_k = [f_k(\psi_1), \dots, f_k(\psi_N)]^T \in \mathbb{R}^N$ and the function value $f_* = f_k(\psi_*)$ at a test coordinate $\psi_* \in \mathbb{R}^d$ follow a joint multivariate Gaussian distribution:
\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{f}_k \\ f_* \end{bmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\left( \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{m}_k \\ \frac{1}{K} \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{K} & \mathbf{k}_*^T \\ \mathbf{k}_* & k(\psi_*, \psi_*) \end{bmatrix} \right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{K} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is the Gram covariance matrix with elements $\mathbf{K}_{i, j} = k(\psi_i, \psi_j)$, and $\mathbf{k}_* = [k(\psi_*, \psi_1), \dots, k(\psi_*, \psi_N)] \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times N}$.

Assuming that the target observations $\mathbf{y}_k$ are corrupted by independent additive Gaussian noise with zero mean and variance $\sigma_n^2$, we write:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y}_k = \mathbf{f}_k + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})
\end{equation}
The joint distribution of the observations $\mathbf{y}_k$ and the test function value $f_*$ is:
\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{y}_k \\ f_* \end{bmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\left( \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{m}_k \\ \frac{1}{K} \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I} & \mathbf{k}_*^T \\ \mathbf{k}_* & k(\psi_*, \psi_*) \end{bmatrix} \right)
\end{equation}

By applying the rules of Gaussian conditioning (or Schur complement block-matrix inversion), the conditional distribution $p(f_* \mid \mathbf{\Psi}_{\text{cal}}, \mathbf{y}_k, \psi_*)$ is also Gaussian:
\begin{equation}
f_* \mid \mathbf{\Psi}_{\text{cal}}, \mathbf{y}_k, \psi_* \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_k(\psi_*), \sigma^2(\psi_*))
\end{equation}
with the posterior predictive mean $\mu_k(\psi_*)$ given by:
\begin{equation}
\mu_k(\psi_*) = \frac{1}{K} + \mathbf{k}_* \left( \mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I} \right)^{-1} \left( \mathbf{y}_k - \mathbf{m}_k \right)
\end{equation}
and the posterior predictive variance $\sigma^2(\psi_*)$ (which is identical across all tasks due to independent GP priors and uniform noise variance) solved as:
\begin{equation}
\sigma^2(\psi_*) = k(\psi_*, \psi_*) - \mathbf{k}_* \left( \mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I} \right)^{-1} \mathbf{k}_*^T
\end{equation}

Since the tasks are modeled independently, we stack these scalar equations into the multi-task vector form, yielding Equation~\ref{eq:post_mean} and Equation~\ref{eq:post_var} in Section~\ref{sec:method}:
\begin{align}
\mu(\psi_*) &= \mathbf{m}(\psi_*) + \mathbf{k}_* \left( \mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I} \right)^{-1} \left( \mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{m}(\mathbf{X}) \right) \\
\sigma^2(\psi_*) &= k(\psi_*, \psi_*) - \mathbf{k}_* \left( \mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I} \right)^{-1} \mathbf{k}_*^T
\end{align}

\section{Lipschitz Continuity of GP-DR Under the RBF Kernel}
\label{app:lipschitz}

We prove that the dynamic routing function $\mu(\psi_*)$ computed via GP-DR is Lipschitz-continuous with respect to the low-dimensional representation $\psi_*$.

\begin{theorem}
Let $\mu_k(\psi)$ represent the predictive posterior mean routing coefficient for task $k$ at coordinate $\psi \in \mathbb{R}^d$. Under the RBF kernel with signal variance $\sigma_f^2$ and lengthscale $\ell$, the function $\mu_k(\psi)$ is Lipschitz-continuous over the unit sphere, i.e., there exists a constant $L_{\text{GP}} > 0$ such that for any $\psi_a, \psi_b$ on the unit sphere:
\begin{equation}
|\mu_k(\psi_a) - \mu_k(\psi_b)| \le L_{\text{GP}} \|\psi_a - \psi_b\|_2
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $\mathbf{w}_k = [w_{1, k}, \dots, w_{N, k}]^T = \left( \mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I} \right)^{-1} \left( \mathbf{y}_k - \mathbf{m}_k \right) \in \mathbb{R}^N$ be the pre-computed offline GP weights for task $k$. We can write the dynamic posterior mean routing coefficient as a linear combination of RBF kernel functions:
\begin{equation}
\mu_k(\psi) = \frac{1}{K} + \sum_{i=1}^N w_{i, k} k(\psi, \psi_i) = \frac{1}{K} + \sigma_f^2 \sum_{i=1}^N w_{i, k} \exp\left( -\frac{\|\psi - \psi_i\|_2^2}{2 \ell^2} \right)
\end{equation}
To prove Lipschitz continuity, we evaluate the gradient of $\mu_k(\psi)$ with respect to $\psi$:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_{\psi} \mu_k(\psi) = \sigma_f^2 \sum_{i=1}^N w_{i, k} \exp\left( -\frac{\|\psi - \psi_i\|_2^2}{2 \ell^2} \right) \left( -\frac{\psi - \psi_i}{\ell^2} \right)
\end{equation}
Using the triangle inequality, we bound the $\ell_2$-norm of the gradient:
\begin{equation}
\|\nabla_{\psi} \mu_k(\psi)\|_2 \le \frac{\sigma_f^2}{\ell^2} \sum_{i=1}^N |w_{i, k}| \exp\left( -\frac{\|\psi - \psi_i\|_2^2}{2 \ell^2} \right) \|\psi - \psi_i\|_2
\end{equation}
We define the auxiliary scalar function $g(r) = r \exp(-r^2 / (2 \ell^2))$ for $r = \|\psi - \psi_i\|_2 \ge 0$. By taking the derivative of $g(r)$ and setting it to zero, we find that the function $g(r)$ achieves its global maximum at $r^* = \ell$, with a maximum value of:
\begin{equation}
g(r^*) = \ell \exp(-1/2) = \frac{\ell}{\sqrt{e}}
\end{equation}
Therefore, for any $\psi, \psi_i$, we have:
\begin{equation}
\exp\left( -\frac{\|\psi - \psi_i\|_2^2}{2 \ell^2} \right) \|\psi - \psi_i\|_2 \le \frac{\ell}{\sqrt{e}}
\end{equation}
Substituting this bound back into the gradient norm inequality:
\begin{equation}
\|\nabla_{\psi} \mu_k(\psi)\|_2 \le \frac{\sigma_f^2}{\ell^2} \sum_{i=1}^N |w_{i, k}| \left( \frac{\ell}{\sqrt{e}} \right) = \frac{\sigma_f^2}{\ell \sqrt{e}} \|\mathbf{w}_k\|_1
\end{equation}
Let $L_{\text{GP}} = \frac{\sigma_f^2}{\ell \sqrt{e}} \|\mathbf{w}_k\|_1$. Since $L_{\text{GP}}$ is finite and bounded (as $\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I}$ is invertible and has finite entries, and $\mathbf{Y}$ has bounded targets), the gradient norm is globally bounded:
\begin{equation}
\sup_{\psi} \|\nabla_{\psi} \mu_k(\psi)\|_2 \le L_{\text{GP}} < \infty
\end{equation}
By the Mean Value Theorem, this implies that $\mu_k(\psi)$ is Lipschitz-continuous with Lipschitz constant $L_{\text{GP}}$:
\begin{equation}
|\mu_k(\psi_a) - \mu_k(\psi_b)| \le L_{\text{GP}} \|\psi_a - \psi_b\|_2
\end{equation}
for all $\psi_a, \psi_b \in \mathbb{R}^d$. This completes the proof of the raw posterior mean's smoothness.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Lipschitz Continuity of the Composed, Clamped, and Normalized Routing Operator}
We now prove that the composed routing operator $\hat{\alpha}^{\text{GP-DR}}(\psi_*)$ remains Lipschitz-continuous under the non-linear, post-hoc clamping and simplex projection layers, highlighting the essential regularizing role of the clamping threshold $\delta > 0$.

\begin{theorem}
Let $\hat{\alpha}_k(\psi) = \frac{h_k(\psi)}{\sum_{j=1}^K h_j(\psi)}$ represent the final, simplex-projected routing coefficient for task $k$, where $h_k(\psi) = \max(\mu_k(\psi), \delta)$ is the clamped posterior mean and $\delta = 10^{-5}$. The composed operator $\hat{\alpha}_k(\psi)$ is Lipschitz-continuous over the unit sphere:
\begin{equation}
|\hat{\alpha}_k(\psi_a) - \hat{\alpha}_k(\psi_b)| \le L_{\text{composed}} \|\psi_a - \psi_b\|_2
\end{equation}
with the composed Lipschitz constant given by:
\begin{equation}
L_{\text{composed}} = \frac{K+1}{K \delta} L_{\text{GP}}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The clamping function $c(x) = \max(x, \delta)$ is 1-Lipschitz continuous since $|c(x) - c(y)| \le |x - y|$ for all $x, y \in \mathbb{R}$. Because the raw posterior mean $\mu_k(\psi)$ is $L_{\text{GP}}$-Lipschitz continuous, the composition $h_k(\psi) = c(\mu_k(\psi))$ is also $L_{\text{GP}}$-Lipschitz continuous:
\begin{equation}
|h_k(\psi_a) - h_k(\psi_b)| \le L_{\text{GP}} \|\psi_a - \psi_b\|_2
\end{equation}
We define the sum of the clamped task coefficients as $S(\psi) = \sum_{j=1}^K h_j(\psi)$. By the triangle inequality, $S(\psi)$ is $(K \cdot L_{\text{GP}})$-Lipschitz continuous:
\begin{equation}
|S(\psi_a) - S(\psi_b)| \le \sum_{j=1}^K |h_j(\psi_a) - h_j(\psi_b)| \le K L_{\text{GP}} \|\psi_a - \psi_b\|_2
\end{equation}
Crucially, because of the clamping bound, we have $h_j(\psi) \ge \delta$ for all $j \in \{1,\dots,K\}$, which guarantees that the denominator sum is strictly bounded away from zero:
\begin{equation}
S(\psi) = \sum_{j=1}^K h_j(\psi) \ge K \delta > 0 \quad \forall \psi \in \mathbb{R}^d
\end{equation}
We now express the absolute difference of the normalized coefficients as:
\begin{align}
|\hat{\alpha}_k(\psi_a) - \hat{\alpha}_k(\psi_b)| &= \left| \frac{h_k(\psi_a)}{S(\psi_a)} - \frac{h_k(\psi_b)}{S(\psi_b)} \right| \nonumber \\
&= \left| \frac{h_k(\psi_a) S(\psi_b) - h_k(\psi_b) S(\psi_a)}{S(\psi_a) S(\psi_b)} \right| \nonumber \\
&= \left| \frac{(h_k(\psi_a) - h_k(\psi_b)) S(\psi_b) + h_k(\psi_b) (S(\psi_b) - S(\psi_a))}{S(\psi_a) S(\psi_b)} \right| \nonumber \\
&\le \frac{|h_k(\psi_a) - h_k(\psi_b)| S(\psi_b) + h_k(\psi_b) |S(\psi_a) - S(\psi_b)|}{S(\psi_a) S(\psi_b)} \nonumber \\
&= \frac{|h_k(\psi_a) - h_k(\psi_b)|}{S(\psi_a)} + \frac{h_k(\psi_b)}{S(\psi_a) S(\psi_b)} |S(\psi_a) - S(\psi_b)|
\end{align}
Because all clamped coefficients $h_j(\psi)$ are non-negative, we have the property $h_k(\psi_b) \le \sum_{j=1}^K h_j(\psi_b) = S(\psi_b)$, yielding the bound $\frac{h_k(\psi_b)}{S(\psi_b)} \le 1$. Applying this and the lower bound $S(\psi) \ge K \delta$:
\begin{align}
|\hat{\alpha}_k(\psi_a) - \hat{\alpha}_k(\psi_b)| &\le \frac{1}{K \delta} |h_k(\psi_a) - h_k(\psi_b)| + \frac{1}{K \delta} |S(\psi_a) - S(\psi_b)| \nonumber \\
&\le \frac{1}{K \delta} \left( L_{\text{GP}} \|\psi_a - \psi_b\|_2 \right) + \frac{1}{K \delta} \left( K L_{\text{GP}} \|\psi_a - \psi_b\|_2 \right) \nonumber \\
&= \frac{K+1}{K \delta} L_{\text{GP}} \|\psi_a - \psi_b\|_2
\end{align}
Setting $L_{\text{composed}} = \frac{K+1}{K \delta} L_{\text{GP}}$, we obtain the desired Lipschitz continuity bound.
\end{proof}

This proof highlights the mathematical necessity of the clamping lower bound $\delta > 0$. If we had used standard unconstrained normalization (i.e., setting $\delta = 0$), the composed Lipschitz constant would approach infinity as $S(\psi) \to 0$, leading to gradient explosion, high-frequency parameter oscillations, and severe routing instability. By establishing a strictly positive clamping threshold $\delta = 10^{-5}$, we structurally prevent gradient explosion, guaranteeing that the final parameter blending weights remain boundedly smooth and stable under arbitrary representation shifts.

This Lipschitz continuity has profound physical meaning for dynamic model merging: it guarantees that small perturbations or noise in the latent representation manifold cannot trigger chaotic, discontinuous jumps in the parameter blending weights. This structurally stabilizes the dynamic forward pass, preventing high-frequency model parameter oscillations during inference.

\section{Auxiliary Analyses and Future Directions}
\label{app:auxiliary}

We present additional analytical investigations and discuss scalable extensions of our Gaussian Process Dynamic Routing (GP-DR) framework.

\subsection{Computational Scaling under Large Calibration Budgets}
A major benefit of the GP-DR formulation is that the $N \times N$ matrix inversion $\mathbf{M} = (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1}$ is computed entirely offline. At runtime, predicting the dynamic coefficients $\alpha$ and the epistemic uncertainty $\sigma^2$ takes $O(NK)$ and $O(N^2)$ FLOPs respectively. For our extreme calibration setup ($N=64$, $K=4$), this requires less than $5 \times 10^3$ FLOPs, which is practically instantaneous on modern hardware.

However, if GP-DR is deployed in larger-scale settings with massive calibration sets (e.g., $N \ge 10^3$ in continuous learning or lifelong multi-task systems), the offline computational cost scales cubically as $O(N^3)$ due to the matrix inversion, and the online forward pass scales linearly with $N$ ($O(NK)$ and $O(N^2)$). To maintain real-time edge viability under large-scale regimes, several sparse Gaussian Process approximation techniques can be integrated directly:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fully Independent Training Conditional (FITC) or Variational Free Energy (VFE):} These frameworks introduce a small set of $M \ll N$ pseudo-inputs (inducing points) to represent the latent function space, reducing offline matrix inversion to $O(N M^2)$ and online inference cost to $O(MK)$ and $O(M^2)$ FLOPs.
    \item \textbf{Localized GP Routing:} Rather than conditioning on the entire calibration set, the router can query only the $k$-nearest calibration landmark neighbors ($k \ll N$) for each incoming test input. This reduces the online inference complexity to $O(k K)$ for predictive mean and $O(k^2)$ for posterior variance, ensuring a completely constant computational envelope regardless of how large the calibration corpus grows.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Scaling of Lipschitz Smoothness Bound with Calibration Budget $N$}
A key theoretical trade-off in GP-DR is the scaling of the global Lipschitz smoothness bound with the number of calibration landmarks $N$. As derived in Theorem 2.1, the raw GP Lipschitz constant $L_{\text{GP}} = \frac{\sigma_f^2}{\ell \sqrt{e}} \|\mathbf{w}_k\|_1$ is directly proportional to the $\ell_1$-norm of the offline pre-computed GP weights:
\begin{equation}
\|\mathbf{w}_k\|_1 = \sum_{i=1}^N |w_{i, k}|
\label{eq:weight_norm_l1}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{w}_k = \left( \mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I} \right)^{-1} \left( \mathbf{y}_k - \mathbf{m}_k \right) \in \mathbb{R}^N$.
Because $\|\mathbf{w}_k\|_1$ sum-aggregates over all $N$ calibration samples, this norm typically scales upwards as the calibration budget $N$ grows. Consequently, in massive calibration regimes, the worst-case composed Lipschitz bound $L_{\text{composed}}$ can escalate, indicating that the routing function may become highly sensitive to local representational noise and lose its global smoothness guarantees. This reveals a fundamental academic trade-off: as we increase the number of landmark coordinates to improve routing representation and accuracy, we risk compromising its worst-case smoothness guarantees.

To resolve this theoretical trade-off and preserve strict Lipschitz smoothness bounds under large-scale calibration sets, we propose two regularization strategies:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Soft $\ell_1$-Weight Regularization:} During the offline weight computation, instead of standard Gaussian ridge regression, we can solve a regularized optimization problem that explicitly bounds or penalizes the $\ell_1$-norm of the weight vector:
    \begin{equation}
    \mathbf{w}_k^* = \arg\min_{\mathbf{w}_k} \|\mathbf{K}\mathbf{w}_k + \mathbf{m}_k - \mathbf{y}_k\|_2^2 + \lambda_1 \|\mathbf{w}_k\|_1 + \lambda_2 \mathbf{w}_k^T \mathbf{K} \mathbf{w}_k
    \end{equation}
    By penalizing $\|\mathbf{w}_k\|_1$ via a Lasso-like soft constraint, we can analytically bound $L_{\text{GP}}$ and structurally secure global routing smoothness regardless of the scale of $N$.
    \item \textbf{Sparse GP and Inducing Point Interpolations:} By utilizing sparse Gaussian Processes (such as FITC or VFE) with a small, fixed number of inducing points $M \ll N$ ($M \approx 64$), the routing function becomes a combination of $M$ kernels rather than $N$ kernels. This bounds the weight norm to a small, fixed sum-dimension of size $M$, completely decoupling the Lipschitz smoothness guarantee from the size of the original calibration dataset $N$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis of Hyperparameter Choices}
The performance and structural stability of GP-DR are governed by the signal variance $\sigma_f^2$, lengthscale $\ell$, and the regularizing noise variance $\sigma_n^2$.
As shown in Appendix~\ref{app:lipschitz}, the composed Lipschitz constant is bounded as $L_{\text{composed}} = \frac{K+1}{K \delta} \frac{\sigma_f^2}{\ell \sqrt{e}} \|\mathbf{w}_k\|_1$.
We characterize the physical and mathematical behavior of GP-DR under extreme parameter configurations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Extreme Lengthscale ($\ell \to \infty$):} The kernel similarities $k(\psi_*, \psi_i)$ approach the constant value $\sigma_f^2$. Consequently, the cross-covariance vector $\mathbf{k}_*$ becomes uniform, and the predictive mean $\mu(\psi_*)$ flattens, reverting completely back to the constant prior mean $\mathbf{m}(\psi_*) = [1/K, \dots, 1/K]^T$.
    \item \textbf{Vanishing Lengthscale ($\ell \to 0$):} The cross-covariance vector $\mathbf{k}_*$ collapses to zero for any test input not exactly matching a calibration sample. The predictive mean collapses back to the prior mean, and the posterior variance surges to its upper bound $\sigma_f^2$, triggering continuous OOD fallback.
    \item \textbf{Vanishing Regularizing Noise ($\sigma_n^2 \to 0$):} While a smaller noise variance allows the posterior mean to fit the training targets more tightly, as $\sigma_n^2 \to 0$ under clustered coordinates, the Gram matrix $\mathbf{K}$ becomes near-singular. The elements of the inverse Gram matrix $(\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1}$ explode, causing the weight norm $\|\mathbf{w}_k\|_1$ to blow up. This dramatically escalates the Lipschitz constant $L_{\text{composed}}$, leading to severe routing instability and sensitivity to representational noise.
\end{itemize}
Empirically, GP-DR is highly robust across a wide range of hyperparameters; we find that setting $\ell \in [0.4, 0.8]$ and $\sigma_n^2 \in [10^{-3}, 10^{-1}]$ yields stable Joint Mean accuracies of $71.8\% - 72.8\%$ and guarantees flawless OOD detection.
