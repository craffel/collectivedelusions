# Soundness and Methodology Evaluation

## Clarity of the Description
The methodology is explained with a high degree of mathematical detail and clarity. Specifically:
* **The Mathematical Proof:** The proof of Theorem 3.1 is highly detailed and rigorous. Instead of bypassing multi-class dependencies by assuming independent sigmoid gating functions, the authors analyze the coupled Softmax layer directly. By integrating Maurer's vector-valued contraction theorem, they resolve a common theoretical shortcut in ensembling literature.
* **Regularizer Formulation:** The progression from the theoretical linear Rademacher bound to the quadratic surrogate (Tikhonov-like weight decay) is mathematically justified.
* **Clarity of Advanced Elements:** While the descriptions of the smoothed $L_1$ variant, regularization scheduling, and hybrid adaptive controllers are clear, they are mathematically dense and introduce substantial conceptual overhead.

## Appropriateness of Methods
* **Precomputed Task Vector Scaling:** Scaling router weight decay by precomputed task-vector norms (Frobenius/Spectral) is a highly appropriate, computationally efficient method. Because task vectors are static after expert fine-tuning, computing their norms is a one-time offline process.
* **Power Iteration for SVD:** The suggested use of power iterations to approximate the spectral norm in larger networks is an elegant, numerically sound choice that prevents SVD-induced bottlenecks.
* **Heuristic Patches for Optimization Failures:** The introduction of regularization scheduling and exponential gradient-norm decay controllers (SR3-Hybrid) to solve optimization failures (the $L_1$ gradient divergence near zero and the over-repression of high-complexity experts) feels less like principled theory and more like complex engineering heuristics. While they attempt to resolve real optimization problems, they introduce an array of arbitrary parameters ($\beta$, $\gamma$, linear/cosine/exponential trajectory types) that run counter to the simplicity of the core idea.

## Potential Technical Flaws and Limitations
1. **Circular Evaluation in Simulation:** As the authors transparently acknowledge, the synthetic weight-merging simulator models test-time generalization using the exact function that SR3 is designed to minimize: $\text{Gap}_k = \eta_{\text{noise}} \|W_k\|_2 \|V_k\|_F$. This creates an inherent circular evaluation setup that naturally biases results in favor of SR3 variants, making the simulated results less representative of true out-of-distribution performance.
2. **Empirical Failure on Real Data:** To break this circularity, the authors execute a physical PyTorch TinyMLP experiment on real handwritten digits. In this unbiased setting, **standard, uniform $L_2$ weight decay (at $92.13\% \pm 2.47\%$) and the simple TSAR heuristic (at $92.13\% \pm 2.92\%$) actually outperform all proposed SR3 variants on average over 10 seeds** (SR3-F: $90.50\% \pm 1.36\%$; SR3-S: $90.93\% \pm 1.94\%$; SR3-H: $91.20\% \pm 1.81\%$). This empirical performance flip is a critical result: it shows that when the generalization gap is real and natural rather than artificially defined by the Rademacher bound, the simpler, uniform $L_2$ regularization is actually superior on average. This strongly validates the minimalist perspective that highly engineered, asymmetric complexity bounds may over-repress expert capabilities without delivering real-world performance benefits.
3. **Global Lipschitz Assumption:** The theory assumes a global Lipschitz constant $L_{\text{net}}$ over the network's parameters. In complex deep architectures, the local Lipschitz constant varies dramatically depending on feature density. Treating $L_{\text{net}}$ as a global constant is a major simplification.

## Reproducibility
The paper provides a highly complete description of all experimental settings, including layer dimensions, learning rates, epochs, rank structures, and specific schedules. The mathematical formulations are complete and unambiguous. Provided the code is available, reproducing the continuous simulator and the physical TinyMLP experiment should be highly straightforward.
