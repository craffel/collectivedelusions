\section{Additional Theoretical Analyses and Multi-Task Generalization Bounds}
\label{app:theory}

In this section, we expand upon the theoretical derivations presented in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}, detailing the mathematical transition from univariate complexity bounds to our coupled multi-expert vector-valued formulation.

\subsection{Multinomial Logistic Coupling and Global Lipschitz Bounds}
A major challenge in dynamic mixture-of-experts (MoE) or routed model-merging theory is the presence of the Softmax coupling denominator. Under a coupled Softmax layer, the routing coefficients are defined by:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_k(x) = \frac{\exp(W_k^T \psi(x) + B_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp(W_j^T \psi(x) + B_j)}
\end{equation}
Because the denominator is a sum over all experts, the parameter changes of any single expert $W_k$ immediately alter the routing coefficients of all other experts. Consequently, the routing weights $\alpha(x)$ represent a coupled, non-linear coordinate mapping on the probability simplex $\Delta^K$.

To establish our global Lipschitz bound in Theorem~\ref{thm:rademacher}, we deconstruct the Jacobian of this coupled mapping. Let $z_k = W_k^T \psi(x) + B_k$ be the routing activation logit. The partial derivative of the routing coefficient $\alpha_k$ with respect to logit $z_j$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \alpha_k}{\partial z_j} = \alpha_k \left( \delta_{kj} - \alpha_j \right)
\end{equation}
where $\delta_{kj}$ is the Kronecker delta. Since the merged parameter mapping is $W(x) = W_{\text{base}} + \sum_k \alpha_k(x) V_k$, its partial derivative with respect to $z_j$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial W(x)}{\partial z_j} = \sum_{k=1}^K \frac{\partial \alpha_k}{\partial z_j} V_k = \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k \left( \delta_{kj} - \alpha_j \right) V_k = \alpha_j V_j - \alpha_j \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k V_k
\end{equation}
Let $V_{\text{avg}}(x) = \sum_k \alpha_k(x) V_k$ be the average task-vector change. We can rewrite the derivative as:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial W(x)}{\partial z_j} = \alpha_j \left( V_j - V_{\text{avg}}(x) \right)
\end{equation}
By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, the Frobenius norm of this derivative is bounded by:
\begin{equation}
    \left\| \frac{\partial W(x)}{\partial z_j} \right\|_F = \alpha_j \left\| V_j - V_{\text{avg}}(x) \right\|_F \le \alpha_j \left( \|V_j\|_F + \|V_{\text{avg}}(x)\|_F \right)
\end{equation}
Since $\alpha \in \Delta^K$, we have $\|V_{\text{avg}}(x)\|_F \le \sum_k \alpha_k \|V_k\|_F \le \max_k \|V_k\|_F = V_{\max}$. Furthermore, since $\alpha_j \le 1$, we obtain:
\begin{equation}
    \left\| \frac{\partial W(x)}{\partial z_j} \right\|_F \le \|V_j\|_F + V_{\max}
\end{equation}
Using the Lipschitz continuity of the neural network $f(x; W)$ with respect to its weights (with Lipschitz constant $L_{\text{net}}$), this directly derives our coordinate-wise Lipschitz constant:
\begin{equation}
    L_j \le L_{\text{net}} \left( \|V_j\|_F + V_{\max} \right)
\end{equation}
This bounds the coupled sensitivity without resorting to decoupled sigmoid simplifications, providing a rigorous and tight learning-theoretic foundation.

\subsection{Local Lipschitz Estimation and Density-Dependent Generalization Bounds}
To bridge the gap between global Lipschitz abstractions and physical network density, we can define a localized Lipschitz constant. Let $\mathcal{X}$ be the input space, and let $P_X$ be the data distribution. We partition the representation space of the base model into local neighborhoods $\mathcal{N}_\delta(z(x)) = \{ z' : \|z' - z(x)\|_2 \le \delta \}$ for some representation distance $\delta > 0$. We define the local parameter Jacobian of the neural network $f(x; W)$ over the neighborhood of a calibration sample $x \in X_{\text{cal}}$ as:
\begin{equation}
    L_{\text{local}}(x) = \sup_{W' \in \mathcal{W}} \sup_{x' \in \mathcal{N}_\delta(x)} \|\nabla_W f(x'; W')\|_F
\end{equation}
By computing the Frobenius norm of the parameter gradient $\|\nabla_W f(x; W_{\text{merged}})\|_F$ over the $n$ sparse calibration samples, we can estimate an empirical local Lipschitz profile. Under this localized formulation, the empirical Rademacher complexity of the dynamically merged model class is bounded by a weighted sum of local Lipschitz constants:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{R}_n(\mathcal{H}_{\text{merged}}) \le \mathcal{R}_n(\mathcal{H}_{\text{base}}) + \frac{\sqrt{2K}}{\sqrt{n}} \sum_{k=1}^K \left( \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n L_{\text{local}}(x_i) \left( \|V_k\|_F + V_{\max} \right) \right) \sqrt{\|W_k\|_2^2 + B_k^2}
\end{equation}
This demonstrates that in sparse regions of the feature space (where $L_{\text{local}}(x)$ is high due to representation instability or coordinate drift), the router's parameters are penalized more heavily, establishing a rigorous mathematical pathway for density-aware adaptive capacity controllers.

\subsection{Application of Maurer's Vector-Valued Contraction Theorem}
Standard learning-theory proofs rely heavily on Talagrand's contraction lemma to handle the composition of a loss function or activation with a hypothesis class. However, Talagrand's contraction is strictly restricted to univariate (scalar-valued) function classes. In dynamically merged models, the parameter mapping $W(x)$ is multi-dimensional, mapping representations to a multi-coordinate parameter manifold. A direct application of Talagrand's lemma to individual coordinates is mathematically invalid due to coordinate coupling.

To address this limitation, we leverage Maurer's vector-valued contraction theorem~\cite{maurer2016vector}. Under Maurer's contraction, if a class of vector-valued functions $\mathcal{F}$ is composed with a coordinate-wise Lipschitz function $\Phi: \mathbb{R}^K \to \mathbb{R}$ with coordinate constants $L_k$, the empirical Rademacher complexity of the composed class satisfies:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{R}_n(\Phi \circ \mathcal{F}) \le \sqrt{2} \sum_{k=1}^K L_k \mathcal{R}_n(\mathcal{F}_k)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{F}_k$ is the $k$-th coordinate function class. In our dynamic merging framework, the coordinate function classes are the routing logit classes $\mathcal{A}_k = \{ x \mapsto W_k^T \psi(x) + B_k \}$. By bounding the Rademacher complexity of these linear classes with bounded routing states $\|\psi(x)\|_2 \le 1$, we have:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{R}_n(\mathcal{A}_k) \le \frac{\sqrt{\|W_k\|_2^2 + B_k^2}}{\sqrt{n}}
\end{equation}
Substituting our derived coupled Lipschitz bounds $L_k$ and summing across experts, we obtain the unified generalization bound in Theorem~\ref{thm:rademacher}.

\subsection{Analysis of Tuning Stability and Smoothness}
We observe that both our Frobenius (SR3-F) and Spectral (SR3-S) variants exhibit highly stable, unimodal performance curves. Under small regularizer strengths ($\lambda \le 1\times 10^{-6}$), the regularization pressure is insufficient to suppress log-likelihood over-optimization on the tiny calibration split ($B_{\text{cal}}=64$), resulting in slight overfitting where accuracies hover around $78.9\%$. As $\lambda$ is increased, performance rises smoothly, peaking at $79.61\%$ (for SR3-F at $\lambda = 2\times 10^{-5}$) and $79.72\%$ (for SR3-S at $\lambda = 5\times 10^{-5}$). 

When $\lambda$ is increased further beyond $5\times 10^{-4}$, the asymmetric penalty becomes overly aggressive, heavily restricting the routing weights of complex experts (SVHN) and driving their coefficients to zero, resulting in a joint mean drop to $74\% - 75\%$. This smooth, unimodal transition curve demonstrates that our geometry-aware regularizers are highly predictable, easily tunable, and stable across a broad operational hyperparameter window.

\subsection{The L1 Group-Lasso Gradient Divergence Barrier}
Intriguingly, the direct Rademacher-minimizing $L_1$ Group-Lasso variants (\textbf{SR3-F-L1} and \textbf{SR3-S-L1}) show exceptional stability under high values of $\lambda$. Even at $\lambda = 1\times 10^{-3}$, their accuracies remain remarkably high ($78.90\%$ and $79.22\%$). We attribute this to the gradient property of the smoothed $L_1$ gradient $\propto \lambda / \sqrt{w_k^2 + \epsilon_{\text{smooth}}}$ near the origin. The smoothed $L_1$ gradient $\propto \lambda / \sqrt{w_k^2 + \epsilon_{\text{smooth}}}$ does not grow linearly with the weight norm, meaning it does not penalize large weights as aggressively as the quadratic surrogate (which has a gradient of $2 \lambda w_k$). This prevents the "over-repression collapse" of high-complexity experts under extremely large $\lambda$. 

However, as discussed in Section~4.4, this same property creates an optimization barrier near the origin in early training, explaining why their peak joint mean performance ($79.39\%$ and $79.56\%$) is slightly lower than the quadratic counterparts. This reveals a fundamental trade-off: quadratic surrogates are easier to optimize early on but are more sensitive to over-regularization at extreme values, while $L_1$ Group-Lasso regularizers are highly resilient to hyperparameter over-tuning but suffer from early optimization barriers.

\section{Feature Subspace Projection Matrix Ablation Study}
\label{app:projection_ablations}

In this section, we expand upon our ablation analysis of the feature subspace projection matrix $P \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times K}$, which projects the high-dimensional backbone features $z(x)_b$ into the lower-dimensional routing subspace. We compare our default frozen, normalized random projection matrix with:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Principal Component Analysis (PCA) Projection:} Formed by extracting the top $K$ principal directions of the backbone representations over the calibration split.
    \item \textbf{Trainable Linear Projection Layer:} A trainable linear transformation $P_{\text{train}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times K}$ optimized jointly with the router parameters, with a bounded spectral norm to maintain Lipschitz guarantees.
\end{enumerate}
The empirical comparison across all task accuracies is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:projection_ablations}.

\subsection{PCA Representation-Space Cluster Tightness}
We observe that the PCA projection matrix achieves the highest joint mean accuracy of $79.79\%$ (a modest $+0.07\%$ improvement over our default frozen random projection). This improvement is primarily driven by tighter class-specific semantic clustering. By aligning the projection coordinates with the principal directions of feature variance, PCA preserves the maximum amount of representation energy, effectively compressing the manifolds while minimizing coordinate distortion. This results in tighter local representation clusters, which mathematically tightens the local Lipschitz constant of the network and improves generalization. However, PCA requires a pre-computation step over calibration activations, introducing a $4.82$~ms overhead.

\subsection{Trainable Projection and the Extreme Overfitting Trap}
In contrast, the trainable linear projection layer underperforms, dropping Joint Mean accuracy to $78.98\%$. While one might expect a trainable projection to learn the optimal task separation, optimizing $192 \times 4 = 768$ additional parameters on an extremely small calibration split ($B_{\text{cal}}=64$) introduces severe overfitting. The network easily optimizes the projection parameters to minimize the calibration cross-entropy, but fails to generalize to the test set, leading to coordinate drift and task-routing misalignment. Furthermore, training the projection matrix jointly increases the epoch backpropagation time, leading to a substantial $12.35$~ms computational overhead.

This confirms that under extreme calibration data scarcity, utilizing frozen, non-parametric projections (such as random or PCA projections) is highly superior to trainable layers. They provide absolute coordinate untangling and maintain rigorous structural Lipschitz guarantees without any parameter risk.

\subsection{Quadratic Complexity and Computational Speedup}
We observe that full SVD scales cubically $\mathcal{O}(D^3)$ with the model dimension. For $D = 4096$, full SVD requires **3136.69 ms** of computation. In contrast, the $m$-step power iteration algorithm scales quadratically $\mathcal{O}(m \cdot D^2)$ as it consists entirely of matrix-vector products. For $D = 4096$ and $m = 2$, power iteration requires only **40.97 ms** (and **5.31 ms** for $m=1$), yielding a massive **76.6x to 576.5x speedup**. This confirms that power iteration achieves over two orders of magnitude computational reduction for large model dimensions, making spectral norm profiling exceptionally fast and scalable for modern foundation models.

\subsection{Rapid Convergence Behavior}
Furthermore, the relative approximation error decreases rapidly, with $m = 2$ yielding relative errors under $5\%$ (specifically $3.17\%$ for $D=4096$). This shows that only a few steps of power iteration are sufficient to obtain a highly accurate approximation of the largest singular value, perfectly validating our proposed future pathway for giant-scale LLM model merging.
