# Experimental Design and Results Check: ELATI

## Comprehensive Assessment of Experimental Setup
The experimental evaluation of ELATI is multi-faceted and includes multiple testing environments:
1. **The PyTorch Sandbox**: Evaluated across 10 independent random seeds, providing solid statistical confidence with reported means and standard deviations.
2. **Real-world Vision Transformer (ViT-Tiny)**: Evaluated on MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10, and SVHN.
3. **Pre-trained Autoregressive GPT-2**: Evaluated on four diverse textual NLP tasks.
4. **Hybrid Domain Drift Stream**: Evaluated across 80 streaming batches under sudden non-stationary task shifts.

## Analysis of Baselines and Results
The baselines compared are highly relevant and representative of the state-of-the-art:
- **Static Merging Baselines**: Static Uniform, DARE, and TIES.
- **Dynamic Routing Baselines**: Linear Routers (Unreg and Reg) and penultimate-layer PFSR + MBH.
- **Accuracy Comparison**: ELATI (56.89%) performs competitively on the Sandbox, falling only 1.36% behind the deep, two-pass penultimate PFSR (58.25%) while offering a 1.40$\times$ physical CPU speedup.

However, several critical experimental weaknesses and anomalies warrant discussion:

### 1. Artificial Collapse of Static Merging Baselines (DARE and TIES)
In Table 1, DARE-Merging (32.56%) and TIES-Merging (37.39%) perform significantly *worse* than Static Uniform Merging (48.27%). 
- **The Sparsity Mismatch**: In real-world model-merging literature, DARE and TIES are designed to mitigate representation conflicts and typically outperform Uniform Merging by wide margins. Their dramatic collapse in this sandbox suggests that the simulated "task adapters" $V_k^{(l)}$ lack the mathematical structure (e.g., sparsity or low-rank decay) typical of real-world fine-tuned parameters. 
- **Destructive Trimming**: Trimming 80% of parameters (TIES) or dropping 20% (DARE) on dense, non-sparse random coordinate matrices collapses the representational capacity of the weights, leading to artificial failure. This indicates that the sandbox environment is highly biased against static sparsification methods, creating an unrepresentative performance gap.

### 2. Low-Data Learning and Extremely Low Expert Ceilings in Physical ViT
For the physical ViT downstream classification evaluation (Table 8), the absolute classification accuracies are remarkably low:
- MNIST: 20.00%
- Fashion-MNIST: 21.00% (near-random guessing)
- CIFAR-10: 27.00%
- SVHN: 18.00%
- Joint Mean: 21.50% (Expert Oracle Ceiling: 26.00%)

While the authors argue that this simulates an "extreme data-scarcity" regime (16 training samples per task for 30 epochs), it leaves a **major empirical gap**:
- **Omission of Full-Data Fine-Tuning**: The paper does not evaluate ELATI under a standard, fully fine-tuned scenario where experts achieve high accuracies (e.g., $>80-90\%$). 
- **Unverified Scaling Behavior**: The authors theorize in Section 4.4.3 that the routing accuracy remains stable because Layer 2 activations are generated by frozen base weights, and that performance will scale upward. However, this is a theoretical extrapolation. Fully-trained downstream experts might exhibit much larger parameter norms or highly divergent gradient updates, which could induce severe "catastrophic ensembling interference" during dynamic soft weight merging. Failing to empirically validate ELATI under fully-trained expert regimes is a significant omission.

### 3. Lack of Physical GPU Execution Timings
While the hardware-level GPU profiling benchmark (Section 4.5) is highly detailed, it relies on a **GPU simulation model** that scales CPU timing characteristics using bandwidth and scheduling constraints:
- **Simulator Limitations**: Simulated/scaled results often overlook physical GPU bottlenecks such as CUDA context-switching overheads, register pressure, memory bank conflicts, and PCIe/VRAM transfer contention.
- **Physical Integration Missing**: A true physical execution benchmark of the entire Pass 1 + Routing + Merging + Pass 2 pipeline on actual GPU hardware (e.g., NVIDIA A100 or L4) integrated with serving frameworks (vLLM or S-LoRA) is missing, leaving systems speedup claims partially unverified in real-world deployment settings.

## Experimental Rating
**Good**. The statistical sweep across 10 seeds and the inclusion of real ViT and GPT-2 models are commendable. However, the artificial degradation of static merging baselines, the reliance on extremely low-accuracy (16-sample) experts for physical downstream evaluation, and the simulated nature of the GPU latency benchmark represent significant empirical limitations.
