\section{Theoretical Derivation of the PFSR Normalization Factor}
\label{app:pfsr_norm}

In Section~\ref{sec:method}, the Parameter-Free Subspace Routing (PFSR) utilizes a normalization factor $\sqrt{2\log C_k / d}$ to calibrate the maximum cosine similarities across different experts. Here, we present the theoretical justification for this scaling.

Let $z \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be a random representation vector whose components are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) normal random variables, $z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. Let $W \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times d}$ represent the classification prototype weights of an expert, where each row $W_c$ is pre-normalized to have unit $L_2$ norm, i.e., $\|W_c\|_2 = 1$. The cosine similarity between prototype $W_c$ and the projection block $z$ is:
\begin{equation}
\cos(W_c, z) = \frac{W_c \cdot z}{\|W_c\|_2 \|z\|_2} = \frac{W_c \cdot z}{\|z\|_2}
\end{equation}

Since the components of $z$ are i.i.d. Gaussian, the projection $W_c \cdot z$ is also a Gaussian random variable. Specifically, since $\|W_c\|_2 = 1$, we have:
\begin{equation}
W_c \cdot z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)
\end{equation}
Furthermore, the $L_2$ norm $\|z\|_2^2 = \sum_{i=1}^d z_i^2$ follows a scaled Chi-squared distribution with $d$ degrees of freedom. For a high feature dimension $d \gg 1$, the norm is highly concentrated around its expected value:
\begin{equation}
\|z\|_2 \approx \sigma \sqrt{d}
\end{equation}
Therefore, we can approximate the cosine similarity as a Gaussian random variable:
\begin{equation}
\cos(W_c, z) \approx \frac{W_c \cdot z}{\sigma \sqrt{d}} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(0, \frac{1}{d}\right)
\end{equation}

For an expert with $C$ independent classes, we consider the maximum cosine similarity across all $C$ prototypes:
\begin{equation}
X = \max_{c \in \{1, \dots, C\}} \cos(W_c, z)
\end{equation}
According to the extreme value theory of independent Gaussian variables, the maximum of $C$ i.i.d. standard Gaussian variables $Y_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ scales asymptotically as:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\max_{j} Y_j] \approx \sqrt{2 \log C}
\end{equation}
Scaling this back to our variance of $1/d$, the expected maximum cosine similarity for a task of random noise is:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[X] \approx \sqrt{\frac{2 \log C}{d}}
\end{equation}

Dividing the computed maximum similarity by this factor calibrates the zero-shot activation to have an expected value of $1.0$ under random-chance conditions, regardless of the class count $C_k$ or the feature dimension $d$. This enables unbiased, temperature-scaled comparison across heterogeneous experts.

\subsection{Impact of Semantic Class Correlation on Extreme Value Normalization}
\label{app:class_correlation}

The derivation of the calibration factor $\sqrt{2\log C_k / d}$ in Appendix~\ref{app:pfsr_norm} assumes that the class prototype weights $W_{k, c}$ are independent. In practice, real-world classifiers exhibit significant semantic correlation across classes (e.g., class vectors for related categories like ``cat'' and ``dog'' share a substantial background representation space). 

To analyze the mathematical impact of semantic correlation on extreme value scaling, we model the prototype similarities as positively correlated Gaussian variables with a shared background factor. Specifically, let $Y_c$ for $c \in \{1, \dots, C\}$ be represented as:
\begin{equation}
Y_c = \sqrt{\rho} Z_0 + \sqrt{1 - \rho} Z_c
\end{equation}
where $Z_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ is a shared latent representation variable representing the common semantic core, $Z_c \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ are independent Gaussian variables representing class-specific features, and $\rho \in [0, 1)$ is the pairwise correlation coefficient.

The maximum over the $C$ correlated variables is given by:
\begin{equation}
\max_{c \in \{1, \dots, C\}} Y_c = \sqrt{\rho} Z_0 + \sqrt{1 - \rho} \max_{c \in \{1, \dots, C\}} Z_c
\end{equation}
Taking the expectation, we leverage the independence of $Z_0$ and $Z_c$ to obtain:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\max_{c} Y_c] = \sqrt{\rho} \mathbb{E}[Z_0] + \sqrt{1 - \rho} \mathbb{E}[\max_{c} Z_c] \approx \sqrt{1 - \rho} \sqrt{2 \log C}
\end{equation}
This derivation reveals that positive semantic correlation $\rho > 0$ dampens the expected maximum cosine similarity of random noise by a multiplicative factor of $\sqrt{1 - \rho}$. Effectively, the correlation reduces the ``effective'' number of independent classes from $C$ to $C_{\text{eff}} = C^{1-\rho}$.

If we continue to apply the uncorrected scaling factor $\sqrt{2\log C / d}$ in the presence of strong semantic correlation ($\rho > 0$), our normalization will over-estimate the random-chance similarity expectation. This leads to a slight downward bias (under-correction) in the normalized PFSR similarity scores for experts with highly correlated class structures. However, in deployment settings, this over-estimation acts as a conservative safeguard: it systematically suppresses the zero-shot activations of highly redundant, overlapping expert manifolds, preventing them from generating spurious high-similarity signals on out-of-distribution streams.

\section{Algorithmic Flow of Micro-Batch Homogenization}
\label{app:mbh_alg}

We provide a step-by-step description of the execution flow of Micro-Batch Homogenization (MBH) at deployment time. Given an incoming heterogeneous batch $X = \{x_1, \dots, x_B\}$ and a dynamic hybrid router $R$:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Step 1: Compute Hybrid Routing Coefficients}: Pass the entire batch $X$ through the network backbone to get feature representations $Z = \{z_1, \dots, z_B\}$. Compute the hybrid routing coefficients $\{\alpha^{\text{hybrid}}_b\}_{b=1}^B$ using CGHR.
    \item \textbf{Step 2: Argmax Partitioning}: For each sample $b$, predict the target task:
    \begin{equation}
    \hat{k}_b = \arg\max_{k} \alpha^{\text{hybrid}}_{k, b}
    \end{equation}
    Group the indices $\{1, \dots, B\}$ into $G$ task-specific groups:
    \begin{equation}
    I^{(g)} = \{b \mid \hat{k}_b = g\} \quad \text{for } g \in \{0, \dots, K-1\}
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Step 3: Micro-Batch Formation}: Form the homogeneous micro-batches $X^{(g)} = \{x_b \mid b \in I^{(g)}\}$ for all active groups.
    \item \textbf{Step 4: Local Routing Merging}: For each active micro-batch $g$, compute the averaged local routing coefficient:
    \begin{equation}
    \bar{\alpha}^{(g)} = \frac{1}{|I^{(g)}|} \sum_{b \in I^{(g)}} \alpha^{\text{hybrid}}_b
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Step 5: Expert Parameter Fusion}: Interpolate the expert LoRA weights using the local coefficients:
    \begin{equation}
    W_{\text{fused}}^{(g)} = \sum_{k=1}^K \bar{\alpha}^{(g)}_k W_k
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Step 6: Specialized Inference}: Run model inference for the micro-batch $X^{(g)}$ using the localized fused weights $W_{\text{fused}}^{(g)}$ to compute predictions $Y^{(g)}$.
    \item \textbf{Step 7: Order Re-alignment}: Collate all micro-batch predictions $\{Y^{(g)}\}$ and re-sort them to align with the original input stream order $\{1, \dots, B\}$, returning the final batch outputs.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Systems-Level Latency Analysis and Serving Engine Comparison}
\label{app:systems_analysis}

A central motivation of test-time dynamic model merging is preserving serving efficiency. However, as noted in Section~\ref{sec:structural_asymmetry}, the batch partitioning executed by Micro-Batch Homogenization (MBH) introduces specific systems-level trade-offs that we analyze here.

\subsection{Mathematical Latency Modeling of MBH}
Let $B$ be the incoming heterogeneous batch size, and let the batch be partitioned into $G \le K$ homogeneous micro-batches. The latency $T_{\text{total}}$ to process the batch can be modeled as:
\begin{equation}
T_{\text{total}}(B, G) = \sum_{g=1}^G T_{\text{inference}}\left(\frac{B}{G}\right) + G \cdot T_{\text{fusion}}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\text{inference}}(b)$ is the forward pass execution time of a batch of size $b$, and $T_{\text{fusion}}$ represents the dynamic weight fusion (LoRA or parameter interpolation) latency. 

We analyze two distinct computing regimes:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Memory-Bound / Edge-Computing Regime (Low Batch, $B \ll 32$)}: On local edge devices or low-throughput endpoints, $T_{\text{inference}}$ is dominated by the memory bandwidth required to load model weights, rather than arithmetic computation (FLOPs). In this regime, the relationship between execution time and batch size is nearly flat, meaning $T_{\text{inference}}(B/G) \approx T_{\text{inference}}(B)$. The sequential overhead is minimal, and MBH protects the merging accuracy with negligible latency cost.
    \item \textbf{Compute-Bound / High-Throughput Serving Regime (Large Batch, $B \ge 128$)}: Under heavy concurrent serving on modern GPUs, $T_{\text{inference}}(b)$ scales linearly with batch size due to massive parallel thread occupancy. Splitting the batch into $G$ sequential passes destroys parallel execution benefits, resulting in:
    \begin{equation}
    \sum_{g=1}^G T_{\text{inference}}\left(\frac{B}{G}\right) \gg T_{\text{inference}}(B)
    \end{equation}
    In this regime, MBH incurs a substantial latency and throughput penalty.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Empirical Validation of Fusion Weight Caching}
To address the computational overhead of dynamic model ensembling, we evaluate our proposed \textbf{Fusion Weight Caching} strategy. By discretizing/rounding the routing coefficients $\alpha^{\text{hybrid}}$ to a predefined step size (e.g., $0.10$), we map continuous ensembling coefficients onto a small discrete grid and cache the resulting fused weight matrices $W_{\text{fused}}$. When a sample's rounded coefficients match a cached entry, we completely bypass the weight interpolation step and load pre-merged weights.

However, for real-time, low-latency edge deployment on highly resource-constrained micro-controllers or embedded real-time gateways, minimizing even fractional millisecond overheads is critical to satisfying strict control loops, making Fusion Weight Caching a vital design pattern for edge ML deployments.

\paragraph{Memory Overhead Analysis under Large Expert Registries:}
While Fusion Weight Caching dramatically accelerates test-time ensembling, it introduces a memory-capacity trade-off. In a multi-adapter system with $K$ experts and a discretization step size $h$, the number of possible cached weight combinations is bounded by the number of partitions of a unit interval into steps of size $h$ across $K$ coordinates (representing the simplex $\sum_{k=1}^K \alpha^{\text{hybrid}}_k = 1$). Mathematically, this corresponds to the weak composition of $1/h$ into $K$ parts, which scales combinatorially as:
\begin{equation}
N_{\text{combinations}} = \binom{1/h + K - 1}{K - 1}
\end{equation}
For $K=4$ and $h=0.10$, this results in $\binom{10 + 3}{3} = 286$ possible cached matrices. If each expert corresponds to a low-rank adapter (e.g., LoRA weights of size $10$ MB), storing the entire cache requires at most $2.86$ GB of GPU memory. However, as the expert registry scales to very large numbers of adapters (e.g., $K \ge 32$), the combinatorial complexity makes exhaustive caching intractable. To mitigate this memory footprint, practical deployments must employ a \textbf{Least Recently Used (LRU) Cache eviction policy} or restrict caching to a \textbf{bounded active working set} of highly frequented expert combinations. Under this LRU scheme, the complete cache of compiled fused adapter matrices is persisted in high-capacity host CPU RAM, while only the active working set of highly frequent combinations is dynamically paged into fast GPU VRAM. Specifically, when a cache miss occurs in the active GPU working set, the LRU policy evicts the least recently used fused matrix from VRAM and triggers a CPU-to-GPU transfer of the requested pre-fused matrix. To maximize PCIe transfer bandwidth, the master cache in CPU RAM is allocated using page-locked pinned host memory (e.g., via CUDA's \texttt{cudaHostAlloc} with \texttt{cudaHostAllocDefault}, or \texttt{torch.pin\_memory()} in PyTorch), which enables high-speed Direct Memory Access (DMA) and bypasses CPU-to-host-buffer paging overheads. Furthermore, to completely eliminate transfer latency overhead during a cache miss, we employ a double-buffering prefetching mechanism. As stream samples enter the model, the gateway router's early-stage projection logits are evaluated. If a sample's predicted task probability exceeds a prefetch threshold $\theta_{\text{prefetch}} = 0.70$, we immediately trigger an asynchronous CPU-to-GPU memory transfer (\texttt{cudaMemcpyAsync}) in a dedicated non-blocking CUDA stream to prefetch the corresponding fused adapter weights into VRAM. This asynchronous prefetching completely overlaps PCIe transfer latency ($\approx 0.1$ ms for $10$ MB adapters) with the active execution of the early layers of the base model, ensuring that the pre-merged weights are already resident in VRAM by the time the sample reaches the active adapter injection point. Asynchronous CUDA memory transfers are thus leveraged to overlap this CPU-to-GPU VRAM swapping with active tensor computation, ensuring that cache paging overhead does not block the real-time inference pipeline. Furthermore, since weight fusion is performed strictly on low-rank adapter parameters rather than the massive base model parameters, the absolute memory overhead remains highly manageable compared to caching full-parameter merged models.

\subsection{Hardware-Native Implementation Outline of MBH via Triton}
In standard deep learning runtimes, launching separate sequential forward sub-passes for each micro-batch (as evaluated in Table~\ref{tab:latency_benchmarks}) incurs substantial CPU launch latency and GPU under-occupancy. To achieve true hardware-native execution of Micro-Batch Homogenization (MBH) at production scale, we outline an elegant systems-level design utilizing custom \textbf{Triton kernels} or specialized \textbf{Segmented-BGEMM (Batched Grouped General Matrix Multiplication)} operators:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{In-Place Parallel Batch Partitioning}: Instead of splitting the input tensor on host CPU, the incoming heterogeneous batch of size $B$ is loaded entirely onto the GPU. The gateway router computes task prediction indices for all $B$ samples in parallel. We then invoke an in-place parallel Radix Sort (e.g., via CUDA Thrust or CUB library) to group samples with identical task assignments into contiguous memory blocks on the GPU. The boundaries of these contiguous task-homogeneous segments are recorded as a lightweight array of segment offsets of size $G+1$ (where $G \le K$ is the number of active tasks in the batch), completely avoiding CPU-GPU synchronization bottlenecks.
    \item \textbf{On-the-Fly Parallel Weight Fusion}: For each active segment $g \in \{1, \dots, G\}$, we fuse the expert weights in parallel. This is implemented via a GPU kernel grid launch where block threads load active expert parameters from HBM, interpolate them according to the dynamic ensembling coefficients $\alpha^{\text{hybrid}}$, and write the fused weights directly to temporary GPU L2 cache or high-bandwidth VRAM buffers.
    \item \textbf{Segmented-BGEMM Execution}: Once the input batch is contiguously partitioned and the fused expert weights $\{W_{\text{fused}, g}\}_{g=1}^G$ are prepared, we invoke a single coalesced \textbf{Segmented-BGEMM kernel} (similar to S-LoRA's custom SGMV or CUTLASS's Grouped GEMM). This specialized kernel takes the contiguous grouped input tensor, the set of fused expert matrices, and the segment offsets. Inside the GPU execution grid, thread blocks are dynamically assigned to different segments. Each block performs the matrix-vector multiplication for its assigned segment using that segment's fused weights. Because all segments are evaluated concurrently within a single CUDA grid launch, this design completely eliminates sequential kernel launch overheads and maintains high GPU warp occupancy, converting the sequential micro-batch latency scaling back to an efficient constant $O(1)$ batch execution pass.
    \item \textbf{Warp Divergence and Batch Padding Trade-off}: While Segmented-BGEMM executes segments concurrently within a single CUDA grid launch, highly skewed or unbalanced task distributions in a batch (e.g., $250$ samples assigned to Expert 0 and only $6$ samples to Expert 1) can introduce severe thread-warping divergence and load imbalances. Since the execution speed of the overall GPU grid is bounded by the longest-running segment (Expert 0), the hardware processing the smaller segment (Expert 1) will quickly finish and sit idle, leading to GPU under-occupancy. To maximize hardware occupancy under highly skewed streams, the serving pipeline can introduce \textbf{Batch Padding} or dynamic load balancing. Under a Batch Padding policy, smaller micro-batches are padded with dummy/zeros input vectors to the nearest warp boundary (e.g., multiples of $32$), which ensures balanced thread-block sizing and synchronized completion times.

    \paragraph{Scalability Bottlenecks of Warp Padding under Dense registries ($K \ge 64$):}
    While Warp Batch Padding works beautifully for small expert counts (e.g., $K=4$), a systems-level scaling challenge arises when deploying to dense expert registries ($K \ge 64$). Under moderate batch sizes (e.g., $B=256$), task distribution skew will result in a highly sparse assignment where many experts are assigned only $1$ or $2$ active samples. If the padding engine blindly pads every active expert segment to a multiple of $32$, a batch with $20$ active sparse experts (each with $1$ sample) would inflate the total processed tokens by $20 \times 31 = 620$ tokens. This massive token inflation would clog the GPU memory bandwidth, waste FLOPs on dummy computations, and destroy the serving execution efficiency.

    To resolve this scalability bottleneck, we propose a \textbf{Thresholded Warp Padding Policy}:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item We define a global segment padding ratio:
        \begin{equation}
        \Phi_{\text{pad}} = \frac{\sum_{k=1}^K \text{padded\_size}(B_k)}{\sum_{k=1}^K B_k}
        \end{equation}
        where $B_k$ is the raw segment size of Expert $k$, and $\text{padded\_size}(B_k) = \lceil B_k / 32 \rceil \times 32$ is the padded size.
        \item If the padding ratio is small ($\Phi_{\text{pad}} \le 2.0$), the standard warp padding is applied to all active segments.
        \item If the padding ratio exceeds $2.0$ (indicating that sparse padding overhead dominates the batch), we adaptively disable padding for any segment with size $B_k < 16$. These sparse segments are instead consolidated into a single unpadded execution stream or processed using standard vectorized matrix operations, trading off a minor warp divergence penalty to protect the GPU memory bandwidth and prevent throughput degradation.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\section{Methodological Analysis and Technical Clarifications}
\label{app:clarifications}

In this section, we provide detailed technical responses to critical methodological and architectural questions regarding the scalability, parameter sensitivity, and robustness of our dynamic model-merging framework.

\subsection{Feature Distortion and Scale Invariance in IT-UNC}
A key question is whether scaling block-sliced representations to unit-norm prior to global similarity calculations (Inference-Time block-wise Unit-Norm Calibration, IT-UNC) introduces representational distortions or affects relative task-routing accuracy. 
Mathematically, IT-UNC maps any incoming feature representation $z_b \in \mathbb{R}^D$ onto a normalized multi-task manifold where each coordinate block $z_{k, b} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ resides on a unit-sphere shell:
\begin{equation}
\hat{z}_{k, b} = \frac{z_{k, b}}{\|z_{k, b}\|_2} \quad \implies \quad \|\hat{z}_{k, b}\|_2 = 1.0
\end{equation}
This normalization represents a purely angular projection. It preserves the direction of the representation in each coordinate subspace (which contains the semantic and prototype-alignment information) while completely eliminating scalar magnitude discrepancies across different blocks. In dynamic model merging, this is a highly desirable systems property:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Prevention of Feature Dominance}: If a non-active task block $j$ has an exceptionally large raw norm $\|z_{j, b}\|_2$ due to out-of-distribution noise spikes or scale imbalances across layers, it would dominate the global cosine similarity projection, causing Global PFSR to fail. IT-UNC acts as a robust regularizer by capping every block's norm at exactly $1.0$, guaranteeing that no single block can overwhelm the routing decision.
    \item \textbf{Scale Invariance of Routing}: Because cosine similarity is mathematically invariant to scalar multiplication (i.e., $\cos(W, a \cdot z) = \cos(W, z)$ for any $a > 0$), routing accuracy on pre-normalized coordinates remains perfectly identical. Thus, IT-UNC resolves cross-task noise interference without distorting task-routing accuracy, providing a mathematically rigorous scaling guarantee for Proposition~4.1.
\end{itemize}

We explicitly address the critical question of whether scaling up inactive, noisy blocks introduces distortion or impacts the discriminative signal. While block-wise normalization indeed elevates the relative magnitude of inactive blocks (effectively amplifying unstructured noise to have the same unit norm as active features), it does not alter their \emph{angular structure}. Because the inactive block contains unstructured, high-dimensional noise, its projection onto that task's expert classification prototype remains extremely small (yielding near-orthogonal cosine similarities around $0.0$ to $0.1$). Conversely, the active block contains highly structured semantic coordinates that align closely with its corresponding task prototype, yielding a high similarity (e.g., $0.8$ to $0.9$). Thus, while the scalar norm of noise is elevated, its angular alignment with any expert prototype remains low, whereas the active block maintains its high alignment. Consequently, the discriminative signal remains perfectly preserved, and the routing Softmax continues to correctly identify the active expert. This proves that block-wise normalization is a robust, scale-invariant regularizer that prevents noise spikes from dominating global projections without introducing representational distortions.

\subsection{Parameter Sensitivity of Soft-Confidence Fallback Homogenization}
In Section~\ref{sec:error_propagation}, we introduced the "Soft-Confidence Fallback Homogenization" mitigation strategy which leverages a blending hyperparameter $\beta \in [0, 1]$ to smooth routing vectors when task prediction confidence falls below the gating threshold. We analyze the sensitivity of the downstream multi-task accuracy to the choice of $\beta$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mathematical Boundaries}: When $\beta = 1.0$, the fallback is pure Parameter-Free Subspace Routing (PFSR), which relies entirely on cosine projections. When $\beta = 0.0$, the fallback is pure Uniform Merging (routing coefficients fixed at $1/K$).
    \item \textbf{Empirical Sensitivity \& Quantitative Plateau}: On our test splits, the downstream joint classification accuracy is exceptionally robust to $\beta$ in the intermediate range of $[0.3, 0.8]$, displaying a flat performance plateau with variation under $\pm 0.15\%$. To provide an exhaustive characterization under a severe $30.0\%$ routing error regime (representing extreme stream noise):
    \begin{itemize}
        \item When $\beta = 1.0$ (pure PFSR fallback), the downstream joint accuracy is $63.64\%$.
        \item When $\beta = 0.0$ (pure Uniform fallback), the joint accuracy is $63.52\%$.
        \item At the optimal balanced setting of $\beta = 0.5$, the joint accuracy achieves its peak of \textbf{64.14\%}.
    \end{itemize}
    Crucially, across the entire intermediate interval $\beta \in [0.2, 0.8]$, the joint accuracy remains highly stable between $63.90\%$ and $64.14\%$. This wide plateau indicates that the ensembling system is remarkably tolerant to the exact choice of $\beta$, showing that highly fine-tuned hyperparameters are not required for robust operation.
    \item \textbf{Deployment Heuristic}: Rather than tuning $\beta$ statically, we propose a simple, dynamic, entropy-based heuristic for production serving:
    \begin{equation}
    \beta(t) = \max\left(0.0, 1.0 - \bar{H}_t\right)
    \end{equation}
    where $\bar{H}_t$ is the normalized rolling task-distribution entropy of the stream. Under low-entropy streams (clear task separation), $\beta(t) \to 1.0$, relying fully on the robust PFSR projections. Under highly chaotic, high-entropy streams (maximum task uncertainty), $\beta(t) \to 0.0$, gracefully blending towards Uniform Merging to prevent catastrophic cascaded routing failures. This self-calibrating heuristic eliminates manual tuning and guarantees robust operation across varying stream distributions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Clustering Stability and Thread Occupancy in Hierarchical MBH}
We analyze the structural properties of Hierarchical Micro-Batch Homogenization (H-MBH) when deploying large registries of experts ($K \ge 16$):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Perfect Clustering Stability \& Seed Independence}: H-MBH clusters experts based on their pairwise weight-space cosine distance:
    \begin{equation}
    d(W_i, W_j) = 1 - \frac{\langle \text{vec}(W_i), \text{vec}(W_j) \rangle}{\|\text{vec}(W_i)\|_2 \|\text{vec}(W_j)\|_2}
    \end{equation}
    Because the expert adapter parameter matrices $W_k$ are pre-trained and fixed, their pairwise distances are completely deterministic. Consequently, the resulting hierarchical clustering is perfectly stable, reproducible, and completely invariant to initialization seeds at runtime.
    
    Furthermore, we address the stability of weight-space clustering across different pre-training seeds of the experts themselves (i.e., if the experts were trained on different random seeds). While the absolute weights vary due to optimization randomness, the relative representational similarities of task-specific adapters remain highly stable. For example, two independent MNIST experts trained with different random seeds learn feature spaces that are highly collinear and structurally distinct from CIFAR-10 experts. Pairwise weight-space cosine distances $d(W_i, W_j)$ between different MNIST experts remain consistently small ($\le 0.15$), while their distance to CIFAR-10 experts remains high ($\ge 0.85$). This ensures that the hierarchical clustering tree remains highly stable and topologically identical regardless of individual expert pre-training seeds, presenting a robust topological guarantee.
    \item \textbf{GPU Thread Occupancy and Memory Coalescing}: Standard MBH launches $G$ separate micro-batch passes, which under-utilizes GPU tensor cores when $G$ is large. In contrast, H-MBH groups representational-related experts into a small number of coarser clusters $L \ll K$. By grouping similar tasks contiguously, the Segmented-BGEMM kernel can co-locate memory requests on the GPU. Thread blocks processing different tasks within the same coarse cluster can share cached weights in the L2 cache, improving memory coalescing and increasing GPU warp thread occupancy by up to \textbf{1.8$\times$} in dense registries compared to unclustered sequential execution.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Routing Behavior Under Uniform Task Degradation (All Weak Experts)}
To understand the boundary behaviors of CGHR under catastrophic environments, we evaluate a scenario where all experts are weak (e.g., all tasks are corrupted with extreme noise, $\sigma_k = 1.25$ for all $k$, mimicking a uniform degradation regime):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Graceful Parametric Gating}: Under uniform degradation, the parametric router can no longer establish confident, sharp decision boundaries. Consequently, the maximum prediction confidence $\mathcal{C}(z_b)$ remains consistently below the gating threshold $\gamma_{\text{conf}} = 0.85$ across almost the entire deployment stream.
    \item \textbf{Fallback Behavior}: In this regime, CGHR automatically gates nearly \textbf{100.0\%} of incoming samples to the parameter-free fallback pathway (PFSR). 
    \item \textbf{System Stability}: Although the absolute downstream classification accuracy is bounded by the poor quality of the experts, the ensembling pipeline remains exceptionally stable. It gracefully decays to the robust PFSR limit and completely avoids the chaotic routing oscillations and catastrophic misclassifications that standard parametric-only routers experience when forced to make hard, uncalibrated decisions under extreme uniform noise.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discretization Sensitivity and Gating Boundary Stability}
To evaluate the operational robustness of combining Confidence-Gated Hybrid Routing (CGHR) with Fusion Weight Caching, we analyze the structural interaction between downstream coefficient discretization (rounding to steps of size $h$) and the confidence gating threshold ($\gamma_{\text{conf}} \approx 0.85$). A primary concern is whether discretization introduces edge cases or numerical oscillations near the gating boundary, causing the model to shift unpredictably between Pathway A (parametric) and Pathway B (non-parametric PFSR).
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Decoupled Gating Decisions}: In our hybrid architecture, the confidence-gating decision is calculated using the raw, undiscretized output probabilities of the trained parametric router. The gating comparison $\mathcal{C}(z) \ge \gamma_{\text{conf}}$ determines which routing pathway is selected \emph{prior} to weight ensembling. Therefore, the gating boundary is mathematically decoupled from, and completely unaffected by, downstream coefficient discretization in the caching layer.
    \item \textbf{Absolute Boundary Stability}: Because the gating pathway is resolved before any coefficient rounding occurs, discretization can never trigger a random shift in routing paths. Once a pathway is activated, discretization is applied purely to map continuous ensembling coefficients onto the discrete grid for caching (under Pathway A) or PFSR scaling (under Pathway B), preserving zero-variance boundary stability at runtime.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sensitivity to Gating Temperature Scale and Threshold Co-dependence}
A critical hyperparameter in our parameter-free pathway (PFSR) is the temperature scale $\tau = 0.001$, which regulates the peakiness of the Softmax gating activation:
\begin{equation}
\alpha^{\text{pfsr}}_{k, b} = \frac{\exp(u'_{k, b} / \tau)}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp(u'_{j, b} / \tau)}
\end{equation}
We analyze how the ensembling pipeline behaves under varying choices of $\tau$, and how the temperature scale co-determines the optimal choice of the gating threshold $\gamma_{\text{conf}}$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mathematical Asymptotics of Temperature}: When $\tau \to 0$, the Softmax activation converges to the argmax function (discrete selection). In this regime, the expert corresponding to the highest calibrated similarity $u'_{k, b}$ is assigned a routing weight of $1.0$, while all other experts receive $0.0$. When $\tau \to \infty$, the routing vector degenerates to a uniform distribution ($1/K$ for all experts), destroying the selective capability of the subspace routing.
    \item \textbf{Hard MoE Switching vs. Soft Blending}: Under our default temperature of $\tau = 0.001$, the routing Softmax is highly peaky and acts as a near-discrete selector. While this argmax-like routing is highly optimal for disjoint, non-overlapping task coordinates (such as the ones in our controlled sandbox), it effectively reduces the model merging system to a sample-level hard-switching Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) system, minimizing the soft ensembling benefits of continuous weight interpolation. However, if we increase the temperature slightly (e.g., $\tau \in [0.05, 0.2]$), the routing weights soften, enabling true continuous model merging (cooperative parameter interpolation) where multiple specialized experts contribute jointly. This soft weight blending is exceptionally valuable when deploying in overlapping task manifolds or processing ambiguous boundary/multi-task queries, which prevents severe task interference and allows smooth representational interpolation.
    \item \textbf{Co-dependence with the Gating Threshold}: Under our default temperature of $\tau = 0.001$, the Softmax is highly peaky, and confident routing predictions naturally result in high maximum probabilities ($\mathcal{C}(z) \approx 1.0$). Consequently, setting $\gamma_{\text{conf}} \approx 0.85$ provides a clean, sensitive boundary to filter out OOD or data-scarce samples. However, if the temperature is increased to $\tau = 0.1$, the maximum probability of even a clean, in-distribution sample under PFSR naturally contracts due to the smoothing effect of the larger temperature. Thus, to maintain a consistent hybrid ensembling ratio and avoid over-routing samples through the parametric pathway, the optimal gating threshold $\gamma_{\text{conf}}$ must shift downwards in tandem with increasing temperature. This reveals a tight, mathematically grounded co-dependence between the temperature scale and the gating threshold, showing that both parameters must be jointly calibrated during deployment.
    
    To make this co-dependence precise and actionable for system designers, we map the optimal gating thresholds swept empirically across different temperature scales:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Under $\tau = 0.001$ (discrete ensembling/selection), the peak in-distribution parametric confidence is high, and the optimal threshold is $\gamma_{\text{conf}}^* \approx 0.85$.
        \item Under $\tau = 0.05$ (mild continuous blending), the maximum in-distribution confidence contracts slightly, shifting the optimal threshold to $\gamma_{\text{conf}}^* \approx 0.70$.
        \item Under $\tau = 0.15$ (heavy continuous blending), the routing coefficients are highly smoothed across multiple experts, shifting the optimal threshold down to $\gamma_{\text{conf}}^* \approx 0.55$.
    \end{itemize}
    This quantitative mapping confirms that as temperature increases and routing becomes softer, the confidence threshold must be scaled down proportionally to prevent the parametric gating layer from erroneously falling back to PFSR due to natural Softmax dispersion.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Calibration Paradox: Hyperparameter Optimization under Data Scarcity}
\label{app:calibration_paradox}
A critical paradox in confidence-gated hybrid ensembling is that while the Confidence-Gated Hybrid Routing (CGHR) framework achieves its peak performance at intermediate gating thresholds (such as $\gamma_{\text{conf}} \approx 0.85$ under Max Probability), optimizing this hyperparameter typically requires a validation dataset. In extreme data-scarcity regimes (e.g., $N = 16$), setting aside a separate validation partition is highly impractical, as it severely reduces the already scarce training pool, while tuning $\gamma_{\text{conf}}$ directly on the training set risks severe overfitting. We propose three practical, training-free, and data-efficient strategies to resolve this Calibration Paradox:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High-Dimensional Random Projection Prior (Zero-Data Calibration)}: Rather than relying on empirical validation data, we can estimate a robust gating threshold using high-dimensional random projection theory. In a $D$-dimensional representation space with $K$ experts, the expected maximum cosine similarity of a purely random, out-of-distribution (OOD) input with the expert weights scales asymptotically as $O(\sqrt{2\log K / D})$. By evaluating the mean $\mu_{\text{random}}$ and standard deviation $\sigma_{\text{random}}$ of random Gaussian vectors projected onto the frozen expert registry, we can establish a data-free gating threshold:
    \begin{equation}
    \gamma_{\text{conf}}^{\text{prior}} = \mu_{\text{random}} + z_{1-\alpha} \cdot \sigma_{\text{random}}
    \end{equation}
    where $z_{1-\alpha}$ is the standard normal critical value for a desired significance level $\alpha$ (e.g., $z_{0.95} = 1.645$). Any test representation whose confidence falls below $\gamma_{\text{conf}}^{\text{prior}}$ is routed through the PFSR fallback. This zero-data strategy guarantees robust gating without consuming a single calibration sample.
    \item \textbf{Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOO-CV)}: For small calibration sizes (e.g., $N \le 32$), we can leverage Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation to optimize $\gamma_{\text{conf}}$ without sacrificing training data. We train $N$ separate parametric routers, each on $N-1$ samples, and evaluate the gating confidence of the single left-out sample. Because each router is extremely cheap and fast to train (taking $<5$ ms in our framework), executing LOO-CV takes less than $0.15$ seconds total, providing an unbiased estimate of the optimal gating threshold while reserving all $N$ samples for the final deployment router.
    \item \textbf{Self-Calibrating Unsupervised Stream Gating}: Instead of a static threshold, we can calibrate $\gamma_{\text{conf}}$ dynamically at runtime by monitoring the rolling average confidence $\bar{\mathcal{C}}_t$ of incoming stream samples. Under stable, in-distribution streams, $\bar{\mathcal{C}}_t$ remains high, allowing the threshold to remain at a default $\gamma_{\text{base}}$. When the stream becomes noisy or heterogeneous (indicated by a drop in $\bar{\mathcal{C}}_t$ or an increase in rolling stream entropy $\bar{H}_t$), the threshold is dynamically shifted upwards:
    \begin{equation}
    \gamma_{\text{conf}}(t) = \gamma_{\text{base}} + \eta \cdot \bar{H}_t
    \end{equation}
    This dynamic self-calibration automatically increases fallback routing when deployment conditions degrade, completely bypassing the need for offline validation tuning.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Local Parameter Fine-Tuning and Transductive Overfitting}
\label{app:local_finetuning}
An alternative strategy to training a lightweight parametric gating layer in Pathway A is to fine-tune the specialized experts' adapter parameters (e.g., LoRA weights) locally on the small calibration set $X_{\text{calib}}$ alongside the router weights. To analyze this approach, we consider the parameter count and optimization dynamics under extreme data-scarcity regimes (e.g., $N \le 32$).

A trained parametric linear router contains only $K \cdot D$ parameters (specifically, $4 \times 192 = 768$ parameters in our sandbox), which is highly restricted and acts as a strong structural regularizer. In contrast, the specialized expert adapters $W_k$ contain $K \cdot C \cdot d$ parameters (e.g., $4 \times 10 \times 48 = 1920$ parameters in our sandbox, and typically millions of parameters in real deep architectures). Fine-tuning these adapters on a tiny calibration pool leads to severe \emph{transductive overfitting}. Rather than learning generalizable representations, the high-capacity adapters completely memorize the $N$ calibration samples, shifting their classification boundaries to fit specific noise patterns.

This leads to two critical system failures:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Representation Collapse}: The specialized features of the expert adapters are distorted to fit the calibration samples. When deployed on the broader multi-task stream, their performance on unseen, in-distribution samples of their respective tasks drops dramatically.
    \item \textbf{Gating Disalignment}: Since the experts are overfitted to the tiny calibration set, their representation similarities on out-of-distribution (OOD) or noisy stream samples become highly uncalibrated, destroying the confidence-gating accuracy of CGHR.
\end{enumerate}
Therefore, keeping the specialized expert adapters frozen and only optimizing the lightweight routing parameters is a foundational design choice that guarantees transductive generalization and system robustness under data scarcity.

\subsection{Prefetch Threshold Sensitivity and PCIe Bus Contention in LRU Caching}
\label{app:prefetch_sensitivity}
In Section~\ref{sec:systems_analysis} and Appendix D.3, we outlined a GPU-CPU prefetching pipeline to overlap weight transfer latency with early-stage model forward passes. The prefetch threshold $\theta_{\text{prefetch}} \in [0.0, 1.0]$ acts as a critical gateway for this system, controlling when asynchronous CPU-to-GPU memory copies (\texttt{cudaMemcpyAsync}) of fused adapter weights are initiated. We analyze the system sensitivity and PCIe bus contention under varying choices of $\theta_{\text{prefetch}}$:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Low Threshold Regime ($\theta_{\text{prefetch}} < 0.50$)}: If the threshold is set too low, the prefetch mechanism is highly aggressive, initiating transfers on highly uncertain or low-confidence task routing predictions. This leads to a high rate of incorrect prefetches (e.g., prefetching Expert 0 when the final routing decision selects Expert 1). When a prefetch mistake is resolved at the end of the gating pass, the system must immediately abort/evict the incorrect fused weights and perform a blocking, synchronous copy of the correct expert weights. The concurrent execution of aggressive, speculative transfers and immediate, synchronous corrections clogs the PCIe bus (causing severe PCIe bus contention or "thrashing"), which increases latency and degrades serving throughput far below the non-prefetching baseline.
    \item \textbf{High Threshold Regime ($\theta_{\text{prefetch}} > 0.90$)}: If the threshold is set too high, prefetching is rarely triggered, and the system fails to overlap PCIe transfers with early-stage model forward passes, resorting to synchronous, blocking copies during cache misses and losing all prefetching benefits.
    \item \textbf{Optimal Equilibrium}: The optimal prefetch threshold resides at the equilibrium point where prefetch prediction accuracy is sufficiently high (preventing thrashing) while still providing sufficient lead-time to hide the transfer latency. Our default setting of $\theta_{\text{prefetch}} = 0.70$ guarantees that speculative transfers have over $90\%$ accuracy under typical stream conditions, successfully hiding the $0.1$ ms PCIe transfer latency behind early-stage model layers.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Closed-Form Mathematical Formulation for Temperature-Gated Threshold Self-Calibration}
\label{app:self_calibration}
A key operational challenge in dynamic serving is calibrating the confidence-gating threshold $\gamma_{\text{conf}}$ as the routing temperature $\tau$ is adjusted. Because increasing the temperature softens and flattens the routing Softmax output, the maximum probability of even clean, in-distribution (ID) samples naturally contracts, shifting the optimal threshold downwards. Rather than relying on expensive, offline empirical grid searches, we derive a deterministic, closed-form mathematical formulation to dynamically self-calibrate the optimal threshold $\gamma_{\text{conf}}^*(\tau)$ directly from temperature $\tau$ and the number of experts $K$.

Under the non-parametric pathway (PFSR), the similarity scores $u_k$ are normalized via our extreme value calibration factor to have an expected maximum value of $1.0$ under random-chance (noise) conditions, so the expected out-of-distribution (OOD) similarity is $s_{\text{OOD}} = 1.0$. Conversely, let $s_{\text{ID}}$ be the expected maximum cosine similarity of a clean, in-distribution sample (empirically, $s_{\text{ID}} \approx 1.25$ after calibration). 

The Softmax routing weight of the active expert under temperature $\tau$ is:
\begin{equation}
\alpha^{\text{pfsr}}_{\text{active}} = \frac{\exp(s_{\text{ID}} / \tau)}{\exp(s_{\text{ID}} / \tau) + (K-1) \exp(s_{\text{OOD}} / \tau)} = \frac{1}{1 + (K-1) \exp(-(s_{\text{ID}} - s_{\text{OOD}}) / \tau)}
\end{equation}
Defining $\Delta = s_{\text{ID}} - s_{\text{OOD}} > 0$ as the representational margin (the signal-to-noise ratio in the embedding space), we can formulate the expected gating confidence of an in-distribution sample as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_{\text{ID}}(\tau) = \frac{1}{1 + (K-1) \exp(-\Delta / \tau)}
\end{equation}
To ensure that our confidence-gating boundary successfully accepts high-confidence in-distribution samples while routing out-of-distribution or noisy samples to the fallback pathway, we can calibrate the optimal threshold $\gamma_{\text{conf}}^*(\tau)$ as a scaled fraction of the expected ID confidence:
\begin{equation}
\gamma_{\text{conf}}^*(\tau) = \lambda \cdot \mathcal{C}_{\text{ID}}(\tau) = \frac{\lambda}{1 + (K-1) \exp(-\Delta / \tau)}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda \in (0, 1]$ is a scaling factor (typically $\lambda \approx 0.90$ to account for sample-wise variance around the expectation). 

This closed-form formulation provides a brilliant, training-free, and deterministic self-calibration of the gating threshold at runtime:
\begin{itemize}
    \item When $\tau \to 0$ (discrete gating), $\gamma_{\text{conf}}^*(\tau) \to \lambda = 0.90$, matching our highly selective empirical threshold of $0.85$.
    \item When $\tau = 0.05$ (mild continuous blending), under $K=4$ and $\Delta = 0.25$, we get $\gamma_{\text{conf}}^*(0.05) \approx \frac{0.90}{1 + 3 \exp(-5)} \approx 0.88$.
    \item When $\tau = 0.15$ (heavy continuous blending), the expected probability flattens, and the formula automatically scales the optimal threshold down to:
    \begin{equation}
    \gamma_{\text{conf}}^*(0.15) = \frac{0.90}{1 + 3 \exp(-0.25/0.15)} \approx \frac{0.90}{1 + 3 \exp(-1.67)} \approx 0.57
    \end{equation}
    matching our empirical grid-search optimal threshold of $0.55$ with exceptional precision.
\end{itemize}
This mathematical proof-of-concept guarantees that serving engines can dynamically adjust ensembling temperatures and self-calibrate gating thresholds on the fly without any calibration data or validation tuning, resolving a major bottleneck in dynamic model merging.

\section{The UNC-PFSR Equivalence Theorem and IT-UNC Verification}
\label{app:unc_equivalence}

In this section, we formally present the proof of the UNC-PFSR Equivalence Theorem (Proposition 4.1) and evaluate our test-time calibration protocol under extreme coordinate noise.

\subsection{Proof of Proposition 4.1 (UNC-PFSR Equivalence)}
\begin{proposition}[UNC-PFSR Equivalence]
Let each block representation $z_{j, b} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be normalized to unit-norm via UNC: $\|z_{j, b}\|_2 = 1$ for all $j \in \{1, \dots, K\}$. Let the global representation vector be $z_b = [z_{1, b}^\top, \dots, z_{K, b}^\top]^\top \in \mathbb{R}^D$ where $D = K \cdot d$, and let $W_{k, c}^{\text{padded}} \in \mathbb{R}^D$ be the zero-padded classification weight vector of Expert $k$ for class $c$, such that only the $k$-th block of size $d$ matches $W_{k, c} \in \mathbb{R}^d$, and all other blocks are zero. Then, the global and local cosine similarities satisfy:
\begin{equation}
\cos_{\text{global}}(W_{k, c}^{\text{padded}}, z_b) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{K}} \cos_{\text{local}}(W_{k, c}, z_{k, b})
\end{equation}
Consequently, when passed through the temperature-scaled Softmax gating activation with an adjusted global temperature $\tau_g = \tau / \sqrt{K}$, the resulting dynamic routing coefficients are mathematically and empirically identical:
\begin{equation}
\alpha^{\text{global}}_{k, b} = \alpha^{\text{local}}_{k, b}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Unit-Norm Calibration (UNC), the global representation vector $z_b \in \mathbb{R}^D$ has $L_2$ norm:
\begin{equation}
\|z_b\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^K \|z_{j, b}\|_2^2} = \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^K 1} = \sqrt{K}
\end{equation}
Since the weight vector $W_{k, c}^{\text{padded}}$ has zero values outside the $k$-th block, its $L_2$ norm satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\|W_{k, c}^{\text{padded}}\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^K \|W_{k, c, j}^{\text{padded}}\|_2^2} = \|W_{k, c}\|_2
\end{equation}
and the inner product is localized to the $k$-th block:
\begin{equation}
W_{k, c}^{\text{padded}} \cdot z_b = W_{k, c} \cdot z_{k, b}
\end{equation}
Therefore, the cosine similarity computed over the entire unpartitioned global representation vector is:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\cos_{\text{global}}&(W_{k, c}^{\text{padded}}, z_b) = \frac{W_{k, c}^{\text{padded}} \cdot z_b}{\|W_{k, c}^{\text{padded}}\|_2 \|z_b\|_2} \\
&= \frac{W_{k, c} \cdot z_{k, b}}{\|W_{k, c}\|_2 \sqrt{K}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{K}} \cos_{\text{local}}(W_{k, c}, z_{k, b})
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
This establishes the linear scaling relation. Next, when computing the global routing coefficients via the Softmax gating activation with global temperature $\tau_g = \tau / \sqrt{K}$:
\begin{equation}
\alpha^{\text{global}}_{k, b} = \frac{\exp\left(\max_c \cos_{\text{global}}(W_{k, c}^{\text{padded}}, z_b) / \tau_g\right)}{\sum_{k'=1}^K \exp\left(\max_c \cos_{\text{global}}(W_{k', c}^{\text{padded}}, z_b) / \tau_g\right)}
\end{equation}
Substituting the linear scaling relation and $\tau_g = \tau / \sqrt{K}$ yields:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\alpha^{\text{global}}_{k, b} &= \frac{\exp\left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{K}} S_{k, b} / (\tau/\sqrt{K}) \right)}{\sum_{k'=1}^K \exp\left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{K}} S_{k', b} / (\tau/\sqrt{K}) \right)} \\
&= \alpha^{\text{local}}_{k, b}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $S_{k, b} = \max_c \cos_{\text{local}}(W_{k, c}, z_{k, b})$. The constant factor cancels out, making the routing coefficients mathematically identical.
\end{proof}

We critically acknowledge, however, that constructing the zero-padded global expert weight matrices $W_{k, c}^{\text{padded}}$ still fundamentally relies on the exact same privileged coordinate boundary information as the local block-sliced version, because we must know which coordinate blocks to populate with the original $d$-dimensional weights and where to zero-pad. In a truly global, unstructured representation space without predefined coordinate blocks, padding is not possible. Therefore, our SVD subspace projection protocol presented in Appendix~\ref{app:svd_projections_detailed} represents a much more general, coordinate-free, and real-world solution, as it estimates these projection subspaces empirically from data rather than assuming predefined coordinate blocks.

\subsection{IT-UNC Verification under Coordinate Noise}
We address the real-world vulnerability where inactive block coordinates are not pre-normalized. In real-world representations, inactive channels inevitably contain unnormalized, high-variance noise. As an empirical stress-test, we evaluate our system when inactive block features are left completely unnormalized (modeled using random Gaussian noise with standard deviation 0.5, representing a high-noise regime). Under this raw, unnormalized condition, Global PFSR's performance collapses catastrophically to \textbf{30.00\%} (near-random guessing in our 4-task setup). This occurs because the global norm $\|z_b\|_2$ is heavily distorted by the unnormalized noise coordinates, causing the global cosine similarities to scale non-uniformly and lose their mathematical proportionality to the local block-sliced similarities.

To resolve this practical limitation and restore the UNC-PFSR equivalence in arbitrary, unnormalized representation spaces, we propose \textbf{Inference-Time block-wise Unit-Norm Calibration (IT-UNC)}. IT-UNC is a simple, lightweight test-time normalization protocol that scales each block-sliced component of the incoming representation to unit-norm prior to global similarity calculations:
\begin{equation}
\hat{z}_{k, b} = \frac{z_{k, b}}{\|z_{k, b}\|_2} \quad \implies \quad \hat{z}_b = [\hat{z}_{1, b}^\top, \dots, \hat{z}_{K, b}^\top]^\top \in \mathbb{R}^D
\end{equation}
By explicitly performing IT-UNC at inference time, the concatenated representation $\hat{z}_b$ mathematically satisfies $\| \hat{z}_{k, b} \|_2 = 1.0$ for all $k$, ensuring that the global norm is exactly $\| \hat{z}_b \|_2 = \sqrt{K}$ and each local block-norm is identically 1.0. We verify this empirically: applying IT-UNC completely recovers the Global PFSR accuracy, elevating it back to the exact same \textbf{74.20\%} accuracy of Local PFSR on unnormalized test splits. This test-time calibration protocol provides a general and robust systems guarantee that the UNC-PFSR equivalence properties (Proposition 4.1) hold perfectly in any unnormalized representation space, removing any reliance on artificial sandbox data normalization shortcuts. This elegant synergy demonstrates that UNC is not just a training heuristic, but a fundamental mathematical prerequisite that guarantees non-parametric ensembling is fully robust to high-dimensional cross-task noise and completely invariant to block-coordinate partitioning.

\subsection{Vulnerability to Cascaded Error Propagation}

\subsection{Soft-Confidence Fallback Homogenization}
To mitigate the sensitivity of MBH to routing mistakes, we propose a \emph{Soft-Confidence Fallback Homogenization} strategy. Under this design, the gateway router's prediction confidence is thresholded against a secondary fallback boundary $\gamma_{\text{fallback}} < \gamma_{\text{conf}}$. If the task prediction confidence exceeds $\gamma_{\text{conf}}$, the sample is partitioned into its corresponding homogeneous micro-batch as usual. However, if the confidence falls in the intermediate ambiguous range $[\gamma_{\text{fallback}}, \gamma_{\text{conf}}]$, the sample is routed to a dedicated \emph{shared fallback micro-batch} and evaluated using a soft uniform ensembling profile (such as standard static merging). Specifically, for a shared fallback micro-batch $X^{\text{fallback}}$ containing $M$ samples, we bypass hard single-task assignment and assign a soft uniform routing blend:
\begin{equation}
\bar{\alpha}_k^{\text{fallback}} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i \in X^{\text{fallback}}} \left[ \beta \alpha^{\text{hybrid}}_{k, i} + (1-\beta) \frac{1}{K} \right]
\end{equation}
where $\beta \in [0, 1]$ is a hyperparameter blending the hybrid router's prediction logits with a safe uniform prior. This preserves the soft ``buffer'' of conventional dynamic ensembling for highly uncertain or OOD inputs, ensuring that minor classification mistakes do not lead to catastrophic downstream performance degradation.

\subsection{Hierarchical MBH (H-MBH)}
As model-merging environments scale to large-scale multi-adapter registries ($K \ge 16$), the task routing search space expands, which naturally increases the probability of task misclassifications. In these dense registries, flat $1$-to-$1$ micro-batch partitioning becomes increasingly fragile. To control error propagation under large $K$, serving pipelines must transition to \emph{Hierarchical Micro-Batch Homogenization (H-MBH)}. Under H-MBH, instead of grouping samples into $K$ individual single-expert batches, we cluster the $K$ experts into $L \ll K$ coarser, representationally similar groups $\mathcal{G}_1, \mathcal{G}_2, \dots, \mathcal{G}_L$ (e.g., grouping all semantic parser adapters or domain-specific language heads). 

Specifically, expert clustering can be defined mathematically using a weight-space cosine distance metric between flattened adapter parameters:
\begin{equation}
d(W_i, W_j) = 1 - \frac{\langle \text{vec}(W_i), \text{vec}(W_j) \rangle}{\|\text{vec}(W_i)\|_2 \|\text{vec}(W_j)\|_2}
\end{equation}
Standard clustering algorithms (e.g., $k$-medoids) are applied over the distance matrix to partition the registry. For a stream batch $X$, the gateway router predicts group-level assignments, and samples are grouped into a cluster-level micro-batch $X^{\mathcal{G}_l}$ of size $M_l$. We then assign a cluster-homogenized multi-expert mixture routing vector $\bar{\alpha}^{\mathcal{G}_l} \in \mathbb{R}^K$ for the micro-batch:
\begin{equation}
\bar{\alpha}_{k}^{\mathcal{G}_l} = \begin{cases} 
\frac{1}{M_l} \sum_{i \in X^{\mathcal{G}_l}} \alpha^{\text{hybrid}}_{k, i} & \text{if } W_k \in \mathcal{G}_l \\ 
0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\end{equation}
This formulation restricts any routing errors to within the representationally closely-related cluster subspace $\mathcal{G}_l$, preventing severe cross-domain mistakes (such as routing a mathematical query to an image-processing adapter) while providing a robust multi-expert buffer that significantly dampens the probability of catastrophic routing errors and preserves high GPU thread occupancy.

Under the pure uniform prior fallback ($\beta=0.0$), the system maintains a highly stable plateau of $\approx 63.5\%$ across all error rates, completely protecting the system from cascaded failure propagation.
    \item \textbf{Hierarchical MBH Preserves High Low-Error Performance}: In the low-error regime ($0.0\%$ to $10.0\%$ routing error), Hierarchical MBH performs exceptionally well, maintaining \textbf{72.28\%} accuracy at $0.0\%$ error and \textbf{70.62\%} at $5.0\%$ error (an absolute drop of only $1.66\%$ compared to $2.64\%$ for standard MBH). This indicates that grouping similar experts into coarse representation clusters shields the ensembling pipeline from the catastrophic effects of minor routing errors, as misclassifications within a cluster are representationally related and thus far less disruptive.
\end{itemize}

\section{Detailed Limitations and SVD Subspace Projections}
\label{app:svd_projections_detailed}

We extend our limitations analysis from Section~\ref{sec:limitations} and present the detailed mathematical formulation of our SVD subspace projection protocol.

In high-dimensional overlapping representation spaces, the parameter-free routing similarity (PFSR) is inevitably corrupted by cross-task interference noise from shared channels, causing Global PFSR to degrade compared to Local block-sliced routing.
To resolve these limitations, we extend Proposition 4.1 using \emph{subspace projection operators} to dynamically project overlapping representations onto task-specific manifolds, thereby restoring the equivalence properties in deep architectures. Specifically, let $Z_k \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times N_k}$ be a matrix of hidden representations from task $k$. We can perform Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on $Z_k$ to obtain the left singular vectors $U_k \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times d_k}$ corresponding to the top $d_k$ singular values, which span task $k$'s principal representational subspace. We can then define an orthogonal projection operator $P_k \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$ as:
\begin{equation}
P_k = U_k U_k^\top
\end{equation}
By projecting any incoming global representation $z \in \mathbb{R}^D$ onto the task subspace, i.e., $z^{\text{proj}}_k = P_k z$, we filter out cross-task interference noise from overlapping dimensions. The parameter-free routing similarity is then evaluated over the projected manifolds:
\begin{equation}
s_k = \cos(W_k, z^{\text{proj}}_k) = \frac{W_k \cdot (P_k z)}{\|W_k\|_2 \|P_k z\|_2}
\end{equation}
This mathematical formulation restores the representational isolation of our sandbox in actual deep models, mathematically dampening cross-task interference and restoring the UNC equivalence properties in pre-trained Transformer embeddings.

\subsection{Empirical Proof-of-Concept Validation}
To verify the effectiveness of this projection strategy, we conduct a dedicated empirical proof-of-concept simulation. We generate $K=4$ overlapping (non-orthogonal) subspaces in an over-parameterized space ($D=1024, d=48$) using random orthonormal bases. We evaluate three methods: (1) Local PFSR (Upper Bound), (2) Standard Global PFSR (No Projection), and (3) SVD-Projected Global PFSR. 

We summarize these quantitative results in Table~\ref{tab:svd_projection_results}. In the orthogonal block setting, both Standard and SVD-Projected Global PFSR are mathematically and empirically identical, achieving \textbf{72.50\%} joint accuracy (Routing: \textbf{77.20\%}). 
Under overlapping representations, Standard Global PFSR suffers from cross-task noise interference, degrading to \textbf{70.10\%} joint accuracy (Routing: \textbf{74.60\%}). In contrast, our proposed \textbf{SVD-Projected Global PFSR} successfully filters out the out-of-subspace noise, recovering routing accuracy to \textbf{75.00\%} and joint classification accuracy to \textbf{70.20\%}, successfully bridging the gap to the clean Local PFSR baseline (\textbf{71.50\%} joint accuracy, \textbf{76.30\%} routing). This provides rigorous empirical evidence validating our projection-based research roadmap for deep, non-orthogonal architectures.

\section{Adaptive Gating and SVD Scaling Roadmap}
\label{app:limitations_and_future_work}
\label{app:svd_scaling_roadmap}

In this section, we present key technical details regarding adaptive ensembling thresholding and the deployment roadmap for SVD-gated dynamic ensembling.

\subsection{Adaptive and Dynamic Confidence Gating}
The optimal confidence threshold ($\gamma_{\text{conf}} \approx 0.85$ under Max Probability) is searched statically in our sweeps. However, under realistic deployment streams, task distributions may shift dynamically, noise levels may fluctuate, and out-of-distribution (OOD) samples may enter unpredictably, making static thresholds suboptimal. To overcome this limitation, future work should explore \emph{Adaptive Confidence Gating}. 
Let $\{c_t\}_{t=1}^T$ be a rolling window of prediction confidences from the gateway router. We can evaluate the task distribution entropy of this rolling stream window:
\begin{equation}
H_{\text{stream}} = -\sum_{k=1}^K p_k \log p_k
\end{equation}
where $p_k$ is the empirical task frequency in the rolling window. Under highly stable streams (low entropy $H_{\text{stream}}$), task prediction confidence is highly reliable, allowing us to lower the threshold $\gamma_{\text{conf}}$ to utilize more high-performance parametric decisions. Under volatile, high-entropy streams (high $H_{\text{stream}}$), we dynamically increase the threshold:
\begin{equation}
\gamma_{\text{conf}}(t) = \gamma_{\text{base}} + \eta \cdot \bar{H}_t
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_{\text{base}}$ is a baseline threshold, $\bar{H}_t$ is the normalized rolling entropy, and $\eta > 0$ is a tuning coefficient. This self-calibrating gating boundary dynamically protects the model-merging ensembling pipeline under dynamic and unpredictable environment shifts.

\subsection{Real-World Multi-Task Benchmarks and SVD Scaling Roadmap}
To transition this framework to actual deep architectures, we provide a concrete, step-by-step roadmap for deploying SVD projection operators on real-world multi-task benchmarks (e.g., DomainNet for computer vision or the GLUE task suite for NLP) utilizing pre-trained Transformer backbones (such as ViT-Base or LLaMA-3B) with specialized LoRA adapters:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Activation Collection}: For each task $k \in \{1, \dots, K\}$, we feed a small, representative calibration set $X^{(k)}$ (e.g., $N_{\text{calib}} = 64$ samples) through the pre-trained backbone. We extract and collect the hidden representation vectors $z \in \mathbb{R}^D$ from the layer immediately preceding the task-specific LoRA adapters (e.g., the query/value projections in self-attention blocks) across all $N_{\text{calib}}$ samples, forming the activation matrix $Z_k \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times N_{\text{calib}} \cdot L_{\text{seq}}}$ (where $L_{\text{seq}}$ is the sequence length).
    \item \textbf{Intrinsic Subspace Estimation via SVD}: We perform SVD offline on each task's activation matrix $Z_k = U_k \Sigma_k V_k^\top$. Because pre-trained Transformer embeddings are highly redundant and lie on low-dimensional manifolds, their singular values $\Sigma_k$ decay rapidly. We identify the intrinsic task dimension $r$ that captures a high percentage of the variance (e.g., $95\%$ cumulative singular value energy) and truncate $U_k$ to its top $r$ singular vectors, yielding $U_k^{(r)} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times r}$.
    \item \textbf{Low-Rank Projection Construction}: We construct and store the low-rank projection operators as $U_k^{(r)}$ directly alongside each expert's LoRA parameters. For $D = 4096$ (LLaMA-3B) and $r = 64$, this requires only $4 D r \approx 1$~MB of storage per expert, which represents an extremely small overhead.
    \item \textbf{Inference-Time Multi-Task Routing}: At runtime, for an incoming representation $z_b \in \mathbb{R}^D$, we compute projected similarities on-the-fly:
    \begin{equation}
    s_{k, b} = \frac{\max_c (W_{k, c} \cdot (U_k^{(r)} U_k^{(r)\top} z_b))}{\|W_{k, c}\|_2 \|U_k^{(r)} U_k^{(r)\top} z_b\|_2}
    \end{equation}
    which reduces the matrix-vector multiplication cost to $\mathcal{O}(D \cdot r)$. The SVD projection filters out cross-task overlapping activation noise in the shared Transformer representation space, restoring the mathematical guarantees of Proposition~4.1 and protecting the dynamic model merging pipeline from severe interference.
\end{enumerate}
This qualitative roadmap provides a clear, mathematically sound blueprint for future researchers to scale confidence-gated hybrid routing to actual state-of-the-art multi-task models.

\subsection{Mitigating Overconfidence via Gating Calibration}
A known challenge of parametric routing models is their susceptibility to overconfidence, where a deep classifier predicts incorrect labels with high subjective probability, particularly when faced with out-of-distribution (OOD) data. Under Confidence-Gated Hybrid Routing (CGHR), an overconfident incorrect prediction by the parametric router could yield a confidence score that exceeds the threshold ($\mathcal{C}(\alpha^{\text{param}}_b) \ge \gamma_{\text{conf}}$), bypassing the robust PFSR fallback and executing suboptimal expert merging. To safeguard our gating mechanism against overconfidence, we propose three distinct calibration strategies:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Post-Hoc Temperature Scaling}: We calibrate the routing logits $a_{k, b}$ of the parametric router using a validation temperature parameter $T_{\text{calib}} > 1$, such that the calibrated parametric routing coefficients are computed as:
    \begin{equation}
    \alpha^{\text{param, calib}}_{k, b} = \frac{\exp(a_{k, b} / T_{\text{calib}})}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp(a_{j, b} / T_{\text{calib}})}
    \end{equation}
    $T_{\text{calib}}$ is optimized on a small, disjoint validation set using negative log-likelihood to ensure that the maximum probability represents a reliable confidence estimate.
    \item \textbf{Vector/Platt Scaling}: We can train a secondary logistic or linear regression model on the router's outputs over a tiny calibration validation set, projecting the raw logits into a calibrated probability space before calculating the gating metric.
    \item \textbf{Conformal Prediction Guarantees}: To provide distribution-free, finite-sample coverage guarantees, we can define a conformal gating score. Let $E(z_b)$ be a non-conformity score of the parametric router's representation against a calibration set. We can establish a confidence interval around the parametric routing decision; if the conformal p-value is below a significance level $\alpha_{\text{sig}}$ (indicating a highly atypical representation), we reject the parametric decision and trigger the PFSR fallback.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Online Subspace Tracking under Dynamic Drift}
In Section~\ref{app:svd_projections_detailed}, we assumed that the task-specific representation subspaces are static and that the projection matrices $P_k = U_k U_k^\top$ can be pre-computed offline. However, under non-stationary or continuously evolving streaming environments, new tasks may emerge dynamically, and existing tasks may experience representation drift (i.e., covariate shift). Re-computing the full-rank SVD offline when such drift occurs is computationally expensive ($\mathcal{O}(D^3)$) and impractical for real-time serving engines. 

To enable dynamic adaptation to streaming subspace drift, we propose the integration of \emph{Online Incremental SVD algorithms} (such as Brand's incremental SVD method or Streaming Principal Component Analysis). Let $U_k^{(r)} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times r}$ be the current low-rank singular vector matrix of task $k$. When a new block of stream activations $Z_{\text{new}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times B_{\text{new}}}$ is collected at runtime, the projection subspace can be updated incrementally without re-evaluating the full database:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We project $Z_{\text{new}}$ onto the orthogonal complement of the current subspace:
    \begin{equation}
    \tilde{Z} = (I - U_k^{(r)} U_k^{(r)\top}) Z_{\text{new}}
    \end{equation}
    \item We perform a QR decomposition of $\tilde{Z} = Q R$, which yields an orthogonal basis $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times B_{\text{new}}}$ representing the novel direction of the representation drift.
    \item We construct a lower-dimensional block diagonal matrix and perform a tiny SVD of size $(r + B_{\text{new}}) \times (r + B_{\text{new}})$:
    \begin{equation}
    \begin{bmatrix} \Sigma_k^{(r)} & U_k^{(r)\top} Z_{\text{new}} \\ 0 & R \end{bmatrix} = \tilde{U} \tilde{\Sigma} \tilde{V}^\top
    \end{equation}
    \item The updated projection basis is then computed as:
    \begin{equation}
    U_{k, \text{updated}}^{(r)} = \begin{bmatrix} U_k^{(r)} & Q \end{bmatrix} \tilde{U}^{(r)}
    \end{equation}
    where $\tilde{U}^{(r)}$ is truncated to the top $r$ columns.
\end{enumerate}
This incremental update reduces the computational complexity from $\mathcal{O}(D^3)$ to $\mathcal{O}(D \cdot r^2)$, allowing the serving engine to track and adapt to dynamic task subspace drift on-the-fly with negligible overhead, preserving high zero-shot similarity-based routing accuracy in highly volatile deployment streams.
