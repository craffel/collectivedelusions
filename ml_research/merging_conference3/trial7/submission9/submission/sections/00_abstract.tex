\begin{abstract}
Test-time dynamic model merging has emerged as a powerful paradigm for ensembling specialized experts without retraining. However, when deployed in realistic, highly heterogeneous streaming scenarios, prior approaches suffer from severe performance degradation known as \emph{heterogeneity collapse}. Existing state-of-the-art solutions attempt to mitigate this by wrapping the model in complex, stateful systems scheduling pipelines, such as Micro-Batch Homogenization (MBH). In this work, we argue that such systems-level complexity is unnecessary. Guided by the principle of Occam's razor, we propose \textbf{SABLE (Sample-wise Activation Blending of Low-Rank Experts)}, a minimalist, network-level alternative that completely strips away stateful dynamic buffers, sorting steps, and stream-partitioning wrappers. SABLE operates natively in activation space: it executes a single forward pass through a shared pre-trained base model, alongside parallel, extremely lightweight low-rank ($r=8$) adapter passes, blending their outputs on-the-fly using sample-wise coefficients derived via non-parametric cosine subspace projections. SABLE requires zero trainable parameters, zero calibration data, and introduces no systems-level latency. In our empirical evaluation across a synthetic 14-layer coordinate-space sandbox simulating multi-task streams, SABLE's default Late Adaptation configuration achieves a flatline joint accuracy of \textbf{68.10\%} under both homogeneous and fully heterogeneous streams, exhibiting perfect robustness (\textbf{0.00\% collapse}) and outperforming the complex, stateful systems-heavy MBH pipeline (\textbf{67.20\%}) natively without any systems-level serving dependencies.
\end{abstract}
