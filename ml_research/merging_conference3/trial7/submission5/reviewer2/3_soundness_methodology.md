# Evaluation Component 3: Soundness and Methodology

## Clarity of the Description
The description of the mathematical framework is exceptionally clear and structured. The mathematical derivations—such as Unit-Norm Calibration, Cosine Similarity Projection, Class-Size Scaling, Temperature-Scaled Softmax, and Vectorized Activation Blending—are complete, rigorous, and easy to follow. The inclusion of Proposition 1 mathematically proving the equivalence of activation-space blending and weight-space merging under homogeneous batches is a valuable touch.

## Appropriateness of Methods
While the mathematical formulation is elegant, several crucial assumptions and scientific boundaries warrant critical scrutiny:

1. **Base Representation Sufficiency (PFAB-BOP):** The two-pass PFAB-BOP pathway relies heavily on the assumption that the frozen base model's representation space ($z_b$) contains sufficient semantic signal to identify the correct task domain *before* any specialized expert adapters are activated. In highly specialized or out-of-domain tasks where the base model is "blind" or collapses representation space, the base representations will remain identical across domains. In this scenario, the prototyping pass will yield highly uncalibrated or uniform routing coefficients, causing incorrect expert routing. Although the authors propose "Entropy-Based Fallback Gating (EBF)" as a safeguard, the practical efficacy and parameters of this fallback are not empirically validated on complex, blind-task environments.
2. **Semantic Representation Mismatch (PFAB-ELC):** In the single-pass PFAB-ELC pathway, projecting early-layer activations ($z_{early}$) directly onto final classification heads is recognized as invalid due to the different semantic abstractions across depth. The authors propose "Calibration-Centroid Early-Layer Gating" to resolve this by pre-computing offline centroids $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k^{(early)}$ via a small calibration set. However, early-layer activations extract low-level, pixel-dependent or token-dependent features, making these centroids highly fragile under severe organic covariate or style shifts (as seen in the DomainNet pilot where PFAB-ELC's accuracy collapses by 36% below the expert ceiling). Thus, the ELC pathway is highly fragile for real-world deployments.
3. **Layer-Constant Blending:** Applying the exact same sample-specific coefficient vector $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_b$ globally across all layers of the network ignores depth-wise specialization. In deep networks, early layers extract general, low-level features, whereas deep layers process task-specific semantics. Forcing the same blending coefficients globally prevents depth-specific adaptation and forces experts to blend general-purpose representations needlessly.
4. **Intermediate Scale Drift and Layer-Wise Adapter Scaling (LAS):** The authors propose LAS to normalize intermediate expert adapter outputs by their Frobenius norms, which is an appropriate non-parametric heuristic. However, this is a post-hoc scaling layer. It does not resolve the fundamental issue that different experts can undergo wildly different non-linear feature deformations across layers, which simple Frobenius norm scaling cannot fully neutralize.

## Potential Technical Flaws
1. **The Generative LLM One-Token Gating Lag:** In Task-Specific Vocabulary-Head Anchoring (TSVHA) for autoregressive language models, the routing coefficients for token $t$ are computed using the penultimate representation $z_{t-1}$ from the previous step. In highly mixed sequence streams with sharp task transitions, this introduces a physical one-token lag where token $t$ is executed with the incorrect adapter. Although the authors argue this is localized and acceptable, this is a fundamental physical limitation of test-time sequence routing that could lead to representation divergence or degraded generation quality in complex autoregressive tasks.
2. **Centralization Constraint in SVD Orthogonalization:** To mitigate subspace entanglement, the authors propose a joint SVD-orthogonalization strategy on the stacked parameter update matrices prior to serving. This requires centralized access to all expert adapter weights simultaneously. In multi-tenant registries where independent adapters are uploaded dynamically by decentralized entities, this centralization is highly restrictive. While the decentralized alternative, Decentralized Subspace Complement Projection (DSCP), is proposed, its quantitative performance and mathematical limits are only discussed qualitatively without any empirical results.

## Reproducibility
The theoretical reproducibility is high due to the detailed equations, concrete algorithm specifications, and the well-documented hyperparameter configurations (e.g., $\tau = 0.001$, batch size $B=256$, calibration split of 64). However, because the primary evaluation is conducted in a closed, simulated sandbox where the representation-space coordinate scrambling and SVD inversions are analytically specified, reproducing these results on real, organic networks with complex trained weights and disjoint training dynamics is highly non-trivial and task-dependent.
