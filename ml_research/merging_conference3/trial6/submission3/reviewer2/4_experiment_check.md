# 4. Experiment Check

## Critical Evaluation of the Experimental Setup and Sandbox
The authors evaluate their proposed BWS-Router using two distinct environments:
1. **Task-Conflict Model-Merging Sandbox:** A simulated low-dimensional representation space ($D = 192$) representing four classification tasks (MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, SVHN). The "layers" are represented as redundant dimensions in a tensor, and the routing coefficients are averaged across layers before being applied to the classification head.
2. **Physical Sequential Model-Merging Framework:** A PyTorch implementation using 3-layer MLP experts, where representation propagation is sequential and weights are physically blended at runtime.

### Weaknesses in the Setup:
- **Highly Stylized and Simulated Sandbox:** The virtual sandbox is an artificial representation-space simulation rather than a real deep neural network operating on image pixels. While this allows for high-throughput sweeps, findings from such a highly controlled, linear, and low-dimensional setup (e.g., $D=192$, single-layer classification heads) may not translate to massive non-linear deep models (such as Vision Transformers or LLMs) where complex, non-linear activation functions and sequential layer dependencies dominate.
- **Artificial Noise Calibration:** To simulate a noisy out-of-distribution (OOD) domain, the authors manually add a high-variance Gaussian noise ($\sigma_{noise} = 0.80$) to SVHN inputs, forcing a very low expert ceiling of 30.16%. This manual degradation is highly artificial and does not reflect realistic OOD shifts. It makes the "OOD SVHN collapse" and its resolution highly stylized rather than representative of real-world OOD generalization.

## Baselines and Comparisons
The paper compares BWS-Router against L3-Router (unshared layer-wise routing), QWS-Merge (wavefunction-inspired routing), and Static Uniform merging. 
However, several key baselines and comparison points are missing or under-evaluated:
- **No Non-Linear Gating or PEFT Baselines:** There is no comparison against standard parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) routing baselines, such as LoRA-MoE, adapter-based routing, or standard multi-task learning models, which would establish a more competitive performance ceiling.
- **Lack of Physical Experiments for Intermediate Block Sizes:** The authors argue qualitatively in Section 4.3 that "intermediate configuration sizes (such as $M=3$ or $M=4$) represent the optimal architectural sweet spot" by preserving "coarse-to-fine functional specialization" while mitigating sequential representation drift. However, in their physical sequential model-merging experiments (Section 4.5), they use a 3-layer MLP expert backbone ($L=3$). For a 3-layer MLP, the block sizes can only be $M=1$ (fully unshared) or $M=3$ (fully global sharing, where $G=L/M=1$). Consequently, **the authors never empirically test or validate intermediate block-sharing sizes inside the physical framework.** Their arguments regarding functional specialization and intermediate block sweet spots remain purely speculative and unvalidated by their physical experiments.

## Evaluation of Results and Claims
A close inspection of the results reveals several discrepancies and weaknesses in the claims:
1. **Unshared Superiority in Homogeneous Physical Streams:** In Table 4 (physical sequential merging), under the Homogeneous stream, the unshared physical router ($M=1$) actually **outperforms** the block-shared router ($M=3$) by 2.78% absolute accuracy (**48.04 $\pm$ 6.71%** vs. **45.26 $\pm$ 10.11%**). This directly contradicts the main thesis that block-sharing is universally superior. It shows that in stable homogeneous streams, unshared routing is preferred because it can specialize at each layer, and the shared model suffers a performance degradation.
2. **catastrophic Variance and Fragility in Physical Merging:** The standard deviations reported in physical sequential merging (Table 4) are extremely large: **22.49%** for $M=3$ Heterogeneous and **21.28%** for $M=1$ Heterogeneous. Under certain random seeds, the model suffers from localized task collapse (e.g., CIFAR-10 accuracy dropping to 3.30%). Furthermore, the overall accuracies achieved (43%--48%) are extremely low compared to the Expert Ceiling (~81%) or the sandbox performance (~79%). This reveals that physical sequential weight-blending remains highly fragile and fundamentally unsolved. The proposed block-sharing heuristic does not provide a robust, mathematically guaranteed solution to representation drift; it merely reduces variance relative to a very poor unshared baseline, while still suffering from catastrophic seed-dependent fluctuations.
3. **Sandbox Global Router Superiority:** In Table 3 (block-wise sweep), the global router ($M=12$, representing $G=1$ block group) achieves the absolute highest Joint Mean accuracy of **79.60 $\pm$ 1.15%**, using only 20 parameters. This outperforms the unshared baseline ($M=1$, 79.30%) and matches or outperforms the intermediate blocks ($M=3$, 79.57%). If a single global router performs best and uses the fewest parameters, the complexity of implementing intermediate block-wise weight sharing is empirically unjustified within their primary sandbox setup.

**Conclusion:** The experimental results do not fully support the paper's sweeping claims. The sandbox evaluation is highly artificial, and the physical validation is limited to a 3-layer MLP where intermediate block-sharing could not be tested. Furthermore, physical merging exhibits massive seed-wise instability and poor absolute performance, demonstrating that BWS-Router is a heuristic stabilizer rather than a robust, theoretically grounded solution.
