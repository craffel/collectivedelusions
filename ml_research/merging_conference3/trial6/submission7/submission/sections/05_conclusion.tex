\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this paper, we have presented a relentless application of Occam's razor to the problem of dynamic weight routing under heterogeneous deployment streams. We systematically deconstructed existing over-parameterized routing architectures, proving that complex, wave-inspired "quantum" metaphors (such as QWS-Merge) suffer from transductive OOD collapse and are easily outperformed by classical $L_2$ regularization. Furthermore, we analytically deconstructed and empirically validated the phenomenon of Layer-Averaging Collapse, demonstrating that layer-wise dynamic parameters collapse to a redundant single-layer search space when merging models with a single joint classification head.

To resolve these failures, we introduced \textbf{Parameter-Free Subspace Routing (PFSR)} combined with \textbf{Micro-Batch Homogenization (MBH)}. By stripping away $100\%$ of the trainable routing parameters and addressing stream heterogeneity at the data level rather than the weight level, our zero-shot framework achieves a high \textbf{75.00\% Joint Mean accuracy} under homogeneous streams and maintains a highly robust \textbf{71.60\% Joint Mean accuracy} under heterogeneous mixed-task streams, coming remarkably close to the expert standalone ceilings ($79.80\%$) with absolutely zero optimization overhead. 

Our findings suggest that many of the difficulties encountered in test-time adaptation and dynamic routing are not fundamental model limitations, but are rather self-inflicted artifacts of architectural over-engineering. We hope our minimalist paradigm encourages a shift in the model-merging community back toward simple, transparent, and robust design principles.
