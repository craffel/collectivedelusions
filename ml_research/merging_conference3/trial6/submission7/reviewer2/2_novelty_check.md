# Novelty and Delta Analysis

## 1. Key Novel Aspects
The paper proposes to solve the issues of optimization bloat, out-of-distribution (OOD) overfitting, and stream heterogeneity collapse in dynamic model merging. Its core novel aspects include:
- **Parameter-Free Subspace Routing (PFSR):** Rather than learning a parametric gating network, the method computes the cosine similarity between penultimate-layer representations and the frozen classification heads of pre-trained expert models to determine merging weights.
- **Micro-Batch Homogenization (MBH):** Instead of modifying the routing network to handle heterogeneous (mixed-task) batches, the authors propose a systems-level scheduling mechanism that dynamically splits a heterogeneous batch into task-homogeneous micro-batches, executes specialized merged inference on each, and re-assembles the outputs.
- **Statistical Class-Size Scaling Calibration:** A scaling factor derived from the extreme value theory of high-dimensional random variables ($O(\sqrt{\log C_k / d})$) to neutralize statistical routing bias toward experts with larger output vocabularies.
- **Co-Design of Systems and Algorithms:** A holistic framework combining Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT/LoRA) with dynamic streaming scheduling to manage memory footprints and PCIe latency bottlenecks on resource-constrained hardware.

## 2. 'Delta' from Prior Work
- **From Parametric to Non-Parametric Routing:** Prior state-of-the-art dynamic routing methods (e.g., QWS-Merge, L3-Linear) utilize trainable routing layers optimized via gradient descent (AdamW) on small calibration splits. The delta here is the complete elimination of trainable routing parameters and calibration data requirements, utilizing the pre-trained classification heads as zero-shot task prototypes.
- **From Algorithmic Robustness to Data-Stream Scheduling:** Previous attempts to mitigate batch heterogeneity (e.g., L3-Softmax) relied on simplex-normalization constraints within the routing network, which the authors expose as a "Robustness-Accuracy Illusion" that merely flattens routing coefficients to a mediocre average. The delta here is to shift the burden of batch mixedness from the model parameters to the data-stream orchestration layer (MBH), preserving high expert specialization.
- **Deconstruction of Complex Formulations:** The paper provides a direct empirical and analytical critique of predecessor methods (QWS-Merge and layer-wise routers), demonstrating that simpler, regularized classical baselines or single-layer global routers perform better than highly parameterized, wave-inspired or multi-layer alternatives.

## 3. Characterization of Novelty
As a critical reviewer, the novelty of this submission is characterized as **incremental and predominantly engineering-focused**, rather than a major scientific or algorithmic breakthrough. The primary reasons are as follows:

- **Algorithmic Simplicity and Prior Art:** The core routing mechanism (PFSR) is conceptually identical to computing cosine similarity against class prototypes, a standard concept in Prototypical Networks (Snell et al., 2017) and zero-shot metric learning. Applying this established metric learning concept to routing in model merging is a straightforward, incremental application.
- **Simplistic Scheduling Heuristic:** Micro-Batch Homogenization (MBH) is essentially a dynamic partitioning and sequential dispatching heuristic. Grouping requests by an identifier (the dominant task coordinate) and running sequential forward passes is a standard request scheduling technique in systems-ML (conceptually similar to request batching in frameworks like Orca or vLLM). The "novelty" lies in applying this standard systems trick to weight-space merging, which is an engineering-level contribution.
- **Redundant "Tautological" Bypass:** MBH solves heterogeneity collapse by explicitly reforming homogeneous batches. Thus, the model itself does not actually learn to handle heterogeneous streams; instead, the infrastructure simply bypasses the problem by running sequential passes. This shifts the complexity from the model architecture to the serving infrastructure, rather than presenting a novel algorithmic solution to heterogeneous merging.
- **Over-Simplified Analytical Novelty:** The mathematical proof of Layer-Averaging Collapse relies on highly idealized, restrictive assumptions (such as the linear Taylor expansion of localized PEFT perturbations, contractive Jacobians, and near-collinear representation manifolds in deep layers). While it provides a convenient explanation for why multi-layer routers overfit in their specific toy sandbox, it does not constitute a general or robust proof for complex, highly non-linear deep neural networks where early and late layers are semantically distinct.
