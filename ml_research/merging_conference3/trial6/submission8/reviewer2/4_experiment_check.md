# 4. Experimental Setup and Evaluation Analysis

## Evaluation of Experimental Setup and Datasets
The paper employs a two-tier evaluation framework: (1) a primary quantitative evaluation within a synthetic "Parameter-Space Representation Sandbox" modeling a 14-layer Vision Transformer, and (2) a physical validation experiment on a 4-layer SimpleCNN trained on real images.

### Dataset Limitations
* **Scale of Datasets:** The evaluation is restricted to four classic, low-resolution, and relatively clean vision datasets: MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR-10, and SVHN. These datasets are highly homogeneous and represent "toy" settings compared to modern production benchmarks. 
* **Lack of Real-World Complexity:** A practitioner would want to see how this method handles real-world domain shifts and high-resolution, complex datasets (such as DomainNet, ImageNet-C, or multi-task natural language benchmarks). Low-resolution classification task vectors do not capture the high-dimensional representation conflicts found in modern multi-modal or large language foundation models.

### Sandbox Proxy vs. Real Architecture
* **Lack of Direct ViT Physical Execution:** While the sandbox is mathematically elegant, it does not involve loading actual Vision Transformer weights or processing raw pixels. All quantitative results for ViT (including the Pareto curves showing 71.3% speedups and VRAM savings) are generated by a simulation proxy. For a production engineer, this introduces significant skepticism about whether these exact numbers and trade-offs transfer to physical GPU/TPU environments.

## Analysis of Baselines
The paper compares the proposed methods against a robust and highly appropriate set of baselines:
1. **Uniform Merge (Task Arithmetic):** Provides the lower-bound baseline representing a zero-latency static merge.
2. **AdaMerging (SOTA Static Merging):** An excellent inclusion representing the state-of-the-art in offline static parameter optimization. Comparing against AdaMerging proves that the latency/VRAM overhead of Hybrid-Router is justified by substantial accuracy gains (+4.44% absolute improvement at $k=4$).
3. **Linear Router (Classical Dynamic):** Represents standard fully dynamic ensembling ($k=14$), showing the upper-bound accuracy under homogeneous conditions.
4. **QWS-Merge (SOTA Dynamic):** A strong, recent dynamic ensembling baseline that uses wave-superposition cosine boundaries.
5. **PEFT/LoRA Serving Frameworks (Punica, S-LoRA):** Included in a qualitative architectural comparison (Section 4.5). This is highly valuable, mapping out the concrete engineering trade-offs regarding VRAM storage, latency, custom kernels, and universal portability.

## Do the Results Support the Claims?

### 1. Latency-Accuracy-Memory Pareto Frontier (Supported in Sandbox Only)
The sandbox sweeps (Table 2) strongly support the claim that layer-wise partitioning ($k=4$) creates a highly favorable Pareto frontier. It reduces weight reconstruction latency by 71.3% and task-vector VRAM by 71.4% while maintaining high multi-task accuracy (76.75%), significantly outperforming static AdaMerging (72.31%). However, because this is evaluated in the sandbox, these exact physical savings remain unverified on real physical Vision Transformers.

### 2. Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox (Not Physically Supported)
The sandbox results (Table 2) support the claim that $k=12$ outperforms fully dynamic $k=14$ ensembling (+0.22% absolute gain) under scarce calibration data (64 samples) due to structural regularization.
However, as shown in the physical SimpleCNN sweep, **this paradox is not physically observed.** The physical CNN accuracy increases monotonically with dynamic depth $k$, where the fully dynamic model ($k=4$) performs the best. While the authors' capacity-based explanation is sound, it means the Overfitting-Optimizer Paradox is not yet verified on real weights and image pixels, remaining a localized sandbox phenomenon.

### 3. Softmax-Sigmoid Scaling Gap (Well Supported)
The ablation isolating the scaling bounds of BSigmoid-Router (Table 1) strongly supports the claim that the performance gap is driven by standard scaling ceilings. Removing the $\lambda_{\text{max}} = 0.3$ constraint and setting it to $1.2$ immediately recovers accuracy to 94.93%, proving that independent sigmoid routing is highly competitive with Softmax.

### 4. Dynamic Batch Filtering (DBF) Efficacy (Well Supported)
The streaming benchmarks (Table 3) and physical CNN validation under heterogeneous streams strongly support the efficacy of DBF.
* In the sandbox at $B=256$, BSigmoid-Router + DBF improves accuracy from 66.63% to 83.18% (+16.55% absolute gain).
* In the physical CNN streaming evaluation, DBF achieves massive absolute accuracy gains (+27.59% at $B=16$ and +30.56% at $B=64$). These physical experiments provide strong empirical validation of the DBF concept under streaming domain shifts.

## Practitioner Critiques on the Experimental Setup
1. **CPU vs. GPU Latency Profiling:** The ensembling latency is profiled on an AMD EPYC CPU. In real-world production, deep models are deployed on parallel GPUs. GPU execution dynamics (including kernel launches, memory-bandwidth saturation, and asynchronous stream scheduling) are vastly different from sequential CPU operations. Although Section 4.6 provides an excellent qualitative discussion of parallel GPU execution and CUDA streams, the lack of **measured physical GPU latency numbers** for the physical SimpleCNN remains a gap.
2. **Streaming Realism:** The heterogeneous stream assumes random shuffling of tasks. In real-world edge scenarios (e.g., smart cameras), data has high temporal coherence (e.g., a camera sees digits for a while, then objects). The authors note that under style-homogeneous streams, DBF falls back to standard single reconstruction ($M=1$) to save latency. However, they do not include a temporally coherent stream baseline in the experiments, which would have shown exactly how much latency is saved in realistic scenarios.
